In the context of the [history of the United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_United_States "History of the United States"), [political violence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_violence "Political violence") is defined as [violence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Violence "Violence") which the state or a [non-state actor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-state_actor "Non-state actor") perpetrates in order to achieve political goals. Political violence has occurred throughout American history—from the [assassinations of four presidents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_United_States_presidential_assassination_attempts_and_plots "List of United States presidential assassination attempts and plots") to [civil unrest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_incidents_of_civil_unrest_in_the_United_States "List of incidents of civil unrest in the United States") and [terrorist attacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrorism_in_the_United_States "Terrorism in the United States"). Experts report that incidents have increased significantly since 2016, reaching levels not seen since the 1970s. 21st-century data indicates that the highest incidence of deadly political violence in the United States has come from [right-wing extremists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-wing_extremists "Right-wing extremists"), with [left-wing extremists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Left-wing_extremists "Left-wing extremists") and [Islamists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamists "Islamists") being the other main ideological categories. Contributing factors toward political violence include dehumanizing [political rhetoric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_rhetoric "Political rhetoric") and [partisan division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_politics "Identity politics"), [authoritarian personality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarian_personality "Authoritarian personality") traits, hostile [sexist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexist "Sexist") beliefs, [online radicalization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Online_radicalization "Online radicalization"), and a sense of threat or increased fear.

Although widely discussed, realized acts of political violence in the United States are rare and constitute about 1% of all [hate crimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hate_crime "Hate crime").[^1]

## Definition

[Reuters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reuters "Reuters") defined political violence thusly: "Incidents linked to an election or a partisan political dispute, or premeditated acts driven by an identifiable ideology. Random hate crimes and violence involving police such as officer-involved killings or aggressive crowd-control tactics at protests were not included." [^2] The violence-prevention organization Over Zero defines it thusly: "Physical harm or intimidation that affects who benefits from or can participate fully in political, economic, or sociocultural life." [^3]

## Chronology

### 18th century

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Hamilton-burr-duel.jpg/250px-Hamilton-burr-duel.jpg)

A 1902 illustration of the Burr–Hamilton duel

Politically motivated violence was common during the [American Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Revolution "American Revolution"). In the late 1760s, [Sons of Liberty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sons_of_Liberty "Sons of Liberty") militants would often attack suspected British loyalists, [tarring and feathering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tarring_and_feathering "Tarring and feathering") them, a practice brought from Europe which resulted in lacerations and scarring.[^4] Hangings, raids, and executions became more frequent in the 1770s.[^4]

[Henry Brockholst Livingston](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Brockholst_Livingston "Henry Brockholst Livingston"), who had survived an assassination attempt in 1785, killed a man in a 1798 gun duel. Livingston's reputation was unharmed by the incident and he later became a [Supreme Court](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States "Supreme Court of the United States") justice.[^4] [Another duel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gwinnett%E2%80%93McIntosh_duel "Gwinnett–McIntosh duel") took place between former Georgia governor and [Declaration of Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence "United States Declaration of Independence") signatory [Button Gwinnett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Button_Gwinnett "Button Gwinnett") and his opponent [Lachlan McIntosh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lachlan_McIntosh "Lachlan McIntosh") in 1777; Gwinnett died after the shooting.[^5] A number of [other pistol duels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_duels_in_the_United_States "List of duels in the United States") took place in the 18th century.[^4]

### 19th century

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Lincoln_assassination_slide_c1900_-_Restoration.jpg/250px-Lincoln_assassination_slide_c1900_-_Restoration.jpg)

A depiction of Lincoln's assassination

Violence continued after the Revolution. In 1804, vice president [Aaron Burr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aaron_Burr "Aaron Burr") killed [Founding Father](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Founding_Fathers_of_the_United_States "Founding Fathers of the United States") [Alexander Hamilton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Hamilton "Alexander Hamilton") in [a gun duel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burr%E2%80%93Hamilton_duel "Burr–Hamilton duel").[^6] The [Know Nothing party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Know_Nothing "Know Nothing") was responsible for a number of attacks against Catholics and immigrants in the early 1800s. In 1844, [Joseph Smith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Smith "Joseph Smith"), the founder of [Mormonism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mormonism "Mormonism") and the [Latter-Day Saint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latter-Day_Saint "Latter-Day Saint") movement, announced his [candidacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Smith_1844_presidential_campaign "Joseph Smith 1844 presidential campaign") for the presidency of the United States. He became the first assassinated presidential candidate in U.S. history when he was [shot and killed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Killing_of_Joseph_Smith "Killing of Joseph Smith") by a mob on June 27.[^7]

The topic of slavery also ignited several episodes of violence near Kansas during the 1850s.[^8] The lead-up to the [American Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Civil_War "American Civil War") saw regional violence like the conflict over the expansion of slavery known as [Bleeding Kansas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bleeding_Kansas "Bleeding Kansas") and local protests against the [Fugitive Slave Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fugitive_Slave_Act_of_1850 "Fugitive Slave Act of 1850") including the [Christiana Riot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiana_Riot "Christiana Riot") and the assemblies organized by the [Boston Vigilance Committee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston_Vigilance_Committee "Boston Vigilance Committee"), as well as incidents at the [U.S. Capitol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Capitol "U.S. Capitol") such as the [caning of Charles Sumner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caning_of_Charles_Sumner "Caning of Charles Sumner") and the [1858 Congressional brawl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1858_Congressional_brawl "1858 Congressional brawl").[^9]

In 1856, abolitionist [John Brown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Brown_\(abolitionist\) "John Brown (abolitionist)") with like-minded settlers [killed five pro-slavery settlers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pottawatomie_massacre "Pottawatomie massacre") north of Pottawatomie Creek in Franklin County, Kansas.[^10] In 1859, Brown led [a raid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Brown%27s_raid_on_Harpers_Ferry "John Brown's raid on Harpers Ferry") on the [United States arsenal at Harpers Ferry, Virginia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harpers_Ferry_Armory "Harpers Ferry Armory") in an effort to initiate a [slave revolt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slave_revolt#North_America "Slave revolt") in [Southern states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antebellum_South "Antebellum South"). It has been called the "dress rehearsal" for the [American Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Civil_War "American Civil War").[^11]<sup><span title="Page / location: 5">: 5</span></sup>

President [Abraham Lincoln](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln "Abraham Lincoln") was [assassinated in 1865](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assassination_of_Abraham_Lincoln "Assassination of Abraham Lincoln"), and he had suffered two assassination attempts before that. The first president to be assassinated, Lincoln was shot by [John Wilkes Booth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Wilkes_Booth "John Wilkes Booth") while watching a theatre production.[^12] One motive attributed to his killing was his support for Black rights.[^12] The [Reconstruction era](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reconstruction_era "Reconstruction era") was also a period of considerable violence, including multiple [Freedmen massacres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedmen_massacres "Freedmen massacres") and assassinations of federal appointees and locally elected officials.[^13] In 1881, president [James A. Garfield](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_A._Garfield "James A. Garfield") was [killed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assassination_of_James_A._Garfield "Assassination of James A. Garfield") six months after taking office.[^14] [^12]

### 20th century

#### 1900–1920

President [William McKinley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_McKinley "William McKinley") was [assassinated in 1901](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assassination_of_William_McKinley "Assassination of William McKinley") by anarchist activist [Leon Czolgosz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leon_Czolgosz "Leon Czolgosz"), who was opposed to "the Republican form of government".[^15] [^14] Eleven years later, former president [Theodore Roosevelt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt "Theodore Roosevelt") survived [a gunshot in 1912](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attempted_assassination_of_Theodore_Roosevelt "Attempted assassination of Theodore Roosevelt") while campaigning to return to office.[^12] President-elect [Franklin D. Roosevelt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt "Franklin D. Roosevelt") survived a [shooting during a rally in 1933](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attempted_assassination_of_Franklin_D._Roosevelt "Attempted assassination of Franklin D. Roosevelt"), but Chicago mayor [Anton Cermak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anton_Cermak "Anton Cermak") was killed.[^15]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/1914_damage_detail%2C_N.Y._house_wrecked_by_Caron_bomb_LCCN2014696452_%28cropped%29.tif/lossy-page1-250px-1914_damage_detail%2C_N.Y._house_wrecked_by_Caron_bomb_LCCN2014696452_%28cropped%29.tif.jpg)

The building where the bomb intended for Rockefeller exploded, 1914

Bomb attacks were relatively common in the early 20th century. A [terrorist bomb exploded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lexington_Avenue_explosion "Lexington Avenue explosion") prematurely inside an apartment of the [Anarchist Black Cross](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anarchist_Black_Cross "Anarchist Black Cross") in 1914. The bomb was intended to kill business magnate [John D. Rockefeller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_D._Rockefeller "John D. Rockefeller"), who had been blamed for the [Ludlow Massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludlow_Massacre "Ludlow Massacre").[^16] Another bomb set up by labor leaders [exploded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preparedness_Day_bombing "Preparedness Day bombing") in San Francisco during a [Preparedness Day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preparedness_Day "Preparedness Day") parade in 1916, killing 10 people.[^17] The [Sacramento Governor's Mansion was bombed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1917_Sacramento_Governor%27s_Mansion_bombing "1917 Sacramento Governor's Mansion bombing") in 1917 during an attempt to assassinate governor [William Stephens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Stephens_\(American_politician\) "William Stephens (American politician)").[^18] About 30 bombs were sent to political leaders in the country, including U.S. [attorney general](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Attorney_General "United States Attorney General") [A. Mitchell Palmer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A._Mitchell_Palmer "A. Mitchell Palmer"), in 1919.[^19]

Tensions over housing and labor following [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_in_World_War_I "United States in World War I") led to the [Red Summer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Summer "Red Summer") of 1919, which involved [racial terrorism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnic_conflict "Ethnic conflict") and riots across the United States.[^20] The death of a Black child at a [segregated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Racial_segregation_in_the_United_States "Racial segregation in the United States") swimming area, combined with labor tensions between White and Black workers, [led to riots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Summer#Chicago_riots:_July_27%E2%80%93August_12 "Red Summer") that killed thirty-eight people.[^20] Black workers negotiating for better pay in [Elaine, Arkansas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elaine,_Arkansas "Elaine, Arkansas"), led to [one of the most violent riots in U.S. history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elaine_massacre "Elaine massacre").[^20]

#### 1920–1940

Political radicals exploded a [bomb in Wall Street](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wall_Street_bombing "Wall Street bombing") in 1920, killing 38 people.[^21] [^22] In 1921, the Black community of [Tulsa, Oklahoma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tulsa,_Oklahoma "Tulsa, Oklahoma"), known for their wealth and prosperity, [was destroyed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tulsa_race_massacre "Tulsa race massacre") by a violent White mob.[^20] In 1932, the [Bonus Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bonus_Army "Bonus Army") was cleared. In 1935, former Louisiana Governor and senator [Huey Long](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huey_Long "Huey Long") was assassinated during a legislative session. Long had been considering a presidential bid at the time. In 1937, the [Ponce massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ponce_massacre "Ponce massacre") was perpetrated.

#### 1940–1960

Following [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), Black veterans were attacked by White Americans who now had more competition for jobs and housing.[^20] President [Harry S. Truman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harry_S._Truman "Harry S. Truman") was the target of [an assassination attempt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attempted_assassination_of_Harry_S._Truman "Attempted assassination of Harry S. Truman") in 1950. He escaped uninjured, but two [White House police officers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_House_Police_Force "White House Police Force") were killed, in addition to one of the assailants.[^12] In 1951, civil rights activists [Harry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harry_T._Moore "Harry T. Moore") and [Harriette Moore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harriette_Moore "Harriette Moore") were [murdered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Murders_of_Harry_and_Harriette_Moore "Murders of Harry and Harriette Moore"). On March 1, 1954, four [Puerto Rican](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puerto_Ricans "Puerto Ricans") nationalists perpetrated the [1954 United States Capitol shooting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1954_United_States_Capitol_shooting "1954 United States Capitol shooting"), seeking to promote [Puerto Rican independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Independence_movement_in_Puerto_Rico "Independence movement in Puerto Rico") from the United States. Five members of [Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Congress "United States Congress") were injured.[^23]

#### 1960–1980

The 1960s have been called a "time of great upheaval and discord and division", which saw several major assassinations.[^24] [^25] Civil rights leader [Medgar Evers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medgar_Evers "Medgar Evers") was killed in 1963, hours after [John F. Kennedy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_F._Kennedy "John F. Kennedy") gave a televised [civil rights address](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Report_to_the_American_People_on_Civil_Rights "Report to the American People on Civil Rights").[^26] [^27] Rushed to a hospital, he was initially refused entry due to being Black.[^26] [^27] Later that year, [President Kennedy was assassinated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assassination_of_John_F_Kennedy "Assassination of John F Kennedy") by [Lee Harvey Oswald](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lee_Harvey_Oswald "Lee Harvey Oswald"); Oswald was killed two days later while being transported.[^12] Activist [Malcolm X](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malcolm_X "Malcolm X") was [assassinated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assassination_of_Malcolm_X "Assassination of Malcolm X") in 1965 by [Thomas Hagan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Hagan "Thomas Hagan"). Civil rights activist [Martin Luther King Jr.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Luther_King_Jr. "Martin Luther King Jr.") and Senator [Robert F. Kennedy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy "Robert F. Kennedy") were both assassinated in 1968.[^28] [King's assassination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assassination_of_Martin_Luther_King_Jr. "Assassination of Martin Luther King Jr.") led to [riots in more than 100 cities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_assassination_riots "King assassination riots"),[^20] and historian [Mark K. Updegrove](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_K._Updegrove "Mark K. Updegrove") stated that his death hindered the [civil rights movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_rights_movement "Civil rights movement") since King was such an effective leader.[^24] Robert F. Kennedy was seeking the presidential nomination of the [Democratic Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_Party_\(United_States\) "Democratic Party (United States)") when [he was killed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assassination_of_Robert_F._Kennedy "Assassination of Robert F. Kennedy"), and had recently won the California primary.[^12] [A large-scale riot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1968_Democratic_National_Convention_riots "1968 Democratic National Convention riots") at the [Democratic National Convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_National_Convention "Democratic National Convention") in Chicago in 1968 involved 10,000 [protesters against the war in Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opposition_to_United_States_involvement_in_the_Vietnam_War "Opposition to United States involvement in the Vietnam War") and 3,000 police officers, and was broadcast on live television.[^20] In 1968, protesters were shot in the [Orangeburg Massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orangeburg_Massacre "Orangeburg Massacre"). Two years later, protesters were shot in the [Kent State shootings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kent_State_shootings "Kent State shootings").

In 1969, [Black Panther Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Panther_Party "Black Panther Party") deputy chairman [Fred Hampton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fred_Hampton "Fred Hampton") was drugged, then shot and killed in his bed during a predawn raid at his Chicago apartment by a tactical unit of the [Cook County State's Attorney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cook_County_State%27s_Attorney "Cook County State's Attorney") 's Office, who received aid from the [Chicago Police Department](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicago_Police_Department "Chicago Police Department") and the [FBI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FBI "FBI") leading up to the attack. Law enforcement sprayed more than 100 gunshots throughout the apartment; the occupants fired once. During the raid, Panther [Mark Clark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_Clark_\(activist\) "Mark Clark (activist)") was also killed and several others were seriously wounded. Presidential candidate [George Wallace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Wallace "George Wallace") was shot at a campaign stop in [Maryland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maryland "Maryland"), in 1972 while seeking the Democratic presidential nomination, which left him [paraplegic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paraplegic "Paraplegic").[^12] President [Gerald Ford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gerald_Ford "Gerald Ford") was the target of two assassination attempts within a matter of weeks in 1975, and escaped both incidents uninjured.[^12] In 1976, [Orlando Letelier was assassinated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assassination_of_Orlando_Letelier "Assassination of Orlando Letelier").

During the 1960s and 1970s, many extremists joined left-wing groups, often spurred by the civil rights movement or the [Vietnam War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam_War "Vietnam War"). " [Underground movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Underground_movement "Underground movement") " radicals associated with groups like the [Weather Underground](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weather_Underground "Weather Underground"), the [New World Liberation Front](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_World_Liberation_Front "New World Liberation Front"), [Chicano Liberation Front](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicano_Liberation_Front "Chicano Liberation Front"), and the [Symbionese Liberation Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbionese_Liberation_Army "Symbionese Liberation Army") claimed responsibility for hundreds of small-scale bombings in the 1970s.[^29] In an eighteen-month period between 1971 and 1972, the FBI recorded 2,500 bombings in the U.S.—nearly five per day.[^29] They were usually detonated with prior warning or at night, resulting in few casualties and limited public outcry.[^29] [^2] The bombings were usually intended to destroy property such as government buildings, with the goal of affecting decision-making rather than taking lives.[^2] The deadliest such attack in the 1970s, targeting a Wall Street restaurant in 1975, killed just four people.[^29] Public acceptance of the bombings was so pervasive that when a small bomb was detonated in a [Bronx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Bronx "The Bronx") cinema, the audience refused to leave so they could keep watching the film.[^29]

#### 1980–2000

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Oklahomacitybombing-DF-ST-98-01356.jpg/250px-Oklahomacitybombing-DF-ST-98-01356.jpg)

Aftermath of the bombing in Oklahoma, 1995

A 1986 [Central Missouri State University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Missouri_State_University "Central Missouri State University") comparative analysis between left-wing and right-wing extremist groups commissioned by the [Department of Justice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice "United States Department of Justice") found that both left-wing and right-wing extremist groups pose dangers to the U.S., but right-wing groups were assessed as having greater potential impact due to their alignment with traditional American values.[^30] By the 1990s, more extremists were joining right-wing organizations, including militias and [white supremacist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_supremacy "White supremacy") groups.[^31]

President [Ronald Reagan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ronald_Reagan "Ronald Reagan") was [shot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attempted_assassination_of_Ronald_Reagan "Attempted assassination of Ronald Reagan") in 1981 after giving a speech in Washington, D.C.[^32] His press secretary, [James Brady](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Brady "James Brady"), was shot in the attack, leaving him paralyzed.[^12] An [assassination attempt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attempted_assassination_of_Bill_Clinton "Attempted assassination of Bill Clinton") of President [Bill Clinton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bill_Clinton "Bill Clinton") was carried out in 1994, which involved gunfire towards the [White House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_House "White House"); the perpetrator was sentenced to 40 years in prison.[^32] In 1984, writer and journalist [Henry Liu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Liu "Henry Liu") was assassinated. Another example of political violence was [1985 MOVE bombing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1985_MOVE_bombing "1985 MOVE bombing") by the [Philadelphia Police Department](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philadelphia_Police_Department "Philadelphia Police Department") during an armed battle with [MOVE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MOVE_\(Philadelphia_organization\) "MOVE (Philadelphia organization)"), a [Black liberation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_liberation "Black liberation") organization. A [bomb in Oklahoma City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oklahoma_City_bombing "Oklahoma City bombing") killed 168 individuals in 1995, which the FBI described as the country's worst act of domestic terrorism.[^2] The bomb was set up by [Timothy McVeigh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timothy_McVeigh "Timothy McVeigh"), who held antigovernment views.[^33] [^34] Tennessee Democratic state senator [Tommy Burks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tommy_Burks "Tommy Burks") was assassinated by a Republican rival in 1998, when he was running for reelection.[^35]

### 21st century

#### 2000–2020

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/2017_Charlottesville_ramming_%28car_involved%29.jpg/250px-2017_Charlottesville_ramming_%28car_involved%29.jpg)

Aftermath of the 2017 Charlottesville car attack

President [Barack Obama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barack_Obama "Barack Obama") was the target of [multiple failed assassination plots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Security_incidents_involving_Barack_Obama "Security incidents involving Barack Obama") during his presidency.[^36] [^37] [^38] In 2009, physician [George Tiller was murdered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Murder_of_George_Tiller "Murder of George Tiller"). In 2011, Congressional representative [Gabby Giffords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gabby_Giffords "Gabby Giffords") was shot in the head in [an attempted assassination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2011_Tucson_shooting "2011 Tucson shooting").[^32] [^39] [^40] The number of threats against members of Congress increased tenfold in the five years after 2016, falling again after 2020.[^41] Several Republican Congressmen were targets of a [mass shooting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congressional_baseball_shooting "Congressional baseball shooting") during a practice session for the annual [Congressional Baseball Game](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congressional_Baseball_Game "Congressional Baseball Game") in Alexandria, Virginia, in June 2017, with then House Majority Whip [Steve Scalise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Scalise "Steve Scalise") being critically injured.[^42] In 2016, [Micah Xavier Johnson shot police](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2016_shooting_of_Dallas_police_officers "2016 shooting of Dallas police officers"). A [car attack during a Charlottesville protest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlottesville_car_attack "Charlottesville car attack") in 2017 killed one person and injured 35 in August 2017.[^43] [Pipe bombs were planted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2018_United_States_mail_bombing_attempts "2018 United States mail bombing attempts") near the homes of Barack Obama and [Hillary Clinton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hillary_Clinton "Hillary Clinton") in 2018.[^44] A paper by the [Center for Strategic and International Studies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_for_Strategic_and_International_Studies "Center for Strategic and International Studies"), published in 2023, reported that three times more violent political acts and plots had been carried out since 2016 than in the last 25 years.[^45]

#### 2020–present

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/2021_storming_of_the_United_States_Capitol_DSC09254-2_%2850820534063%29_%28retouched%29.jpg/250px-2021_storming_of_the_United_States_Capitol_DSC09254-2_%2850820534063%29_%28retouched%29.jpg)

Supporters of then-President Donald Trump attempted to stop the counting of electoral votes on January 6, 2021, during the January 6 Capitol attack.

There were about 300 acts of political violence in the United States, from the [January 6 attack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/January_6_United_States_Capitol_attack "January 6 United States Capitol attack") to the 2024 election.[^46] [^45] According to the research, that was the largest surge since the 1970s.[^45] Political [violence during the 2024 election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_violence_in_the_2024_United_States_presidential_election "Political violence in the 2024 United States presidential election") was also at its highest since the 1970s, and most recent violence came from right-wing assailants.[^46] [^2]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Jan_6_pipe_bomb.jpg/250px-Jan_6_pipe_bomb.jpg)

A pipe bomb was placed near the DNC building during the January 6 attacks. Vice President Kamala Harris was driven 20 feet near one of them. 47

As of 2023, political violence comes "overwhelmingly from [the right](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-wing_politics "Right-wing politics") ", according to the [Global Terrorism Database](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Terrorism_Database "Global Terrorism Database"), FBI statistics, and other research.[^3] [^41] [^48] The [Anti-Defamation League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-Defamation_League "Anti-Defamation League") found that all of the 61 political killings in the U.S. from 2022 through 2024 were committed by right-wing extremists.[^49] A Princeton University study reported that the number of cases involving harassment and threats against local public officials had increased 74% in 2024 compared with 2022, totaling 600 cases.[^50] Serious threats against federal judges doubled from 2021 to 2023, according to the [U.S. Marshals Service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Marshals_Service "United States Marshals Service").[^25]

In one notable event, then [Republican presumptive nominee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2024_Republican_Party_presidential_primaries "2024 Republican Party presidential primaries") and former president [Donald Trump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Trump "Donald Trump") was [shot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attempted_assassination_of_Donald_Trump_in_Pennsylvania "Attempted assassination of Donald Trump in Pennsylvania") during a [rally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_demonstration "Political demonstration") in [Butler, Pennsylvania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Butler,_Pennsylvania "Butler, Pennsylvania"), in July 2024. The bullet narrowly missed his temple, hitting his ear before killing an audience member behind Trump and injuring two others.[^51] Later that year, there was a [second attempt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attempted_assassination_of_Donald_Trump_in_Florida "Attempted assassination of Donald Trump in Florida") on Trump's life in Florida.[^52] [^53] [^25]

The early 2020s saw [protesters cleared in D.C.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Trump_photo_op_at_St._John%27s_Church#Clearing_Lafayette_Square_and_St._John's "Donald Trump photo op at St. John's Church"), [a plot to kidnap the Democratic governor of Michigan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gretchen_Whitmer_kidnapping_plot "Gretchen Whitmer kidnapping plot"), [Gretchen Whitmer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gretchen_Whitmer "Gretchen Whitmer"), in October 2020,[^32] the [January 6 Capitol attack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/January_6_United_States_Capitol_attack "January 6 United States Capitol attack") in 2021,[^54] [^55] [^56] the [hammer attack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attack_on_Paul_Pelosi "Attack on Paul Pelosi") on Democratic Representative [Nancy Pelosi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nancy_Pelosi "Nancy Pelosi") 's husband [Paul Pelosi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Pelosi "Paul Pelosi") in 2022,[^57] [^25] and the [attempted assassination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brett_Kavanaugh_assassination_plot "Brett Kavanaugh assassination plot") of conservative Supreme Court justice [Brett Kavanaugh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brett_Kavanaugh "Brett Kavanaugh") in 2022 due to his position on the overturning of *[Roe v. Wade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roe_v._Wade "Roe v. Wade")*.[^25] [^32] [^58] In December 2024, [UnitedHealthcare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UnitedHealth_Group "UnitedHealth Group") CEO [Brian Thompson was killed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Killing_of_Brian_Thompson "Killing of Brian Thompson") in an allegedly politically motivated attack.[^59] [^60] In 2024, [a pro-Israeli group attacked protesters at UCLA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2024_UCLA_protests#Counter-protester_attack "2024 UCLA protests").

In April 2026, a [Kimberly-Clark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kimberly-Clark "Kimberly-Clark") warehouse was [set on fire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_Kimberly-Clark_Distribution_Center_fire "2026 Kimberly-Clark Distribution Center fire"). The arson was reportedly committed over labor grievances and pay grievances, in reportedly an [anti-capitalist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-capitalist "Anti-capitalist") sentiment. No deaths or injuries have been documented, but the property damage is estimated at $600 million, and the warehouse was considered a total loss.[^61]

#### Threats against local officials

Several U.S. cities reported increased threats against [local officials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_government_in_the_United_States "Local government in the United States") in 2024 and 2025, often connected to [municipal policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Municipal_government "Municipal government") decisions. In Utah, the mayor of [Salt Lake City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt_Lake_City "Salt Lake City") received repeated violent threats related to the adoption of new [city flag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flag_of_Salt_Lake_City "Flag of Salt Lake City") designs and other policy positions. A [Farmington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Farmington,_Utah "Farmington, Utah") man was arrested after police said he attempted to enter the [Salt Lake City–County Building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt_Lake_City_and_County_Building "Salt Lake City and County Building") and posted messages online encouraging violence toward the mayor and her family.[^62] He later pleaded guilty to misdemeanor [stalking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stalking "Stalking") in December 2025 and was subsequently sentenced to jail.[^63]

In 2025, there was an [arson attack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_Pennsylvania_Governor%27s_Residence_arson "2025 Pennsylvania Governor's Residence arson") on Democratic Pennsylvania governor [Josh Shapiro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Josh_Shapiro "Josh Shapiro") 's residence in April,[^64] [^65] a [fatal shooting of two Israeli embassy staffers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_Capital_Jewish_Museum_shooting "2025 Capital Jewish Museum shooting") in Washington, D.C., in May,[^66] [^67] and a June [anti-Zionist fire attack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_Boulder_fire_attack "2025 Boulder fire attack") in [Colorado](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colorado "Colorado").[^68] [Solomon Peña](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solomon_Pe%C3%B1a "Solomon Peña") was convicted of hiring men to shoot at various Democratic politicians in response to his loss in the [2022 New Mexico House of Representatives election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_New_Mexico_House_of_Representatives_election "2022 New Mexico House of Representatives election"), and [shootings of two Democratic Minnesota legislators and their spouses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_shootings_of_Minnesota_legislators "2025 shootings of Minnesota legislators") in June.[^69] [^70] In September 2025, [right-wing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-wing_politics "Right-wing politics") political activist [Charlie Kirk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Kirk "Charlie Kirk") was [assassinated during a public debate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assassination_of_Charlie_Kirk "Assassination of Charlie Kirk") in [Orem, Utah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orem,_Utah "Orem, Utah").[^71] Following this, historian [Mark K. Updegrove](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_K._Updegrove "Mark K. Updegrove") warned that political violence and rhetoric in the 2020s mirrored that of the 1960s.[^24] Mike Jensen of the [University of Maryland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Maryland,_College_Park "University of Maryland, College Park"), which has tracked political violence since the 1970s, said that 2025 experienced 150 politically motivated attacks in the first six months, almost twice as many as there were at the same point in 2024.[^72]

## Contemporary landscape

Data shows that political violence had "skyrocketed" in the years prior to 2021, with threats against election administrators reaching unprecedented levels in 2020.[^3] Speaking to [Reuters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reuters "Reuters") in 2023, [University of Maryland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Maryland,_College_Park "University of Maryland, College Park") criminologist [Gary LaFree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gary_LaFree "Gary LaFree") said political violence was at the worst level since the 1970s.[^73] Although widely discussed, realized acts of political violence in the United States are rare and constitute about 1% of all hate crimes.[^1]

The primary trend is the "ungrouping" of political violence as people self-radicalize via the [Internet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet "Internet"), with most political violence committed by people who do not belong to any formal organization.[^3] Journalist Stefan Grobe writes that extremist ideologies spread on [social media](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_media "Social media"), where "a crude language of memes, slang and jokes blurs the line between posturing and provoking violence, normalising radical ideologies and activities".[^74] Since 2016, there has been a large increase in attacks on politicians and government employees.[^75] [START](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Consortium_for_the_Study_of_Terrorism_and_Responses_to_Terrorism "National Consortium for the Study of Terrorism and Responses to Terrorism") researcher Michael Jensen recorded 150 politically motivated attacks in the first half of 2025, almost twice the number in the same period in 2024.[^76]

Studies of political violence in the United States have typically listed [right-wing extremist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-wing_extremist "Right-wing extremist"), [left-wing extremist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Left-wing_extremist "Left-wing extremist") and [Islamist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamist "Islamist") as the main ideological categories.[^75] [^77] [^78] [^79] [^80] There is no federal database of political violence in the United States, and studies have produced varied tallies depending on how violence and ideology is defined. Perpetrators of political violence, who may be mentally ill, often defy a clear ideological classification.[^75]
