I believe in gun control, but it is a fairy tale to believe that tougher laws are the answer.

Cole Tomas Allen, the 31-year-old Caltech graduate who tried to attack the White House Correspondents’ Dinner, acquired his weapons legally, years ago, according to the Justice Department. He trained. He took a [three-day train ride](https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/suspect-white-house-correspondents-dinner-shooting-charged-attempt-assassinate-president) to transport them to Washington, D.C., apparently without fear of detection. He [checked in](https://www.justice.gov/opa/media/1438246/dl) to the hotel hosting the dinner, President Donald Trump, and his cabinet, at 3 p.m. the day before. He methodically used his intellect to get close to the dinner, and then he rushed a secure area so full of Secret Service agents that he had no hope of reaching the door, much less crashing through it. What was the point of all that reconnaissance and forethought when there was no way to effect an assassination?

I’ll go with President Trump’s description of Allen. He’s a whack job. A whack job with an impressive level of intellect culminating in climactic stupidity.

Trump attracts assassins. At his black-tie briefing later last Saturday night, the president seemed to revel in this fact a bit, saying that assassins shoot only at the impactful presidents, Gerald Ford and James Garfield excepted. Then Trump laid some statistics on us about race car drivers having safer jobs than presidents. Exaggerated, but four presidents assassinated out of 45 is actually a pretty high percentage.

A presidential assassination attempt is a terrible thing. That’s why last year, the Secret Service’s budget for protective operations ran to $1.2 billion. If a president were killed, the devastation to public life and politics would justify 10 times that amount.

---

But even amid the bona fide life-and-death danger, there is still the possibility of overreaction. One such overreaction is the idea that this latest attempt on Trump’s life proves the need for a presidential ballroom. It doesn’t. A second overreaction is one that invariably arises in the wake of a mass shooting or attempted assassination. It comes from the gun-control crowd. Which, to put my cards on the table, includes me.

My side says gun violence must stop. My side says if we just had the right laws, it would stop. We point to countries with tighter laws, like Japan, where there are fewer than 10 gun murders a year. Yet Prime Minister Shinzo Abe was [assassinated in 2022](https://www.bbc.com/news/world-asia-62089486) by a man with a homemade weapon built in his apartment.

My side says the National Rifle Association (NRA) is the obstacle. In fact, the NRA is in collapse. Its former CEO, Wayne LaPierre, resigned. Membership fell from 5.5 million in 2018 to 3.8 million by 2023, and some board members put the real figure closer to 3 million. Revenue cratered from [$353 million](https://projects.propublica.org/nonprofits/organizations/530116130/201923189349313177/full) in 2018 to [$176 million](https://projects.propublica.org/nonprofits/organizations/530116130/202443189349307909/full) in 2023. In 2024, the NRA liquidated [nearly $40 million](https://www.notus.org/money/nra-national-rifle-association-selling-investments-stock-money-audit) from its investment portfolio to stay solvent. The NRA is not stopping the policy my side wants; the will of the people is.

My side says Americans support gun control but the politicians won’t listen. In fact, Americans support *some* gun-control measures but reject the underlying premise that guns should be banned. They’ve proven this not just in polls but at the ballot box and in their lives. Tens of millions of Americans feel connected to guns not because the NRA told them to, and not because they were lied to. They have that connection because they use guns. They like guns. They identify with guns. They respect guns. They find meaning in guns. We live in a democracy, and this is the will of the people.

There is no workable gun-control law that would have prevented those circumstances in acountry with a constitutional amendment allowing guns and a series of Supreme Court decisions strengthening that right.

My side says [we need a conversation](https://www.bostonglobe.com/2026/04/27/opinion/white-house-correspondents-dinner-shooting/). We don’t need a conversation. We can recite the beats of the conversation by rote because we’ve engaged in it a thousand times. As is true of many issues, Americans have fundamentally different views of what kind of gun laws we should have, if any. My side keeps losing because [more Americans disagree](https://www.pewresearch.org/politics/2024/06/06/gun-attitudes-and-the-2024-election/) with us than agree with us. Certain slivers of reform [do poll well](https://www.bradyunited.org/press/poll-trump-voters-gun-safety), but slivers aren’t the solutions that would end the horrors of gun violence and the corrosive threat of political violence. Do Americans want their guns? Yes, they do. Will they accept the occasional horrific mass casualty and the occasional assassination attempt as the price of that access? As we have all learned, they will. Rather than admit that, my side pretends the other side is captured, brainwashed, or bought.

Saturday’s alleged assassination attempt at the Washington Hilton is a very poor case for the passage of gun control. As far as we know about the accusations against Allen, a legal possessor of a long gun (not a handgun) brought it into Washington, D.C., and was quickly arrested after [running through](https://www.justice.gov/opa/media/1438246/dl) a security checkpoint. There is no workable gun-control law that would have prevented those circumstances in acountry with a constitutional amendment allowing guns and a series of [Supreme Court](https://nysba.org/the-supreme-courts-bruen-decision-and-its-impact-what-comes-next/?srsltid=AfmBOoolcXMl8yM-Z6Ji_xsN8-Cbj9M-MRRXFrhNbPLuElMAvKDJcdsm) decisions strengthening that right.

This is why I conclude, with sadness at the fact but annoyance at the discourse, that the answer to the question “What will solve the tragedy of gun violence in America?” is “nothing.” This is our condition. It is chronic and it is costly. Like a diabetic on insulin or a kidney patient requiring dialysis, we have to mitigate the damage, because there’s no way to converse our way out of carnage. You might call that pessimism. I call it realism.

---

Of course, the most destructive arguments after this attempt aren’t the ones saying we need gun control on the one hand, or more hardened targets on the other. Those are positions held in good faith. The most destructive arguments come from the false-flag and hoax crowd. The worst whack job isn’t the gunman. The worst are the people who claim there was no actual gunman or that the attack was staged.

Somewhere behind the nutters is the well-intentioned cadre of people on my side telling you that an achievable policy could fix this. It can’t. We don’t want to admit that, but the same commitment to realism that rejects the notion that this was a staged event also demands jettisoning the fairy tale that gun laws are going to turn the U.S. into something other than a nation with an extremely high number of [gun crimes](https://www.commonwealthfund.org/sites/default/files/2024-10/PDF_Gumas_health_costs_gun_violence_chartpack_final.pdf). We are an actual country actually awash in actual guns. The best response is a bad one, but it’s the one we have: to be guarded by better-trained and better-funded people carrying their own, more sophisticated guns, if you’re lucky enough to enjoy their protection. It isn’t a good place to be. But it’s where we are.
