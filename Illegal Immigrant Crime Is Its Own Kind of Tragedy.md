Few Democrats acknowledge what most Americans believe: Tolerance should be far lower for crimes committed by people who entered the country illegally.

Stephanie Minter, a 41-year-old single mother, was waiting at a bus stop in the Washington, D.C., suburb of Fairfax County, Virginia, on February 23, when Abdul Jalloh, an illegal immigrant from Sierra Leone with a long criminal record, approached and allegedly began slashing her with a knife. Minutes later, she was dead.

[The case](https://www.dhs.gov/news/2026/02/28/illegal-alien-more-30-prior-arrests-fatally-stabs-woman-virginia-bus-stop-ice) has caused a sensation in the D.C. area, with local media focusing on the role that lenient policies by Fairfax County’s progressive prosecutor and sheriff might have played in the horrific event. The prosecutor, Stephen Descano, has a policy of shaping criminal charges to [avoid putting defendants](https://wjla.com/news/local/fairfax-county-leaders-face-grilling-on-capitol-hill-over-sanctuary-policies) at risk of deportation. The sheriff, Stacey Kincaid, [will not hold](https://wjla.com/news/local/fairfax-county-steve-descano-stacey-kincaid-jeff-mckay-immigration-crime-sancutuary-policy-sheriff-commonwealths-attorney-abigail-spanberger-stephanie-minter-virginia) illegal immigrant detainees for Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) without a judicial warrant, which for technical reasons are rarely obtained in these cases.

The Republican-controlled House Judiciary Committee [held a hearing Thursday](https://judiciary.house.gov/committee-activity/hearings/fairfax-county-virginia-dangerous-consequences-sanctuary-policies) to highlight their stories. Titled “Fairfax County, Virginia: The Dangerous Consequences of Sanctuary Policies,” its emotional center was [testimony from](https://judiciary.house.gov/sites/evo-subsites/republicans-judiciary.house.gov/files/evo-media-document/minter-testimony_0.pdf) Stephanie Minter’s mother, Cheryl Minter. Her appearance tapped the narrative of “Angel Moms”—mothers of people victimized by illegal immigrants—that President Trump has repeatedly and successfully deployed politically.

“I just believe what I believe,” she said, choking up. “These vicious criminals—the criminals—that keep hurting our people should be outta here.”

Democrats on the committee pushed back against the Republican narrative and, to be sure, I found myself nodding in agreement with many of the points they made. There is no evidence that immigrants, legal or illegal, increase aggregate crime rates (Fairfax County itself is generally safe). Trump’s mass deportation campaign has swept up far too many innocent people. And Republicans are seizing on what happened in Fairfax County to score political points on immigration at a time when the president’s approval rating is tanking.

Yet what I didn’t hear from Democrats was any indication they understand why the Angel Mom stories resonate, politically and substantively, with so many Americans. The Democrats duly expressed condolences to Minter but showed no real appreciation for the gut feeling her words encapsulated: Every murder is repugnant and tragic, but there is something especially galling about violent crimes committed by people who shouldn’t be in the country in the first place. No appeal to favorable overall crime data can assuage it.

There has to be a middle ground between Trump’s demonization of all immigrants on one hand, and knee-jerk, open-borders progressivism on the other. Part of finding that place is validating the public’s moral intuitions about the crimes illegal immigrants are accused of committing.

There are several reasons ordinary voters have a right to feel especially aggrieved at crimes committed by illegal immigrants. The most salient is simply that the federal government had both the legal responsibility and the power to keep illegal immigrant perpetrators out of the country—meaning that these violent crimes are directly traceable to government failure.

Of course it’s a statistical truism that citizens and legal immigrants commit most crimes, including heinous ones like murder. But crime by people lawfully present is a risk society has implicitly bargained for. What the social contract does not include is the notion that uninvited guests can come in and cause catastrophic harm.

And while they’re not a very high percentage of all violent crimes, murders, rapes, and assaults by illegal immigrants aren’t rare. On April 2, Rolbert Joachin, a 40-year-old Haitian, allegedly [bludgeoned to death](https://www.foxnews.com/us/haitian-illegal-immigrant-fatally-beat-florida-store-clerk-hammer-face-death-penalty) a 51-year-old naturalized citizen named Nilufa Easmin at a gas station in Fort Myers, Florida. Jose Medina-Medina, a 25-year-old Venezuelan citizen in the U.S. illegally, [is charged](https://www.cbsnews.com/chicago/news/jose-medina-murder-loyola-student-sheridan-gorman-dna-sample/) with the March 19, 2026, murder of 18-year-old Loyola University Chicago freshman Sheridan Gorman. Famously, Venezuelan illegal immigrant Jose Ibarra is currently [serving a life term](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c23829939rjo) in Georgia for the murder of nursing student Laken Riley in 2024.

In Fairfax County, there have been only four homicides so far this year—but three of them were allegedly committed by undocumented immigrants.

Some progressives have argued deportation is an excessively harsh “collateral consequence” for criminal conduct, to which U.S. citizens are not subject, and that local prosecutors should tailor their charging and plea-bargaining decisions accordingly. As Talia Peleg, a law professor at the City University of New York, put it in a [2021 article](https://www.law.georgetown.edu/immigration-law-journal/in-print/volume-36-number-1-fall-2021/the-call-for-the-progressive-prosecutor-to-end-the-deportation-pipeline/), the “duty” of progressive prosecutors “is simple—to utilize their powers to avoid the double punishment of criminal sentence and deportation.”

Every murder is repugnant and tragic, but there is something especially galling about violent crimes committed by people who shouldn’t be in the country in the first place.

But there are collateral consequences throughout the criminal justice system, often pretty severe. A conviction can cost you a professional license, voting rights, or the right to own a weapon. These are administered through a civil process separate from the criminal process that is the prosecutor’s primary concern.

Nor is deportation merely an arbitrary add-on. Under U.S. law, noncitizens remain in the country subject to conditions established by Congress. One of those conditions has always been removability for serious criminal conduct. Imprisonment punishes the crime itself; deportation reflects the forfeiture of permission to remain.

And yet Descano, elected in 2019 as part of the “progressive prosecutor” movement, declared on his campaign website that “If two people commit the same crime, but only one’s punishment includes deportation, that’s a perversion of justice and not a reflection of the values of Fairfax County.” He took the website down in the wake of Stephanie Minter’s murder.

Where exactly the failure lies in the Minter case is a matter of debate. Descano correctly noted at the hearing that his office’s actual written policy is more nuanced than his campaign promises; it suggests that immigration consequences for defendants should be weighed on a “sliding scale,” with minor offenders receiving more consideration and violent offenders less. Still, the idea that there’s differential weighing of immigrants and nonimmigrants is the basis of a [civil-rights probe](https://www.foxnews.com/politics/soros-backed-prosecutor-hit-rebuke-dismissing-probe-trump-partisan-attacks-hey-scumbag) of Descano that the Justice Department opened earlier this month.

Despite police warnings about Jalloh’s dangerousness, [Descano’s office dismissed](https://wjla.com/news/local/descano-says-victims-wouldnt-aid-abdul-jalloh-prosecution-heres-what-we-found-illegal-immigrant-fairfax-county-commonwealths-attorney-steve-descano-testimony) several previous stabbing-related charges against Jalloh for reasons unrelated to his immigration status; the victims were homeless and could not be located to testify in court, a spokesperson for his office told me.

Despite warnings from local police regarding Jalloh’s escalating violence, Descano’s office [reportedly dropped](https://wjla.com/news/local/steve-descano-hot-seat-congress-fairfax-county-sanctuary-policies-sheriff-stacey-kincaid-house-lawmakers-illegal-immigrants-stephanie-minter-murder-abdul-jalloh-virginia-abigail-spanberger-ice-policy?fbclid=IwY2xjawRxu0hleHRuA2FlbQIxMQBzcnRjBmFwcF9pZBAyMjIwMzkxNzg4MjAwODkyAAEeWJPFqTzKvYEkqrN9OkUweb9UJuCybml4yZiI3OyQbFsX2oNM4TRv96qKDts_aem_voyysWcdObxKF9AK9Z1QyQ) multiple prior cases and reached an agreement that allowed Jalloh to remain unsupervised following a probation violation that could have triggered prison time.

It is also true that ICE actually had Jalloh [in custody](https://wjla.com/news/local/ice-held-abdul-jalloh-judges-ruling-forced-release-stephanie-minter-fairfax-county-illegal-immigrant) for most of 2019 and 2020, under the first Trump administration, but had to release him following a judge’s ruling that he could not be removed to his home nation of Sierra Leone. With no third country available to take him, ICE had to let him go. However, the fact that Fairfax County does not honor ICE detainers—requests to hold illegal immigrants in its custody pending deportation—makes it more difficult to deport them.

Crime by people lawfully present is a risk society has implicitly bargained for. What the social contract does not include is the notion that uninvited guests can come in and cause catastrophic harm.

There is a strange convergence between the political extremes on this issue. The restrictionist right favors deporting everyone, criminal or not, even if it means sending ICE agents to every car wash and Home Depot. The open-borders left is reluctant to deport anyone, even if they have committed a crime and might commit more. Both sides deny what Cheryl Minter’s plea implied: that the government should set priorities, and that illegal immigrants who engage in illegal conduct should be the focus of immigration enforcement.

For a moment at the beginning of the second Trump administration, there was actually a measure of convergence on this point. Chastened by their 2024 electoral defeat, some Democrats struck a tougher posture toward illegal immigration and acknowledged the public’s concern about crimes committed by people in the U.S. unlawfully.

In January 2025, Congress passed the [Laken Riley Act](https://abcnews.com/Politics/house-vote-laken-riley-act-bill-new-congress/story?id=117419935), which added burglary, theft, larceny, and shoplifting to the list of alleged offenses that trigger ICE detention. Forty-six House Democrats supported it, along with 12 in the Senate. The law corrected an omission that occurred in the Riley case; before her murder, the perpetrator, Ibarra, was arrested for shoplifting in Georgia and later released without any notification to ICE. Trump signed the act into law on January 29, 2025.

While mostly symbolic—local authorities still aren’t required to report alleged offenders to ICE or to hold them until ICE agents take them into custody—the law nevertheless embraced a “criminals first” attitude toward deportations.

Trump’s later indiscriminate roundup of suspected illegal immigrants, which culminated in the deaths of two U.S.-citizen protesters at the hands of ICE in Minneapolis, blew up that fragile consensus. A campaign to “abolish ICE” now enjoys substantial support among Democrats—and the Laken Riley Act is toxic to the party’s primary electorate. Democrats who supported the legislation are now disavowing it, including Maryland Rep. [April McClain Delaney](https://www.thebanner.com/politics-power/state-government/laken-riley-immigration-mcclain-delaney-DRKDSGP5OJHVJER4TAOXLZ2BCM/), who is running for reelection, and Minnesota Rep. [Angie Craig](https://www.foxnews.com/politics/democrat-key-senate-primary-says-she-regrets-vote-laken-riley-act-draws-gop-backlash), who is running for Senate.

If anyone seems to be course-correcting, it might be Trump, who has toned down his deportation drive since the bloody debacle in Minneapolis. Central to the revised approach—now in the hands of a new Homeland Security secretary, Markwayne Mullin, and White House border czar Tom Homan—is a more traditional focus on immigrants with criminal records or pending deportation orders.

It won’t be easy for Trump to undo the damage. Nevertheless, according to a recent survey by Third Way, a moderate Democratic policy shop, a 77 percent “supermajority” of voters, including 68 percent of Latinos, favor “targeted enforcement to remove undocumented immigrants with a criminal record, or those who are recent arrivals without legal basis to stay.”

This is the political sweet spot, and the future belongs to the party that comes closest to hitting it. What an irony—and what a failure for the Democrats—if, after all of Trump’s excesses, that party still ends up being the Republicans.
