## Breaking free from Notebook Engineering.

So, you and I, we're gonna have a chat … I'm gonna have a real-life no-bull discussion about hard topics. I truly hope you don’t get your poor feelings hurt and go running to Ma and tattle on me. Do what you have to; I can’t take it.

> Methinks that a Notebook Engineer is more of a way of life. Is it not?

It’s an identity, it’s who someone is. Or a team. Or a company.

```markup
What a “Notebook Engineer” is

A notebook engineer is someone who uses tools like Jupyter or Databricks notebooks 
as their primary way of building, running, and even deploying data workflows—often 
far beyond what notebooks were originally intended for.
```

Look. There is no longer any excuse. You don’t even have to write the flipping code yourself anymore. You can one-shot your way to Claude or Cursor glory while lying on the couch scrolling through Netflix. Don’t lie, I see you.

- *The main problem is that the Notebook Engineering mindset follows you down the AI path and probably just amplifies the bad habits you have already adopted.*

This is why a junior developer armed with Claude Code might ship more while still being a Junior Developer. Yes, with the capital letters and all. Everything has changed, and nothing has changed. **Code was never how someone got promoted to Senior+ levels in tech.**

Here is my list of ways people can break free from Notebook Engineering … but doing the OPPOSITE of what I list below. These are the traits of someone trapped in Notebook Engineering.

---

### Traits of Notebook Engineering Mindset

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!TPyq!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa77e8017-0886-467e-9d48-fa4bcba30313_1086x502.png)

- They do most (or all) development in notebooks rather than in proper codebases, scripts, or structured pipelines.
	- *It is no longer a helper or an exploratory tool; it’s a way of life.*
- They treat notebooks not just as a place to explore data, but as the final implementation, even in production environments.
- Optimizes for speed and ease, not rigor
- Avoids (or lacks) traditional SWE practices
- Testing is never done, or rarely done
- There is no system design or thinking. They see the world as one giant Notebook attached to Serverless.
- Common gaps:
	- *testing*
		- *modular code*
		- *version control hygiene*
		- *reproducibility*

Save me your speech on how you unit-test your Python Notebooks. I believe probably 1 out of 100 people do that. The truth is, there are no more excuses, not with Cursor and Claude becoming our fingers.

> *Do you struggle with writing code that reflects software best practices?*

There is almost nothing AI is better at than writing unit tests for you, making your code clean and modular, heck, it’s great at setting up CI/CD pipelines and the rest. All that busy work that kept you from doing the right thing in the first place, **you don’t even have to do it yourself anymore!**

### AI is widening the skills gap. And by skill, I don’t mean code.

The truth is, we’ve long lived in a bell curve when it comes to software and data. That isn’t going to change anytime soon; in fact, it’s going to be exaggerated. The best will get better, the worst will amplify their mistakes, and high rates.

- The Notebook mindset starts with a solution first.

That’s why someone gets stuck in the Notebook Engineering mindset in the first place. They weren’t taught, or never learned to treat software problems as esoteric. Thinking before writing, it never occurred to them.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!BvYR!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F819c43e3-7205-493c-8d81-76a67c55cd01_1598x1108.png)

AI, most of the time and on most teams, will not help that Notebook Engineer who’s only focused on Jira Task → PR within 15 minutes or less.

They don’t care how that code fits into the bigger picture; they don’t really understand what Claude did or why, and they never bothered to ask those two really important questions to the business. Cost implications? Yeah right. Downstream impacts? That’s laughable.

- Even AI won’t save them.

Instead of using the new tools we have at our disposal to deploy hardened, well-thought-out solutions, they use them to ship long-term tech debt and mediocre solutions at a rapid pace.

### Strange Things.

The belief that because AI is upon us, and we are going to all lose our jobs … ([an idea that is a bunch of clickbait](https://dataengineeringcentral.substack.com/p/will-ai-kill-data-engineering-software)), so we must each ship as much as possible and use every new AI tool available to do so … is simply … stupid.

It’s not either or.

It’s both. You can, and should, view the creation of Software as a very specific skillset that should be studied and honed over time, taken seriously as a craft, because that’s what it is.

> **You can use AI to generate your code, and escape the Notebook Engineering mindset, all at the same time.**

In fact, you really have no excuse for keeping the Notebook Engineering mentality anymore. Not with AI to help you do everything you were avoiding before.

Indeed, you should have more time to simply ask the important questions, understand the business context, think at a systems level, look for edge cases, test things, and set up deployment pipelines.

*Use that beautiful mind God gave you.* Think critically about something before you do it. Clean up your code, test it, talk to the business, set up smooth deployment lifecycles… then reap the rewards.
