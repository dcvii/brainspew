People have asked me to suggest a philosophy of mind reading list, something people could read to understand the literature that informs the topics I write about. The following is a suggested reading list for the philosophy of mind!

Now, it goes without saying, there is a ton of literature in this area, and I couldn’t possibly have surveyed it all before making these suggestions. But the following is a number of important texts to understand the core debates and history surrounding the ‘hard problem’ of consciousness.

The following are my suggestions with comments

## What is the Mind-Body Problem?

- **Ludwig, ‘Mind-Body Problem: An Overview’,** the first six pages are a good introduction to the mind-body problem as an ‘ontological’ problem. He also explains the difference between ‘consciousness’ and ‘intentionality’. These two things (consciousness and intentionality) are considered to be what constitutes ‘the mind’ in philosophy.
- **Churchland,** ***Matter and Consciousness*****,** ***A Contemporary Introduction to the Philosophy of Mind**.* This is another good source for the general issues in the philosophy of mind/mind-body problem space. See, e.g., the first chapter.

## Historical Background

- **Descartes,** ***Meditations*** (There are six meditations, and the sixth is where Descartes lays out most clearly his view on the separation of mind and body)
- **Gilbert Ryle,** ***The Concept of Mind*****.** The first three chapters of this book are the most relevant to understanding how analytic philosophy of mind views a variety of issues in the philosophy of mind. The introduction is enough to get an overview of the main issues.
	I also recommend the edition that has *Julia Tanney’s* introduction. Her introduction is very helpful and written 60 years after Ryle’s book was first published. Ryle’s reading of Descartes is essential to understanding how the philosophy of mind pans out in the 20th and 21st century. ***His interpretation of Descartes is more or less considered what Descartes said.***

Ryle is considered to have undermined dualism. As a result, much of the history preceding the emergence of the ‘hard problem’ are physicalist theories of mind.

## Behaviorism

Behaviorism — the view that we can explain the mind purely in behavioral terms —was one of the first physicalist responses to explaining the mind. While some contest that Ryle is a behaviorist (such as Tanney) you can read him as a behaviorist to understand this view. I think he is the best ‘philosophical’ source of behaviorism without getting too into the weeds on the different kinds that emerged.

So, you can read Ryle to understand behaviorism as a view.

## Identity Theorists

Identity theories, the view that consciousness *is* a physical brain process was a second big attempt to explain the mind in physical terms. See e.g.,

- **Smart, ‘Sensations and Brain Processes’**
- **U.T. Place ‘Is Consciousness a Brain Process?’**

## Functionalism

Functionalism, the view that we can explain the mind in terms of the role it plays in a particular cognitive system, emerges as a response to the perceived inadequacies of identity theories and behaviorism. See e.g.,

- **Putnam, ‘The Nature of Mental States’**
- **Block, ‘What is Functionalism?’**

## Problems With Functionalism

The problems that emerge for functionalism are in large part the reason the ‘hard problem of consciousness’ emerges. Two papers that discuss these issues are:

- **Block, ‘Troubles with Functionalism’**
- **Searle, ‘Minds, Brains, and Programs’**

## The Hard Problem of Consciousness

The hard problem of consciousness emerges from the perceived inadequacies of functionalism, I,e., for its inability to explain ‘what it is like’ to experience things. ***The hard problem is considered a major and unique obstacle to physicalism.***

Some papers on this include…

- **Nagel, ‘What is it like to be a bat?’**
- **Chalmers, ‘Facing up to the Hard Problem of Consciousness’**
- **Jackson, ‘Epiphenomenal Qualia’**

## Phenomenal Externalists

Phenomenal externalism is a kind of physicalist approach that argues the hard problem of consciousness is *not an obstacle to physicalism.* According to this view,we can explain experience in terms of representational content, the same way many philosophers claim we can explain intentionality (see Ludwig for a definition of intentionality, its also discussed in Searle). Examples include…

- **Tye, ‘Visual Qualia and Visual Content’**
- **Dretske, ‘Phenomenal Externalism’**

## What is the Physical?

Another major issue in this debate, which receives less attention than it deserves, is the fact that we have no definition or clear understanding of ‘the physical’.

Arguably, without an understanding of the physical, we cannot cogently state what the mind-body problem is (e.g., Chomsky) The literature often ignores this issue, but some have written about it. Some examples include…

- **Montero, ‘The Body Problem’**
- **Crane & Mellor ‘There is No Question of Physicalism’**
- **Chomsky, ‘Science, Mind, and the Limits of Understanding’**

## Panpsychism

An increasingly popular view the mind-body problem is panpsychism, the view that at least some of the fundamental physical world is experiential. The argument is intimately connected to admitting that we have little understanding of the physical, per the above readings. As such to explain consciousness, we need to posit that matter is in some sense conscious. Some examples include…

- **Strawson, ‘Why Physicalism Entails Panpsychism’**
- **Goff,** ***Galileos’s Error: Foundations for a New Science of Consciousness***

*Chalmers* has also written extensively on panpsychism. A short google will find his view. A lot of his papers are relatively inaccessible though, I think. Fair warning!

## Illusionism

Another important voice in the debate today are illusionists about the hard problem. Perhaps the best known is Keith Frankish. See e.g.,

- Frankish, Illusionism as a Theory of Consciousness

## Miscellaneous

This is more or less where the literature on the hard problem remains. There are physicalists, dualists, and panpsychists, as well as those who reject the cogency of the problem. Some other stuff worth reading to see some of these views include:

- **Gertler, ‘In Defence of Mind-Body Dualism’** (a dualist approach)
- **Kathleen Wilkes, ‘Is Consciousness Important?’** (a paper arguing that consciousness is not important to science. I read her as undermining the hard problem of consciousness)
