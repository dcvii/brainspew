The story of how an Iranian F-5 managed to bomb Camp Buehring in Kuwait — past its air defenses, in the opening days of a war the U.S. had been building toward for months — is damning on its own. But Mooch goes deeper this week, laying out evidence that the March 1 Iranian attack was a coordinated, multi-axis, multi-platform strike far more sophisticated and damaging than anything the Pentagon has acknowledged. For all the regional buildup ahead of Epic Fury, there were gaps in CENTCOM’s force protection posture that the Iranians found and exploited. It’s an uncomfortable story, and an important one.

Elsewhere, the aviation world is marking the loss of Denys Overholser, the Lockheed mathematician who cracked the physics of radar cross section and set the “Hopeless Diamond” — and eventually the F-117 Night Hawk — in motion. He was 86. Two encore episodes this week dig into Iran’s own aviation legacy: how Tehran kept its aging F-14 Tomcat fleet airborne despite decades of sanctions, and the remarkable combat record of Iranian ace Fereidoun Ali-Mazandarani, who racked up 11 kills during the Iran-Iraq War. ## RIP…

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!HOiB!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F96de6f8e-54f1-427f-b273-9ee08787129d_1280x720.jpeg)

The aviation world lost an icon recently. Denys Overholser, the Lockheed Martin engineer who unlocked the mathematics behind radar cross section and set in motion the development of the first operational stealth attack aircraft — forever transforming how combat planes are designed and how they fight — died April 28 at the age of 86. [Air & Space Forces Magazine](https://www.airandspaceforces.com/denys-overholser-stealth-pioneer-whose-work-led-to-the-f-117-dies-at-86/) has an excellent writeup on this mathematical genius and his contribution to the “Hopeless Diamond” — the precursor to Skunk Works’ “Have Blue” and F-117 Night Hawk (Photo: Lockheed Martin).
