Six days ago, in the early hours of Saturday morning, the Iranian regime was decapitated. In a joint military attack, the U.S. and Israel struck military sites across the country, killing Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, alongside dozens of senior Iranian leaders. “When we are finished, take over your government,” President Donald Trump urged the Iranians in the hours after the operation. “It will be yours to take.”

Then Iran started fighting back.

The regime’s retaliatory strikes have targeted Israel and U.S. installations across the Middle East, and countries across the region, including the United Arab Emirates, Qatar, Iraq, and Azerbaijan. In Kuwait, an Iranian drone strike killed [six U.S. Army Reserve service members](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/us-service-members-killed-iran-war-what-we-know/) —the first American casualties of the war. According [to Trump](https://x.com/realDonaldTrump/status/2028505632123326484), “there will likely be more before it ends.”

At home in the United States, polls show the public is skeptical of the war, with just [one in four](https://www.reuters.com/world/us/just-one-four-americans-support-us-strikes-iran-reutersipsos-poll-finds-2026-03-01/) saying they back the strikes. Some Trump supporters who backed him for his pledge to end overseas conflicts now fear that the country is sliding into another war. Others argue the opposite: that decisive, overwhelming force may prevent a far larger conflict later.

A week in, much remains unclear: How long could this war last? Could Iran slide into civil war? What kind of government might emerge after the fighting? How have the strikes changed the balance of power in the Middle East? And what does it mean for the world order?

To help us make sense of these questions and more, we asked a range of *Free Press* contributors—some skeptical of the war, others supportive—what they learned from what has been a seismic week. Here’s what they had to say.

#### Aaron MacLean: The Weakness of Iran Has Been Revealed

It turns out that after all these years of bullying its neighbors and pushing America around through a grand strategy of relentless terror, destabilization, and neo-imperialism, the mullahs were doing it with America’s permission all along. The Trump administration has now withdrawn that permission, and what’s left of Iran’s leadership manifestly cannot defend its revolutionary project effectively.

The revealed weakness of Iran in the face of largely unrestrained American power has two sets of consequences worth reflecting on.

Abroad, both in the Middle East but around the world, the potency of American deterrence will be increased. If the Trump administration succeeds in accomplishing its stated objectives of destroying the Iranian missile and nuclear programs and sinking its Navy—let alone if Israel prosecutes its goal of regime change with effectiveness—China, Russia, and North Korea will need to take notice. For all the reasonable concerns about interceptor stockpiles and other questions of resource allocation, these sorts of intangibles matter a great deal in international politics.

It turns out that after all these years of bullying its neighbors and pushing America around through a grand strategy of relentless terror, destabilization, and neo-imperialism, the mullahs were doing it with America’s permission all along.

At home, Donald Trump is revealed as a hawk. Not an idealistic nation-builder or a liberal internationalist by any means, but a man who is utterly comfortable with the use of military force abroad if it suits his broader objectives. Those objectives are sometimes without much in the way of antecedent in recent American politics. (If Donald Trump walked into a board meeting of the East India Company in, say, 1820, he would probably feel right at home with the agenda.) But the so-called “restrainers” don’t have much to show for their enthusiasm for this second term, as their unfolding anguish on X and in various podcasts shows.

#### Martin Gurri: The President’s Ambitions Are Revolutionary

The question is about Iran. But Iran wasn’t the first country on the Trump administration’s hit list, and it won’t be the last. This geopolitical moment resembles the final scenes of *The Godfather*, in which Michael Corleone violently “settles the family business.” Bullets are flying, bodies are falling, and President Trump is clearly intent on scouring the post–Cold War landscape of hostile regimes he considers to be a legacy of past American weakness. Once Iran is dealt with, Cuba will certainly follow.

What we have learned is that the president’s ambitions in the world are nothing less than revolutionary. If he fails, there will be severe retribution against the United States. But if he succeeds, the world will be unrecognizable. Russia will be tremendously weakened. Many of China’s sources of petroleum will be under American control. Weak nations, as always, will gravitate toward a dominant U.S. A clear line will have been crossed between 2025 and 2026—from the old world to the new—and we’ll have to think fast and hard if we don’t want it called the Age of Trump.

***Martin Gurri is a former CIA analyst and current visiting research fellow at the Mercatus Center.***

#### Christopher Caldwell: This Is a Reckless War

A sober-minded leader would not have exposed the United States to the risks of this war—no matter how oppressive the Iranian regime, no matter the security benefits to our Israeli allies. War bottles up not just Iran’s oil and gas but those of its neighbors. It is already immiserating friendly countries in Europe and consolidating unfriendly ones around China. Longer-term, the policy has elements of our July 1941 energy blockade of Japan. It is reckless.

If the United States is as low on critical munitions as Joint Chiefs chairman Dan Caine [reportedly warned](https://www.washingtonpost.com/national-security/2026/02/23/dan-caine-iran-risk-trump/), it could face unimagined consequences. If Iran lacks the economic wherewithal to keep its revolutionary guard loyal, this could be a shorter than anticipated war. That will not make it a just one.

The United States should not be assassinating people. Israel does so because it is small, vulnerable, and shadowed by genocide. Our alliance does not entitle us to “borrow” their justifications. Whether this style of defense is still effective for Israel is an open question. It is not right for a country like ours, unthreatened at home and increasingly dangerous abroad.

***Christopher Caldwell is a contributing opinion writer for The New York Times and a contributing editor at the Claremont Review of Books.***

#### Elliott Abrams: Israel Is a Model Ally

A week of war has taught several lessons, though not all appear to have sunk in. First, the evil nature of the Iranian regime is even clearer as we have watched it attack civilian targets in neighboring countries that did not attack Iran. This matters because shortcut deals with regime remnants should be off the table: They will maintain their hostility to us and our allies, and seek to rebuild the murderous power of their regime.

Second, the value of Israel as a model ally should be even clearer to any American with an open mind, though that lesson won’t penetrate the dark recesses of antisemites’ brains. In fact, watching Iran’s complete isolation and our partnership with Israel and the Gulf Arabs should remind us that our alliances are critical to our security, and should be strengthened in Europe and Asia rather than derided or forgotten.

Third, we have relearned that some forms of evil cannot be negotiated away or palliated, but must be fought and conquered.

Fourth, it follows that the importance of maintaining U.S. military strength is equally clear. The war is demonstrating the importance of a larger defense budget and a strengthening of the defense industrial base, so that the military power demonstrated this past week grows and can meet the new challenges we will face during this century.

***Elliott Abrams is a senior fellow for Middle Eastern studies at the Council on Foreign Relations. He served as Special Representative for Iran and Venezuela in the first administration of Donald Trump.***

#### Elliot Ackerman: Whose Boots on the Ground?

Early this week, it seemed possible that the Trump administration might replace the hard-liners within the Iranian regime with moderates. This would be a strategy similar to the one used in Venezuela. That option seems increasingly remote as the U.S. and Israel continue to eliminate regime leadership. Currently, it would appear the U.S. and Israel are pursuing a strategy that demands wholesale regime change, and for that to work, it will require boots on the ground.

The question becomes: Whose boots? Even though President Trump hasn’t outright rejected the idea of American troops on the ground, it would seem highly improbable in the current political environment, particularly as his administration has sold this war as being fundamentally different from Iraq and Afghanistan. Were he to commit large numbers of U.S. troops to Iran, it would make the current conflict almost indistinguishable from those two military misadventures.

It would seem boots on the ground will have to come in one of two forms. First, and preferable, would be a large, nationwide protest movement similar to those seen in January. Since the outset of this conflict, Trump has encouraged the Iranian people to “take back your country.” If Iranian civil society (protected by American arms) were to snatch the reins of power for the Iranian regime, that would be the best of all possible outcomes. The longer this war goes on, the less likely that outcome seems to become. The second form of boots on the ground comes in the form of tribal or dissident militias. Already, the Kurds in northern Iran, backed by the CIA, have moved into position to engage the regime. This could lead to regime change, but it could also lead to civil war.

The first week of the war has shown us that boots on the ground will be necessary to change the regime in Iran. The most pressing question in the conflict today is, whose boots?

***Elliot Ackerman is a Free Press contributor and senior fellow at Yale’s Jackson School of Global Affairs. He is a veteran of the Marine Corps and CIA special operations.***

#### Haviv Rettig Gur: The Regime Is Proving Too Dangerous to Be Allowed to Survive

One week into the war, the Iranian regime can no longer pretend, as it has since its founding, that a theology of martyrdom can substitute for a functional state. For 47 years, the Khomeinist “revolution” has brutalized its people to maintain power while ignoring infrastructure, education, and the needs of its people. Despite controlling 12 percent of global oil and 17 percent of gas reserves, Tehran has condemned its citizens to decades of sanctions-driven poverty since 1995 to chase a nuclear shadow and fund a proxy empire.

Ruhollah Khomeini’s *muqawama* (resistance) ethos—which was supposed to compensate militarily for the weaknesses imposed on the country by its anti-modern clerical rulers—has collapsed under the weight of its own stupendous incompetence. In seven days, the empire has been decapitated, leaving the regime to shoot wildly at neutral neighbors like Oman and Qatar. Iran, ever a one-trick pony, now plans to survive the whirlwind with the same destabilizing violence that built its power. Yet, instead of proving it is too dangerous to let fall, Tehran is convincing the Arab world of the Israeli view: The regime is proving too dangerous to let it survive. Abandoned by Russia and China, the theocrats in Tehran now gamble that American patience or Israeli munitions will expire before it does. The regime would call such a stalemate a “victory,” but—and this is the point—for the Iranian people, it would be a continuation of the same disaster.

Whatever the outcome of the war, Iran’s “Islamic revolution” will never again be able to convincingly claim it is anything more than the empty, tyrannical ideological husk that everybody in the region now sees so clearly.

***Haviv Rettig Gur is the Free Press’s Middle East analyst and a senior analyst at The Times of Israel. He also hosts the podcast “Ask Haviv Anything.”***

#### Ayaan Hirsi Ali: The End of Mr. Consensus

I have learned that the era of Mr. Consensus is finally and mercifully [over](https://www.commentary.org/articles/ayaan-hirsi-ali/consensus-politics-suicidal-worldview/).

For nearly four decades, the post-Cold War leaders were in the grip of seeking to resolve all conflict through conversations seeking consensus. I came to call this type of leader “Mr. Consensus.” Mr. Consensus came to believe that military action was inherently bad and must be replaced with “soft power.” That all human conflict could and should be resolved through diplomacy. This led to such idiotic policies as allowing China to join the World Trade Organization on terms that were never enforced; allowing Russia to annex Crimea with only mild sanctions in response; and in Europe spending less and less on national and North Atlantic Treaty Organization defense.

Dialogue with the leaders of Hamas and the Palestine Liberation Organization was supposed to persuade them to accept Israel’s existence and to agree to coexist in peace with it. The theocratic leaders of Iran were to be sweet-talked into enriching less uranium. Military action for Mr. Consensus was simply not an option. The correct response to aggression was “de-escalation.”

As a result of this approach, we witnessed bad actor after bad tyrannize their populations and terrorize their neighbors with impunity. Russian president Vladimir Putin invaded Ukraine. The Chinese Communist Party took control of Hong Kong and menaced Taiwan. The mullahs subsidized terror across the Middle East and continued building their bomb.

Well, what we have learned this week—and perhaps since the elections that kicked Mr. Consensus to the curb in November 2024—is what we can achieve with hard power in just one week. Hard power is effective not only in dealing with dictators like Ali Khamenei (not to mention Nicolás Maduro). It is also a more effective deterrent than soft power. Soft power not backed by hard power is simply suicidal. Other dictators are watching and learning. Mr. Consensus took early retirement. General Dan Caine took over. And, as President Trump [posted on Truth Social](https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/116121997925438698) last month, “He only knows one thing, how to WIN.”

***Ayaan Hirsi Ali is a research fellow at the Hoover Institution and founder of the Ayaan Hirsi Ali Foundation.***
