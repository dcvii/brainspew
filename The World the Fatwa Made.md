Thirty-seven years ago, Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini, Supreme Leader and founder of the Islamic Republic of Iran, [sentenced Salman Rushdie](https://archive.nytimes.com/www.nytimes.com/books/99/04/18/specials/rushdie-khomeini.html) to death on Valentine’s Day for writing a novel.

It is hard to write that sentence without feeling it is a parody of the opening line of Franz Kafka’s *The* *Trial*: **“** Someone must have slandered Josef K., for one morning, without having done anything truly wrong, he was arrested.” Nevertheless, that is what happened. And as Rushdie [told Ezra Klein](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/04/26/opinion/ezra-klein-podcast-salman-rushdie.html) in spring of 2024, “We live in Kafka’s world.”

I remember reading about the *fatwa* —a word everyone suddenly knew—in the newspaper at the time. I had recently dropped out of graduate school and was trying to become a writer myself, and it seemed absurd that a best-selling novelist born in India, living in London, and famous around the world might be murdered because the octogenarian ruler of an Islamist theocracy thousands of miles away had called for his death, like the Red Queen shouting “Off with their heads!” in *Alice in Wonderland*.

The ayatollah died four months later, but it soon became apparent that Rushdie’s 1988 novel was no match for a few sentences read over the radio calling on “all valiant Muslims wherever they may be” to kill the author of *[The Satanic Verses](https://bookshop.org/a/93116/9780812976717)* and anyone else who helped bring his blasphemous book into the world.

Ever since, the fatwa has hovered over the West it was designed to destroy, waxing and waning like a moon. Sometimes it is a pale crescent, barely there; sometimes, as now, a round blood moon full of portent. But a portent of what?

It is a few sentences read aloud on Radio Tehran—mere breath—that will remain the Islamic Republic’s most dangerous weapon.

The question remains urgent even after the joint American and Israeli strikes on the early morning of February 28 marked a new phase in the long war waged by the Islamic Republic of Iran, which came into the world chanting “Death to America” and “Death to Israel.” I have been thinking a great deal about the fatwa now that the murderous regime the ayatollah founded in 1979 is teetering on the bloody brink. Because it may yet be that of all the proxies nurtured over the years by the Islamic Republic for its apocalyptic showdown with the Jewish state and the West, and after all the billions spent on Islamist militias, ballistic missiles, and global terrorism— plus [half a trillion dollars](https://www.businessinsider.com/irans-nuclear-program-has-been-an-astronomical-waste-for-the-country-2015-6) on its nuclear weapons program in pursuit of its endlessly repeated aim of “wiping Israel off the map”—it is a few sentences read aloud on Radio Tehran, mere breath, that will remain its most successful method of exporting the Islamist revolution, even after the murderous regime it created goes down.

The fatwa’s ability to erase borders—not only between Tehran and London or New York, but between words and violence—made it a sort of spell. It was at once a death sentence, a wanted poster, a call to arms, a license to kill, a pardon before the fact, and a reward after it. At the same time that it dissolved distinctions, it reconstituted the world into Manichaean absolutes impervious to argument or appeal. When Rushdie was persuaded to make a public apology in the early days of his death sentence, the dying ayatollah [fired back](https://www.latimes.com/archives/la-xpm-1989-02-20-mn-29-story.html): “Even if Salman Rushdie becomes the most pious man of all time, it is incumbent on every Muslim to employ everything he has got, his life and his wealth, to send him to hell.”

The Supreme Leader who succeeded Ayatollah Khomeini, and ruled for 37 blood-soaked years until America and Israel took him out, made it clear on taking power that the fatwa had no fail-safe, but had been “fired like a bullet that won’t rest until it hits its target.” Like the unforgivable Rushdie and the indelible sin of his book, the fatwa was forever. It made Rushdie “a dead man on leave,” as the writer [Jean Améry](https://www.auschwitz.at/jean-amery-hans-mayer-en) realized about himself when he read the Nuremberg Laws in a Vienna café in 1935 and understood that the laws, which revoked the humanity of Jews, had made him “someone to be murdered.”

One of the amazing things about the fatwa was how soon it began working. Ten days after it was issued, two bookstores in Berkeley, California, where I’d been getting a PhD in English literature, were firebombed. Both had been selling *The Satanic Verses*. One of them, Cody’s, was a place I’d spent many hours, and though I’d moved back east by then, I felt an uncanny reverberation. A day after Cody’s blew up, a bomb scare emptied the Barnes & Noble in downtown Manhattan around the corner from the college where my father taught German literature. The big annex still sold used books, and after meeting my father for lunch at the coffee shop next door, we would rummage through the bins looking for bargains. At some point my mother, who was a writer, gave me a button she’d gotten at a literary gathering that said, “I am Salman Rushdie.” I wish I could say I wore it, but I kept it in a box with a handful of novelty buttons collected by an older cousin who passed it along to me, including an early Nixon campaign button that said, “They can’t lick our Dick!”

The fatwa created an ever-widening zone for the ayatollah’s jurisdiction until it stretched across continents.

The fatwa spread by making Rushdie and his book contagious, so that in addition to the author, who was not even named, the kill order included anyone connected to the production, editing, translation, or dissemination of *The Satanic Verses*. This created an ever-widening zone for the ayatollah’s jurisdiction until it stretched across continents, inviting the willing executioners it summoned to cross borders after it.

Rushdie’s Japanese translator was [stabbed to death](https://www.upi.com/Archives/1991/07/12/Japanese-translator-of-Satanic-Verses-killed/6569679291200/) in July of 1991 outside the university in Tokyo where he taught. His Italian translator had been stabbed in Milan the week before but survived. In October of 1993, his [Norwegian publisher](https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/politics/1993/10/12/norwegian-publisher-of-satanic-verses-is-shot/658e8f45-4331-4023-967e-34344fbdb2b6/) was shot in Oslo and, though gravely wounded, survived. Aziz Nesin, a Turkish editor and intellectual who had announced his intention to translate *The Satanic Verses* into Turkish, narrowly escaped being burned alive in July of 1993 when his hotel in Eastern Turkey, where he was attending a conference, was [torched by an angry mob](https://www.latimes.com/archives/la-xpm-1993-07-03-mn-9699-story.html) after the artists, musicians, and writers inside refused to send out the 78-year-old Nesin to be killed. Thirty-seven people died in the fire; Nesin was helped down a ladder by firefighters who began beating him once they realized who he was, and someone cried, “This is the devil we really should have killed.”  

Muslims who defended the book, or failed to anathematize it, were also targeted. In *[Joseph Anton](https://bookshop.org/a/93116/9780812982602)*, Rushdie’s excellent memoir of his time in hiding, he writes that “Muslims began to be killed by other Muslims if they expressed non-bloodthirsty opinions. In Belgium, the mullah who was said to be the spiritual leader of the country’s Muslims, the Saudi national Abdullah Ahdal, and his Tunisian deputy Salim Bahri, were killed for saying that, whatever Khomeini had said for Iranian consumption, in Europe there was freedom of expression.” Both men were found [shot to death](https://www.latimes.com/archives/la-xpm-1989-03-30-mn-884-story.html) by unknown assailants.

Days after rejecting Rushdie’s apology, the ayatollah declared that “All the Zionists and the arrogant of the world are now supporting Rushdie.” By “Zionist,” the ayatollah did not mean to suggest that Rushdie and his defenders were supporters of the 19th century self-liberation movement of the Jewish people, whose goal was to establish a national home in a portion of their decolonized historic homeland, which culminated in the creation of the modern state of Israel in 1948. He meant that just as *The Satanic Verses* is “a text written, edited, and published against Islam, the Prophet of Islam, and the Quran,” as the fatwa puts it, so the Jewish people, who exist in opposition to everything good in the world, are contagious figures of emblematic wickedness. And just as the ayatollah treats Rushdie’s novel as a dangerous contaminant threatening Muslims around the world, so the corrupting wickedness of the Jews—interchangeably called Zionists in Islamist demonology—is bound up with their desire to conquer the whole world, which made them walking provocations. Rushdie appeared in an Iranian edition ofHenry Ford’s *The International Jew: The World’s Foremost Problem*, [sold at the Frankfurt Book Fair](http://www.matthiaskuentzel.de/contents/the-protocols-of-the-elders-of-zion-at-the-frankfurt-book-fair) in 2005. The preface began: “The grip of the Jewish parasitic influence has been growing stronger and stronger ever since \[Henry Ford’s time\]. The Jewish danger—now called Zionism—is not only threatening one nation. As a matter of fact, it is directed against the entire humanity.” *The Satanic Verses* was offered to illustrate the peril.

### How a Writer Becomes a Monster—and Murder Becomes Cosmic Justice

Salman Rushdie is the author of *The Satanic Verses.* (Photo by Reg Innell/ *Toronto Star* via Getty Images)

That’s a lot to lay on an Indian-born British writer and non-practicing Muslim man of the left. Frankly, it’s a lot to lay on Jews and actual Zionists, who have to deal with the fact that they are a touchstone of evil [in someone else’s paranoid cosmology](https://www.thefp.com/p/what-anti-zionism-really-is).

The real Rushdie, for all his command of language, was powerless to unseat the imaginary Rushdie the fatwa created. Kafka knew what it was to wake up inside a slander of someone else’s devising. Jews had been inscribed as killers of God and charged with a hereditary guilt they could not expunge, and though many hoped the modern world would dismiss the charges with prejudice, Kafka was keenly aware that trials in his lifetime had charged Jews in Hungary, Russia, and Czechoslovakia with killing Christians and using their blood for ritual purposes. Whether these modern blood libels were the last gasp of an atavistic hatred, just as Jews were entering the culture, or a dark glimpse of the future, was unresolved when Kafka—who did not live to see his three sisters deported to concentration camps—died in 1924.

A mysterious feature of the fatwa was the way it brought out apologists, appeasers, and peacemakers who misunderstood its motivations. Former President Jimmy Carter [blamed Rushdie](https://www.cartercenter.org/news/doc1381/) in *The New York Times* for “vilifying the Prophet Mohammed and defaming the Holy Koran.” The former president, who had been in office when the ayatollah held American citizens captive, blindfolded and abused for 444 days, did not seem to recognize the nature of the trap he was falling into. “While Rushdie’s First Amendment freedoms are important,” Carter wrote, he nevertheless agreed the work of fiction was a “direct insult” to Muslims whose sacred beliefs had been “violated.”

Those like Carter, who approached the fatwa with rational expectations, wound up saying irrational things, ratifying its terms even if they quarreled with them. Blaming Rushdie’s novel for inflicting “the kind of intercultural wound that is difficult to heal,” as though the author had assassinated an archduke, Carter chose to see the fatwa as a mirror of offended religious sentiment, even though it was a license to commit transnational murder issued by a cleric who had named a street after Anwar Sadat’s assassin.

Carter’s hope that “tactful public statements and private discussions could still defuse this explosive situation” was like waiting for a hostage to be released. He failed to understand that the fatwa’s rejection of borders, laws, national sovereignty, and individual autonomy was the whole point. Or that a decree that made the victim the aggressor, murder a virtue, and suicide a sacrament did not permit common ground.

The message Carter failed to understand was received loud and clear by a 24-year-old American named Hadi Matar.

Thirty-three years after Rushdie was sentenced to death, Matar traveled from Fairview, New Jersey, to Chautauqua, New York, where [he attacked Rushdie](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2022/aug/12/salman-rushdie-attack-suspect-identified-hadi-matar) with a knife from behind as he sat onstage at the Chautauqua Institution waiting to give a speech about free expression and the importance of keeping writers safe. Matar, who told a reporter that he had only read “a page or two” of *The Satanic Verses* but knew it was an “attack on Islam,” stabbed the 75-year-old writer in the face, the eye, the neck, and the midsection, 15 times before being tackled by bystanders.

That was the logic of the fatwa. Khomeini hadn’t read *The Satanic Verses* either but had revoked its creator’s right to exist anyway. This helps explain how Matar, who was [found guilty](https://abcnews.com/US/salman-rushdie-stabbing-sentencing-attempted-murder-hadi-matar/story?id=121844226) of attempted murder last year, could tell the court before his sentencing: “Salman Rushdie wants to disrespect other people. He wants to be a bully; he wants to bully other people. I don’t agree with that.”

You don’t need magic to turn a writer into a monster; the sleep of reason will do, and the overriding presence of conspiracy theories that do the thinking for you, and turn whole categories of humanity into stock villains in a long-running play.

You don’t need magic to turn a writer into a monster; the sleep of reason will do.

If you believed the fatwa made Rushdie’s life forfeit, then stabbing him could hardly be a crime; on the contrary, it was a combination of self-defense and cosmic justice. This was how supporters of Hamas could see its bloodthirsty invasion of Israel on October 7, 2023, as a heroic act of resistance, even though they broke a truce by firing thousands of rockets into a sovereign country before sending thousands of armed fighters across an internationally recognized border to murder, torture, kidnap, and rape civilians in southern Israel. They did not see Israel as a sovereign country any more than Matar saw Rushdie as a sovereign person with inalienable rights stronger than the deep magic that had revoked them. Attacking Israel, like attempting to kill Rushdie, were both acts of resistance and liberation, which helps explain why Matar proclaimed, “From the river to the sea, Palestine will be free,” in the Chautauqua courtroom where he was found guilty of attempted murder.

### The Blurring of Borders—and the Burning of Books

Muslims protest Salman Rushdie’s *The Satanic Verses* in a demonstration in Derby, England, on March 15, 1989. (Photo by Staff/Mirrorpix/Getty Images)

Looking back at the Iranian revolution of 1979, when Marxist students hoping for a great leap forward and Islamists hoping for a great leap back, looked up and saw the ayatollah’s white-bearded face in the moon, it is possible to see the concentric circles that have radiated outward from the fatwa and the murderous ideology that produced it. There was the stabbing of Rushdie on August 12, 2022, by a young American who discovered the fatwa while visiting his father in a Hezbollah stronghold in southern Lebanon. There was the massacre of civilians in Israel on October 7, 2023, perpetrated by Hamas, whose [genocidal covenant](https://avalon.law.yale.edu/21st_century/hamas.asp) was published the same year as *The* *Satanic Verses*, though to much less controversy. There was the burning of Pennsylvania governor [Josh Shapiro’s house](https://abcnews.com/US/alleged-arsonist-targeted-pennsylvania-gov-josh-shapiro-palestine/story?id=120860365) on the first night of Passover, with his family inside, by a man convinced he was helping Palestine by hurting Jews, and the [burning of Jews](https://www.cpr.org/2025/06/01/boulder-attack-pearl-street/) in Boulder, Colorado, by an Egyptian gardener who set 12 human beings on fire and shouted “Free Palestine” as he sprayed them with gasoline, murdering an elderly woman because he “wanted to kill all Zionists,” as he told police afterward, and had made a sacred pledge.

There was also Elias Rodriguez, the 31-year-old Chicago native who flew to Washington, D.C., in May of 2025, [where he murdered](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/05/16/nyregion/salman-rushdie-hadi-matar-sentenced.html) Sarah Milgrim and Yaron Lischinsky, a young couple leaving the Capital Jewish Museum that made them a target. Like the October 7 terrorists, Rodriguez was [wearing a camera](https://www.timesofisrael.com/man-wore-body-camera-to-record-killing-of-israeli-embassy-staffers-us-prosecutors/#:~:text=US%20prosecutors:%20Suspect%20wore%20body,shootings%2C%20according%20to%20the%20filing.) that livestreamed his cold-blooded murders of an American Jewish woman and Israeli Christian man who were planning to spend their lives together. After [emptying his pistol into Milgrim](https://www.justice.gov/usao-dc/pr/new-terrorism-related-charges-filed-against-alleged-killer-israeli-embassy-employees) as she tried to crawl away, and tossing away his gun only after it jammed, he took out a keffiyeh like a calling card, and began chanting “Free, free Palestine” as the police led him away.

Human sacrifice was another feature of the fatwa, which called for murder and promised martyrdom to those who died with blood on their hands. Matar was given a [dunam of land](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dunam) for stabbing a 75-year-old writer courtesy of an [Iranian charity](https://thefederal.com/news/iran-foundation-offers-rushdies-attacker-1000-square-metres-land-as-reward), theFoundation to Implement Imam Khomeini’s Fatwas, which promised more land to whoever finished the job. For shooting a young couple in the back, Rodriguez was [hailed as](https://www.theyeshivaworld.com/news/israel-news/2403888/iranian-tv-praises-d-c-terrorist-as-american-sinwar-he-sent-2-zionists-to-hell.html) an “American Yahya Sinwar” by Iranian TV, which likened him to the mastermind of the October 7 massacres [for killing](https://www.memri.org/reports/iranian-regime-mouthpiece-kayhan-praises-washington-dc-murder-israeli-embassy-staffers) “two wild Zionist beasts... sending them to hell,” and for bringing the Iranian resistance to the U.S. He was already heroic in his own eyes, and posted his manifesto to inspire others to kill those who had “forfeited their humanity.”

Chants and slogans can’t actually erase a people or a country. But exhortation and repetition do cast a spell.

Chants and slogans can’t actually erase a country and its population by changing its name—“From the river to the sea, Palestine will be free”—or make time run backward: “We don’t want two states, we want 1948.” But exhortation and repetition do cast a spell, and the more people who put their faith in incantation over evidence and argument, the easier it becomes to [blur the border](https://critiqueanddigest.substack.com/p/intifada-globalized), not only between countries but between word and deed.

I have watched the blurring of that border in my own small way as a writer who has seen two literary festivals fall apart since October 7, 2023. Not because of literary disagreements, or anything I would call politics, but because of something much more fundamental that challenged the very notion of common ground and the shared assumptions about language necessary to sustain it.

It feels strange, in the middle of a shooting war with the Islamic Republic of Iran, the murderous regime that produced the fatwa and [slaughtered tens of thousands](https://time.com/7357635/more-than-30000-killed-in-iran-say-senior-officials/) of its own unarmed citizens in January, to write about the collapse of literary festivals, one in New York and one in Adelaide, Australia. We are long past the days when Winston Churchill “took the English language and sent it into battle,” but the connection between war and words remains as essential as it has ever been.

The ayatollah knew what he was doing when he chose to try out his weapon on a novelist, and took aim at all that went into the making of books in a free society, from the editors and publishers to the translators and booksellers. In his memoir, Rushdie writes about the burning of *The Satanic Verses* in Bradford, a city in West Yorkshire with a large Muslim population where, even before the fatwa, his book is “nailed to a piece of wood and then set on fire: crucified and then immolated.” He recalls Heinrich Heine’s famous words, “Where they burn books, they will in the end burn people too,” which are engraved in the ground at Opera Square in Berlin to mark the spot of the infamous [Nazi book burning of 1933](https://www.pbs.org/wgbh/americanexperience/features/goebbels-burnings/).

The festivals are like bookends on a shelf with no books, symbols of the collapse of something important, some common language that makes community, free speech, and literature possible. Both succumbed to the attraction of something bound up with the fatwa, some ominous urge to alter language itself, dissolve the relationship between words and what they represent, deny the equal application of meaning, confounding cause and effect, accusation and conviction, cultural safety and physical safety.

I watched as Western writers succumbed to the attraction of the fatwa, dissolving the relationship between words and what they represent, confounding cause and effect.

The [first to be canceled](https://www.theguardian.com/books/2024/apr/26/pen-america-world-voices-festival-cancelled) was PEN America’s World Voices Festival, created by Salman Rushdie to bring writers together, despite their differences, in the fraught aftermath of September 11, 2001, when Islamist hijackers murdered almost 3,000 people on American soil. I was scheduled to speak at the festival in 2024, which was shut down by writers demanding that PEN America [fire its CEO](https://lithub.com/the-pen-awards-are-on-the-brink-of-collapse/) for “longstanding commitments to Zionism, Islamophobia, and imperial wars in the Middle East.” They also insisted that PEN adopt language that explicitly accused Israel of genocide, and apologize to a protester who had been prevented from shouting down the actress Mayim Bialik—accused of being a Zionist—at a PEN event where Bialik was interviewing another writer onstage.

One reason I did not expect PEN America to pull the plug on World Voices was because Rushdie had been sentenced to death by the Islamic Republic of Iran, the regime that had armed, trained, and funded Hamas. Another was that the leader of Hezbollah, a larger proxy of Iran, which had joined the war the next day, had inspired the man who had tried to kill Rushdie in 2022.

Needless to say, I was wrong.

Nine past presidents of PEN America had [signed a letter](https://pen.org/press-release/letter-from-salman-rushdie-and-past-pen-america-presidents-keep-faith-in-the-community-that-we-have-built-together/) “at a time of crisis worldwide and within our own community,” appealing to members at the basic level of literary fellowship: “We urge writers to keep faith in the community that we have built together.”They reminded us that “this year’s festival is a tribute to our fellow former President Salman Rushdie, the visionary who conceived the festival and has championed it ever since, as he launches his new memoir, *Knife*, an emblem of courage and of the role of literature and truth as vessels of human resilience.”

Rushdie’s commitment to freedom of expression had cost him an eye, the use of his writing hand, and very nearly his life, and if that weren’t enough, they reminded readers that World Voices “was conceived amid conflict to draw together diverse authors and thinkers at a time of deepening and deadly geopolitical tension after 9/11.”

The problem was that they were appealing to the very pluralism and free speech principles that the protesters rejected. “PEN America has always embraced dissent in its ranks,” the letter explained, “recognizing that writing is an act of conscience, and that authors must be free to follow their own.” But the protesters did not want writers following their own individual conscience. The protesters were not demanding the inclusion of anyone, only the exclusion of those they labeled Zionists, and a denunciation of Israel in the formula they prescribed. They were only being asked to stop trying to shut down, rather than merely sit out, an event that was an opportunity for young writers to connect with an audience. None of it was enough. Not Rushdie showing his scars like Coriolanus, nor appeals to artistic solidarity as a way to safeguard difference, rather than political uniformity, which demonizes it.

The same Orwellian inversions and looking-glass logic have been painfully apparent in the implosion of this year’s Adelaide Writers’ Week, Australia’s biggest literary festival. It [fell apart](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/01/13/world/australia/australia-adelaide-writers-palestinian-cancel.html) after the board disinvited Randa Abdel-Fattah, an Australian writer, activist, and academic who believes that “if you are a Zionist, you have no claim or right to cultural safety.” The decision to remove Abdel-Fatah was made after the deadliest terrorist attack in Australia’s history, in which gunmen targeted Jews gathered at the annual Chanukah by the Sea celebration at Bondi Beach and [murdered 15 people](https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/articles/czxp998rd9ko), including a 10-year-old girl, and wounded 40 more.

Before the attack, which would have been far worse had the IEDs flung into the crowd gone off, the two terrorists—a young Australian man and his father—filmed themselves with guns, ISIS flags, and a Quran, blaming “Zionists” to designate Jews with no claim or right to physical safety.

To the board of the festival, and the premier of South Australia, Peter Malinauskas, who supported the board, “it would not be culturally sensitive” to showcase a writer who wanted to deny cultural safety to others “at this unprecedented time so soon after Bondi.” Especially a writer who called Zionism “a genocidal slaughterhouse” but did not consider Hamas a terrorist organization; who experienced a “glimmer of hope” on October 7 that “was palpable, real, and exhilarating,” and changed her Facebook cover on October 8 to a paraglider using a Palestinian flag as a sail. This was one day after the Al-Qassam Brigades she did not consider terrorists paraglided into southern Israel, which she did not consider Israel, murdering, torturing, kidnapping, and raping young revelers at a music festival, though she denied the reports of sexual violence as “rape atrocity propaganda,” even though they were true.

The vast majority of writers walked out in support of Abdel-Fattah, tanking the festival.

### When the Fatwa Came to Australia, What Did I Do?

I myself had participated in Adelaide Writers’ Week two years before, five months after October 7. I don’t think I had a single conversation about literature, even with the writers I met; most had written nonfiction books, as I had, that could be discussed as subjects however much you hoped the words would live in their own unfolding on the page. But it was a general atmosphere of politics where something else should be.

At the time, I was reading a book called *Great State: China and the World* by art historian Timothy Brook, who tells a story about studying in Beijing as a young man in 1974, when the country was barely open to Westerners. He decides to visit the Yellow Temple in Beijing, built in the 17th century on the orders of the Qing emperor to house the visiting fifth Dalai Lama. The Yellow Temple was unmarked, but Brook knew what it looked like and where it was. He knew its history, too, but out of politeness asked the custodian, in Chinese: “What place is this?”

“This isn’t any place,” came the reply.

The Dalai Lama was no longer an honored guest, of course, but his temple—“a palace fit for the Buddha’s highest emanation on Earth”—was in plain sight. Nevertheless, as Brook understood, “it was best for both of us if the Yellow Temple simply failed to exist.” So that was that.

I thought about that story a lot, especially when my wife and I tried to talk about Israel. The Chabad rabbi conducting the service was the father-in-law of the murdered Chabad rabbi, a father of five, who had walked with his hands over his head toward the shooters, appealing to their humanity and perhaps buying a few precious seconds for others to take shelter before the father and son terrorists killed him.

The fatwa’s inversion of inclusion and exclusion, speech and heresy, no longer requires the formulation spoken 37 years ago, but has found its answering echoes.

It was deeply moving to see someone who had lost so much so recently give consolation with so much conviction. Acknowledging that “the darkest form of evil violated this sacred place,” he said, emphatically: “Darkness does not get the final word. Light will win!”

What struck me was how palpably he seemed convinced this was so. The day his son-in-law was killed, “he had not had a chance to light even one candle. Today we light all eight.” Over and over, that was his theme. “More light, more love, more unity.” The Lubavitcher Rebbe taught, he said, that darkness is not defeated by anger or force. Darkness is transformed by light. We owed that to each of the 15 “precious souls,” he said, and each person was named, with an attribute for everyone to honor going forward. He began with a victim who was not Jewish, “yet who came to stand with her husband and the community. Let us show up for one another, even when we are different, and be allies to those who need us.”

His idea of inclusion was based on the belief that everyone is made in the image of God. “Sydney can and must become a beacon of goodness, where people look out for one another, where kindness is louder than hate,” he said. And after he had gone through every name, including the 10-year-old girl, he spoke about the laws of Noah, the seven precepts given after the flood to bind godly people together everywhere. “To live with decency and responsibility and to honor God. To respect the sanctity of life.” Astonishing how beautiful and basic and elusive they seem. “And for us Jews,” he said, “it means to live proudly and visibly as Jews.”

His community had been taunted with genocidal chants, doxxed, ignored, belittled, terrorized, and killed, but that did not define their experience, or Australia; Arsen Ostrovsky, [wounded by a bullet at Bondi](https://www.thefp.com/p/what-i-learned-on-bondi-beach), wrote about how many ordinary Australians rushed to help.

It feels almost sacrilegious to compare such a capacious understanding of community with the inversions of the Hamas charter, or the shrunken community of collapsed and politicized literary festivals, that promise harmony through elimination. And so I will end in New York City, where I went on the fourth night of Hanukkah, three days after the Bondi massacre. The victims were on my mind, and to be honest, so was the possibility of violence. But that was a reason to go.

The giant menorah was made of ice and modeled on the one used in the Temple in Jerusalem, depicted on the Arch of Titus in Rome, though it had more branches. I do not love crowds, and it was crowded on Fifth Avenue, jammed onto a sidewalk between a building and traffic, but the joy of the people dancing, and the words of one of the songs in particular, moved me: “The people of eternity are not afraid of a long journey.”

There were some police; nobody told us they couldn’t keep us safe, even though there were any number of perches for snipers, and I did take a quick scan. But it was important to notice the faces of the people dancing or watching or walking past, on our side or across the street, or passing in cars, who shared basic, binding values. And I had found a chant that does not erase me or my history but honors it, and honors the freedom my country still promises and guarantees.

The people of eternity are not afraid of a long journey. Nobody should be.
