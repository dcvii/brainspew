Introducing a month-long celebration of great Americans, starting with the author of the Declaration of Independence.

***There are signs that Americans are finally warming up to the country’s semiquincentennial, and there’s a tantalizing array of events nationwide over the next month. (We can’t be the only folks excited about a tall ships parade!) Alongside the outdoor fun, though, there’s a glut of articles and books considering the ideals of the American revolution and the men behind them. Given how much is out there on the great men who founded America (including Jonathan Horn’s Free Press newsletter, [This Week in American History](https://www.thefp.com/t/this-week-in-american-history)), we thought we’d try something a little different for our final countdown to the country’s big birthday. This month, we’re taking a break from the Founders to celebrate some of the Americans the revolution enabled over the last 250 years.***

***The idea is not to downplay the brilliance of the names that you usually see on a list of Great Americans. It is to acknowledge a few names that are less familiar, but nonetheless helped make this country what it is. Did the inventor of air conditioning, Willis Carrier, not have as profound an effect on the country as anyone who, like Mr. Smith, went to Washington? So, while we will continue to revere Lincoln and Washington, it’s time for a little love to Sandy Koufax, Louis Armstrong, and John Steinbeck.***

***We’re not trying to come up with a definitive list of the greatest Americans who ever lived. We’re offering something more personal: a writer we love on an American they love. Every weekday between now and July 4.***

***To kick things off, we’re allowing ourselves one essay on one of the great men who founded this country: the historian Jane Kamensky, president and CEO of Monticello, on Thomas Jefferson’s misgivings about over-reverence for the Founding generation. Read Kamensky’s piece below, and then look out for our series on Great Americans starting in The Front Page tomorrow. —The Editors***

By 1826, Thomas Jefferson knew his sun was setting. He turned 83 in April, rheumatic, dyspeptic, and fever-racked. The letters he wrote that year—169 of which survive—are haunted by debt and by death. “I must meet what futurity has in reserve for me,” Jefferson [told his grandson](https://founders.archives.gov/documents/Jefferson/98-01-02-5954) Francis Eppes in March.

A week later, he [wrote his will](https://founders.archives.gov/documents/Jefferson/98-01-02-5963). Around the same time, he designed his tombstone. It was to be a simple obelisk six feet tall—less than his own human height—and feature an epitaph listing three accomplishments curated from four decades in public service “& not a word more.” Posterity would see him claim credit for the American Declaration of Independence, that avatar of self-government; for Virginia’s Statute for Religious Freedom, an originary statement of freedom of conscience; and for the University of Virginia, a beacon of citizen education. *Author*, Jefferson called himself in the inscription, and *Father*. Not founder, much less Founder. Across the vast course of his correspondence, Jefferson used that f-word just a handful of times, most often to talk about the working of iron.

As he mused on his own ending in 1826, Jefferson was also keenly aware that the Declaration of Independence, whose drafting he had come to count as his greatest contribution to the nation and indeed to the world, was approaching the ripe age of 50. For some time, he had kept track of the dwindling number of its living signers: In 1812, he thought there were seven, as he [tallied for John Adams](https://founders.archives.gov/documents/Adams/99-02-02-5743). A year later, he counted four, or possibly six. “I am the only one South of the Potomac,” [Jefferson supposed](https://founders.archives.gov/documents/Adams/99-02-02-6041). In his last public letter, penned on June 24, 1826, [Jefferson described](https://founders.archives.gov/documents/Jefferson/98-01-02-6179) the pride he felt as “one of the surviving signers of an instrument, pregnant with our own, and the fate of the world.” There were then only three. On July 5, the day after Jefferson and John Adams both perished, only Charles Carroll of Maryland endured.

It had long been the ideals, not the author, which Jefferson wanted Americans to revere. “I have declined letting my own birthday be known, & have engaged my family not to communicate it,” he [had written](https://founders.archives.gov/documents/Jefferson/01-41-02-0225) in 1803, toward the end of his first term as president. He felt “disapproving” of any attempt to transfer “the honours & veneration for the great birthday of our republic, to any individual, or of dividing them with individuals.”

The widespread [celebration of George Washington’s birthday](https://www.mountvernon.org/library/digitalhistory/digital-encyclopedia/article/commemoration-of-george-washingtons-birthday) was likely top of mind when Jefferson wrote. Americans had publicly marked the general’s natal day as early as 1779, when the Continental Army was still in rags. Washington was seven years dead and Jefferson in the White House in 1806, when Mason L. Weems’s *[Life of George Washington](https://www.loc.gov/item/62044994/)* began its long run in the nation’s libraries and classrooms, creating a secular saint.

---

Jefferson was as skeptical of saints and “relicks,” in civil society as in his vague religious life. Vesting a national identity in individuals was what monarchies did, with profiles on coins and royal occasions organizing the state calendar. Jefferson despised monarchy and all its trappings. He had been in Paris when he first read the Constitution that citizens of the United States were being asked to ratify. “I confess there are things in it which stagger all my dispositions to subscribe to what such an assembly has proposed,” [he wrote](https://founders.archives.gov/documents/Adams/06-19-02-0151) to John Adams in November 1787. Article II, in particular, worried him. The proposed American “President seems a bad edition of a Polish king,” Jefferson thought. “I wish that at the end of the 4 years they had made him for ever ineligible a second time,” lest the nation he had helped to declare into being come to be governed by “an officer for life,” or every succeeding election fall prey to “intrigue,... bribery,... force, & even... foreign interference.” Saints had feast days; monarchies celebrated royal birthdays. Virtuous republics ought to have neither.

Nor did Jefferson preach veneration for the founding documents. While the ideals of the nation were “self-evident” and eternal, its charters must remain vital, mutable. “Some men look at Constitutions with sanctimonious reverence, & deem them, like the ark of the covenant, too sacred to be touched,” [he wrote](https://founders.archives.gov/documents/Jefferson/03-10-02-0128-0002) in 1816, in response to proposals to amend Virginia’s state constitution. He explained that such *sanctimonious reverence* involved a logical fallacy: to “ascribe to the men of the preceding age a wisdom more than human.”

Hard experience had taught him otherwise. “I knew that age well,” Jefferson continued. “I belonged to it, and labored with it.” Those labors had done well by the country—but not perfectly. For there had been, and would be, no perfect men. And thus there had been, and would be, no golden age. The founding moment, he wrote, had been “very like the present, but without the experience of the present.”

With experience came wisdom, which must beget not reverence but revision, he insisted. “Laws and institutions must go hand in hand with the progress of the human mind,” Jefferson explained. To “weakly believe that one generation is not as capable as another of taking care of itself, and of ordering its own affairs” was to freeze time and to reject growth. “We might as well require a man to wear still the coat which fitted him when a boy,” he joked.

Jefferson was 33 when he wrote the Declaration: a man not that long out of a boy’s coat. In later years, John Adams would feel obliged to [answer Federalists’ questions](https://founders.archives.gov/documents/Adams/99-02-02-7674) about “why so young a man as Jefferson was placed at the head of the Committee for preparing a declaration of Independence?” Jefferson hadn’t wanted the job, though even the curmudgeonly Adams had to admit that he’d done it superbly—best, in some ways, in his [original draft](https://www.loc.gov/exhibits/declara/ruffdrft.html), with its doomed and “vehement Phillipic against Negro Slavery.” In his old age, Adams grew jealous of the credit that increasingly redounded to his Virginia committee-mate—to whom he had handed the pen!—for writing the nation’s vision statement. “There is not an idea in it, but what had been hackney’d in Congress for two years before,” he carped in 1822.

About this, Jefferson could only agree. His future tombstone notwithstanding, he didn’t hoard the credit to himself. “Neither aiming at originality of principle or sentiment, nor yet copied from any particular and previous writing, it was intended to be an expression of the American mind,” [he reflected](https://founders.archives.gov/documents/Jefferson/98-01-02-5212) in 1825. “All its authority rests then on the harmonising sentiments of the day.” On the *people’s* sentiments, in other words, including those who expressed their politics in the streets, in the household, in the quarters, or even by stealing themselves.

So, too, Jefferson insisted the country’s authority rested on the people, not the notables. When a lawyer from Kentucky [wrote to express](https://founders.archives.gov/documents/Jefferson/03-18-02-0417-0001) to Jefferson his “profound regard for one whose fame is identified with his country’s glory,” the would-be worthy demurred: “I am very thankful for the kindness of its expressions respecting myself. but it ascribes to me merits which I do not claim,” [Jefferson answered](https://founders.archives.gov/documents/Jefferson/03-18-02-0449). “I was one only of a band devoted to the cause of independence, all of whom exerted equally their best endeavors for its success, and have a common right to the merits of its acquisition.” Republics were forged by bands, choirs, orchestras; their strength comprised of blended voices and even of dissonance. Monarchies had the reedy thinness of solo acts.

At the top of [the page](https://www.monticello.org/encyclopedia/jeffersons-gravestone) on which he sketched the design for his tombstone, Jefferson quoted, in the original, the ancient Greek poet Anacreon, and wondered if the dead could “feel any interest in Monuments or other remembrances of them” once they were beyond joy, dread, and envy. With only an amorphous belief in heaven, he seemed less focused in his own moldering fate. He wanted the stone only to instruct future generations in the “testimonials that I have lived.” The base was to be “a plain die or cube... without any moldings.” The obelisk would be “of the coarse stone of which my columns are made”: a drab quartzite, hewn from the outcrops of the Blue Ridge Mountains. You can see that stone on the porticos of Monticello even today. The family chose a rusticated granite, but the effect was much the same: nothing honed, nothing polished.

Jefferson explained that he didn’t want the stone to be worth stealing. The marker should be so plain “that no one might be tempted hereafter to destroy it for the value of the materials.” But the vandals came almost as soon as the obelisk was installed in 1833. The granite was worthless, but visitors wanted pieces of this One True American Cross. They carried home chips of the monument “as trophies or mementos from a sacred shrine,” as *[The New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/1864/12/01/archives/the-sale-of-monticello.html?searchResultPosition=3)* later put it, till its [edges were softened](https://www.atlasobscura.com/places/thomas-jeffersons-original-tombstone) and the inscription effaced.

“Shame! shame upon our thoughtless countrymen,” the *Times* reporter continued. “Why should they be so disrespectful to the sepulcher of the great patriot of the Revolution?” Jefferson would have apportioned the shame differently, urging those trophy hunters to find the engine of their patriotism in the mirror rather than in the graveyard—to *be* rather than to emulate American worthies.
