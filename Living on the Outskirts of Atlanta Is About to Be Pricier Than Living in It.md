Home buyers flock to America’s exurbs because they are willing to live farther from city centers in exchange for cheaper home prices. In Atlanta, that traditional discount is about to evaporate.

The median sale price of homes in Atlanta’s exurbs was $380,962 in the fourth quarter of 2025, or about $4,000 shy of the prices in the neighboring metropolitan area and surrounding suburbs. That gap has narrowed from a high of $51,000 in the second quarter of 2021. If the trend continues at the current pace, sometime this year Atlanta will become the only major U.S. metropolitan area where exurban home prices exceed those of the accompanying city.

Most other exurbs are still relative bargains. The Wall Street Journal’s analysis of Redfin data shows an average $85,000 discount for homes in exurbs across the nine-largest U.S. metros with such areas.

Redfin applied the Brookings Institution definition of an exurb as a rapidly growing, low-density community on the fringe of a metropolitan area where at least 20% of workers commute to the urban core.

Historically, exurban home prices have rarely exceeded core-area prices. This was the case in Houston’s exurbs for part of the past decade, but this trend reversed and the price gap of core over exurb widened as the city’s population increased postpandemic.

[Atlanta’s population growth slowed](https://www.wsj.com/economy/atlanta-population-growth-ending-32aa642c?mod=article_inline) in recent years after a higher cost of living and increased congestion made the city a less popular destination. In Fulton County, where the city is located, the population rose by 3.9% from 2019 to 2024, according to U.S. census data.

But Jackson County, an exurb about 60 miles from the city center, grew 25% over that period. Growth in recent years has been helped by increasing suburban migration and a [large SK Battery America plant](https://georgia.org/press-release/sk-battery-america-exceeds-hiring-goal-track-reach-3000-workers#:~:text=SK%20Battery%20America%20\(SKBA\)%20is%20a%20global,*%20Started%20mass%20production%20in%20early%202022), announced in 2018, which delivered more than 2,500 jobs.

Amelia Finch, 41 years old, moved to Atlanta exurb Auburn with her husband, Charlie Finch, in 2017 from Washington, D.C. The couple purchased their first home, with a private gate, two-car garage and bonus room on a 5-acre lot for $337,000.

In 2021, they sold the Barrow County home for $550,000.

As residents flocked to the once-sleepy exurban corners of the region, housing supply didn’t keep up with demand, denting affordability for renters and first-time home buyers, said Richard Porter, director of the Master of Real Estate Development program at the Georgia Institute of Technology.

The squeeze is felt by residents like Gracie Daniel, 21, who moved to Winder last year from nearby Athens to live with her boyfriend and escape higher rents. The barista, who works at the Finches’ specialty coffee shop, Night Sky Coffee Roasters, is now on the hunt after her landlord decided to sell the property.

Finding it difficult to secure a local apartment within their $1,300 monthly budget, Daniel said they have considered moving beyond the exurbs to find a home they can afford, but she would have to find a new job. “It’s very discouraging,” she said.

In Walton County, residents looking for a night out don’t need to travel far. Downtown Monroe, a historic district in the county, is abundant in Southern charm. Its narrow streets are lined with storefronts sporting pastel-colored brick facades, occupied by boutiques and restaurants.

One of these storefronts houses The Story Shop, a children’s bookstore, known for its annual Unicorn Day event. Melissa Music, the bookstore’s owner, said the city’s historic Southern ambience was the perfect setting.

“The appeal is that families are able to go there, and it’s not just a place to shop. It’s a place to experience,” Music said of Monroe’s downtown district.

The Atlanta area’s expansive topography has created job centers outside of the core area, making the commute to the city increasingly irrelevant.

Large-scale employers are anchoring these fringes. Rivian’s planned plant near exurban Covington is expected to create 7,500 jobs by 2030, while poultry giant Wayne-Sanderson Farms is among the largest employers in Jackson County.

For entertainment, the 3,500-acre Chateau Elan estate—which spans into Barrow County—features multiple golf courses, luxury villas and spa suites, and a winery.

“These places are not terribly far from Atlanta, but have attractive features and thriving local economies that are not dependent on the city,” said Kimberly Skobba, a professor of financial planning, housing and consumer economics at the University of Georgia.
