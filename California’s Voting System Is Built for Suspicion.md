The slow count of votes in Los Angeles led to Spencer Pratt’s defeat and allegations of a stolen election. There’s no evidence of impropriety, but the state’s slow counting is a recipe for mistrust.

If you wanted an election system meant to sow doubt and undermine faith in the republic, it’s hard to imagine beating Los Angeles and the way in which it chooses its mayor.

Last Wednesday afternoon, less than 24 hours after the polls had closed in the jungle primary, Spencer Pratt, the former reality TV star and registered Republican, was in [second place](https://www.thefp.com/p/california-wont-die-without-a-fight) —five points behind Mayor Karen Bass and eight points ahead of city councilmember Nithya Raman.

It seemed very likely—at least, to most voters—that Bass and Pratt would face off against each other in the general election: 69 percent of the vote had been counted. Raman had [broken down](https://www.yahoo.com/news/politics/articles/far-left-la-mayoral-candidate-nithya-raman-breaks-down-in-tears-as-she-falters-behind-spencer-pratt-and-karen-bass-143154637.html) in tears at her election night party! (Yes, it was true, she was addressing her 10-year-old twins, but the emotion seemed to convey loss.)

For the next couple of days, as LA County’s ballot processing center tabulated results from the mayoral race, the gap between Pratt and Raman narrowed a bit, to seven points and then six—but even Raman’s supporters were privately telling me it looked like it was over.

Then, late Friday, Raman suddenly started to outpace Bass and Pratt in the new ballot dumps, dramatically narrowing the gap to two points, then less than two points, then a little less than one.

On [Sunday morning](https://deadline.com/2026/06/nithya-raman-spencer-pratt-la-mayor-race-latest-vote-count-1236949341/), with 87 percent of the votes counted, Raman pulled ahead of Pratt—just barely. Bass had 250,871 votes; Raman, 196,198; and Pratt, 193,085.

By late afternoon, Decision Desk Headquarters [had called](https://x.com/DecisionDeskHQ/status/2063772730437075457?s=20) the second-place spot for Raman. The race was over, and Pratt was out.

Even before the call, the usual suspects—starting with Donald Trump—were casting doubt on the results.

“They’re cheating on the election,” [Trump told](https://x.com/atrupar/status/2063633879613805030?s=20) Kristen Welker of *Meet the Press* in an interview on Friday that aired Sunday. When Welker asked Trump if he had any proof of cheating, he replied, “All I have to do is look.”

On Monday, [he reiterated](https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/116715381418144428) his claim on Truth Social: “Not possible for Spencer Pratt to have lost the LA runoffs after the big lead he had. 3rd World Nation. Rigged Elections!”

It wasn’t just Trump.

On Thursday, as Raman started to pick up a little momentum in the vote count, Elon Musk [posted on X](https://x.com/elonmusk/status/2062676140766937427?s=20): “They’re not even trying hard to hide the fraud anymore.”

On Sunday morning, [Meghan McCain](https://x.com/MeghanMcCain/status/2063656375046094877?s=20), daughter of former Republican presidential contender John McCain, posted: “For whatever it’s worth, people in my life who have never ever spoken about stolen elections in any capacity are now saying this about California...”

Gerald Posner, the investigative journalist who has written award-winning books about Lee Harvey Oswald and Josef Mengele, [posted](https://x.com/geraldposner/status/2063794039481127066?s=20): “How many ‘late count’ coincidences does it take before people stop believing elections are clean?”

And on, and on.

The problem is that it takes forever to count ballots in California, which fuels conspiracy theories the likes of which the president of the United States, in his supreme recklessness, is fond of promulgating.

Even the state’s governor, Gavin Newsom, among the leading contenders for the Democratic presidential nomination in 2028, admits as much.

“We must acknowledge that the longer the voting count takes, the more mis- and disinformation spreads,” [Newsom said](https://www.gov.ca.gov/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/5.4.2026-GGN-Letter-to-ROVs-SIGNED.pdf) in a May 4 letter to county election officials across the state.

The problem is that it takes forever to count ballots in California, which fuels conspiracy theories the likes of which the president of the United States is fond of promulgating.

The reason for the delay is not complicated: mail-in ballots.

It started in 1993. Before then, only a relatively small number of Californians voted absentee. But then the state [began an experiment](https://www.latimes.com/archives/la-xpm-1993-10-31-mn-51786-story.html) in universal mail-in ballots.

Officials tested this new approach in Republican-leaning Stanislaus County. A Democratic legislature in Sacramento passed the bill enabling mail-in ballots; GOP governor Pete Wilson signed it. Conservatives thought it would save money and boost turnout.

Over the years, the practice spread across the state, and in 2008, for the first time, a majority of California voters (58 percent) [mailed in](https://capitolweekly.net/california-election-day-election-month/) their ballots.

In 2020, to limit the spread of Covid, California—now run by a Democratic governor with Democratic supermajorities in both houses of the state legislature—started mailing ballots to all voters in advance of elections.

In 2021, California became the [eighth state](https://www.reuters.com/world/california-becomes-8th-us-state-make-universal-mail-in-ballots-permanent-2021-09-28/) to make universal mail-in voting permanent. In this year’s vote, ballots could arrive up to seven days after the polls closed and still count, so long as they were postmarked on or before Election Day.

Democrats like to talk about their deep, principled commitment to the franchise and everyone exercising their right to vote, and there’s some truth to that.

“It’s a good idea, one that Republicans invented,” Mike Murphy, a former Republican consultant who, in 2020, endorsed Joe Biden for president, told me. He was referring to mail-in voting. But the conversation around mail-in voting has acquired a distinctly partisan flavor. “Democrats, over the years, have embraced it, because they believe higher turnout is good for them—which is why Republicans now oppose it.”

Newsom, for one, appears to appreciate that the optics of the long vote count are unhelpful, and last year [he signed a bill](https://calmatters.org/politics/2025/10/california-election-results-counting/) into law that bumps up the deadline for ballot-counting officials in the state’s 58 counties—without allocating any more money to speed up the process.

That has done little, if anything, to expedite the vote counting in LA.

It didn’t help that Raman had been trailing Pratt by as much as 40,000 votes and then, at the last moment, picked up enough votes to eke out a win.

Democrats, especially Raman supporters, are quick to point out that the vote fraud charge makes zero sense: This is not the general election the mayor was hoping for. She would have preferred to run against a challenger to her right than one to her left in an overwhelmingly progressive city that Bernie Sanders won in the 2020 Democratic presidential primary.

“The LA mayor stuff is the dumbest conspiracy theory I’ve ever heard & makes no sense,” CNN election analyst [Harry Enten posted](https://x.com/ForecasterEnten/status/2064004493696512344?s=20) Monday. “Bass crushes Pratt in a 1-on-1 by 18 pt, while Raman beat Bass by 4.”

Democrats add that there is nothing surprising about Pratt’s defeat. Even though the former reality-television star did not run as a Republican (the mayoral race is nonpartisan), and even though there was nothing explicitly ideological about his agenda—he mostly ran against Bass’s handling of the 2025 fires, which razed Pratt’s home, and her failure to curb homelessness, which most Angelenos acknowledge. They say he was doomed because he comes across, as Raman put it, as a “mini-Trump.” Pratt, they say, built a fake movement out of social media and AI videos and little else.

That’s kind of, sort of believable if you strip nearly 200,000 voters of their agency, and pretend that lots of smart, thoughtful Angelenos who care deeply about this city don’t have serious qualms with both Bass and Raman.

For his part, Pratt [has speculated](https://x.com/spencerpratt/status/2063784193688056310?s=20) about a ballot harvesting scandal that almost definitely is not, in fact, a ballot harvesting scandal, and tried to keep everyone’s spirits up. “Folks,” he [posted to X](https://x.com/spencerpratt/status/2064033671753982019?s=20) Monday, “we’re dealing with a fraction of a percentage point difference, there’s still hundreds of thousands of votes outstanding, and LA officials have given us the next 3 weeks to count! Let’s git-r-dun!”

“The appearance of impropriety with regard to elections is as important, or even more important, than the underlying process.”  
—Adrian Alvarez

Pep speeches notwithstanding, it’s almost certainly true that Raman came in second place fair and square, and it’s also true that Trump and Musk and their ilk are recklessly, baselessly claiming otherwise, *and* it is no doubt the case that Democrats, by implementing a system meant to prolong the Democratic monopoly in California, have unwittingly cast doubt on Raman’s success—and, more importantly, further undermined Americans’ faith in the whole process. In California and across the country.

[Adrian Alvarez](https://www.thefp.com/p/spencer-saca-la-bassura-will-las), the entrepreneur and AI-video producer behind Latinos por Pratt, told me that the current kerfuffle might present an opportunity to get to the bottom of the rot.

On Sunday, Latinos por Pratt announced that it would conduct its own audit of the vote. “Every legitimate vote in LA deserves to count, but a broken system dilutes our community’s voice,” [it said](https://x.com/LatinosPorPratt/status/2063773542404251931?s=20) in a statement.

Alvarez is unsure where the audit will lead. “The appearance of impropriety with regard to elections is as important, or even more important, than the underlying process,” he told me. “The length of this process, the weird changes in the voting patterns—they raise reasonable suspicions, in my mind, that there’s something off.”

The confusion surrounding the mayoral primary is emblematic of a larger, more amorphous confusion about the Democratic Party itself, Democrats and former Democrats told me.

“On the outside, there’s a process, but you don’t really know what’s going on,” one ex–Democratic activist said. The people in charge—Newsom, Bass, all the Democrats running to be the next governor of California—speak in happy, cartoonish platitudes disconnected from the California of right now, he added, and anytime anyone brings up anything they don’t want to talk about, “You just scream, ‘Trump! Trump! Trump!’ ”

“You really have to want to believe them and their anointed opinions,” he said.
