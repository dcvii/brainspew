Shawn still remembers where he was when he saw it: the little blue doodle that ended his career.

It was the spring of 2022. After 20 years of developing software, Shawn had worked his way up the ladders of Silicon Valley to a senior engineering position with a $150,000 salary. He was coding for the next big thing, [the metaverse](https://www.meta.com/metaverse/?srsltid=AfmBOoq_MIoDAq9Qe_OUIYnIk8m2I2xB8Z99UspM8rnsQZ6GlrbRLNYh) —a new version of the internet that existed as a virtual world people could enter with headsets. Shawn had reached the point in his career at which you should be able to breathe a sigh of relief.

But as he sat at his desk that day, something caught his eye: OpenAI had gone live with a special demonstration of their first model that could code. He clicked the link—and, in the span of five seconds, he watched as all his assumptions about what his future would look like came crashing down.

The men doing the demonstrating, a group that included Sam Altman, “just spoke commands— *Draw a square on this HTML page, and color it in blue* —and then the language model just did it,” Shawn said. “It took me five seconds of going through the whole range of emotions before I was like: *Okay, my career is cooked*.”

By April 2024, Shawn’s prediction proved accurate: He lost his job, and in short order, everything else he had come to count on fell away too. Soon, he would be living in a trailer by the woods, driving DoorDash to put food on the table. In his spare time, Shawn set up camp in his local supermarket and used the free Wi-Fi to send out hundreds of job applications, and got nothing back but silence. He had spent his life honing what he thought was the most recession-proof craft—and now, in the blink of an eye, he was suddenly obsolete.

“Nobody should learn coding; that’s just done,” Shawn told me.

If that sounds jarring, that’s because the exact opposite was until recently conventional wisdom. Fifteen years ago, coding was supposed to be the future: As Marc Andreessen said that “ [software is eating the world](https://a16z.com/why-software-is-eating-the-world/),” nonprofits like [Code.org](https://code.org/en-US) popped up, raising [$12 million](https://techcrunch.com/2026/02/15/the-great-computer-science-exodus-and-where-students-are-going-instead/) from celebrities like Bill and Melinda Gates, and collaborating with the likes of [Ashton Kutcher](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_RrmereHcTQ) and [Shakira,](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/shakira-joins-forces-with-codeorg-and-amazon-to-expand-computer-science-education-for-hispanic-youth-302270075.html) to teach underprivileged kids to program. In 2016, the Obama administration piloted a “ [Computer Science for All](https://obamawhitehouse.archives.gov/blog/2016/01/30/computer-science-all) initiative, describing computer science as “a ‘new basic” skill necessary for economic opportunity and social mobility”; and, in the run-up to the 2020 election, Joe Biden [went viral](https://thehill.com/changing-america/enrichment/education/476391-biden-tells-coal-miners-to-learn-to-code/) after he told a group of struggling miners that they should spend their days programming, rather than climbing inside a dirty hole.

And the people responded: Between 2009 and 2024, early-career computer science majors [exploded from less than 70,000 to over 250,000](https://www.aei.org/op-eds/should-you-still-major-in-computer-science/); in the [job market](https://www.adpresearch.com/the-rise-and-fall-of-the-software-developer/), the median base pay for developers grew by 24 percent between 2018 and 2024, until it was more than double the median salary in the U.S.

When Shawn started building software, it was because he thought—like most people—that it was a golden ticket. “I came from generational poverty,” he told me. His dad was a drug addict who disappeared for long stretches at a time, taking the rent money with him; his mother is disabled, and had a hard time finding work. When Shawn got into college, his biggest dream was financial stability, which is why he opted for computer science. *That’s where the money is*, he remembered thinking.

Now, the market has been flooded with AI agents like Anthropic’s Claude Code, OpenAI’s Codex, and Anysphere’s Cursor—all of which are capable of elite coding, and have [wiped out](https://x.com/a16z/status/2040203337698803981?s=20) the value of programmers’ skills. Today, the models are so good that anyone with an internet connection can be a “ [vibe coder](https://www.thefp.com/p/suzy-weiss-its-tech-week) ”—just type in a prompt, and out comes a website, [zero technical knowledge](https://www.thefp.com/p/can-vibe-coding-make-me-a-better) required.

“It took me five seconds of going through the whole range of emotions before I was like: *Okay, my career is cooked*,” Shawn said.

Last November, when Amazon cut more than 14,000 jobs, software engineers in New York, California, New Jersey, and Washington were [hit the hardest](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/11/21/amazon-cut-thousands-of-engineers-in-its-record-layoffs-filings-show.html#:~:text=Amazon%20's%2014%2C000%2Dplus%20layoffs,WARN%2C%20filings%20to%20state%20agencies), accounting for nearly 40 percent of the layoffs across those states. In February, when Jack Dorsey’s company Block cut 40 percent of its workforce, he noted that “engineering work that would have taken weeks to complete be done by a small team in [a fraction of the time](https://www.businessinsider.com/jack-dorsey-block-hiring-senior-ai-talent-iafter-layoffs-2026-2).” Just last week, Oracle announced it too is laying off thousands, with software engineers [significantly affected](https://www.latimes.com/business/story/2026-04-01/oracle-lays-off-thousands-in-latest-sign-of-tough-times-for-tech-industry).

It’s gotten so bad that even the executives themselves are starting to mourn the loss of their craft: As OpenClaw founder Peter Steinberger [recently put it](https://www.instagram.com/reel/DVXrYb4kkQm/), programming has become like knitting—something people do “because they like it, not because it makes any sense.” X’s Head of Product [Nikita Bier posted,](https://x.com/nikitabier/status/2040297547974291463?s=20) “We’re a few weeks away from where there will be no designers or engineers, but a third secret thing.”

Depending on the day, the world seems convinced we’re either headed toward a future where [everyone’s a billion-dollar tech titan](https://www.thefp.com/p/second-thought-the-playboy-mansion-for-nerds), armed with the world’s smartest coder in their back pocket—or a world of mass unemployment, where coding is as archaic as needle and thread. But for now, one thing is clear: The software engineers are freaking out. Their script was tossed out the window, and now they’re being forced to recode their lives.

“I know I’m not the only one,” said Shawn.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!3rV7!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F10f6eab3-4f00-4e88-bcae-7bab1598400c_1320x30.png)

In fact, there are those whose computer science careers might end before they begin. When Yonas Amha, a 23-year-old graduate student at Brown University, chose his major, he also chose it for financial security—as a freshman he saw alumni going into software engineering and “getting salaries that are upward of $90,000, and $100,000,” he said: “I thought, *Oh my goodness, this could potentially be me.*”

And so he majored in computer science, and for a while, things went great: In his junior year, he landed an engineering internship, and it made him excited for his future career. But by the time he came back to school in the fall of 2024, AI had suddenly changed everything. “It really just exploded,” he said. By February, Claude Code had been released, making it the first coding assistant to hit the market that’s [capable of acting autonomously](https://tasmayshah12.medium.com/claude-code-how-a-side-project-became-the-ai-coding-tool-google-engineers-prefer-in-2025-73aaa6a54371). Just like that, the skills Amha had learned were curdling like sour milk. “Everything is antiquated now,” he told me.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!hJN7!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe96395cd-13b8-49fd-9b75-ff46f45215cf_1024x697.jpeg)

“Everything is antiquated now,” Amha said. (© CORBIS/Corbis via Getty Images)

And the new technology was swallowing up all the entry-level positions for software engineers. Since ChatGPT’s public release, employment for software developers ages 22 to 25 has fallen by [almost 20 percent;](https://sfstandard.com/2025/08/27/ai-entry-level-jobs-decline/) in 2025, the number of tech-job postings dropped by nearly [one third](https://www.hiringlab.org/2025/11/20/indeed-2026-us-jobs-hiring-trends-report/) compared to early 2020.

So when graduation rolled around last spring, the job Amha thought would be a guarantee was nowhere to be found. He applied for over 700 jobs, and still couldn’t get an offer. “People are just getting ghosted,” he said. “I think you just have to not take it personally. I know a lot of people in computer science are frustrated right now.”

It has made him realize, he said, “You really have to adapt.” For now, that means continuing to study computer science in grad school, and working part time at a stock portfolio start-up with some friends, where he’s learning to be “a jack of all trades.” But when I asked him about a five-year plan, he told me there’s no point making one. “Ideally, I’d be able to work full time,” he said, but: “I can’t really tell you what is going to happen.”

“We might have systems and capabilities that I can’t even really fathom at this point,” he added.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!3rV7!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F10f6eab3-4f00-4e88-bcae-7bab1598400c_1320x30.png)

The fact is, if the AI takeover is already underway, computer science majors are best-placed to become experts in harnessing its power; there has never been a better time to learn the ropes of our brave new automated world than today.

For Daniel Bicalho, a 21-year-old computer science student, coding with a personal assistant is all he’s ever known. “My freshman year, AI could already do any coding assignment,” he told me. “It could knock out anything I got, by itself, literally in a minute or two.”

You’d think computer science professors would have noticed that the tools they’ve been teaching are no longer valuable. But Bicalho said they haven’t even budged. “The curriculum has still stayed the same,” he said. “I don’t think professors have found a way to work around AI yet.”

“When AI finishes that in a few minutes, you have a little bit of emptiness in your mind—like, *What am I?*” Han said.

Now, with everyone on campus carrying around an automatic agent, and no one teaching them to harness it, Bicalho believes computer science students like him are complicit in de-skilling themselves. “You’ll find that a lot of these students don’t actually know how to code,” he said. “They’re just blindly writing AI slop.”

“There’s not a single employer that wants to hire them,” he explained, “because when it comes to discussing coding topics and fundamentals, they can’t actually say anything on the topic.”

Bicalho doesn’t reject the machine—in fact, as he told me, “I use AI for literally everything”—but he said he gets his competitive edge by acting as an editor: someone who knows that “their main purpose from now on is refining” their AI agent’s work—but knowing enough about coding to fix it if it goes wrong. After all, we need to preserve technical craftsmanship—the [final masters of C++](https://www.geeksforgeeks.org/cpp/cpp-programming-intro/), fighting to keep human coding skills alive in case the machines suddenly [blip out](http://google.com/url?q=https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/03/iran-war-uae-drone-strikes-aws-data-centers.html&sa=D&source=docs&ust=1775500750207274&usg=AOvVaw1ZtVNTyHlFcqb_YEQ_-of8).

As Bicalho sees it, even if AI gets good enough to essentially carry out all the work we need doing without us intervening, the people in charge of it still need to know how it’s being done. Maybe the most worrying fact is that human beings are forgetting how the machines we invented work.

“I know a lot of software engineers in big tech, at Microsoft or Amazon, who literally haven’t written lines of code in years,” said Bicalho. Why would they, when an AI agent can do it for them? “That’s already the software-engineering mindset: They just vibe their way through what they need to do.”

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!3rV7!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F10f6eab3-4f00-4e88-bcae-7bab1598400c_1320x30.png)

Maybe outsourcing all their work to a machine will be great for the workers of Silicon Valley—after all, these are the people who used to grind so hard that the internet often joked that they forget to shower. But does using AI actually make us happy? A recent [Harvard study reported](https://www.hks.harvard.edu/faculty-research/policy-topics/science-technology-data/daily-ai-use-associated-depressive-symptoms) that rates of moderate depression were 30 percent higher among those with daily AI use. And when I talked to Patrick Han, who works as head of growth at an AI software company called Cliwant, he said he has noticed that his coders are beginning to feel worthless.

“We have two engineers at our company, and they have expressed that they have AI depression since last year,” Han said. While they used to spend their days coding solutions, now they mostly spend their time planning instructions for the model. They spend 30 minutes brainstorming prompts, he said, and then: “When AI finishes that in a few minutes, you have a little bit of emptiness in your mind—like, *What am I?*”

As Han talked to more and more people in his industry, he kept hearing the same thing: AI was making them more productive than ever—but it was also eating away at their self-esteem. At one coffee chat, a senior engineer at Meta told him that he’d gone from excited to hopeless in the span of a single [Opus update](https://www.anthropic.com/claude/opus); at another, a Stanford MBA told him he was considering dropping out of tech entirely, for the “very old-fashioned” practice of building a restaurant. A third engineer confessed that she had gone to her weekly music class and left distraught, after her instructor vibe coded a piano game that even *she* couldn’t have made.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!8ute!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0934a9a4-4bd6-4476-b335-43c5c43384af_1024x697.jpeg)

“If you’re not feeling Claude Blue,” Han said, “you haven’t used AI that much.”

The AI depression has gotten so widespread among his peers that Han has even given it a name: “Claude Blue.”

“If you’re not feeling Claude Blue,” he said, “you haven’t used AI that much.”

The fear isn’t just job insecurity. “It’s not just about how are you going to make money,” said Han. Rather, the engineers are concerned that the machine’s intelligence will soon outpace their own—and upend our role as human beings entirely. “Our mental intelligence has made us on top of the food chain,” Han said. But his team is working with tech that “will eventually be stronger than us, and that feeling is making us inferior.”

For now, Han says the feeling is mostly confined to the engineering community, because the models are better at approximating their thinking patterns, whereas his work on the growth team—crafting creative business solutions for a changing market—is safe for a bit longer. But he’s worried about lulling himself into a false sense of security. “I’m trying my best to feel as Claude Blue as I can,” he said.

When I asked him why exactly he’s leaning into a feeling of purposelessness, he said he wasn’t quite sure. He just knows “AI is going to be the future”—and he wants to be at the “forefront.”

In fact, many of the tech workers I spoke to insisted that the machines that would replace them would also bring them the keys to the kingdom. “Everybody needs to embrace AI,” said Shawn. “I didn’t spend even a minute thinking about that.”

Now that he has accepted that learning to code was a mistake, he has started to see the potential of learning to master the machines. “There’s no limits now,” he said. With software like [OpenClaw](https://www.thefp.com/p/whos-excited-to-be-replaced-by-ai) allowing bots to manage businesses and make transactions, he added, “It’s already possible today to run a zero-person company,” with “agents going 24/7, just running it for you.” This year, he predicts a fully automated staff will be all you need to fuel a billion-dollar company.

And Shawn is now on his way toward that future. After he lost his job, he spent up to five hours a day studying the latest AI developments—and now, after a year of unemployment, it’s finally paying its dividends: He recently landed a job as director of development at ServiceHub, working closely with automated models, and running a small group of programmers beneath him.

His first goal? To make his team vanish. “I think exactly what we should do is automate ourselves away,” he said. “That’s what I’ve been building and working on, and there’s no contradiction there.”

If it’s not a contradiction, it’s a change of perspective: The software engineers of Silicon Valley who are going to survive have to speak about unemployment as if it’s the dream, and purposelessness as if it’s the goal.

“Let the robots do the work,” said Shawn. “That’s going to be the beginning of the *Star Trek* utopia.”

But in the meantime, he just wants to do what he has always done: grind. “It’s going to take a long time to get there,” he said, “and it’s not going to be easy.”
