CHICAGO— [Barack Obama’s](https://www.wsj.com/topics/person/barack-obama) new presidential center isn’t a cheap date, and neither is his adopted hometown.

When it opens June 19, it will set at least three modern-era records for a former White House occupant: time taken to be completed, project cost and the price to get inside.

At $30, adult admission to see the 44th president’s story is more than at any other U.S. presidential library, a Wall Street Journal review shows. That is 59% higher than the average for presidents from John F. Kennedy through George W. Bush.

The top admission for the Obama Presidential Center is in keeping with the record expense of the project in a city known for complex and costly urban development, steep taxes and premium cultural attractions.

Despite the price, Chicago is certain to become a Democratic mecca for those eager to reconnect with their party’s most popular living [former president](https://www.wsj.com/politics/elections/barack-obama-dnc-speech-kamala-harris-election-2024-fb851a40?mod=article_inline). The center is expected to attract approximately 700,000 visitors annually and be an economic engine for the city, while also potentially helping transform the surrounding lower-income neighborhood.

The crowds and fundraising success (the Obama Foundation [has disclosed](https://www.obama.org/about/contributors/) donors of $1,001 or more) contrast with the struggles of the next Democratic president after Obama.

Former President Joe Biden has gotten off to a slow fundraising start for a center in Delaware, with some donors saying raising the necessary funds will be a [heavy lift](https://www.wsj.com/politics/the-week-that-derailed-joe-bidens-post-presidency-1eb0d353?mod=article_inline) given how his [presidency ended](https://www.wsj.com/politics/elections/biden-drops-out-presidential-race-6b1c74fd?mod=article_inline). Presidents typically raise as much money as possible in the year after leaving the White House because it becomes more challenging as time passes, they become less relevant and major donors pass away.

While the Obama project was initially estimated at $300 million, the final price tag hit about $850 million. The 19.3-acre campus includes a museum, foundation offices, a public library and recreational spaces.

That makes it the nation’s most expensive. Roughly $500 million was raised for the most recently built presidential shrine, the George W. Bush Presidential Library and Museum in Dallas.

Illinois residents, who helped underwrite some infrastructure costs, will get in free on Tuesdays and receive a $4 discount other days. They must show proof of residency, which conservatives say is hypocritical given Democratic opposition to voter ID requirements.

“Our campus is free and open to the public, with the exception of the four floors of the museum,” said Emily Bittner, a foundation spokeswoman. “We offer tremendous new amenities that no other presidential center provides, like an NBA-regulation-size basketball court, two-level playground, recording studio, classroom spaces and more than two dozen newly commissioned pieces of public art.”

Reduced and free admission will be available to those receiving federal food assistance and active duty personnel.

Admission is in line with other major Chicago attractions. The Kenneth C. Griffin Museum of Science and Industry, not far away on the city’s South Side, is $25.95 for an adult. The Adler Planetarium is $25, while the Art Institute of Chicago is $32.

Visitors will also face other high costs in Chicago, a city that President Trump, [who is planning](https://www.wsj.com/politics/elections/trump-reveals-presidential-library-design-a-glass-tower-in-miami-93869254?mod=article_inline) a library in Miami featuring a glass tower emblazoned with his name, has long criticized as a Democrat-ruined hellscape.

Those in larger hotels downtown, near the McCormick Place convention center and some other areas, will pay the nation’s highest hotel tax at 19%. The new rate, up from 17.5%, starts May 1, and the extra funds will be used to market Chicago to conventions and tourists.

Purchases at the Obama center’s gift shop will include a combined state and local sales tax of at least 10.25%, one of the highest in the nation. There is also a 15 cent shopping bag tax (the retailer gets a penny and the city receives 14 cents).

For those who want an adult beverage after looking at all that Obama history, expect a double-digit tax that varies by alcohol content. For the children, there are water bottle and soda taxes.

If visitors want to smoke—something Obama enjoyed doing as a younger man—it will cost them dearly. Chicago has the nation’s highest combined state-local tax of $7.16 a pack, according to Campaign for Tobacco-Free Kids.

The city’s short-term parking rates—some of the nation’s highest—have climbed since former Mayor Richard M. Daley sealed a deal in 2008 to lease Chicago’s parking meters for 75 years in exchange for an upfront payment of more than $1.1 billion—an arrangement that has proven profitable for the private investors and a boondoggle for taxpayers.

Joe Ferguson, president of the Civic Federation, a Chicago watchdog group that tracks local and state finances, said the city fails to look at taxes in their entirety. “It’s always: ‘We need some more money and what we can ratchet up next?’ ” he said.

Higher costs associated with the Obama center will give Republicans a “fresh round of substantiation” for the narrative that Democrats favor higher fees and taxes, Ferguson said.

Still, the GOP should tread cautiously. One of their own isn’t far behind: A ticket to the [Richard Nixon Presidential Library and Museum](https://www.wsj.com/articles/nixon-library-gets-a-new-look-will-the-ex-president-also-get-one-1476378903?mod=article_inline) in Yorba Linda, Calif., costs $29.

The Obama center’s opening is roughly five years behind what was originally planned after preservationists and activists slowed construction in court. Pandemic-related disruptions also delayed work.

The project’s urban environment created additional costs and complexities, as did Obama’s 2016 decision to locate the center in a park listed on the National Register of Historic Places. The site triggered multiple federal reviews and legal challenges.

Presidential libraries opened in recent decades in about half the time as this one, Wall Street Journal [calculations show](https://www.wsj.com/politics/policy/obama-presidential-center-to-set-modern-record-for-timetable-to-opening-11623848401?mod=article_inline). The Ronald Reagan Presidential Library opened just more than 1,000 days after he left office. Bill Clinton’s took 1,398 days. The libraries of George H.W. Bush and George W. Bush averaged 1,653 days. Obama’s is set for 3,437 days.

Obama’s center won’t technically be a library. Instead, his foundation is paying $5 million to support digitizing millions of pages of unclassified records for online use. It will be run by the foundation rather than the National Archives and Records Administration, the federal agency that traditionally operates the libraries and museums.
