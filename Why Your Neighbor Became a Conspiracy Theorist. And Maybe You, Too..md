They were “kidnapping our loved ones and replac\[ing\] them with a bitter hollow shell of what they once were.”

This sounds like a line from the campy 1978 sci-fi horror flick *Invasion of the Body Snatchers*, about aliens from a dying planet that come to Earth and replace humans with clones that are devoid of their old personality.

But it’s not.

It’s actually a quote from a [recent study](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/pdf/10.1177/02654075251328116?utm_source=consensus) in the *Journal of Social and Personal Relationships* about the QAnon conspiracy theory, which alleges that the governing elite is dominated by a secret cabal of Satan-worshipping, cannibalistic, deep-state pedophiles. Participants in the study described watching the theory take hold of their friends and family members, transforming them beyond recognition.

Perhaps this scenario sounds familiar. You too may know someone who started “doing his own research” on politics, science, or the economy—and then went down an internet rabbit hole of posts claiming that some event or phenomenon was plotted by a clandestine group of powerful people or organizations, usually with sinister or malevolent intentions. Before you know it, he seems like a different person. His new beliefs dominate conversations and ruin relationships.

Our current news cycle has no shortage of online influencers peddling ever more outlandish theories. A great deal of research has been dedicated to the quest for an explanation of why people might adopt conspiracy beliefs. Some scholars suggest that such susceptibility has to do with personal traits [such as narcissism](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0092656622000423?via%3Dihub) or [deficiencies in intelligence](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/ijop.70110).

But these arguments are too facile. Think of the now-infamous *[Vanity Fair](https://www.vanityfair.com/news/story/trump-susie-wiles-interview-exclusive-part-1?_sp=f57f8cc6-ef1b-49eb-a079-6ae23bb7d9f8.1774568856224)* [interview](https://www.vanityfair.com/news/story/trump-susie-wiles-interview-exclusive-part-1?_sp=f57f8cc6-ef1b-49eb-a079-6ae23bb7d9f8.1774568856224) in which the White House chief of staff Susie Wiles said that Vice President J.D. Vance had been a “conspiracy theorist for a decade.” Vance—who, whether you like him or not, is certainly not unintelligent— [cleverly responded](https://www.cleveland.com/news/2025/12/jd-vance-embraces-conspiracy-theorist-label-i-only-believe-the-ones-that-are-true.html) by saying that he believes in only the “conspiracy theories that are true.”

He has a point: Many outlandish-seeming scandals have turned out to be absolutely factual, such as the squalid [Untreated Syphilis Study at Tuskegee](https://www.cdc.gov/tuskegee/about/index.html) that lasted from the 1930s to the 1970s. Present-day cases have also occurred, such as the [official discrediting](https://www.vox.com/2020/3/4/21156607/how-did-the-coronavirus-get-started-china-wuhan-lab) of the lab-leak origin of the coronavirus pandemic—later shown to be the most plausible explanation of Covid-19’s emergence—as nothing more than an anti-Chinese conspiracy theory.

But even conspiracy theories that are completely fictitious and, well, *nuts*, don’t require a person to be a stupid narcissist to embrace them. Rather, [psychologists believe](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/pdf/10.1177/0963721417718261?utm_source=consensus) that these narratives can be so compelling to regular people because they meet three deep human needs. The first is *epistemic*, which refers to the need to understand one’s environment in an increasingly complex world. The second is *existential*, which is to feel safe and in control of one’s environment. The third is *social*, referring to the need to maintain a positive image of oneself and one’s social group.

Another way to account for these needs is that people crave *coherence*: in other words, an answer to the question of why things happen the way they do, from the weather to political events and world affairs. According to the psychologists Frank Martela and Michael F. Steger, coherence is [one element](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/2016-30427-009) of the meaning of life; the other two are *purpose* (where you are going in life) and *significance* (why your life matters). Without a sense of coherence, one’s life is bereft of meaning. Meaning, in turn, is [central to human happiness](https://www.arthurbrooks.com/the-meaning-of-your-life).

The embrace of conspiracy theories is a way to gain a sense of coherence for people who otherwise lack it. Viewed another way, conspiracy theorizing is a lamentation of helplessness—because life feels *meaningless*. This crisis of meaning [lies behind](https://www.thefp.com/p/arthur-brooks-why-your-perfect-life-feels-so-empty) the psychogenic epidemic of depression, anxiety, and loneliness that afflicts our society today.

That feeling of meaninglessness has exploded in the last two decades. The research group Monitoring the Future has shown that from 2008 to the present, the percentage of young adults who agreed with the statement “Life often seems meaningless” approximately doubled, to more than one in five. Isolate this survey to college students and the pattern is the same; indeed, most of these young people have given up even *looking* for life’s meaning. It pains me as a Catholic to report that, [according to data](https://www.heri.ucla.edu/monographs/50YearTrendsMonograph2016.pdf) from the Higher Education Research Institute at the University of California, Los Angeles, the number one goal of American undergraduates in the mid-1960s was “developing a meaningful philosophy of life,” an aim that was shared by nearly 90 percent of students. Today, fewer than half of undergraduates say this is one of their goals at all.

The embrace of conspiracy theories is a way to gain a sense of coherence for people who otherwise lack it.

Traditionally, people could find coherence in both the physical and metaphysical realms, through news coverage, scientific knowledge, and religion. But Americans have lost trust in all of the institutions that support these sources of coherence. According to the polling organization Gallup, the percentage of Americans who have a “great deal” or “quite a lot” of confidence in newspapers has [dropped by half](https://news.gallup.com/poll/1597/confidence-institutions.aspx) since 2002 (from 35 percent to 17 percent). Those with a great deal of confidence in the public-health system fell by a similar margin (from 22 percent to 12 percent) since the 2020 onset of the pandemic. Meanwhile, the percentage of people who express “very little” confidence in the church or organized religion increased by half since 2002 (from 18 to 27 percent). [According to data](https://www.pewresearch.org/religion/2025/02/26/religious-switching/) from the Pew Research Center, more than eight people now leave the Catholic Church each year for every one individual who joins.

So where do people turn for coherence if they don’t trust journalists, doctors, scientists, and clergy to tell them the truth? The answer is the internet, where once-fringe ideas can propagate with astonishing speed and efficiency. Online, people can connect with fellow alienated souls in a world that feels as if it’s somewhere between a rigged game and total chaos. The internet provides answers, assigns culpability, creates something like a community, and channels rage.

I always assumed that conspiracy theories were totally ineffective for providing the coherence and meaning people seek. But based on the research, my assumption was incorrect. In fact, the [data demonstrate](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2405844024105889) that for those who are alienated from society—who feel discriminated against and downtrodden—conspiracy beliefs do, in fact, provide a sense of meaning to their lives. Other work has [shown no evidence](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/21677026251370092) that conspiracy theories themselves lead to psychological distress.

The problems—in addition to the concrete societal harm caused by the dissemination of dangerous beliefs—come with the typical *delivery mechanism* of conspiracy theories, and the effects they have on a theorist’s relationships. In excess, internet use is [clearly linked](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1089/cyber.2021.0300) to depression and anxiety. And although online connection can provide a limited sense of community, a virtual friend group is no emotional substitute for love relationships in real life—and these are [generally eroded](https://www.cambridge.org/core/services/aop-cambridge-core/content/view/414F5DA8C1A46B1B3AB77673E606193C/S1138741623000082a.pdf/div-class-title-does-belief-in-conspiracy-theories-affect-interpersonal-relationships-div.pdf?utm_source=consensus) by conspiracy beliefs. This [leads to](https://bpspsychub.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/bjso.12865) social isolation and loneliness; it also harms loved ones, as the first study I cited succinctly expresses—and as your own experience might confirm as well.

So, what’s the solution? At the public level, it is to reestablish broader confidence in our institutions that previously provided a wide sense of coherence; namely, the news media, academia, and religious organizations. As a columnist, scholar, and churchgoer myself, I am deeply embedded in all three—and I’d have to acknowledge a tendency among members in all of my institutions to feel aggrieved, to shift blame onto malevolent influencers, bad political actors, even the distrustful people themselves. I realize it is much more productive to look inward at how these institutions have too often become politicized and faddish, and thus lost the public’s trust. By facing the problem fair and square, we can see it as an opportunity to improve.

The solution at the individual level, which involves asking what you can do to win back the loved one who has gone down the rabbit hole, starts with a bit of neuroscience. Researchers [have found](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-03723-z.pdf?utm_source=consensus) that conspiracy believers exhibit high activation of the ventromedial prefrontal cortex, a brain region that’s important for self-understanding and emotional associations. What else, do you suppose, involves similar high activation that provides coherence in our lives? [Romantic love](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0028393224002185?via%3Dihub). As does [deep friendship](https://srcd.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/pdfdirect/10.1111/cdep.12450?utm_source=consensus). And, not coincidentally, these phenomena are negatively associated with holding conspiratorial views. Researchers [have hypothesized](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/2023-87261-001) that love acts as a protective agent against these ideas, whereas social isolation [increases the likelihood](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S0022103116304711) of holding these beliefs.

I know: Getting closer can be hard when your loved one is spouting what seems to you like crazy nonsense. What comes more naturally is a desire to confront the person with facts or insults. But no one has ever been brought to their senses with hatred and rage. On the contrary, this will make the problem worse.

As with so many other parts of life, the answer to what is tearing you apart is to give more love, in spite of how you may feel.
