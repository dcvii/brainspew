For nearly five weeks, President Donald Trump has bombarded Iran with missiles and bombs—and the American public with contradictory messaging about the aims and duration of this conflict.

On Wednesday night he did it the old-fashioned way, with a [20-minute television address](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/04/01/us/politics/trump-transcript-speech-iran.html). The U.S. is “on track to complete all of America’s military objectives shortly,” he said, “very shortly. We are going to hit them extremely hard over the next two to three weeks.”

He also disclaimed all responsibility for reopening the Strait of Hormuz, saying that “the countries of the world that do receive oil through the Hormuz Strait must take care of that passage.” Earlier that day, Trump extended this invitation not only to the European members of NATO, whom he blames for failing to provide support for his military action, but also to South Korea, Japan—and China. Not for the first time, he could offer no details on the talks he claims to be having with Iranian representatives.

Only future historians will be able to work out if Trump has been procrastinating while talking. Could earlier action have been taken to secure the Strait? Could U.S. ground forces have been deployed sooner to reopen it? Does he still intend to deploy them? We cannot yet know.

But history does provide a useful point of comparison here.

Consider [an interview](https://unherd.com/2025/04/jd-vance-my-message-to-europe/?edition=us) J.D. Vance gave to *UnHerd* on April 15, 2025. “Just going back through history, I think—frankly—the British and the French were certainly right in their disagreements with Eisenhower about the Suez Canal,” he said.

It will be a richly ironic development if, in just a few years, the vice president of the United States finds himself in the position of Harold Macmillan, who was British prime minister Anthony Eden’s successor after the [Suez Crisis](https://www.britannica.com/event/Suez-Crisis) of 1956.

To summarize: In 1956, after Gamal Abdel Nasser’s Egypt nationalized the Suez Canal, Britain, in a concerted effort with France and Israel, attempted to seize the waterway and topple the Egyptian regime. President Dwight D. Eisenhower refused to back the operation. The ensuing debacle left control of a key choke point in the hands of a radical regime and undermined the stability of pro-Western governments in Iraq and Lebanon. Vance’s point last year was that Eisenhower had been mistaken not to back Britain.

Seventy years later, however, the administration Vance is part of has put the United States in precisely the role of Britain in 1956, joining forces with Israel to seize a crucial waterway and to overthrow the regime that aspires to control it. This was a venture about which the vice president was prudently skeptical—which may be why the Iranians have now [expressed a preference](https://www.trtworld.com/article/b89be113016e) for negotiating peace terms with Vance.

There is now a rising probability that Trump’s war backfires on him much as Eden’s did—economically, politically, and geopolitically. Which raises the question: Is this the American Suez?

Imagine that, as Trump keeps hinting to the media, he decides to declare victory in a matter of weeks, leaving the Islamic Republic not only intact but also in control of the Strait of Hormuz.

That would be a lot like Suez ’56. The crisis begins as an attempt to overthrow an unfriendly regime in the Middle East, in concert with the state of Israel. It is also a struggle for control of a key commercial waterway. Despite lacking a plausible basis in international law and popular support at home, the military operation is nevertheless a success in its own terms. The armed forces of the invaded state are swiftly overwhelmed.

But the economic consequences of the conflict—not just the closure of a crucial oil choke point, but also the financial ramifications of the crisis—result in political damage and strategic defeat. The result is humiliating withdrawal.

For the United States, ceding control of the Strait of Hormuz to Iran in 2026 would not be quite as swift a humiliation as the Anglo-French failure to wrest control of the Suez Canal from Egypt in 1956. However, the consequences for the credibility of the *Pax Americana* would be similar to Suez’s consequences for the *Pax Britannica*. It would permanently damage both the legitimacy and the credibility of American primacy—and pave the way for a new international order, in which a rising power would have the option to play the leading role.

“Great Britain has lost an empire,” former secretary of state Dean Acheson [declared in 1962](https://www.jstor.org/stable/2639732), “and has not yet found a role.” If Trump folds, the United States too would have lost something a lot like an empire. The challenge for the successor to Donald Trump—Vance, if history repeats itself—would be to find a new role for a post-hegemonic America.

Only future historians will be able to work out if Trump has been procrastinating while talking.

Let’s start at the beginning. Just as a wartime coup has put the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) in charge of Iran, so too was Egypt under military control before, during, and after the Suez Crisis, due to Gamal Abdel Nasser’s Free Officers [overthrowing King Farouk](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/history/king-farouk-egypt-overthrown) in 1952. The IRGC has not overthrown the theocracy that ruled Iran between 1979 and the assassination of Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei on February 28, but it has co-opted it through the selection of the IRGC’s ally, Khamenei’s son Mojtaba, as his successor on March 8.

Of course, there are obvious differences between the Free Officers of 1956 and the IRGC of 2026. The IRGC embedded itself in Iran’s politics, economy, and society over decades before assuming overt control, whereas the Free Officers penetrated the Egyptian state and society only after toppling the monarchy. More importantly, Nasser subordinated Islamism to Egyptian nationalism and pan-Arabism, while the IRGC began life as a theocratic Praetorian Guard. By the time of the Suez Crisis, Egyptian politics had become a cult of personality under the charismatic Nasser. The Iranian regime, by contrast, is proving as difficult to decapitate as the Hydra of ancient legend.

Yet there are striking similarities, too. Both regimes outmaneuvered communist forces during the throes of revolution. Egypt’s poor performance in the 1948 war with Israel motivated Nasser’s move against King Farouk, just as the IRGC rose in response to the conventional Iranian army’s lackluster display against Iraq in the 1980s. Hostility toward Israel and Western powers served as a guiding principle for the Free Officers. The same is true for the IRGC. Both opposed Western-backed regional blocs: the British-led [Baghdad Pact](https://2001-2009.state.gov/r/pa/ho/time/lw/98683.htm) for Nasser and the U.S.-organized [Abraham Accords](https://www.thefp.com/p/the-middle-east-is-ready-for-the) for the IRGC.

Above all, each of the two regimes grasped the vital importance of a strategic waterway.

[

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!0sca!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2d04be14-6bd8-4189-9d64-377cc5cb78b9_1320x30.png)

While today’s war in Iran may initially have been intended to bring about regime change and is still officially about regime disarmament, it very quickly became about the Strait of Hormuz. In the 1950s, the strategic waterway was the Suez Canal and the war was precipitated by its nationalization. As today, its closure was an unintended consequence.

In 1954, Egypt and Britain signed a [Suez Base evacuation agreement](https://treaties.fcdo.gov.uk/data/Library2/pdf/1955-TS0067.pdf). The agreement ended the 72-year British occupation of Egypt, set up a phased evacuation of British troops, and envisaged handing over control of the Canal—from the Anglo-French company that ran it—to Egypt in 1968.

But 12 years before that, in 1956, Nasser decided to nationalize the Canal. The decision was prompted by a seemingly unrelated American decision to pull funding from Egypt’s project to build the Aswan High Dam. After Nasser bought arms from the communist government in Czechoslovakia, there was a growing suspicion that the USSR was, [in the words of](https://dokumen.pub/israels-border-wars-1949-1956-arab-infiltration-israeli-retaliation-and-the-countdown-to-the-suez-war-0198278500-9780198278504.html) Secretary of State John Foster Dulles, “plotting and planning to gain control of the Middle East.”

Nasser anticipated that Britain and France might take military action if he nationalized the Canal. So he sent agents to Malta and Cyprus to collect the latest military intelligence on British deployments. The final withdrawal of British troops from the Canal Zone was completed a month before Nasser’s decision to nationalize it.

A few hours before his [July 26 speech](https://history.blog.gov.uk/2016/07/26/whats-the-context-26-july-1956-nasser-announces-the-nationalisation-of-the-suez-canal/) announcing the operation, Nasser’s briefing to the Egyptian cabinet was met with consternation. He dismissed his colleagues’ references to Mohammad Mossadegh—the Iranian prime minister toppled by MI6 and the CIA in 1953 for attempting to nationalize the Iranian oil industry. But they were right to worry. The empire would indeed strike back.

**Protesters demonstrating against the Suez Crisis in Trafalgar Square, London, November 1956. (Stan Meagher via Getty Images)**

Why did Britain take that risk of invading Egypt to reclaim the Suez Canal, with only France and Israel on board? At the time, the country looked to be going through something of a “vibe shift.” The Conservatives had just been reelected with an enlarged majority in the House of Commons. Sir Anthony Eden was a glamorous, popular leader. The Tories were benefiting from an economic boom and the deregulation of what, for years after 1945, had remained a wartime economy. The Labour Opposition seemed, by contrast, tired, out of date, and in need of fresh leadership.

Moreover, despite or perhaps because of his Orientalist education (he had excelled in “Oriental languages” at Oxford), the ailing Eden conceived a violent animus against Nasser. In January 1956, he even compared the Egyptian leader to Benito Mussolini. In March 1956, Eden [told a startled](https://www.upi.com/Archives/1985/05/24/Prime-Minister-Sir-Anthony-Eden-demanded-the-assassination-of/8643485755200/) Sir Anthony Nutting—the minister of state for foreign affairs—that he “wanted him \[Nasser\] murdered.”

How did the rest of Eden’s Egypt Committee come around to his view that military action against Nasser was warranted? Partly because the imperialist hard-liners got the upper hand. Partly because Selwyn Lloyd, the foreign secretary, was excessively deferential to Eden. Partly because Harold Macmillan, the powerful and ambitious chancellor of the Exchequer, misread American signals and assumed that support from Washington would be forthcoming for military action.

British officials also saw Egyptian control of the Canal as a grave threat. In May 1955, even before the nationalization, the British Cabinet Office noted: “Not only will the Canal revert wholly to Egyptian control in 1968 but the Constantinople Convention \[which since 1888 had guaranteed free international transit through the Canal\] is no longer an effective instrument. Moreover, when Egypt acquires full control, it is to be expected that she will seek to force up the Canal dues and possibly to indulge in flag discriminations at our expense.”

The economic threat was not imaginary. In 1955, 62 million metric tons of crude oil and 5 million tons of oil products passed northward through the Canal—around 9 percent of the global oil market. Twenty million tons headed to Britain through the Canal. And the volume was projected to grow as the UK economy switched from coal to oil as its primary energy source. Yet there was little clear discussion of what kind of government would replace Nasser’s if the Anglo-French-Israeli intervention succeeded in toppling him. Nor is it entirely clear why Eden gave up on the American effort to negotiate an international agreement on the Canal’s management.

Which brings us back to Iran. In a similar way, back in February, there seems to have been surprisingly little opposition to the decision to go to war within Trump’s tightly knit circle of advisers. Vance, though a long-standing critic of “forever wars,” [advised Trump](https://www.realclearpolitics.com/articles/2026/03/03/vance_defends_new_war_middle_east_after_years_condemning_neocons_153901.html) to “go big and go fast.” The risk of the Strait of Hormuz being closed [was acknowledged](https://www.msn.com/en-us/news/world/trump-knew-the-risk-of-iran-blocking-the-strait-of-hormuz-he-still-went-to-war/ar-AA1YAewn?cvid=1aea07d6fb284805a9cb2e8ae7153ec5&ocid=a2hs&apiversion=v2&domshim=1&noservercache=1&noservertelemetry=1&batchservertelemetry=1&renderwebcomponents=1&wcseo=1) by the chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, General Dan Caine, but downplayed in the White House, in part due to the lack of interagency coordination. The fact that Marco Rubio acts as both secretary of state and national security adviser—posts previously held jointly only once, by Henry Kissinger—has left the National Security Council diminished.

Key Iran experts, notably Brian Hook, special representative for Iran in the first Trump administration, were [effectively purged](https://www.nytimes.com/2020/08/06/us/politics/iran-us-brian-hook.html) last year. They might have warned Trump against the belief that he could find a collaborator analogous to the Venezuelan vice president, Delcy Rodríguez, in Tehran. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent was preoccupied with the much-anticipated (but since postponed) U.S.-China presidential summit. The president’s son-in-law, Jared Kushner, was focused on disarming Hamas—a key part of the Gaza peace deal he negotiated last year—and brokering a compromise peace between Russia and Ukraine.

Like the attack on Iran, the Suez Crisis was a joint venture from the outset. The Tripartite Alliance was agreed between Britain, France, and Israel in October 1956. Israel would attack the Sinai and create a threat near the Canal; Britain and France would then issue an ultimatum to both Egypt and Israel to stop fighting and withdraw from the Canal area; once Egypt refused, Britain and France would intervene under the pretext of protecting the Canal. This collusion was supposed to be kept secret, but the French and Israelis insisted on a signed document, much to Eden’s dismay. British attempts to destroy all copies were unsuccessful.

The arguments for attempting regime change in Iran in 2026 were as appealing as the arguments for attempting it in Egypt 70 years ago.

The coordination between the Trump administration and Israeli prime minister Benjamin Netanyahu has been much more open. But in both cases Israel’s strategic goals were fundamentally different from those of its partner.

In the 1950s, the Israelis were mainly interested in stopping attacks by Egyptian-backed Palestinian terrorists (the fedayeen) from Gaza and Sinai, preempting Egypt’s arms buildup, and acquiring additional territory as a security buffer.

Meanwhile, though French prime minister and socialist Guy Mollet shared Eden’s view of Nasser as a 1930s-style dictator, his motives are impossible to disentangle from the growing struggle against Algerian separatists. The view in Paris was that toppling Nasser could weaken the Algerian Front de Libération Nationale (FLN), which was fighting a brutal war against French settlers. Between August and September 1956, France and Israel engaged in secret military planning for an invasion. The reason for involving Britain was the need for access to British air bases in Cyprus.

The Israeli campaign, Operation Kadesh, was the opening move, scheduled for October 29. It had three principal military goals: to create a military threat to the Canal, to seize the Straits of Tiran, and to destroy the Egyptian military presence in northern Sinai.

Britain and France launched their attacks on October 31, the day after Egypt, as planned, rejected their ultimatum. Their forces swiftly achieved air superiority. French paratroops captured Port Fuad on the eastern side of the Canal, and British paratroops seized El Gamil airfield, opening the way for a seaborne assault. Amphibious landings at Port Said began on November 6.

Ruins and destroyed weapons in Port Said, Egypt, in November 1956. (AFP via Getty Images)

The Israeli campaign was a clear success. It captured Gaza and most of Sinai within days, despite stiff Egyptian resistance, and broke the Egyptian blockade of the Straits of Tiran via Sharm el-Sheikh. However, by November 6, when Eden announced a ceasefire, British and French forces had captured only the northern entry point of the Canal. Meanwhile, Egyptian forces had sunk and disabled old ships during the assault, thereby blocking the Suez Canal. It remained closed for five months. The Egyptians had lost the military battle, like the Iranians today. But they had not lost the economic war.

Back in the U.S., President Dwight D. Eisenhower was surprised and enraged by the Anglo-French-Israeli attack on Egypt.

It was an election year in the United States, and the war had broken out within days of the presidential vote on November 6. A key component of Ike’s campaign was his reputation as an experienced statesman, a great war leader who now was the custodian of world peace. He and Secretary of State Dulles regarded Arab nationalism as too powerful a force in the Middle East to be resisted by the old European empires that had carved up the Ottoman Empire between themselves after World War I. Moreover, the Suez Crisis coincided almost exactly with the Soviet decision to use the Red Army to crush a liberalizing government in Hungary.

Dulles [told Vice President Richard Nixon](https://history.state.gov/historicaldocuments/frus1955-57v16/d442) that only two things were important. One was that they might be witnessing “the beginning of the collapse of the Soviet Empire.” The other was that “the idea is out that we can be dragged along at the heels of Britain and France in policies that are obsolete. This is a declaration of independence \[that\] for the first time that they cannot count upon us.”

U.S. Secretary of State John Foster Dulles speaks with President Dwight Eisenhower in the Oval Office on August 3, 1956. (Abbie Rowe via Getty Images)

The Eisenhower administration’s formal response to the Suez Crisis ran through the United Nations. A Security Council resolution was predictably vetoed as two of the permanent members were parties to the attack on Egypt. On November 2, 1956, the UN General Assembly adopted [Resolution 997](https://www.gov.il/en/pages/3-general-assembly-resolution-997-es-i-2-november-1956), which called for an immediate ceasefire, withdrawal of forces behind the armistice lines, and the reopening of the Canal. The vote was 64 in favor and five opposed, with six abstentions.

Yet a UN resolution is not something a country is bound to comply with, as Israel has repeatedly shown. Why, then, did the British government fold so rapidly that on November 6, Eden issued the order to abort the operation?

One explanation is the fraught Cold War context. On October 23, six days before Israel invaded Sinai, the [Hungarian Revolution](https://www.hoover.org/library-archives/histories/1956-hungarian-revolution) began. Soviet troops were immediately sent to Budapest, but they failed to bring the situation under control. On November 1, Hungary withdrew from the Warsaw Pact and declared its neutrality. On November 4, Soviet tanks entered Budapest, crushing the revolution. On November 5, in letters to the American, British, French, and Israeli leaders, Soviet premier Nikolai Bulganin threatened to intervene in Suez.

To Eden, Bulganin wrote, “We are fully determined to crush the aggressors and restore peace in the East through the use of force.” Writing to Mollet, he asked how France would fare if “she were attacked by other States having at their disposal modern and terrible means of destruction,” including rockets. “Rockets,” [according to](https://www.cia.gov/readingroom/document/03176909) U.S. intelligence, likely meant “guided missiles with nuclear warheads.” “The very existence of Israel as a State,” Bulganin warned Prime Minister Ben-Gurion, was in jeopardy.

U.S. intelligence correctly judged Bulganin’s threats to be bravado, intended to burnish Soviet anti-colonial credentials, distract attention from Moscow’s brutal repression of an uprising against communism in Hungary, and divide the Western alliance. The Soviets were indeed bluffing. The Black Sea Fleet could not easily have passed through the Dardanelles, which were controlled by Turkey, a NATO member since 1952. Moreover—unbeknownst to the West—the USSR did not yet possess the nuclear missiles Bulganin threatened to use.

However, from Moscow’s perspective, the threats appeared to work. Eden announced a ceasefire on November 6, the day after the Bulganin threats, warning neither France nor Israel beforehand.

In reality, it was not the Soviet threat that forced Eden to fold. It was the economy.

In 1956, the UK’s economy was the fifth-largest in the world, just 22 percent the size of the U.S. economy. Sterling’s share of international reserves was in decline—from more than one-half in 1949 to about 36 percent in 1957, while U.S.-denominated assets already accounted for about half of global reserves in 1957. Decline was also visible in the UK’s fiscal situation. True, the UK was in a period of fiscal consolidation prior to the Suez Canal crisis. [Debt to GDP](https://ourworldindata.org/grapher/uk-government-debt-as-a-percentage-of-gdp-17272016) had declined from 207 percent in 1949 to 114 percent in 1956, and [Macmillan’s 1956 budget](https://hansard.parliament.uk/Commons/1956-04-17/debates/6d7210a3-ffee-4512-9335-d97d9d215f9f/BudgetProposals) projected a £445 million (then $1.25 billion) surplus for 1956–57. But the UK had large dollar-denominated liabilities to the U.S. The 1946 Anglo-American loan and the similar Canadian loan’s 50 annual installments started in 1950 and were due in dollars. This gave Washington decisive leverage—which it used.

The UK was vulnerable because its fixed exchange rate under the [Bretton Woods system](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/b/brettonwoodsagreement.asp) —at a rate to the dollar of $2.80—came under pressure from the moment Nasser announced nationalization. UK officials regarded the $2.80 rate as essential to maintain the [Sterling Area](https://eh.net/encyclopedia/the-sterling-area/), within which many trading partners held substantial sterling balances, as well as the currency’s wider reserve status. As the governor of the Bank of England said on October 17, 1956: “We should regard a further devaluation of sterling \[one had already happened in 1949\] as a disaster to be fought with every weapon at our disposal.” But UK international reserves fell steadily and dipped below the informal target of $2 billion during November. The porous postwar system of capital controls was insufficient to stem outflows.

The exchange rate was also critical because paying for American oil in dollars would further deplete the reserves. The closure of the Canal exposed that Achilles’ heel. The pressure was exacerbated by the disruption to oil flows from Iraq to Europe after the Syrian government blew up the Kirkuk-Baniyas pipeline in retaliation for the invasion. Saudi Arabia also imposed an oil embargo on Britain and France. In Britain, gasoline had to be rationed between December 1956 and May 1957.

Drivers lined up for gas during the Suez Crisis. (Universal History Archive via Getty Images)

The UK, like Israel, Egypt, and France, turned to the International Monetary Fund (IMF) for support due to the conflict. Unlike the other three countries, however, the UK’s current account remained in a surplus. This placed the IMF in the awkward position of being asked to support a country that had not only a current account surplus, but also the second-largest Fund quota after the United States. The Eisenhower administration bluntly declined to allow the IMF to provide funds—or to grant concessions on the Anglo-American loan—until the UK agreed to comply fully with the terms of UN Resolution 997. Eisenhower even asked his treasury secretary, George M. Humphrey, to prepare to sell U.S. holdings of sterling bonds. Having been a supporter of the attack on Egypt, Macmillan now had to tell his cabinet colleagues that Britain had no choice but to accept Eisenhower’s terms. The alternative would have been a currency crisis.

Only on December 4, after a deadline had been set for all troops to be withdrawn from Suez by December 22, was Macmillan able to announce that the UK would receive $561 million in upfront support from the IMF, with another $739 million on standby, which instilled enough confidence to check the attack on sterling. The U.S. also provided a $500 million loan from the Export-Import Bank, which was announced on December 21, 1956.

The Suez Crisis was politically disastrous for Eden. Though there was an initial increase of public support for military action against Egypt, once Eden folded, his domestic position collapsed. The British left was united in moral indignation. More liberally inclined Conservatives were appalled at Eden’s collusion with France and Israel. Two junior ministers resigned. On January 9 Eden had to resign as party leader and prime minister, pleading ill health.

As was true in Britain in 1956, the current war against Iran has been divisive. The far-right charge is gaining traction that the war is being fought for Israeli, not American, interests. Trump also began his war on Iran a far less popular leader than Eden when he began his war on Egypt. On February 26, Trump’s net [approval rating](https://www.realclearpolling.com/polls/approval/donald-trump/approval-rating) was −11.8 percent, compared with −0.4 percent at the beginning of his second term. The latest figure is −15.6 percent, roughly where it was in late July 2020, the low point of the pandemic year.

Unlike Britain in 1956, the United States went to war against Iran with midterm elections eight months away. And unlike British voters in 1956, who were soon to be persuaded they had “never had it so good,” American voters are in a mood of disgust about many aspects of their national life, especially its affordability. The impact of the war is certain to give a significant upward bump to the inflation rate, especially for food and energy. Republicans were already resigned to losing the House in November. If the oil shock drags on for more than a few weeks, the Senate might soon be in play, too.

Crucially, though, the biggest consequences of Suez were geopolitical, not domestic-political. Everyone knew after 1956 that Great Britain was finished as a top-tier power. After Suez, there was no making Britain great again. The pace of decolonization quickened.

Meanwhile, the United States understood that it had incurred much greater responsibility for the Middle East than ever before. The [Eisenhower Doctrine](https://history.state.gov/milestones/1953-1960/eisenhower-doctrine), unveiled on January 5, 1957, pledged military and economic aid to Middle Eastern countries resisting communism. But the outcome of the Suez Crisis, and the successful crushing of the Imre Nagy government in Budapest, encouraged the Soviet leader Nikita Khrushchev to believe that the Soviet Union could get the better of the United States in the Cold War. This set the world on a perilous course toward the peak moments of danger in superpower relations: the Berlin Crisis of 1961 and the Cuban Missile Crisis of 1962.

Everyone knew after 1956 that Great Britain was finished as a top-tier power.

In the Middle East, the American bet on Arab nationalism proved to be a serious strategic miscalculation. Nasser was the clear winner of the Suez Crisis. He restored navigation in the Canal quickly and bought out the French and British shareholders of the company. Egyptian control and management of the Canal worked better than many had expected. And he doubled down on building economic ties with the Soviet Union and China. By 1958, Egypt sent half of its exports to the Soviet bloc, while Eastern Europe became a market for Egypt’s manufacturing exports. Soviet funds and engineers helped build the Aswan Dam.

Conversely, the crisis weakened traditional Arab rulers allied with France or Britain. This was exemplified by the toppling of the Iraqi monarchy in 1958. [Abd al-Karim Qasim](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Abd-al-Karim-Qasim), the Iraqi officer who seized power, was heavily influenced by Nasser. In the same year, Egypt and Syria united to form the United Arab Republic (UAR). Nasserism also influenced the Lebanese civil conflict in 1958. President Camille Chamoun refused to break with France and Britain in 1956, while the formation of the UAR polarized the region into “pro-Nasser” and “pro-Western” camps. Nasser publicly denounced Lebanon’s rulers for having stabbed Egypt in the back during the Suez crisis. The creation of the UAR delivered the final blow to stability in Lebanon, and pro-Nasser groups launched attacks against the Lebanese army. Chamoun requested American assistance, and U.S. Marines landed on the beaches of Beirut in 1958.

All of this, as British Conservatives were not slow to point out, called into question the American decision to oppose Suez and effectively back Nasser over Eden. Only gradually were the limits revealed of Egypt’s political and economic capacity. Meanwhile, Israel’s collusion with France and Britain had increased its leverage over its Arab neighbors. Efforts to increase Arab military capabilities were exposed as an utter failure in the Six-Day War of 1967. Increasingly, the U.S. found itself caught between the irreconcilable demands of Arabs and Israelis, a conflict that exploded into war once again—not to mention a much bigger oil shock—in 1973.

After the Suez Crisis, France decided that NATO could not be trusted and that Paris must accelerate the development of its own nuclear weapon. In 1957, President René Coty selected a nuclear testing site in the French Sahara, and the first successful nuclear test was conducted there a year later. Suez was thus the origin of France’s independent nuclear deterrent, the *force de frappe*. (Israel went down the same road, but covertly.) Moreover, the French realized that the economically weak UK was no longer a reliable partner. West Germany’s Chancellor Konrad Adenauer said to Mollet, “Europe will be your revenge.” In 1957, France, West Germany, Italy, and the [Benelux countries](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Benelux) signed the Treaty of Rome, which established a customs union, the European Economic Community (EEC), and the European Atomic Community (Euratom).

At the same time, France kept up its “savage war of peace” in Algeria. The French officer corps became increasingly disillusioned with the political choices of its government, who they also blamed for the loss of Indochina in 1954. This division between military and civilian leadership culminated in the May 1958 collapse of the Fourth Republic and General Charles de Gaulle’s return to power. Encouraged by Suez to pursue a “third way” in foreign policy, de Gaulle improved relations with the USSR and eventually recognized the People’s Republic of China in 1964. In 1966, he withdrew France from NATO’s integrated military command.

[

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!uCrk!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7fc12ed5-0ec4-4be9-bf25-907bdf6402b9_1320x30.png)

The geopolitical consequences of an American Suez over Hormuz would be comparably momentous. Today, for example, Russia is playing a more active operational but less assertive role than the Soviet Union did in 1956. Yet the Russian-Iranian relationship today is much deeper than the Soviet-Egyptian relationship prior to 1956. In January 2025, Russia and Iran signed a 20-year strategic partnership agreement. They have reciprocal defense ties. Iran has purchased advanced Russian systems, such as the S-300 air defense and Su-35 fighter, and Russia has purchased Iranian Shahed drones, artillery ammunition, and close-range ballistic missiles. Their sanctions evasion networks are also deeply intertwined.

As a result, Russia today is much more actively aiding its partner than was true in 1956. It is providing real-time intelligence and targeting data on U.S. assets, enabling precise hits on command-and-control infrastructure, radars, and temporary structures. It is also sending drones to Tehran. Since localizing Shahed production in 2023, Russia has significantly improved Iranian designs, upgrading their engines, navigation, and anti-jamming capabilities.

True, Russian diplomacy lacks the bluster of 1956. While the foreign ministry has condemned the U.S.-Israeli operation, President Putin’s official comments on the war have simply expressed condolences for Iranian deaths and called for a cessation of hostilities. This is because Putin cannot afford to lose Trump amid negotiations over the war in Ukraine. Russia is therefore quietly reaping the benefits of the conflict where it can. As in 1956, the U.S. is now distracted from Russia’s aggression in Eastern Europe. The U.S.-led peace talks over Ukraine have stalled. Indeed, the U.S. is considering diverting some of the aid on the Prioritized Ukraine Requirements List, including air defense interceptors, to the Middle East. The U.S. has also temporarily lifted sanctions on Russian crude oil.

Meanwhile, Russia appears to have begun its spring offensive against Ukraine. Last week, it launched its largest 24-hour drone and missile attack on Ukraine since the war began. Finally, higher oil prices ought to aid Russia’s straining budget—although Ukraine appears determined to limit Russia’s strategic upside via repeated drone strikes on Russia’s Primorsk and Ust-Luga export hubs.

Today, Europeans remain, by and large, bystanders in Gulf War III. While in Suez, they were actors; this time, they were not even consulted. British and EU leaders prevaricated when asked to lend their support to the U.S. war effort. A French offer to help police the Strait of Hormuz came with a caveat: “after the war is over.”

Anglo-French troops land at Port Fouad, Egypt, on November 5, 1956. (Guy Touchard/AFP via Getty Images)

Trump has made clear how much he resents this. The widespread mood of disillusionment with the Atlantic alliance vividly recalls 1956. France’s response to Suez—seeking military independence and diversifying foreign relations—is becoming more attractive to other Europeans in Trump’s second term. Last week, Germany’s chancellor Friedrich Merz even floated the idea that the EU could negotiate a free-trade agreement with China. Behind closed doors, voices in Germany’s business community continue to argue for a return to importing Russian natural gas.

Both options seem far-fetched. China’s already huge export surplus with Europe means there is almost no support among EU businesses for a free-trade agreement. Russian gas is only a realistic option after a durable Ukraine-Russia peace has been agreed. That said, the argument for “buy European” clauses in defense contracts has been greatly strengthened, amid reports that U.S. suppliers cannot deliver because of the demands of the Gulf war. French president Emmanuel Macron’s offer to strengthen the European dimension of the nuclear *force de frappe* has been well received in Berlin, Stockholm, Warsaw, and Copenhagen. This is a rational response to the growing fear that NATO itself is on its last legs and Europe may soon have to stand up to Putin on its own. It will be ironic if a war fought ostensibly to prevent nuclear proliferation in the Middle East ends up promoting it in Europe.

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!a12C!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2d6e8e62-9fc2-4966-b60b-6d8a6ab0ae7d_1320x30.png)

The biggest question at the heart of the Suez-Hormuz analogy has to do with China. Is China to the 2020s what America was to the 1950s—in a position to benefit from the end of an Anglophone empire and to inherit the role of global superpower?

On the surface, China’s decision not to work with the Americans to reopen the Strait recalls the stance of the United States at the time of Suez. Like the U.S. in 1956, China’s initial response to the brewing Iran crisis was to push for a diplomatic resolution. When diplomacy failed, Eisenhower opted for economic coercion and public condemnation of Britain and France. As we have seen, Washington used financial coercion against Britain to terminate Eden’s Suez adventure. The United States then stepped into the dominant role previously played by Britain and France in the Middle East. Does China have comparable options today?

As I have argued in an [earlier essay](https://www.thefp.com/p/niall-ferguson-this-is-how-the-iran), the biggest risk is that President Xi Jinping seizes the opportunity presented by the Iran crisis and challenges the status quo in Taiwan. But China’s preferred strategy is probably to sit back and watch the U.S. once again make unforced errors in the Middle East. China’s relations with both Iran and the other Gulf states seem certain to deepen after this conflict, unless there is an unambiguous American victory. At the same time, China will work hard to advance its parallel financial and payments infrastructure through increased use of “petro-yuan” for trade settlement and by ramping up its central bank digital currency “bridges” to Middle Eastern economies.

The biggest question at the heart of the Suez-Hormuz analogy has to do with China.

That said, the extent to which China can challenge dollar dominance should not be overstated. The U.S. had an overwhelming amount of financial and monetary leverage in the 1950s thanks to the Bretton Woods system, which pegged currencies to the dollar. Virtually all international trade, including oil, was settled in greenbacks. By contrast, China’s currency represents only about 3 percent of global payments, 2 percent of global FX reserves, 2 percent of global trade invoicing, and 8.5 percent of global FX transactions.

Moreover, China is running a far larger trade surplus than the U.S. did in 1956. China’s trade surplus with the rest of the world hit [$1.2 trillion last year](https://medium.com/the-geopolitical-economist/chinas-1-2-trillion-trade-surplus-is-breaking-the-global-economy-93ad88df668f), equivalent to around 6 percent of GDP. The U.S. trade surplus prior to the 1970s never exceeded around 1 percent of GDP. The U.S. in the 1950s was also the principal creditor of a heavily indebted postwar Europe. China is not in this position today. The best it can hope for is to exert leverage over the countries in the Global South that dominate the [Belt and Road Initiative](https://www.chathamhouse.org/2021/09/what-chinas-belt-and-road-initiative-bri).

All this means that, while many countries in the West may rhetorically claim they want better ties with China, in reality they remain wary of China’s economic, technological, and military statecraft. A free-trade agreement between the EU and the PRC would be a death sentence for European industry. And as long as the Chinese capital account remains closed, there will be a limit to the West’s appetite for Chinese money.

In short, China today is not in as strong a position as the United States in 1956. Nor is the U.S. in as weak a position as Eden’s UK.

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!v_Hf!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ffb02849e-8ec8-473c-8335-ca9b28a4e101_1320x30.png)

Nevertheless, there are causes for concern. As I [pointed out last week](https://www.thefp.com/p/niall-ferguson-brace-yourselves-a), a recession becomes more likely the longer the Strait of Hormuz remains closed. But the big risk of an American Suez is the potential that the world reassesses the United States as a declining hegemon.

To a much greater extent than Britain in 1956, the United States is in a weak fiscal position. The federal deficit is 5.8 percent of GDP this year, almost twice the treasury secretary’s stated target for 2028. The deficit is projected to rise to 6 percent in 2029, 7 percent in 2041, 8 percent in 2048, and to reach 9.1 percent in 2056—as far forward as the Congressional Budget Office can project. Whereas Britain was running a tight fiscal policy in the 1950s, reducing the debt burden relative to GDP, the opposite is true of the United States in the 2020s. The federal debt in public hands is projected to exceed 100 percent of GDP this year. It could be 120 percent in 2036, 144 percent in 2046, and 175 percent in 2056. Foreign investors are becoming less eager to finance a government so manifestly running an unsustainable excess of spending over revenue. Over the past two decades, and especially in times of crisis, the Federal Reserve has had to play an increasing role in de facto debt management.

One result of this has been the violation of [Ferguson’s Law](https://www.hoover.org/research/fergusons-law-debt-service-military-spending-and-fiscal-limits-power): The U.S. now spends more on interest payments than on defense. This was very far from true of British public finance in the 1950s. The projected downward trend of the U.S. defense budget is manifestly at odds with the administration’s geopolitical ambitions as set out in the recent [National Security Strategy](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/2025-National-Security-Strategy.pdf).

If there is further upward pressure on 10-year yields, which have risen by nearly half a percentage point since the war against Iran began, Scott Bessent may find himself playing a similar role to Macmillan 70 years ago—arguing that the Iran “excursion” must urgently be wound up as much for the sake of fiscal stability as for the sake of the dollar’s global role.

The United States, in short, needs to learn from British imperial history. If President Trump does decide to cut and run in a few weeks’ time, leaving others to sort out the mess he has created in the Strait of Hormuz, the U.S. risks paying a price comparable with that paid by Eden’s Britain in 1956.

The arguments for attempting regime change in Iran in 2026 were as appealing as the arguments for attempting it in Egypt 70 years ago. But the costs of enemy action to block a vital strategic waterway proved unexpectedly high in both cases. The geopolitical price of acting with a single ally—at least Britain had France as well as Israel—also looks excessive. The United States has alienated not only its European allies, but also those in East Asia and above all the Gulf. And the Axis of China, Russia, Iran, and North Korea currently looks like the net winner, just as the Soviets and their satellites looked like main beneficiaries of Suez.

It’s true, as Trump said on Wednesday, that five weeks is not a long time in war. For all I know, I may be writing on the eve of a U.S. ground operation that will wrest control of Hormuz from the IRGC. Out of this war may yet come a permanent disarmament of the Iranian regime, and perhaps even its downfall. We may ultimately arrive at a better arrangement to secure the vast commerce that passes through the Strait. One can still hope for all these things. But the core lesson of Suez is that time is not on the side of an overstretched hegemon, because the economic costs of war pile up faster than the strategic benefits can be reaped.

David Low summed up Eden’s downfall in a [cartoon of the prime minister](https://www.canalzoners.co.uk/Suez%20Crisis/Political%20Cartoons.htm), standing drenched by the side of the Suez Canal, watching his ship sink. The disappearing vessel flies the flag: “Go-It-Alone Police.” Considering the scale of the disaster, his successor did remarkably well to prevent the Conservative Party sinking, too—securing the Tories eight more years in power and earning the nickname “Supermac.”

We know Vice President Vance has thought about Suez. He must have thought about that part, too.
