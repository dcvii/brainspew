Unless you’ve been living under a rock, you probably noticed [a certain viral “Open Letter”](https://hamburgersstand.substack.com/p/an-open-letter-to-chris-best-ceo) come across the bow. I saw it first, the day it dropped (June 3rd - 2026), when it was at less than a hundred likes and shares. The piece currently stands, as of June 8th, at just shy of 5k on this platform alone, and has probably *escaped containmen* t, as the kids say, several times over—meaning being shared outside its niche, even shared off-platform and across the broader internet. It did this in spite of (or maybe because of) its humble beginnings and insipid flaws. More on that later.

The “Letter” was addressed to Substack CEO [Chris Best](https://open.substack.com/users/2-chris-best?utm_source=mentions), who addressed it dismissively days after it dropped, on a rainy June 5th evening, by calling it “ [horrible, obvious AI slop.](https://substack.com/@cb/note/c-271417569)“ The substance of the piece, which was rather short, lay unaddressed. And soon, battle lines were drawn. The article’s author, [Robert M. Hamburger](https://open.substack.com/users/58039743-robert-m-hamburger?utm_source=mentions), alongside his supporters, on one side—call it the Burger-Faction. Best and his devotees, many of them either longstanding, often uncritical Substack simps and reply guys, or just anti-AI zealots who simply relished finding a new scalp to claim, on the Best-Brigade. Few were thinking beyond vibes or tribes.

Then there’s moi. Your far-from-humble servant. To me, the real story isn’t about who was right, especially since I can honestly say no side looks great here. No, to me this whole episode is salient for what reveals about the platform and its culture. Hamburger’s piece was decrying the platform’s drift toward noise. And yet the “Letter” traveled via the exact noise machinery it lamented, written by someone who launched his newsletter less than a full four weeks ago, and who apparently let an LLM write the thing for him, perhaps in its entirety. In a way that seems not only duplicitous but frankly **hypocritical**, especially given the arguments he/it was making.

No, I’m not here to defend Hamburger or Best. I’m not here to defend Substack, be it as a company, a platform, or a community. I’m here to hold up a mirror and ask us all to reckon with the divergence, the dissonance, between what we say we want, what we say are our values, and what we actually do when the rubber meets the road. And hopefully to help anyone who saw this trainwreck, whether they recognized it as such or not, to think rigorously about what actually happened and why.

## The Letter Itself: Weak Thinking, Period

I won’tt let the AI question swallow the substance question. Whether or not Hamburger used AI is less interesting than why the letter’s actual argument fails. (The fact is, [he largely admits he used AI now](https://substack.com/@hamburgersstand/note/c-271667195?r=e0eqs&utm_source=notes-share-action&utm_medium=web), tho he seems to retroactively reframe *getting caught* as a failure of others to do understand him, not of him failing to be forthright or rhetorically consistent.) Since his piece is rather short, I can actually just quote him verbatim here and pick it apart straight-away:

> “ **An Open Letter to Chris Best, CEO of Substack**
> 
> <sub>Substack Is Drifting — Don’t Become What You Replaced</sub>
> 
> **[Robert M. Hamburger](https://substack.com/@hamburgersstand)**
> 
> **Jun 03, 2026**
> 
> Dear Chris,
> 
> Days. Even weeks. That’s how long some writers spend on a single essay — researching, drafting, cutting, rebuilding. It publishes. Within hours, it’s buried beneath a flood of Notes. The essay gets forty views. A quick-hit AI-generated reaction gets four hundred.
> 
> This is Substack in 2026 — one of the few remaining refuges for long-form thought on the internet.”

As the astute [Parker Peevyhouse](https://open.substack.com/users/42320812-parker-peevyhouse?utm_source=mentions) pointed out to me, cuz I frankly missed it in my hazy recollection (I’d ignored the piece initially as random detritus), this very introduction sets the stage for what sounds like an impassioned appeal to realness, to craftsmanship, and at least implicitly, to anti-AI sentiments. “Days”? “Even weeks”? Building and “rebuilding” one’s “single essay”? Presumably from scratch, without assistance? Against a tide of “quick-hit AI-generated“ content? Odd way to frame things for a guy who says, in his confessional note days later, that AI is a legitimate collaborator in the creative process. But whatever, let’s move on.

> “For many writers, Substack restored something the internet had nearly lost: the essay. A place where thoughtful people could develop ideas instead of compressing themselves into engagement bait. In a digital world increasingly shaped by outrage, distraction, and algorithmic manipulation, many of us came here because it felt fundamentally different. Slower. **More human.** More rooted in substance. That’s why I came. And stayed.”

I added bold there, full disclosure. Couldn’t resist.

The problems are already mounting, even if some were only most obvious in hindsight. Hamburger really is trying to, at minimum, *have it both ways* on the AI thing. He keeps signalling like he’s written this from scratch and that he wishes scratch-made content had a fairer hearing. He even has the balls to talk about the ills of “engagement bait” as he lets an LLM draft what is, by any objective measure, one of the Top 10, if not Top 3, most successful pieces of engagement bait to grace/pollute this platform in the month of June. Moving on…

> “But lately, many creators are sensing a drift.
> 
> **Notes** increasingly dominate the culture and energy of the platform. While they can help foster connection and discovery, they also risk reshaping Substack into something that feels uncomfortably close to Facebook, X, TikTok, or every other endless-scroll feed competing for human attention. Quick reactions begin outperforming *carefully constructed essays*. Velocity begins overshadowing depth. The gravitational pull of modern platforms always bends toward stimulation because stimulation keeps people clicking.
> 
> The problem is that many of us did not come here for that.
> 
> ==We came for thoughtful writing. For original ideas.== For the experience of sitting with another person’s perspective long enough for something meaningful to unfold. The article is the soul of Substack. Notes should support that ecosystem, not replace it.”

Emphasis mine again. Okay, after some rambling and overly sentimental, manipulative preamble, he’s starting to get to the point. He’s criticizing the Notes feature and saying it makes Substack feel too much like other social media. A common complaint. He adds that he’s concerned Notes are supplementing articles in a zero-sum war for space and attention on the overall Substack platform. Another evergreen gripe.

These are real issues and worth reckoning with honestly. I’ll try. Let’s tackle this causation problem, which I think he has inverted. Substack introduced Notes and let us do with it as we willed. If the feature weren’t popular, it wouldn’t be used, it certainly wouldn’t be so prominent. Compare with other features, like the Polymarket integration (barely used except by wonks), or the much debated Substack TV app. People didn’t flock to those. They still exist, but they’re marginal. Other stuff, some of which largely predates my own time on the platform, has literally come and gone. (Anyone remember Office Hours, local meetups, or writers helping writers groups?)

Notes have helped some creators, myself included, build up subscribers and break out. My strengths arguably lay in micro-blogging. I came from Twitter (it shows in my demeanor). Notes are purely additive for me. **I don’t have to use them**, but I can benefit from doing so. Likewise others, surely.

But let’s get back to Burger-ville. We have a few more paragraphs yet.

> “Another concern creators discuss constantly is discoverability, especially for smaller writers. The platform increasingly moves in circles. Large newsletters recommend other large newsletters. Established writers elevate established writers. Paid tiers get prioritized. Growth becomes dependent on proximity to existing networks rather than the resonance of the work itself. Emerging voices, doing original work, often describe the experience the same way: shouting into the void.
> 
> That pattern slowly narrows the ecosystem.
> 
> Readers lose in this environment too. When thoughtful essays disappear beneath endless reactions and algorithm-friendly posting, readers encounter less depth, less originality, and fewer unexpected voices. The platform becomes narrower, louder, and more repetitive. Substack becomes indistinguishable from everything it was supposed to replace.
> 
> One of the internet’s original promises was that a genuinely brilliant unknown voice could still find its audience. Substack once felt like a place that might actually honor that promise. It should fight harder to keep it.“

Ah, the fabricated golden age. Notes launched 2023, the discoverability problem predates it. His concerns about Substack not living up to its own hype and self-reported ethos are actually the closest thing he has to a valid critique, but it’s marred by, and buried under, what sounds like a call for cultural or digital clientism. No one is owed an audience just for showing up. They need to make content that actually resonates, even if the way some creators do that is by manipulating the public. A thing which, ironically enough, Hamburger can be accused of himself.

> “You’re sitting on something genuinely rare, Chris. A platform people came to because they still believed ideas matter. Because they wanted writing, not noise. In 2026, **when AI is flooding the internet with frictionless content** and legacy media is hollowing out, that matters more than ever.
> 
> The ask: prioritize the essay more visibly.
> 
> ==Create a true “emerging writers” discovery section independent of subscriber count.== Give essays longer visibility windows before they disappear beneath Notes and feed activity. Build recommendation systems that reward the writers readers actually stay with, not simply the ones who game the feed.
> 
> You already built the alternative. Protect it.
> 
> Sincerely,
> 
> Robert M. Hamburger
> 
> NOTE TO FELLOW CREATORS: If this resonates, share it. Open letters only work when they stop being one voice.“

My emphasis still. Okay, cool. That’s the end.

Again, the irony is stark. This guy had the most viral essay I’ve seen all year from someone who wasn’t already a bestseller. He made his account barely a month ago. Hamburger himself may not have actively used Notes to promote his piece, but the community around him sure did. And honestly, I don’t think that’s bad in a vacuum. Notes exist to help us promote ourselves, our ideas, and our long-form content.

Yet Hamburger has become the poster-boy for the very thing he was complaining about. He worked people, many of whom surely assumed he wrote the piece without AI. He was criticizing something, Notes, that indisputably helped him and his message reach thousands. And he was doing it, full of grievance and ressentiment, after being here officially for all of…20-odd days? Uh, entitled and impatient much?

### My Take on His Take, Plus Some Bright Spots

My feeling is, and has always been, that the piece wasn’t well written, independent of whether he prompted it and lifted it directly from an LLM. My position is that Notes are fine and people don’t *need* to use them. But since many do use them, and like them, Notes shouldn’t be artificially suppressed or down-regulated just to appease the folks who don’t. That would be no less unfair than what the detractors claim the status quo to be. Let the public decide, let them vote with their attention.

“ ==Create a true ‘emerging writers’ discovery section independent of subscriber count.==“ You know what? Legit good idea. Buried under trash, but still. I can imagine there’d be tons of logistical problems here tho, and it would be marred with charges of astroturfing if it didn’t occasionally surface people who might, ahem, scare the hoes. Anyone remember the “Nazis on Substack” discourse from a few years back? Yeah, well, gross people would inevitably pop up if the “emerging writers” tab was truly democratic. It’s almost inevitable, I’m afraid. Substack probably doesn’t want the headache of being seen *recommending* controversial content. Or suppressing it, to avoid offense. Still, I do think something like what this guy is talking about has merit.

Ultimately tho, like I’ve been saying, the “Open Letter” *is* the phenomenon it criticizes. The masses flocked to it, and boosted it, because it flattered their priors. Soothed and stoked their [Soul-Aristocratic sense](https://caffeineandphilosophy.com/2020/03/04/a-critique-of-nietzschean-nobility/) of being *owed* something, essentially just for existing on the platform. Used the exact sort of language we’d expect from an anti-AI apparatchik mixed with a twee smol bean type blogger. Wrapped itself in sentiment, idealism, and platform mythology. And it proved hollow, based on its execution, despite a few good ideas and potentially valid issues raised.

## Folly of the Crowd: Grievance Over Grit

This is the section that needs the most care because it’s the most damning of the median Substack user, including people who—statistically speaking—might think of themselves as part of our little Edokwin community here. But I haven’t pulled my punches thus far, so why start now?

The “Letter” went mega viral. And it did so not from careful analysis, not even per se ideological agreement with Hamburger’s thesis and prescriptions. **It went viral on vibes.** The success was the recognition of a *feeling*.

The letter told a large constituency of underperforming writers that their underperformance is someone else’s structural fault. It told a vocal minority on the platform, the Notes haters, that they were right. It told people that the algorithm, the same thing that propelled the essay itself to unfathomable heights, was the enemy. That Substack as a company was adrift, maybe even arrayed against them. It was a convenient myth, and ultimately false.

But myths are powerful emotional devices, completely independent of whether they’re true. I could draw on several examples of how the discourse degraded. Nobody was reading carefully. They were feeling recognized. And the flipside? The anti-Hamburger crowd was largely just simping for the platform’s CEO, which is its own revealed preference failure. Both sides simply chose a team.

## Best’s Reply: AI Accusation & Deflection

Credit where it’s due, the irony Best clocked is real and the one-liner lands. But a CEO dismissing a user’s concerns via screenshot without engaging the substance is its own failure. He used the genetic fallacy, [a problem](https://edokwin.substack.com/p/avowed-ai-writers-arent-the-enemy?utm_source=publication-search) [I’ve criticized](https://edokwin.substack.com/p/using-ai-detectors-is-stupid-and?utm_source=publication-search) [before](https://edokwin.substack.com/p/the-patrons-obligation?utm_source=publication-search) when [AI content gets pilloried](https://edokwin.substack.com/p/anti-ai-zealotry-is-reactionary-classist?utm_source=publication-search) in public. Origin doesn’t determine validity. Besides which, there’s an institutional incentive to deflect. Indeed, Best has, like the rest of his staff, been a bit duplicitous and non-transparent before. Says one thing, does another. Or says nothing, even directly when asked, because the answer is inconvenient.

Whereas Best has been [on record in 2023](https://on.substack.com/p/algorithm) calling algorithmic feeds “junk food” that serve users’ “basest desires,” his own platform has become algo-pilled and fairly similar to all the rest in some regards. Notes are a symptom of that, and a dominant social layer on Substack, but they’re not the only thing. This “writing platform” has increasingly prominent video features, native podcasting tools, and more.

The Substack company built a version of the thing Best and others used to say they were building against. Maybe they built it into a better version of the same mousetrap, but that’s a different argument. One he didn’t make. Just like we haven’t gotten clarity on [sponsorships & ads](https://edokwin.substack.com/p/native-sponsorships-substack-introduces), or on [verified badges](https://edokwin.substack.com/p/substacks-secret-verified-badges?utm_source=publication-search). Hamburger’s letter gestures at that gap, however clumsily. Best’s response avoided reckoning with it.

## What Nobody Said

In my mind, the actual solutions to discoverability and overall platform health are boring and unglamorous. ==For creators:== write well; engage in good faith—which means morally and as a factualist, not just with faux civility; make arguments, or art, or both… ***that can’t be ignored***. And most especially, ==for audiences:== ***be a discerning reader***. None of that easily fits what most people want to restack. None of it generates 5,000 likes. And none of it was said by anyone with a significant platform during this whole episode. Not Hamburger, not Best, nor any of their supporters. Yet it needs to be said desperately regardless. Because this isn’t about them. It’s about you.

I’ve tried throughout my time on this platform to speak truth to the public *and* to power. Not one over the other, always both. None of you are powerless uwu smol beans. None of you are The Man keeping me or each other down. You’re regular people, of varying backgrounds and predilections, making choices every day. On what to say, what to share, what to believe or doubt. I’m asking you, really daring you, to make good ones. Great ones even. And I’m not gonna flatter your ego to do it.

Hamburger thought he could prompt an essay from an LLM, run it out, and maybe get some positive reactions. I don’t presume he’s an ingenious, nefarious schemer exactly. In fact, I’m positive he didn’t think through all his moves too carefully, or he wouldn’t have made the obvious blunders he did. But still, he was working an angle. I’d like to see that angle less rewarded. Whether it’s worked via 100% human-generated content or 100% AI-generated.

## Closing: The Work Is the Answer

I don’t have an inspirational-poster or [advice animal](https://knowyourmeme.com/memes/advice-animals). What I have is essentially a series of omnidirectional rebukes. No open letter has ever substituted for the work. The community that co-signed Hamburger’s grievance without reading it carefully is the same community that will scroll past my best essay, or those from my friends, because a smoother, more flattering piece arrived first. That’s not a platform problem. That’s a human problem, and it predates Substack, Notes, and Chris Best by decades, if not centuries. The only answer that has ever worked is making something that can’t be ignored—and having the patience and discernment to recognize it.

Moreover, if we want to make Substack better, we do that not by demanding others do the work, but by doing it ourselves. We creators decide what we put out, and what’s ethical in terms of creation. You audience members decide what to like, share, and reward with actual money. If we all keep doing it, keep *living* our values rather than just talking about them, others will see and do the same.

I’ll end on something that implicates me too, if lightly. I’ve been on this platform long enough to have watched the drift happen. I wrote the verified badge expose and the sponsorships essay. I wrote the progressivism piece and the leftists article. Nobody restacked or liked any of them north of four thousand times, not even cumulatively. Tho the latter two did quite well, in part because they were red meat for a certain type of audience. But I try not to just be a red meat guy, or a cheap, ersatz Burgermeister. I think I’m better than that. I think you are too. ***Let’s prove it.***
