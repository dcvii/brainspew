Graham Platner’s success in the Maine primary shows how easy it’s become for radical candidates to attract major campaign donations.

“Money is the mother’s milk of politics,” as legendary California politician Jesse Unruh put it. It’s hard to get ahead in politics without money, and the more of it, the better.

It used to be that the road to dollars in Democratic politics went straight through the party establishment and the big donor networks that were linked to it. Lots of people didn’t like it, but that was the game you had to play, with the endless rounds of calls to big donors, begging political action committees, unions, and party committees for money and attending big-dollar events. While those are still important, another model has started to emerge that circumvents the establishment and relies on small donations and nationalized politics.

There is no better example of this new dynamic than the wildly successful Senate primary campaign of Graham Platner, a Democrat in Maine. Platner, though from an upper-middle-class background, sells himself as a fire-breathing, working-class populist who will stand up to the billionaire class and President Donald Trump, unlike the feckless Democratic establishment. Moreover, Platner is the *right kind* of populist: an economic populist who also wants to abolish Immigration and Customs Enforcement, supports biological boys in girls’ sports, and brushes off debate about Democrats’ unpopular cultural positions as a “billionaire-funded distraction.” This is music to the ears of the party’s progressive left and its base in the professional class, who are happy to believe that economic populism absolves them of any need to moderate their intransigent cultural liberalism.

---

Platner the populist was set to be opposed in the primary by Janet Mills, Maine’s 78-year old governor, who was recruited by Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer and other elements of the Democratic establishment. They worried that nominating Platner would undercut Democrats’ ability to beat moderate incumbent Republican Susan Collins, given Platner’s radical views and long history of controversial social media comments—not to mention the tattoo whose Nazi symbolism he claims he was unaware of when he got it. Party elders drafted Mills into the race believing she would hoover up money from major donors and easily outraise the pesky populist.

That gambit by Schumer and the Democratic establishment turned out to be a complete failure. Not only did Platner crush Mills in the polls from pretty much the moment she entered the race, he also vastly outraised her in campaign contributions. It was this last, unexpected development that led Mills to finally throw in the towel and end her candidacy on April 30. She just did not have the money to continue a competitive campaign and to hope that somehow she could reclaim the momentum.

This fundraising flop by Mills and success by Platner is remarkable. If money is the mother’s milk of politics, Platner was virtually swimming in it. By the time Mills quit the race, Platner had raised about $12 million, compared with her roughly $5.4 million. And it wasn’t because Mills entered the race late; Platner outraised Mills by millions of dollars in both quarters during which their candidacies overlapped.

Where that money came from is key. Platner’s cash streamed in from small, unitemized donations of less than $200. Early in his campaign, his small donations averaged $31. Platner’s share of money raised from small donations (64 percent) was more than twice Mills’s share (31 percent).

The progressive online Democratic community has become a new, *distributed* establishment. If you can get the ear of this new establishment, suddenly that money barrier starts to disappear.

Basically, Platner’s new model of fundraising crushed Mills’s old model. Mills depended on itemized, large-donor contributions and institutional-style support. Platner, by contrast, sought and received national media coverage, national endorsements by progressive figures like Bernie Sanders, and a high profile in the online, generally progressive and antiestablishment Democratic discourse. That was then translated into massive numbers of small online donations through the ActBlue fundraising platform. Mills simply could not match this energy—and money.

The implications go well beyond the Maine nomination contest. It’s always been possible to mount insurgent campaigns for statewide offices, but getting enough “mother’s milk” to be truly competitive against an establishment-backed candidate was a barrier. That barrier has now been dramatically lowered. In a sense, the progressive online Democratic community has become a new, *distributed* establishment. If you can get the ear of this new establishment, suddenly that money barrier starts to disappear. Indeed, this new model of fundraising can actually become an *advantage* relative to the old model.

This marks a new era for insurgent candidates with radical views. In the past, upstarts on the fringe could succeed in House or local campaigns that don’t require nearly as much money, simply by ginning up viral moments or drawing fawning media coverage—think New York City’s Zohran Mamdani and Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez. But in Senate campaigns, candidates this far to the left could usually be edged out by better-funded establishment opponents, since the stakes are higher and the outside money more abundant.

The recent breakthrough in small donations will not be lost on aspiring Democratic politicians nationwide. Scheming and plotting to get into that online progressive Democratic discourse will increasingly replace worrying about call time and other traditional fundraising tools. Naturally, this will advantage hard-edged progressives who are adept at rhetoric that attracts attention and are more than willing to take positions that excite the online Democratic community. For these politicians, there is little to lose and much to gain.

The same cannot be said of the Democratic Party as a whole. It faces the daunting task of making its brand salable in non-blue parts of America. This new fundraising model will make it ever easier for progressive flamethrowers to mount competitive primary campaigns that will reward those progressives but do nothing to change the toxic image of Democrats in much of working-class and rural America. That disadvantage will show up in general elections rather than primaries, but by then, the damage will already have been done
