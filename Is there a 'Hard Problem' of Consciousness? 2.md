### I don't think so, but not because I believe consciousness is an illusion

Ive written a few times now on questions of consciousness, and in all of those posts I’ve assumed there is a ‘hard problem of consciousness’, i.e., the problem of how to explain the subjective or qualitative aspects of consciousness or experience in scientific/metaphysical terms.

I’m coming clean in this post about what my own stance is on this issue. I don’t think there is a hard problem of consciousness AND I do not think this means that the subjective or qualitative aspects of consciousness are not real.

I just don’t think that the subjective aspects of experience are the kinds of things that science/metaphysics can explain. And I don’t see this as a problem. As I see it, there are lots of things that science/metaphysics doesn’t explain that we accept as real (friendship, moral values, political facts, etc). How we experience the world is no different. Of course, there is a lot that goes into my believing this (I wrote a whole dissertation on it). But I’ll do my best here to lay out the main points.

#### What is the Hard Problem of Consciousness?

The ‘hard problem of consciousness’ is a term introduced by Chalmers in the 1990’s.[^1] The idea is to set apart the hard problem of consciousness from what Chalmers calls ‘the easy problems of consciousness’. Although the easy problems of consciousness are by no means ‘easy’, the point is that these problems are within the purview of being explained by modern science. As Chalmers puts it,

‘‘The easy problems of consciousness are those that seem directly susceptible to the standard methods of cognitive science, whereby a phenomenon is explained in terms of computational or neural mechanisms’’ (p,2) [^2]

Examples of ‘easy problems’ of consciousness could include things like, reporting on a mental state, processing information, or categorizing stimuli.

Consider, then, my brain retrieving a certain mental state, such as ‘this chocolate cake is so delicious’

Suppose further that a neuroscientist explained exactly *how* my brain retrieves this mental state. Chalmers point is that even with an explanation of how I retrieve the mental state, it still doesn’t explain the associated experience I have when the mental state is retrieved, that is, *what it is like* for me to taste the delicious cake

I can describe that feeling to you using language, for example, or I can just have the experience in qualitative terms and not share it with anyone. The point is that no science explains these qualitative feelings to us AND that currently at least, it’s not clear how science *would ever* explain the existence of these experiences

Another example might be science explaining how I retrieve the mental state ‘I am in pain’. A neuroscientist could figure out the underlying mechanisms associated with my brain retrieving this information (presumably the same mechanism that explains how I retrieve any mental state) and still it wouldn’t explain how I feel when I am in pain. The feeling of being in pain seems conceptually different from things like retrieving the mental state that I am in pain. The hard problem is about explaining *what it is like* to, e.g., be in pain

In another terminology, the hard problem is about explaining ‘phenomenal consciousness’, a term I discussed in various other posts (e.g., [here](https://substack.com/@ellennoraburns/p-181003391), [here](https://substack.com/@ellennoraburns/p-179949297), and [here](https://substack.com/@ellennoraburns/p-182125497))

Explaining the easy problems of consciousness, then, —that is, the mechanisms underlying brain function— is not enough. There is a further problem, which is how, for any such mechanism, there is an associated *experience.*

This requires explaining too. This is the hard problem.

#### Metaphysics and the Hard Problem

There tends to be a metaphysical worldview of one kind or another that underlies the easy-hard distinction. Not everyone who values this distinction has the same metaphysics of course, and many who reject the hard problem share the same metaphysics as those who accept it, so this isn’t a clear cut association by any means. It’s more of an observation, that some kind of metaphysical worldview tends to underlie the distinction.

I'll give just two examples of such a metaphysical worldview. The reason I mention them is because I think that if we give up on believing in any particular worldview, it’s easier to see an argument for rejecting the hard problem.

An increasingly popular kind of metaphysics underlying the easy-hard distinction is what’s sometimes called Russelian Monism.[^3] This metaphysics basically says that there is one kind of stuff in the world that underlies consciousness *and* the physical properties of the universe, and that that kind of stuff may be physical or it may be mental). One popular form of Russian Monism goes something like this:

Physics only tells us about the relational or structural properties of the world. It doesn’t tell us about its non-structural or non-relational properties. This is sometimes described as physics telling us about the *extrinsic* properties of the world, without telling us about its *intrinsic* properties.

The most relevant example of a structural or relational property in this context are the properties described by the laws of physics. The laws of physics describe the behavioral/structural/relational elements of nature, without ever telling us anything deeper about what the nature of the world really is. Thus physics may tell us about the nature of the world in terms of mathematical equations, and by specifying, for example, how a system of electrons interact with one another (a ‘structural’ or ‘relational’ property), but physics does not really tell us what an electron *is* (it doesnt tell us about the intrinsic properties of electrons). More broadly, physics does not tell us what the intrinsic nature of matter is.

Interpreting the easy-hard distinction in this framework, we can fairly describe the easy problems as dealing with aspects of the brain that we may reasonably assume reduce in a clear way to the *structural* or *relational* properties of the world.[^4] The hard problem of consciousness however, that is, the qualitative experiences we have on a daily basis, cannot be. The hard problem is therefore a uniquely difficult problem in this sense.[^5]

Another kind of metaphysics that can underlie the easy-hard distinction is a *physicalist* metaphysics. Physicalism claims that there is nothing in the world ‘over and above’ the physical. Now, there are some problems for this view —perhaps most notably the question of what the physical even is![^6] That said, very often physicalism takes a particular enough form: the physical is the world as described by physics, that is to say, a world in which there is nothing but solid bits of matter causally moving through space and time in accordance with the laws of physics. This is sometimes called a ‘materialist-mechancial’ worldview.[^7]

On this worldview, the ‘easy problems’ of consciousness can be explained pretty well in terms of the laws of physics (in other words, we can hope to explain the easy problems in terms of physical mechanisms and the like), but the hard problem resists this kind of explanation. It resists simple computational or neuronal mechanistic explanation.[^8]

#### Why Believe in the Hard Problem?

Now, don’t get me wrong. There is something appealing about believing in the hard problem, and I am on board with those who adamantly defend the hard problem *because* they recognize the importance of acknowledging the reality of our experiential life (Goff is one excellent case of someone who does this).[^9]

That said, I’m also on board with fervent critics of the hard problem, because as I see it, they capture something important about the limits of science, and, in my opinion, are more tethered to the reality of how much science really does understand about the brain and consciousness today, than many other views.

Patricia Churchland is one scathing denier of the hard problem (honestly, I use the word ‘scathing’ in a positive way. I admire her argumentative skills).[^10]

She basically argues that there is no reason to draw a principled distinction between the hard and easy problems of consciousness. After all, the distinction is more or less conceptual (even Chalmers would admit this much), and so, once science develops, the chances are that our belief in this distinction will dissolve.

Thus, Churchland writes, ‘‘what exactly is the evidence that we could explain all the easy phenomena and still not understand the neural mechanisms for consciousness? \[…\] That someone can *imagine* the possibility is not evidence for the real possibility. It is only evidence that somebody or other believes it to be a possibility. That, on its own, is not especially interesting. Imaginary evidence, needless to say, is not as interesting as real evidence, and what needs to be produced is some real evidence’’ (p, 403)

What to make of this debate?

Well, in a sense I think both perspectives are right. I agree with Chalmers that we are likely to retain the intuition that consciousness is not explained by the various neural mechanisms that science will (we hope) one day discover AND I agree with Churchland that we shouldn’t really be asking of science that it do more than discover these mechanisms. The disagreement is complex, but in a sense, I wonder if it boils down to what we believe science can and cannot achieve.

In other words, I think we have to ask *what exactly are we asking science to achieve*? *Should science offer us an intuitive explanation of our experiences of the world?*

At the end of the day, science doesn’t owe us an explanation of anything. And I think that’s something we need to reckon with more in thinking about the mind.

If we look at the history of science/philosophy (of which there was no real distinction before the 17th century), I think we find that science doesn’t offer us intuitive explanations of a lot of things. Why should it offer us an intuitive explanation of experience?

Take a concrete example: Descartes, Huygens, Hobbes, Galileo, (even Newton) hoped that physics would offer us an intuitive explanation of gravity, that is, a causal explanation, but the explanation never arrived (even today we see some scientists and philosophers try to interpret gravity in relatively intuitive, but the reality of the science is that we have virtually no idea what gravity is).

The aspiration to offer an intuitive - i.e., causal and mechanistic explanation was more or less put to bed with Newton (this is a controversial claim in my field, but I wrote a whole dissertation claiming it’s true, and I do think it is true). And if you don’t believe the aspiration was undermined by Newton, many accept that is was undermined by later physics and physicists.

Consider further the fact that much of the physics we once believed explained the true nature of the world — a scientific project that is known as the ‘Mechanical Philosophy’ in the history of science [^11] \- turns out is largely a reflection of how we innately interpret the world from infancy.[^12]

This picture of the world (think billiard balls knocking into each other, and levers and gears generating motion) is now studied by psychologists, not theoretical physicists, whose work is focused on understanding the underlying innate picture of nature that babies begin to acquire as young as 3 months old.[^13]

Recently, Iris Berent, also a psychologist, argued for something very similar in relation to the hard problem of consciousness. She argues that the hard problem is an artefact of human psychology, not a reflection of how the world really is.[^14]

My own view is definitely consistent with Berent’s.

Pretty simply it’s this: I don’t think that science explains the nature of our experiential life, or *even tries too*.

Sure, science can look for the neural correlates of consciousness and plausibly enlighten us about consciousness in all sorts of different ways. This is incredibly difficult valuable work that is being done all of the time. But the aspect of consciousness that the hard problem wants it to explain, the *qualitative* feels we have in our daily life, are, in my view, far to individualized, context-dependent, and specific for science to even care to try to explain them.

Science aims to capture general truths of nature, but our experiences of what it is like to experience things don’t generalize in this way at all.[^15] What it is like to feel pain, see a sun set, or enjoy a nice cocktail can vary from day to day, minute to minute, person to person (when you’re sick you might not enjoy your otherwise favorite cocktail, and what it is like to drink your favorite cocktail will vary accordingly). But if we believe that qualia or some similar metaphysical entity explains our experiences, I worry that this kind of variability is not accounted for.

Take more complex examples and it becomes ever harder.

#### Russelian Monism, Physicalism and The Hard Problem

I think the hard problem gets at something important, the fact that we are living breathing conscious creatures, and that those experiences are *real.* And I think criticisms like Churchlands also get at something important, the fact that we shouldn’t over-extend what science can and cannot achieve.

Where I get off the philosophy train is in upholding any kind of metaphysical worldview. Why do I get off this train? That’s a long story.

For now, I’ll just say this.

Committing to one or another metaphysical and/or scientific worldview implicitly commits us to a certain conception of science, whereby science can explain all of nature (with perhaps the exception of consciousness and the intrinsic nature of matter). But this assumption is too gratuitous in my opinion. We have no science that confirms this is true, only metaphysical worldviews that assume it.

Does science, for instance, explain gravity? I would argue that it doesn't. It assumes its existence, and it makes excellent predictions about how the world works by positing its existence. To me, not understanding the nature of gravity is a strong case in point that the fundamental nature of the world, not just the intrinsic nature of matter, is deeply mysterious to us.

A metaphysical view of nature commits us to something different. It commits us to believing that the nature of the physical world is largely understood by us., that gravity is, at it were, pretty un-mysterious. Whichever picture you choose, it tends to assume that there is very little in the world that science doesn’t understand. But I wonder whether we should believe this.

If I take science as my starting point, I ask: does science give me any reason to be a Russelian monist, a physicalist, an idealist? etc. I think the answer is again, no. Science suggests nothing about what the fundamental nature of reality is. So, I remain agnostic on what it is.

Now, if we remain agnostic on what the fundamental nature of the world is like, the next question that arises is this: is there a hard problem of consciousness that science needs to explain? For reasons more or less similar to Churchlands, I don’t see any science that tells us that there is.

However, I also don’t see any science that tells me that for something to be *real,* science must explain it. My love for friends and family is real. My values are real. My hobbies and interests are real. My daily joys and struggles are real. No scientist explains any of it. Why should my experience of seeing a sun set, experiencing pain, or tasting cake be any different?
