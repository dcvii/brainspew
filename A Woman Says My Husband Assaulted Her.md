*Dear Abigail,*

*I have been happily married for seven years, and I’ve known my husband for eight and a half. Like any couple, we’ve had our ups and downs, but overall we’ve grown closer, while building a life together and raising our three young children.*

*This week, completely out of the blue, I received an email from someone I don’t know. She said she felt I deserved to know something important—that over 10 years ago, my husband sexually assaulted her.*

*I spoke to my husband immediately. He told me that around that time, he had a brief relationship with this woman, and they hooked up a few times. He said she became unstable and obsessive, and that he cut off contact. According to him, she later accused him of rape, which he firmly denies.*

*Understandably, this has shaken me.*

*I trust my husband. Our marriage is built on that foundation. He has earned my complete trust in a thousand ways in our time together and has always been a man of upstanding character. I know in his heart he is a kind and good man.*

*And yet, I can’t ignore the pit in my stomach.*

*I’m torn about what to do next. Should I respond to her and hear her side of the story? Or is trusting my husband enough?*

*I don’t want to invite unnecessary chaos or give attention to someone who may be unstable. But I also don’t want to dismiss something serious or risk being naive.*

*—A deeply conflicted wife, 35*

Dear Deeply Conflicted Wife,

A brilliant friend, a fellow law student at the time, once pointed out to me an ontological unfairness in criminal law. “You ever notice that the criminal code mostly punishes male-typical forms of violence?” he said. “Female-typical social violence—rumor, cruel innuendo, ostracism, alienating friends from you—largely goes ignored?” It was the sort of unconventional insight that came at me sideways and stuck. Because, of course, he was right.

Pop another guy in the nose, and you’ve committed an assault. You face expulsion from school or even a criminal record. But spread a devastating rumor, level a false accusation—and you can inflict bottomless, ongoing anguish on your target without anyone calling the cops on you. You’ll rarely even suffer reputational consequences yourself.

Over a decade after she briefly dated your husband, a woman has detonated a dirty bomb in your family. Her conduct will leave no physical trace. But by inviting you to mistrust your husband, the emotional pain and havoc visited on your family are potentially limitless. Do you imagine she is honestly trying to protect you or your children from your husband of nearly eight years? Does that seem even remotely credible? Don’t lie to yourself: It isn’t.

If their relationship had been recent, and you and your husband were newly dating, there might be good cause for taking this type of unsolicited email seriously. But seven years into your marriage to a “kind and good man” who has won your “complete trust in a thousand ways” and “has always been a man of upstanding character”? You shouldn’t be indulging her story at all; she has no benign reason for contacting you.

This is the dark act of a woman who is trying to hurt your husband by poisoning his marriage—alienating him from his wife and children. She very well may be obsessed with him, as your husband contends. Why else wait until a man is happily married with three young kids before sending this sort of message to his wife? She is attempting to plant ideas in your head as another might casually sprinkle arsenic in his drink.

Deeply Conflicted, surely you’ve noticed: Nearly every man who’s spent a decade dating before marriage has at some point gone out with an unstable woman. That there are men who rape and batter women hardly needs to be said. But also, there *are* crazy women*.* And very normal men date them.

You want to know if you should ask to hear her side of the story, “Or is trusting my husband enough?”—as if this would require an act of blind faith.

Eight and a half years and three children have given you *tens of thousands* of data points on your husband—life’s equivalent of Big Data. Why does the aggregate mean so little to you that you would entertain calling a malevolent stranger to get her take on your husband?

And if the situation were reversed? A resentful ex-boyfriend calls your husband out of the blue to tell him what a snake you are. You’re only after his money. You’re a serial cheater. If your husband were to put aside nearly a decade of knowing you, proclaim himself “deeply conflicted” in your marriage, consider calling this guy to let him unfurl his tale, what would you think of him? You’d think him disloyal and foolish, even cruel. You’d wonder how his life with you could count for so little that he was willing to offer it as collateral against the rantings of a stranger.

She is attempting to plant ideas in your head as another might casually sprinkle arsenic in his drink.

You’ve talked all this through with your husband, and he gave you a coherent explanation. That this woman prompted an embarrassing conversation between you and your husband about their sexual relationship before you met is, for her, a minor victory.

Put yourself back together before you hand her a major one.

You’ve allowed yourself to become a gaping point of vulnerability in your family. You tell me you don’t want to “dismiss something serious”? And what, exactly, would that “something serious” be? You’ve already said that your husband has only ever been good to you. Perhaps you’re worried about losing your sisterhood solidarity card? (A counterfeit object, if ever there was.)

Don’t go down this foolish road, Deeply Conflicted. It’s not vigilance that tempts you to hear out this mischief-maker. It’s more likely ennui—and the attendant impulse to self-destruct. “I can’t ignore that pit in my stomach,” you say. But, of course you *can* ignore it. And of course, you should.

You’re not the producer of a true-crime podcast about your husband’s bachelor days. You’re a wife and mother contemplating a move that would jeopardize the peace of your children. And for what?

Life has been good to you, Deeply Conflicted. You’ve built a beautiful family, and it needs protection. No one understands this better than the arsonist who sent you that email, striking a match, hoping to watch it all burn.

Onward,

Abigail
