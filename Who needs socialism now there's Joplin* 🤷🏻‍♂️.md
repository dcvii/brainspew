The latest personal project in my world is to migrate my website ([24thcavrecon.org](https://www.24thcavrecon.org/)) from a rent-seeking annual subscription platform to Wordpress dot org. This will save 204€ a year, for no loss of utility or quality.

Last week I cancelled the note-taking app, with which I run much of my creative and work life, installed a free open-source equivalent, imported ten years’ of notes and saved a £79 annual subscription. During the same life admin session I also cancelled a 35€ annual password manager service, exported every password and set up a free open-source password manager.

In both cases I now spend no money while also taking control of my own data. This is pleasing, especially since my personal notes are no longer sitting on someone else’s servers and my passwords are no longer vulnerable to the kinds of data breach suffered by my previous password security provider.

A month ago I used Claude AI to guide me through the process of having 24thcavrecon.org translate into French and German at the touch of a button, again at zero financial cost. I needed minimal technical nous to achieve this. Just typing request prompts, copying output, pasting it somewhere and a bit of patience when things initially went wrong.

The historical material I work with - now thousands of documents and photographs - had become impossible to organise without a professional archive tool and I baulked at the cost of those. But then I found a free open-source archive organisation tool, built by academics for academics, sucked up the learning curve and never looked back.

If I hadn’t been short of money I’d have bought various services and would now be paying north of 1,000€ a year for the privilege of using tools that I’d then feel locked into using, the more useful they became. Because that’s how subscription platforms work. They become woven into your life to such an extent that losing one is like losing a metaphorical limb.

Having just achieved the lifelong dream of assembling a studio at home, evenings are spent learning and making music. You already guessed that much of the software I use for this is free and often open-source. Some of it is so valuable to me that I donated money to the projects by way of thanks, but I didn’t have to do that. These tools are freely given for anyone who wants them.

The remarkable thing is that all of this feels normal. It’s just life as usual. And I have to step back to reflect on what teenage me would have thought of the power and utility freely available to older Mike.

And yet, if you engage in common chit chat, consume bogstandard news media or dwell for long in Substackistan, there is a relentless mood of civilisational decline.

---

**Everything is broken.**

The popular ‘discourse’ is familiar.

Modernity is spiritually empty. Technology is enslaving us, stealing from us, misleading us. Men and women mistrust or even hate each other. There is a ‘loneliness epidemic’ due to social atomisation and those pesky little screens. The algorithms are ruining everything. Institutional trust is at a record low.

Not that there isn’t a *there* there. Of course we haven’t yet reached whichever utopia you care to name and the arc of civilisation isn’t bending as smoothly toward everyone’s chosen ideals as we’d like. And it’s easier to write about these things for an anxious audience than just quietly go about re-jigging how you might be personally contributing to them.

I’m tired of the decline discourse when I contrast it with my own experience of modernity. And I’m sceptical about the honesty of this discourse because any fool knows that we’re in an environment here that disproportionately rewards pessimism. Even worse, pessimism is deemed to signal sophistication. It feels nice to signal that you can see below the surface of things - I know because I’ve done plenty of it myself.

*Ah, but look at what’s going on under the surface, for I am very intelligent and intelligent people are always cynical.*

My intuition is that much of the decline discourse flows from the personal anxiety and unhappiness of the people who promote it. And they are rewarded for performative despair, rhetorical superiority and fashionable cynicism. I think of this as the prestige economy of doom.

I’m sorry that so many people feel this way.

No I’m not. I don’t care, really. Except insofar as their misery leaks into my world.

A good example of that is how it is becoming kind of low status to talk about using AI. Because, you see, AI has failure modes which disproportionately make news - and news is what normaltons absorb. So, when you say ‘hey, I used ChatGPT to help me wire the lighting in my studio’ they come back with something about how much water and energy you consumed to do it. Before they bob off to eat their lunch of something that used 100 times the water to produce as your entire year’s worth of LLM interactions.

**I love modernity**

A personal phobia is the sense of feeling incapable or powerless in the face of tasks and projects. Believe me, I know what self-loathing is.

I’ve had a lifetime of feeling too intimidated to attempt practical things because I’m clumsy with my hands and my brain refuses to rotate shapes. If there’s a 50/50 chance of screwing something to the wrong end of something else I’ll manage it with infuriating reliability. The granularity of the guidance I need is beyond the patience span of normal humans, but with a bot on hand that doesn’t care how stupid my questions are my productivity is transformed. So what if I don’t know how to do elementary things and my brain won’t parse the YouTube videos about it, there’s ChatGPT to consult as much as I want. And now I’ve done things like making a studio space beautiful, with electric wall lights and everything I feel better about myself. Lol.

The kind of archival work I do was once only possible by flying across the Atlantic and visiting places like the US National Archive in person. Last month, after a brief email exchange, I was able to plug my Mac (here in Normandy) directly into [NARA](https://www.archives.gov/) and access everything I need, using technology I can only explain in the vaguest terms that Claude wrote for me. Sure, modernity is shit, tech is ruining our lives and AI is turning us into morons. Sigh.

The genuinely amazing thing to me is that global communication and information retrieval now feels trivial. You’re reading this now, wherever you are, doubtless without appreciating what a remarkable thing is happening here. Thoughts transmitted from my mind into yours, with distance no object and neither of us knowing each other.

I don’t pay to do this, beyond what I need to be online (Starlink in my case) and I receive a small amount of money for publishing Rarely Certain from readers I’d never have reached before Substack existed. Yes, paying subscribers fund my luxury beer membership of the [Moersleutel](https://moersleutel.com/) Guild.

I’ve done more highly remunerated client work this week from the comfort of my kitchen in a way that is now deeply ordinary for those of us in the ‘knowledge economy’. On my morning walk yesterday I remembered sitting for 5 hours in stop-start traffic a few years ago every Monday morning and laughed out loud while a pair of buzzards rode the thermals overhead. It was a moment of sincere gratitude before starting work, invigorated rather than depleted as I always was in those days.

**Overlooking civilisational achievement is passé, if you ask me**

Of course we’re wired to overlook life improvements once the novelty is passed. Like no longer dying suddenly from things that became treatable over the past century, we just take improvements in our steady state existence for granted. The [hedonic treadmill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hedonic_treadmil) is real, so it’s nice to actively reflect on how good life is with countless amazingly skilled and capable, yet unknown, people building useful things that I can just take and use at whim.

Whether they’re doing it for the reputational benefit they accrue, ideological reasons or through curiosity and just the pleasure of digital craftsmanship, what’s going on is truly liberating to me.

The softwares I’m now using were designed to empower and free users, not trap them, and most of us just shrug off the utility that this represents (or don’t even really know about it).

Nor is it just about what’s useful to me. There’s a philosophical aspect to celebrate in the way that open source communities are one of the areas online where competence and generosity visibly coexist and status-seeking leads to concrete enhancements to real-world life.

If you’re thinking this is just an idealistic paean to tech, there’s more to it.

I prefer to own my own life and too much of it for comfort has been sitting on other people’s servers, subject to arbitrary pricing strategies and risk of theft. I want to be as self-directing as possible, rather than subject to the constraints that work for rent-seekers and profiteers. And I feel psychologically nourished by tools that are just serving my needs rather than harvesting parts of my life to sell on. To sum up the non-techy aspect of this and why I celebrate it the word *autonomy* comes to mind.

There’s also a political aspect to this, because of the relentless sense we have of constantly being *managed* by corporate and governmental whims. Everything I can do that is truly self-directed feels precious in our normal climate, filled as it is by the pressure of normative constraints from idiots online and essentially faceless entities.

It’s also interesting to me how the gift of technological modernity is consistently overlooked and a few reasons come to mind.

One is that the best, most useful and empowering things are invisible. Just as mostly feeling completely safe in a place full of strangers long ago stopped feeling remarkable - even though we’ve only enjoyed this privilege for about 5 minutes since the aeons of previous history.

Another reason is that gradual improvements to life lack any kind of narrative drama. This is doubtless why AI is causing such a stir, because it is improving sufficiently fast to become a story. And, of course, it’s mostly a negative story in the kind of normalton media that trades in pessimism because that has more social valence.

Also, it isn’t fashionable to be quietly satisfied and glad for how things are. This essay will convert no one who loves a nice negative framing, which is what you see across all legacy and most independent media as well as among Substack writers. We know what sells and being cheerful isn’t it.

The reality is that how damned wonderful everything mostly is has just become a background hum, drowned out by the anxious and neurotic who love to worry, share their negative perceptions and offer the direst predictions for a catastrophic moment of collapse that actually never arrives. This is one of the main drivers of the ‘climate catastrophe’ canard, which ironically provides a pleasant form of reassurance to certain people that their constant underlying sense of existential dread has its roots out there, rather than in their minds.

Amusingly, I consulted with various chatbots on the themes of this piece and they all warned me not to come across as too cheerful. They wanted me to express *cautious gratitude* at most. Lol. They specifically warned me against implying that technology will save us. Again, LOL. (Because I don’t think we need saving anyway).

They also urged me to ensure I recognise that there are genuine problems in the world. As if anyone needs reminding.

Above all, I was strongly encouraged not to seem *naive*, because of course I’m supposed to be writing a sophisticated newsletter for sophisticated people and sophistication means being appropriately gloomy.

But I’m not going to pretend to feel gloomy about the present and fearful for the future when I don’t. So I won’t. This is an unapologetic celebration of how damned much my life has improved thanks to clever people who share wonderful things with literally anyone who wants them.

It’s a hell of a time to be alive and I would not swap it for any prior period in my life. And definitely no time from before I was alive.

Sorry to disappoint. But you’ll find no shortage of accounts elsewhere on the app explaining how broken ‘society’ is, so go and enjoy them.
