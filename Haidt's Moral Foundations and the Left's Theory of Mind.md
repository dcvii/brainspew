[David Dennison](https://open.substack.com/users/195610704-david-dennison?utm_source=mentions) points out in his characteristically excellent [Fix Shenanigans —> Fix Racism](https://substack.com/@dennisonwrites/p-181214507) something that has been nagging at me for a while.

> The reason it’s impossible to explain to liberals why fixing misbehavior will fix racism *too* is because they have no real idea what racism is.
> 
> Per the left’s construction - and this is so unexaggerated, it hurts - the world is full of people who are evil (racists) and people who are good (them). Evil people are evil because they are evil, and they hate people with different languages and religions and skin tones because their evil tells them that they must. Meanwhile, good people are just not like this.

Libs do not have a useful theory of mind for conservatives or classical liberals. Progressives aren’t, as a rule, good at modeling the thoughts of anyone to their right, and their preference when they deign to argue is to strawman rather than to steelman conservative arguments [^1], so it’s hard to know how well most libs actually *understand* those arguments. I’m not the first to point out that they don’t persuade people anymore (either [Tired Moderate](https://open.substack.com/users/50715667-tired-moderate?utm_source=mentions) or [Drunk Wisconsin](https://open.substack.com/users/12430670-drunk-wisconsin?utm_source=mentions) did an entire article on this, and I’m too lazy to look up which it is, but you should be reading both of them), preferring instead to shout people down or use actual violence to keep conservative and classical liberal voices from being heard.

Among the people they’ve tried to shout down is [Jonathan Haidt](https://open.substack.com/users/5791770-jonathan-haidt?utm_source=mentions), a clinical psychologist and free speech advocate who was in part responsible for [moral foundations theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moral_foundations_theory). For those too lazy to click the link, MFT posits that there are six foundational principles that undergird moral decision-making: Care/Harm, Fairness/Cheating, Liberty/Tyranny, Sanctity/Degradation, Authority/Subversion, and Loyalty/Mistrust. Haidt suggests, though, that progressives mostly rely on two of them, Care/Harm and Fairness/Cheating, as the bases for making or analyzing moral decisions.

If you think this through, it’s why their theory of mind breaks when it comes to conservative politics.

Example: When the concept of personal loyalty gets brought up in the context of Trump, the progressive response is inevitably some version of “yeah, the loyalty of the Mafia.” It’s not wholly without merit as a criticism—there is substantial evidence of financial corruption in the Trump White House, and that’s at least within spitting distance of gangsterism—but it’s a misunderstanding of a foundational reason a lot of conservatives approve of him. Conservatives think it’s a moral imperative to be loyal to those who give you a job or a hand up in your career, and it doesn’t really occur to progressives to frame the question in those terms.

Or we could talk about sanctity. Progressives have abandoned it as a basis for moral decision-making—they’re actively hostile to moral criticisms of sex work, for example, and from a conservative standpoint, the trans issue is in no small part a disagreement over whether the sanctity of the body has any relevance in the context of the amputation of healthy tissue in teens and young adults.

How about liberty? I challenge you to find a mainstream liberal commentator who thinks patriotism is an unalloyed good. Perhaps more to the point, Democrats are the party of technocratic speech censorship, which is all that really needs to be said about their conceptualization of liberty.

Authority? Progressives think they’re the counterculture, with all the anti-authority swagger that entails.

Progressives tend to strawman principled conservative arguments. After all, the moral bases that underlie them aren’t real, so they can’t really have any merit, right? And caricaturing one’s opponent’s position is an effective use of intellectual dishonesty to win an argument. But if you get in the habit of using a parody of someone’s position as a talking point, your capacity to steelman atrophies. Eventually your capacity to engage with someone with a different point of view withers entirely, because you have to at least occasionally parse arguments honestly to understand them yourself.

That, in a nutshell, is why progressives talk past conservatives. They genuinely don’t understand the arguments being made by conservatives, because they don’t engage with the bases that undergird them.

Does that make them immoral? I think it makes them *less* moral. Like water, moral arguments fit the shape of their containers, and at some point progressives traded a stable moral container for a precarious, highly specialized one—if conservative moral thought is like a pyramid (imposing, broad-based), progressive moral thought of late has developed into something like a skyscraper (narrow base, so tall it sways in the wind). They have to fit every moral impulse into either a care-based or a fairness-based discursive framework, which requires genuine ingenuity but comes with substantial limitations—there are arguments you simply can’t make with those frameworks.

I’m not sure they realize what they’ve done to themselves, argumentatively. There’s probably some kind of organic brain atrophy on that side of the political fence akin to what happens when you overuse Google Maps to find places and you stop being able to understand a gas station atlas (when we still had those).

I’m also not sure what the solution is (or if there is one) to the talking past people problem this creates. There’s a chicken/egg element to taking weird political positions based on a misunderstanding of morality, too, that probably merits exploration—I’d like to know which came first, the opinions or the inability to understand. But for now, that’s what I got.
