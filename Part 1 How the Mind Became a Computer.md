**The Birth Of Cognitive Science**

I gave a talk this month that led me down a rabbit hole about the history and birth of a variety of different fields including, cognitive science, AI, neuroscience, computer science, and theoretical linguistics. It’s worth knowing that all of them emerge more or less at the same time, the 1950s and 1960s.

A personable short account of the time period is given by [George A Miller in his paper ‘The Cognitive Revolution: A Historical Perspective](https://www.cs.princeton.edu/~rit/geo/Miller.pdf) ’. He is considered one of the founders of the field of cognitive science. In this paper, he describes a time when computer science and neuroscience were coming into being, and anthropology and linguistics were being re-defined. Interdisciplinary work was called for, and Miller offers an account of how some of that happened.

But alas, the inter-disciplinarity doesn’t seem to have gone far enough, because some crucial unfounded assumptions, in particular from the philosophy of mind, crept into the birth of cognitive science that remain, to this day, largely unquestioned. And they should be. What follows is not Miller’s account of how cognitive science came to be, but my own views on what might need questioning in main stream ‘cognitive science’.[^1]

#### The Remarkable Feats of Turing Computation

In 1936, Alan M Turing published a [paper](https://www.cs.virginia.edu/~robins/Turing_Paper_1936.pdf) in which he formally defined the notion of a *mechanical procedure* (also known as a ‘computation’). This was a transformative moment in the history of the sciences of the mind, in computer science, and in mathematics. Prior to Turing, there was nothing but an intuitive idea of what a ‘mechanical procedure’ was: it was some set of rules that you could follow in a rote (‘mechanical’) manner, to produce some output.

The calculations performed using Newton’s laws of motion and of gravity, for example (computing trajectories or calculating the positions of the planets) are instances of mechanical procedures. But before Turing, nobody could state *why* these calculations were mechanical procedures or what distinguishes those calculations from calculations that aren’t.

Turing, by formalizing the notion of a mechanical procedure did several remarkable things. One is that he showed a number of different calculations which were intuitively understood to be procedural could be unified in some sense. For example, that arithmetic and euclidean geometry are two instances of the same kind of abstract mechanical procedure, what we now call a *computation*.

His formalization of a mechanical procedure also clearly delineated, for deterministic finite state machines, the class of processes that are (and are not) computable, showing for example, that certain processes fall outside of the realm of possible computations. He showed that this was true, not for practical reasons of engineering but as a matter of mathematical fact (the [halting problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halting_problem) is the most famous example).

The question I am interested in asking is how these remarkable results map onto studying the human mind/brain. More particularly, how has cognitive science interpreted these results and made use of them in understanding the mind/brain?

#### The Rise and Fall of Behaviorism

On the face of it, Turing’s results are remarkable and interesting for mathematics, but it's not immediately obvious how they would help for studying the mind/brain. But there seems to have been something in the air in the 1950s and 60’s that made Turings work (among the work of many others) foundational to how cognitive science came to be understood.

Miller’s article offers some insight. As he points out -and as is well known- behaviorism at this time was becoming widely unpopular. Behaviorism emerged in elite US universities in the 1930s and 40s with thundering force—claiming that psychology could only ever be the study of behavior. Because mental events are not publicly observable, it would be unscientific to claim they exist.

The behaviorist solution was to say that they didn’t exist. Humans and other organisms were therefore reduced to complex input-output systems that responded to stimuli and produced behavior based on those stimuli.

All of this came crashing down in the 1950’s. A passage from Miller explains it better than I will. He writes that,

‘‘behaviorism was an exciting adventure for experiential psychology but by the mid 1950’s it had become apparent that it could not succeed. As Chomsky remarked, defining psychology as the science of behavior was like defining physics as the science of meter reading. If scientific psychology was to succeed, mentalistic concepts would have to integrate and explain the behavioral data’’ [^2]

In reading this, it seems there was a —fortunate or unfortunate, depending on your persuasion— colliding of interests at this time for psychology. On the one hand, behaviorism was failing miserably to explain the mind. And a stunning part of that failure was its refusal to posit the existence of mental processes.

On the other hand, Turing’s formalization of a mechanical procedure just a few decades earlier had laid the foundations for computer science, offering a digital blueprint for the modern digital computer. But the inspiration didn’t stop there.

This digital blueprint inspired people to think: what if this digital blueprint might also be the blue print of *the mind?*

#### The Rise of Cognitive Science

The rest is history. Or so it seems. Because when you read many of the works of the ‘founding fathers’ of cognitive science —with notable exceptions, like Chomsky, who does ‘cognitive science’ in a very different way — the transition looks seamless. Undefended claims about minds being computers are everywhere.

Google the founding fathers of cognitive science and you’ll get some list approximating the following: George Miller, Noam Chomsky, Allen Newell, Herbert Simon, Marvin Minsky, Urlic Neisser, Jerome Bruner. In reality, these authors were all doing very different things. But here are some core historical moments aside from Turing’s work, that were foundational to the field:

(1) Claude Shannon’s mathematical theory of information. Miller notes, for example, that there was a time when he and others tried to apply Shannons theory of information to psychology.[^3]

(2) Chomsky’s *Syntactic Structures* which used formal methods to analyze language, and his review of Skinner’s verbal behavior in 1959, which is widely regarded as the paper that put the nail in the coffin of behaviorist thinking (Turing formally definition a computation was also foundational to the formal study of linguistics)

(3) Newell and Simon’s Logic Theorist in 1956, which is widely regarded as one of the first computer programs. Their work preceded coining the term ‘artificial intelligence’ which would be coined later that summer.[^4]

(4) The Dartmouth Conference in 1956 which brought together John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, Nathaniel Rochester, and Claude Shannon who coined the term ‘artificial intelligence’. You can read their proposal for the conference [here](http://jmc.stanford.edu/articles/dartmouth/dartmouth.pdf).

Of course one could point to others, and the many groundbreaking work that was done. But in looking at the history, what’s of most interest is the works that came a little after, which began to name what was happening in terms of the birth of different academic fields of study.

For cognitive science, that would be Urlic Neisser’s 1967 book which was one of the first books to name all that was happening ‘cognitive psychology’.

His book *Cognitive Psychology* offers interesting insights into the assumptions that helped shape this field. He isolates a few points that I think are correct and essential steps to take towards understanding the brain.

For example, he opens the book by claiming that the central problem of cognition is that ‘the world of experience is produced by the man who experiences it’ [^5]

In other words, human beings are not passive beings in the world. Our brains actively construct how we view and understand the world around us. He points out that this wasn’t always believed, citing the theory of *eidola,* a Greek theory of perception, which claimed that material replicas of objects would pass through the air and into our brains. The cognitive approach argues that this isn’t true.

Rejecting this assumption, cognitive psychology was concerned with studying how the brain constructs the world around us, through the study of e.g., perception, memory, attention, and so on (what are called ‘mental processes’).

This approach is also an outright rejection of behaviorism which refuses to engage with the internal workings of the brain to understand it.

The next question Neisser asks is again, a good one: he asks, how should we go about studying these cognitive mechanisms? His answer is what interests me. He very quickly moves to an answer that sneaks in heavily ontological commitment.

That is, he moves to study mechanisms independently of their implementation, and argues that computer programs are a good model to study brains on. The same way we can understand the software of a computer as independent of the hardware that implements it, we can understand the ‘mental processes’ of the brain independently of how the brain ‘implements’ them.

There is, I think, a harmless way of believing this is true and a harmful way. The harmless way is believing that in studying the brain one needs to construct *explanatory theories* of whatever aspect of it you are trying to understand (language, memory, perception, etc). This will inevitably require building an explanatory framework of different aspects of the brain that are built, technically, ‘independently’ of the brain, but only in the trivial sense that the focus is a higher level description of the brain, and not things like neuronal firings and the like.

The harmful way of believing this is true is the way that a certain dominant strand of cognitive science takes it to be true, namely, believing that ‘mental processes’ in the brain are independent of the brain in the ontological sense.

This kind of view is implicitly or explicitly upheld in much of the historical literature I’ve read. Good examples include, Allan Newell’s ‘ [The knowledge level’,](http://lidecc.cs.uns.edu.ar/~grs/InteligenciaArtificial/Allen%20Newell%20-%20The%20knowledge%20level.pdf) Neisser’s [‘Cognitive Psychology](https://stanford.edu/class/psych205/papers/Neisser-1967_ch1.pdf) ’ or *[Mind Design](https://direct.mit.edu/books/edited-volume/4889/Mind-Design-IIPhilosophy-Psychology-and-Artificial)* written in 1981.

The general approach in these texts is to think of the mind/brain as an *information processor*. Crucially, this move is *philosophical,* not methodological, and the move is never really defended.

Some striking examples of this move include Newell asserting in ‘the knowledge level’ that computers are a ‘reflection of nature’. No argument. Just asserted.[^6]

This is false. Computers are human artifacts and should be understood as such. The distinction between ‘hardware’ and ‘software’ is a human classification. Not a reflection of reality. There is no real sense in which the brain has ‘hardware’ and ‘software’ though we have become so used to talking of the brain in this way we might have convinced ourselves by now that it is true.

Another example is Neisser in his book *Cognitive Psychology* when he writes that ‘‘computer programming, has much to offer \[to psychology or cognitive science\]. A program is not a device for measuring information, but a recipe for selecting, storing, recovering, combining, outputting, and generally manipulating it. As pointed out by by Newwell, Shaw, and Simon (1958) this means that programs have much in common with cognition.’’ [^7]

Again we see an assertion that computers and brains are similar, but no argument as to why, except the ‘intuition’ that the brain works like a computer program does. A circular claim to say the least. Neisser even goes on to claim that one of the advantages of the ‘‘program analogy’’ is that it offers us ‘‘philosophical reassurance’’. Maybe. But only if we do bad philosophy in my opinion.

In later years, the appeal to this perspective becomes even more egregious. Haugeland writes, for instance, in an introduction to the book *Mind Design:*

‘‘To put it coldly: why not suppose that people *just are* computers (and send [philosophy packing](https://cse.buffalo.edu/~rapaport/575/F01/haugeland.pdf))?’’ [^8]

What’s interesting here is how Haugeland thinks of cognitive science as getting rid of the philosophy, when in reality it is merely choosing, in my view, to assume some seriously *bad philosophy*, viz, that humans are computers.

There is so much more to say about all of this. This particular strand of cognitive science, for instance, is not the only strand, though often more sensible cognitive science work gets subsumed under this title. There is also interesting overlap between the rise of cognitive science and how it intersects with philosophical issues about the mind (for instance, the mind-body problem).

For now, let me leave you with this: perhaps the biggest irony is that cognitive science set out to put the mind back into psychology after behaviourism had gotten rid of it. But somewhere along the way, it just replaced one set of unargued assumptions for another. The question worth asking, for AI, consciousness, and what we think we know about the mind, is whether we ever even noticed.
