On Saturday morning, the U.S. attacked Iran. American and Israeli forces targeted the infrastructure of the Islamic Republic in air strikes. President Donald Trump said that he started his campaign to allow the [Iranian people](https://www.wmtv15news.com/2026/02/28/read-president-trumps-statement-iran-full/) to “unleash the prosperous and glorious future that is close within your reach.”

The strikes come after weeks of talks with Tehran. Last week, when the threat to Iran was beginning to build, U.S. special envoy Steve Witkoff told Fox News that President Donald Trump was “curious” about Iran’s failure to accede to American demands over its nuclear and missile programs, even as vast American naval forces and airpower was being moved into the region. The president was articulating a frustration that has haunted Western strategic thinking about the Middle East for years.

The Iranian regime, it is assumed, is a rational state actor that can be deterred by the threat of “unbearable costs.” Yet Islamic revolutionary movements and regimes don’t seem to behave rationally, in the sense that Western analysts typically understand.

Why walk to the brink? Why risk Trump’s willingness to start a war that could devastate or even topple the Iranian regime, instead of coming back to the table, conceding on some elements of their ballistic missile or uranium enrichment programs?

To understand why the movement of aircraft carriers into the Persian Gulf often results in the regime hunkering down rather than capitulating—and to understand why the U.S. is now striking Iran—one must understand the invisible framework through which the ayatollahs view the world, and which most Western analysts have never heard of: the doctrine of *muqawama*.

The Muqawama is at once the Islamic Republic’s greatest source of resilience—and the engine of its unraveling.

*Muqawama* is an Arabic word that means “resistance.” The idea, like the word, has been loaned to Persian as “moghavemat,” though it is often pronounced in Iran in the original Arabic.

And that translation, “resistance,” doesn’t begin to cover the term’s meaning or significance. It is something vast, a comprehensive theory of war, of history, of suffering, and of victory. “Resistance,” when used by leaders of Iran or Hezbollah or Hamas or the Houthis, refers to a sustained, never-ending campaign of violence accompanied by a willingness to absorb catastrophic levels of damage.

It is the sacralization of that violence—and of the sacrifice that it entails. As the damage sustained to one’s own polity grows, so the sanctity and religious meaning grows with it.

Muqawama is a deliberate inversion of the logic of conventional strategic thinking where damage inflicted on the resistance is a validation, and destruction is not defeat, but devotion. And sacrifice, sanctified by God, is the ultimate tool available to the weak against the strong.

To understand why bombing Iran or moving battleships close to its shores has never been and never will be sufficient to bring this regime to heel—though these are emphatically *part* of the kind of campaign that would—you have to understand where this idea came from, what it actually argues, and, most urgently, why it is now collapsing from within.

The Muqawama did not begin in Tehran. It was assembled across the 20th century from a remarkable confluence of sources: Palestinian nationalist thought, Maoist guerrilla theory, Algerian anti-colonial strategy, and Shia Islamic theology. Understanding this genealogy is the only way to grasp what the Iranian regime believes it is doing and why it believes it can win.

Thousands of Algerian Muslims demonstrate on December 11, 1960. (AFP via Getty Images)

The first archetype in this intellectual lineage is a Syrian-born preacher named Izzedine al-Qassam, who was killed in 1935 in the hills near Jenin, surrounded by British police, shouting, “This is jihad, victory or martyrdom.” A graduate of Cairo’s Al-Azhar University, al-Qassam fused Islamic piety with anti-colonial militancy, organizing the dispossessed poor into armed cells, and obtained a religious ruling authorizing armed struggle. His death in that shoot-out was not, in the eyes of those who followed him, a defeat. It was a catalyst. It was the spark that ignited the Great Arab Revolt of 1936–39. Hamas named its military wing, and its most commonly used rocket, after him. He established the template: a fighter who does not fear death, a man whose death itself becomes the engine of revolution.

Into this template, Palestinian intellectuals of the 1960s added two additional ingredients. The first was Mao Tse-tung’s theory of guerilla warfare; the idea that a militarily inferior force embedded in a sympathetic population could exhaust a technologically superior enemy simply by refusing to be eliminated. As Mao taught, the masses were the sea and the guerrillas were the fish.

The second was Frantz Fanon, a Martinique-born psychiatrist who joined the Algerian revolution in the 1950s. Fanon argued that for the colonized “native,” violence is a “cleansing force” that restores self-respect. It is not merely a political act but a psychological one: The colonized person, dehumanized by the colonizer, found his dignity restored by picking up a gun. Violence gave him agency, transforming him from history’s passive object into its subject and protagonist.

Algeria’s National Liberation Front proved the theory when its eight-year terror campaign collapsed French colonial rule in the country. By forcing the French to defend the injustice of their rule with wanton brutality—perhaps half a million dead over years of brutal conflict—they forced the French to seriously contend with the ultimate costs of that injustice, right up to the collapse of Algérie Française.

The Palestinian intellectuals in the 1960s who absorbed these lessons drew a strategic conclusion that shapes how they fight today: All you have to do is survive. You do not need to win in any conventional sense. You need to make the cost of occupying you unbearable in moral, political, and economic terms, and let the inherent fragility of colonial power do the rest.

And Palestinian intellectuals like Ghassan Kanafani drew another lesson, the final piece of the intellectual puzzle. Kanafani, writing in Beirut in the ’60s, articulated a theory of “intersectionality” before the term existed. He argued that imperialism was a single biological organism: The head was in East Asia, the heart was in the Middle East (because of oil), and the arteries reached Africa and Latin America.

Therefore, a strike against the Jews in Israel was not a local nationalist act; it was a “heart attack” for the entire global capitalist system. The “resistance,” then, is a vanguard of a global revolution, against all the imperialist “beast’s” many organs and arteries.

It’s all here: the interlocking of all anti-imperialist struggles; Mao’s teaching that the weak could exhaust a technologically superior enemy through permanent popular uprising; Qassam’s that a martyr’s death was no defeat, but a victory that ignites the next generation; and Fanon’s moral justification for inflicting and absorbing unlimited violence.

It is the potent mix that produced Hamas’s strategic concept, according to which pushing the oppressor to ever greater and more brutal war is not a tragedy; it is the way you fight and the way you win.

Only one piece is missing: the recasting of Shia theology into this mold.

That would come in the 1970s, and its chief architect was the Sorbonne-trained sociologist Ali Shariati, whose 1971 speech “Red Shi’ism vs. Black Shi’ism” laid the intellectual scaffolding of the Islamic Republic.

Shariati raged at traditional Shi’ism’s focus on spiritual contemplation and rituals of mourning the death of Imam Hussein, an early Shi’ite leader, at the Battle of Karbala in 680 CE, where he was defeated by the caliph Yazid I. The quietist, passive religion produced by this ritual life was constructed by the ruling class since Iran’s Safavid dynasty (beginning in 1501) to keep the masses pacified and submissive.

A strike against the Jews in Israel was not a local nationalist act; it was a “heart attack” for the entire global capitalist system.

But true Shi’ism, Shariati declared, was inherently revolutionary. Imam Hussein died to upend an unjust social and political order. His example was a call to emulate martyrdom that could embarrass and overturn the ruling order. The stagnant “black Shi’ism” of the old clerical establishment must be replaced by “red Shi’ism” of justice, revolution, and resistance.

It was a profound ideological surgery. Just a year after the Peruvian Catholic priest Gustavo Gutiérrez published *A Theology of Liberation,* reimagining Jesus as a Marxist revolutionary, Shariati did the same for Shi’ism’s Imam Hussein.

This was the ideological ferment in which the preacher and organizer Ruhollah Khomeini conceived of the Islamic Republic that has now ruled Iran for 47 years. Khomeini found the Islamic language to express this powerful mix of ideas.

He drew from the Quran’s reference to the “musta’dafin,” the weak or humble who would inherit the world, in contrast to the “mustakbirin,” the arrogant and powerful.

Speaking to the great theological problem of Islamic nations’ geopolitical weakness in the modern age, Khomeini recast this weakness as a spiritual superpower.

The weak, he said, are spiritually purer than the “arrogant” but powerful West—arrogant for its reliance on worldly economic and military power, arrogant for its claim that individualistic democracy was morally superior to religious dictatorship.

The weak and humble are saved from this arrogance, and so have an easier time remaining spiritually “clean.” The weak, therefore, enjoy the divine protection that makes eventual victory inevitable.

Material weakness was transformed into evidence of authentic piety, and thus of God’s favor. One pious fighter, Khomeini declared, could defeat 10 “decadent” Western soldiers, as when the Prophet Muhammad’s small force defeated their far larger enemy at the Battle of Badr in 624. And the mechanism for that asymmetric victory: martyrdom, modeled on the reimagined figure of Hussein—and on Qassam.

Death in battle was not the price of resistance; it was its highest expression. Death itself became desirable, a closeness to God, a taste of the eventual redemption.

This is the Muqawama: permanent, generationally sustained, religiously validated holy war in which the willingness to absorb unlimited destruction is the weapon.

It is why Khamenei could tell Iranians in January, in Persian, that hundreds of thousands of martyrs built this revolution, and that it would take hundreds of thousands more to bring it down. He was not boasting. He was warning his own people what he was prepared to do to them to keep his Islamic Republic intact.

The Muqawama is powerful because it is sacrificial, and thus undeterrable.

But it is also dying.

Not from American pressure, not even primarily from Israeli military success, but from the accumulated weight of its own internal contradictions—and from the fury of the peoples it has destroyed in order to sustain itself.

The cracks began in Syria. When Hezbollah entered the Syrian civil war in 2012 to prop up Iran ally Bashar al-Assad, its leader, Hassan Nasrallah, was probably the most admired leader in the Arab world, topping polls even in Sunni-majority states that had peace treaties with Israel.

But his reputation would not survive the great brutalities of his forces in Syria. The movement that had framed itself as the eternal champion of the oppressed—the musta’dafin—was suddenly the death squad of a tyrant massacring Muslims. Who was arrogant and who was humble in the Syrian civil war? Sunni clerics who had lionized Shi’ite Hezbollah despite their theological misgivings now turned on it viciously. The jewel of the Muqawama seemed to have switched sides in the cosmic moral contest.

Then came the military embarrassments. Israel’s systematic penetration and dismantling of Hezbollah’s command structure—the pager operation, the decapitation strikes, the erasure of 30 years of built military capacity—stripped away the myth of invincibility that the Muqawama had wrapped around itself. During Israel’s direct confrontation with Iran in June 2025, Iraqi Shia militias—supposedly part of the same axis—publicly announced they were sitting it out. Even the battered and bleeding Hezbollah, built for precisely this moment, did not join the fight. Iran’s “ring of fire” around Israel had become a ring of exhausted, fractured, financially drained proxies who no longer believed in the success of the project.

But the most decisive collapse is taking place inside Iran itself. Data collected through encrypted VPN-based surveys by the [Group for Analyzing and Measuring Attitudes in Iran](https://gamaan.org/survey-reports/), tells a story of ideological bankruptcy. (There are unavoidable selection biases at work in these polls, but they’re the best data we have, and they’re in line with a great many other data points about the deep shifts in Iranian public opinion in recent years, not least the mass protests and the murderous crackdowns they drew from the regime.) After the 12-day war with Israel, support for Khamenei and the principles of the 1979 revolution collapsed to 11 percent. Seventy percent of respondents said they wanted the regime gone. Nearly two-thirds said [the war with Israel last year](https://gamaan.org/2025/11/05/12-day-war-survey-english/) had nothing to do with Iran’s national interests—it was the regime’s war, not theirs. Sixty-nine percent want the regime to stop calling for the destruction of Israel—not because they love Israel, but because the Muqawama’s forever war has gutted the country they actually live in.

What has followed is something that looks less like ordinary protest and more like the early stages of an ideological revolution; a counterrevolution. Protesters in the streets of Tehran in early 2026 chanted slogans that read like a precise surgical dismantling of Khomeini’s founding arguments. “Neither Gaza nor Lebanon, my life for Iran”—a direct repudiation of Khomeini’s insistence that Iranian nationalism was “idolatry,” that Islam had no borders, that Iran’s wealth existed to fund the global resistance. “Our enemy is right here; they lie that it’s America,” protesters shouted. In the Grand Bazaar, traders chanted: “They take our bread to buy rockets for Lebanon.”

The currency, the Iranian rial, is in free fall. Water and electricity shortages are chronic. The regime’s response to mass protests has been to shut down the internet and kill tens of thousands in the street. The constant internet shutdowns are gutting the freelance economy that young Iranians depend on for dollar-denominated income. And the population blames the regime for this crash, not the West. The regime that claimed to represent the people is impoverishing the people to sustain a military empire the people never wanted.

And the young are not just angry. They are rewriting the Iranian story. On encrypted platforms, in clandestine online networks connecting Tehran, Beirut, and Baghdad, a generation of young Iranians are producing parodies and “counter-epics,” retellings of the Muqawama’s martyrs as figures who sacrificed their people’s future for a mirage of empire.

They have begun to film themselves dancing in place of the traditional Shia mourning ceremonies. They take Shariati’s own critique of “black Shi’ism”—the stagnant, passive, state-serving religion—and turn it on the Islamic Republic itself: You are the new Safavid court. You are the oppressor. The protesters dying in Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) prisons are the true Hussein. The commanders who kill them are the new Yazid, Hussein’s enemy.

This is not a fringe protest. This is the ideological immune system of a society rejecting a harmful pathogen. The Muqawama promised to redeem the weak. It has become the instrument of their degradation. The gap between the promise and the reality has become impossible to paper over.

Understanding all of this reshapes the strategic question entirely. The question is not whether to strike Iran’s nuclear and missile infrastructure; that is necessary and inevitable. The question is whether strikes alone can accomplish anything durable, or whether they will simply give the regime another martyrdom narrative to hide behind.

The Muqawama was designed to absorb military punishment and convert it into moral capital. Every bomb that falls without a corresponding collapse of the regime’s credibility is a bomb the regime can point to and say: See how they hate us, see how we suffer and endure, see how we are the true heirs of Hussein. Military strikes must be embedded in a broader campaign to make each blow land as evidence of hypocrisy rather than evidence of Western aggression.

That means, first, the pragmatic instruments of financial strangulation—not as a background measure but as a primary weapon. The IRGC is structured less like a military and more like a multinational conglomerate. It controls the majority of Iran’s oil and gas infrastructure, operates shadow banking networks, and funds its proxies through Hawala money transfer systems that bypass conventional banking. Hezbollah runs hospitals and schools—not out of generosity but to purchase political loyalty. Designate the entire Muqawama axis as a transnational criminal organization. Intercept ships. Blacklist banks. Disrupt currency auctions in Iraq that launder IRGC revenue. Force the regime to choose, again and again, between funding its war machine and feeding its people—and make sure Iranians know who is making that choice.

The Muqawama was always going to end this way.

Second, and more fundamentally: Attack the story. The Muqawama’s leaders have grown extraordinarily rich. They live in villas. Their children attend Western universities and post about their lavish lifestyles from Dubai. A top Iranian official’s daughter was [recently married](https://www.iranintl.com/en/202510192118) in a revealing white Western-style wedding dress—while the regime forces women into hijabs under threat of violence. These details go viral in Iran. They should go viral everywhere. Leak the bank records. Publish the property deeds. Amplify the children’s Instagram accounts. The luxury paradox—rank-and-file fighters and ordinary citizens told to embrace poverty while commanders accumulate wealth—is the spear at the heart of the entire moral architecture.

What if God really does exact a cost from the arrogant and lift his protection from the powerful, and thus renders fragile and vulnerable the corrupt and wealthy regime in Tehran? This isn’t just a Western critique of a tyrant’s incompetence; in “Red Shi’ism” terms, it’s a genuine theological crisis.

Third, and most importantly: Take the Iranian people seriously as a strategic actor, not merely as a victim. The protesters are not merely asking for Western intervention. They are dismantling the Muqawama’s legitimacy from the inside, using its own theological vocabulary against it. That process needs to be protected and enabled, not exploited. We must provide the communications infrastructure that lets them organize. We should build the decentralized financial tools that let them survive outside the regime’s economic grip. Treat the new revolution they are calling for as a primary allied force, not a secondary consideration.

While the Western Generation Z, adrift and uneducated, often flirts with the very ideas that built Iran’s architecture of oppression and mass murder, their Iranian counterparts have flipped Shariati’s script. They have taken Red Shi’ism’s call for rebellion against the stagnant, corrupt, state-serving religious establishment and applied it to the Islamic Republic itself. This is not a Western idea being imposed on Iran. It is Iran’s own revolutionary tradition turning, finally, on the revolution.

The Muqawama was always going to end this way. A doctrine that defines victory as never acknowledging defeat, that treats the suffering of its own people as sacred fuel, that can only sustain itself through escalating oppression—such a doctrine contains its own destruction. It cannot build. It cannot govern. It can only tear down, and when there is nothing left to tear down but its own people, it turns on them.

The Islamic Republic has been a catastrophe for Iran, for its economy, its culture, its talent, its women, its future. The brightest Persian minds are either refugees or martyrs. The regime has spent 47 years calling itself a revolution and produced nothing but a more bloody and totalitarian apparatus of control than any that country had ever known. It is not, as some Western analysts lazily suggest, simply irrational or backward. It is a sophisticated ideological project that has stolen the worst ideas of the 20th century—Marxist-Leninist vanguardism, Fanonian redemptive violence, Maoist people’s war—and fused them to a version of Shia theology that previous generations of Shia would not recognize.

And it is failing. The Muqawama has lost its moral immunity in every polity that was supposed to be its base. It has no answer to the one thing its subjects have finally decided they want: a life. Not martyrdom. Not the grave. A coffee shop, a future, a country that works.

When Khomeini said that putting “Iran first” was idolatry, he was trying to conscript an entire civilization into an endless war it never agreed to fight. The protesters shouting “My life for Iran” are refusing the conscription. They are not just demanding a change of government. They are demanding the death of an ideology—because they understand that the ideology and the country’s survival are incompatible.

The West’s task is not to save Iran. Iranians are already trying to do just that, at enormous personal cost, with extraordinary courage. The task is to make sure the regime cannot massacre its way back to stability. And to make sure that when it falls, what follows is not another iteration of the same.

The humble, as the Psalmist wrote and the Quran agreed, shall inherit the land. In Iran, they are already reaching for it.
