OpenAI’s Sora video generation tool was hailed as a breakthrough when it was introduced in 2024. Journalists called it “ [jaw-dropping](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/10/02/technology/openai-sora-video-app.html),” especially in its later versions. Disney signed a multiyear deal with OpenAI for Sora to use its characters.

But on Tuesday, OpenAI canceled Sora. One view is that [this is evidence](https://slate.com/technology/2026/03/ai-openai-sam-altman-disney-sora-shutdown.html) of AI getting overhyped, and OpenAI is retreating from an area in which it had failed to find success and fallen behind competitors like Google and ByteDance. It’s being trotted out as an example of how AI is not living up to its promise.

I have a different view.

The leading AI companies need to maximize their computing resources. OpenAI appears to be dropping some of its ventures (including plans for an [adult erotic chatbot](https://www.ft.com/content/de9bf0af-b241-424f-8229-5870b1c0d93d?syn-25a6b1a6=1), which it also abandoned on Thursday) to direct scarce “compute” (the industry neologism for computing power) to the more important tasks of making the top models truly superb at problem-solving and also at recursive self-improvement. They are quickly getting closer to the goal of reaching what is sometimes called “artificial general intelligence,” and I expect both OpenAI and Anthropic, and possibly Google, to make major progress within the calendar year.

That AI skeptics would jump on the end of Sora as a sign of AI failure is not a surprise. It’s a natural reaction for many of those faced with the technology: People are starting to get nervous. They are trying to deny the reality of an AI-drenched future that, every day, is more clearly on the way.

Most people just do not like the idea that they will have to radically change how they work, and that they will have to specialize in different skills and master new workflows.

And so the excuses are proliferating.

At first we were told that the large language models hallucinated too much to ever be useful. But then the rate of hallucinations plunged.

Then we heard that the models were mere “ [stochastic parrots](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3442188.3445922),” capable only of stringing words together and incapable of creativity. But recently they have started proving mathematical theorems and coming up with new scientific results. An AI scientific discovery start-up, Periodic Labs, is now in talks to possibly be [valued at $7 billion](https://x.com/nmasc_/status/2036813408864207322). That is because of its potential to generate new scientific ideas.

Most people just do not like the idea that they will have to radically change how they work. And so the excuses are proliferating.

Yes, there are skills AIs haven’t mastered. But if your skill still appears to be the exclusive province of humans, that might mean the major AI companies do not yet consider it very important to master right away. Eventually it will rise to the top of the list.

Don’t worry; these advances will not destroy all our jobs, because only humans can operate effectively in the physical world and most jobs still require that. OpenAI, for one, is looking to hire thousands of people, in large part [to help companies](https://x.com/EpochAIResearch/status/2036882915305677307) incorporate AI into their workflows. The more accurate prediction is that strong AI will change virtually every job. To the extent the job involves brainpower, you will end up working with the AIs.

One view you should take seriously these days: You will not lose your job to an AI, but you may lose it to someone who knows how to work the AI better than you do.

If your skill still appears to be the exclusive province of humans, that might mean the major AI companies do not yet consider it very important to master right away.

A whole new dimension of economic competition is opening up. No matter how well or poorly you may fare in that, to many people it feels like a new headache at a time when life already is stressful.

Sam Altman recently [stated that](https://www.theinformation.com/articles/openai-ceo-shifts-responsibilities-preps-spud-ai-model) OpenAI has a new model coming, it is expected to be a “very strong model” that will “really accelerate the economy,” and that “things are moving faster than many of us expected.” A company division [has been renamed](https://x.com/kimmonismus/status/2036547535272296516) “AGI deployment,” referring to the achievement of a very powerful artificial general intelligence. Anthropic maintains a no less torrid pace of advance, even if their headlines are more concerned with their recent spat with the Pentagon.

To be sure, CEOs have been known to exaggerate the virtues of their companies. But here the timelines are so short, and new AI models come under such intense scrutiny, that the wisest thing to do is to heed these predictions. Recent AI systems have [accelerated their pace of advance](https://www.thefp.com/p/a-day-that-will-go-down-in-history), using recursive self-improvement, with no obvious end to that process in sight. Keep in mind for any current model you are observing, there are superior models that already exist and are just not yet ready for release for reasons of safety or cost.

The best models, such as Claude 4.6 and GPT 5.4 Pro, are now so good that outright skepticism or denialism is difficult to sustain, and AI’s coding capabilities have risen faster yet. What I am seeing is partial recognition of what is happening, a lot of “copium,” and then a focus on the shortcomings of AI circa early 2026. Most people are not working hard to think through the rate of progress and what that will mean for their jobs.

One reaction is to try to regulate AI out of existence. That’s the approach of AI critics like Vermont senator Bernie Sanders. I disagree with him on both politics and economics, but he does understand that AIs are steadily and rapidly getting better and will upend the labor market. That’s one reason he’s calling for a moratorium on data centers.

That doesn’t seem like a very promising answer. I would rather adapt to the new world than try to halt a major scientific revolution in its tracks.

These days, I think less and less about a person’s political persuasion and [more about their AI stance](https://x.com/deanwball/status/2028619280774828114). Are they outright denialists? Are they conceding the rapid-pace AI progress but trying to cope by minimizing the significance and avoiding looking into the future? Or are they doing everything they can, individually and socially, to adapt to one of the biggest scientific revolutions in human history?

That last category is the right one to be in. But in terms of both workload and psychological adjustment, it is also the hardest.
