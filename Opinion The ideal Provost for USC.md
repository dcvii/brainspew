*On June 22 <sup>nd</sup>, USC’s Provost office circulated an email from the co-chairs of the Provost Search Committee, USC Trustee David Bohnett and immediate past Dean of the Viterbi School of Engineering, Professor Yannis Yortsos. The note **[solicited](https://www.research.net/r/uscprovostsearch)** community input on search priorities as they select the next Provost.* ***[Jim Moore](https://viterbi.usc.edu/directory/faculty/Moore/James)**, Professor Emeritus of Industrial and Systems Engineering, provides his recommendations.*

**What do you see as the key priorities and objectives for the incoming Provost?**

I am a tactical creature, so I will be specific. The incoming Provost should:

1. Focus on merit in student admissions, faculty hiring, and faculty promotion. Overtly and publicly abandon any identity criteria. Repudiate equity, diversity, and inclusion programs and the race grifters who advocate for these activities. Openly support viewpoint diversity. Declare institutional neutrality. Reject social justice and commit to the pursuit of asocial justice. Adopt the **[Chicago Statement](https://www.fire.org/research-learn/adopting-chicago-statement)**. Commit to merit, due process, and equal opportunity in all matters that touch the Provost’s role. In short, he or she should cut through the shit and break with the rest of the tattered remnants of the American academy.
2. End the institution’s undeclared war on tenure. Only a quarter of USC’s full-time faculty are tenured or probationary: Focus on tenure-track hiring. Resist adding to the oversupply of special faculty. The university asserts that these non-tenure-track faculty have academic freedom, but this is a lie. Special faculty work on fixed-term contracts. They are easy to remove as their contracts expire, making them easy for the administration to control.
3. Focus on cutting administrative bloat at the school and central levels. Too many of the 900+ souls USC laid off improved the USC experience for students and faculty. Too few were the fruits of years of internal empire-building. Here are some examples:
	1. The University has a Chief Financial Officer/Senior Vice President for Finance, and a Senior Vice President for Administration, one of whom is actually competent. We do not need more parasites, so let go of the new Executive Vice President for Finance and Operations to whom these existing Senior Vice Presidents would report.
		2. The former Vice Dean for Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion in the Price School of Public Policy is now the Vice Dean of Student Success. Neither label describes a real function. Eliminate the position.
		3. The Viterbi School of Engineering has 10 Vice Deans (maybe 9, since the thoroughly academically unqualified Vice Dean for Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion fled to the Rossier School of Education). The School needs between two and a half and three administrators in these roles. Eliminate the other positions. They exist to extend the Dean’s professional reach, not to serve the faculty or students.
4. Disband the Rossier School of Education, and place the faculty at liberty. Schools of Education are the intellectual ghettos of the academy, and the Rossier School is no exception.
5. Reinstitute USC’s intercenter bank and commit to the principles of Revenue Center Management as the organizing paradigm for both USC’s finances and central decision-making. If RCM procedures had been in place, USC would have been positioned to weather the financial challenges of the pandemic and the unfortunate set of priorities that burdened the head of former President Carol Folt.

**After three to five years, how will you know the new Provost is successful? What will have happened?**

If the incoming Provost pursues the priorities above and related ones, they will be successful in furthering USC’s interests. If he or she proceeds otherwise, then they are part of the problem.

I am having trouble remembering the last time we had a Provost who served for five years in their role. It was probably Lloyd Armstrong, who should not have. People look back wistfully at USC President Stephen Sample’s administration. I am more wistful about Cornelius Pings’ work as Provost. Pings was an administrator who did not mince words, knew how to say “No,” and when to say “Yes.”

**What do you think are the essential qualifications and experiences of an ideal candidate?**

1. The essential qualifications for the incoming Provost are largely the same as for a University President. First and foremost, he or she should be a faculty member and an accomplished scholar. The Provost’s faculty role is especially important because USC President Beong-Soo Kim is a part-time faculty member at the USC Gould School of Law, which means he is only a faculty member in the narrowest, most limited sense. “Academic staff-teaching” would be a more accurate label than “faculty member.” Faculty members are grateful for President Kim’s work to date, but the jury is still out on whether they will ever take a staff member seriously as a President or Provost. The President’s position has an external aspect that the Provost’s does not, so there is a rationale for an experienced, sophisticated staff member in the President’s role. USC has one, and the institution will greatly violate faculty’s expectations (yet again) if the incoming Provost were not a prominent, tenured scholar.
2. He or she should have administrative vision. In USC’s case, this means a commitment to Revenue Center Management, Responsibility Center Management, or a similar management framework. RCM makes large organizations transparent. It identifies the trade-offs needed to meet goals and parameterizes the opportunity costs of maintaining those goals.
3. They should recognize and subvert identity-based virtue signaling among their subordinates in all quarters, never rewarding it.
4. They should be risk-tolerant and hungry to make a difference by positioning the University of Southern California as a place that rejects the pervasive, so-called progressive institutional characteristics and intellectual homogeneity that have bankrupted the public’s faith in academe.
5. The incoming Provost should genuinely respect the student affairs function, but their experiences to date should not have focused primarily on that professional sphere. School of education graduates largely populate Student Affairs offices.
6. He or she should not hold any academic credentials from a school of education.
7. The incoming Provost should resist the President’s inevitable efforts to turn them into a fundraiser. They should keep their focus internal and should be allowed to do so. Let the President torture the Deans over fundraising. Provosts have more than enough to do just maintaining quality and solvency. USC’s success in either dimension would be a refreshing change of pace.

**What are the personal qualities and attributes most important for a person in this role to have?**

Intelligence, utter fearlessness, drive, a tolerance for levels of tedium that would be lethal to most humans, and a crystal-clear understanding of the market paradigm and the crucial role of markets, i.e., what markets deliver for society, how they deliver for society, and why they deliver.

I taught for 35 years in the Viterbi School of Engineering, including the 21 during which Yannis Yortsos served as the Dean, and I know his mind better than he likely prefers. Unfortunately, these are not criteria that I think he likes.

I do not know David Bohnett at all, so I reviewed the Bohnett Foundation website, and the following language struck me: “Our Mission: David Bohnett and the David Bohnett Foundation are committed to improving society through social activism.” Social activism is an unfortunate institutional focus, which makes me suspicious of Mr. Bohnett’s judgment and, by extension, the quality of this search.

I would be much more optimistic about this search process if the Bohnett Foundation’s focus were more like that of the **[Foundation for Individual Rights and Expression](https://www.fire.org/)**, the **[National Association of Scholars](https://www.nas.org/)**, the **[Manhattan Institute](https://manhattan.institute/)**, the **[Academic Freedom Alliance](https://academicfreedom.org/)**, the **[Heterodox Academy](https://heterodoxacademy.org/)**, or the **[James Martin Center for Academic Renewal](https://jamesgmartin.center/)**. The advice David Bohnett, Professor Yortsos, and their search committee need most is the body of work collectively provided by these and similar organizations.
