Sebastian Maniscalco helped crown comedy’s arena-tour era.

Since the late 2010s, he has routinely performed in venues intended for pro-sports crowds. In 2024 he set a record as the first comic with five straight shows at Madison Square Garden. Last year he sold $43.5 million worth of tickets to rank as the second-highest grossing comedian on tour, according to Pollstar.

Maniscalco pulls audiences in with contortionist levels of body language that he uses to demonstrate his observations of absurd human behavior, from splitting dinner tabs with Venmo to taping our mouths shut for sleep.

In April he joined the comedy lineup on SiriusXM with his own channel, featuring a weekly talk show, “The Sebastian Maniscalco Show,” and curated clips from other stand-up acts.

Maniscalco, 52, lives in Los Angeles with his wife and two children. Here, he talks comedy injuries, horseplay with Justin Bieber and the most surprising boost to his profile: the hit impression of him that “Saturday Night Live” cast member Marcello Hernández debuted on the show last year.

**What’s your morning routine when you’re not on the road?**

Like a lot of people I look at social media for how to be a productive individual. I was setting my alarm for 4:30 in the morning to work out and get everything out of the way. Then I said to myself, why the hell am I getting up when I really don’t have to? I pushed my workouts to later, and now I get up when my body tells me to, usually around 6:30. I generally have two cups of coffee, then the whole family gets in the car and goes to school. I have two kids, 7 and 9 years old. That 22-minute drive is great because they’re strapped in. They can’t move. You can talk to them.

**Any other morning hacks you’ve tried?**

I do a steam for 20 minutes then jump in the cold plunge. My dad is like, “I’ve never done any of this and I’m living til 80.” I go, “Yeah, but you look like a crustacean.” The guy is a mule. He’s still working as a hair stylist. He’s going to die giving somebody a dye job.

**Physicality defines your style on stage. Have you injured yourself telling jokes?**

Twice. In Detroit, around 2017, as I was leaving the stage I kind of leaned back and popped my calf. I had to limp off and then be in a wheelchair the next day at the airport. Then, a year and a half ago, I snapped the other calf doing a joke at the Wynn in Las Vegas. This was mid-set and I mentioned it to the crowd because I had to be stationary. It’s not like I’m playing football, but when you’re jumping and leaping and doing this, that and the other on stage, then going to the next city, sleeping in a different bed, it all wears and tears on your body.

**How does it feel to have the channel next to Howard Stern’s on the SiriusXM dial?**

Howard is the god of radio, so being there is a little daunting. I’m not saying I’m a radio personality, but I’ve had a podcast for 13 years. It was \[co-host\] Pete Correale and I discussing our personal and professional lives, but now the show is more guest driven. I want the channel to be a platform for young comedians, and as it grows I’ll give it a more personal touch by infusing the comedy with music, plus voice memos for little thoughts that might be bigger bits moving forward.

**How did you end voicing the hook on a dance song from DJ Steve Aoki?**

The title is actually something I said in my head when I was going to nightclubs in my 30s. I would look at these girls and think, “Gee, does your father know you dance like that?” and it just kind of came out in the studio. I’m 52 years old. I’m not a young guy but I don’t want to get pigeonholed. Steve Aoki is like 48 and he’s still jumping around on stage with his shirt off. So you know what? Let me get out there and test the waters.

**Is that the attitude that landed you on Justin Bieber’s livestream last fall?**

I was extremely nervous doing that because that’s really out of the box for me. But when the request came in—Bieber wants to carve pumpkins with you at his playhouse—I was like, let’s see what that turns into. We hung out in a ball pit. I actually hurt myself in that thing because I didn’t know how deep it was. I kind of went in head first and it felt like I broke my sternum.

**How has Marcello Hernández’s impression of you on “Saturday Night Live” boomeranged back to you?**

I’ve been doing comedy for 28 years, but I don’t know exactly where I land in the popularity scale of pop culture. So when he first told me he was going to do the impression, I was like, “Are people even going to know it’s me, or will they think this is a new character you came up with?” I’ve found that when I get on an airplane I’m getting noticed a little bit more now. Is that because of Marcello or my last special? I don’t know.

**What advice would you give someone trying to make it in show business?**

Stay on your own path. It’s easy to compare yourself to others and say, “When is it my turn?” But the only thing you have control over is being the best at what you do. I had to perform at a dry cleaner’s for four people. I had to go to a restaurant in Santa Barbara and stand on a crate while people were eating chicken parm. There were things you needed to do as a comedian back then that aren’t as prevalent today because people are using social media as a launching pad, but you can’t fake it.
