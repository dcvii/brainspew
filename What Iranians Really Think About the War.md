Events in Iran are unfolding across my screen. The flash of air strikes followed by rising plumes of smoke—now the country’s bleak daily rhythm—streams remorselessly onto my feed. Mullahs howl. Politicians gesticulate. Washington and Jerusalem threaten and promise. Fragments of Persian, half learned and half forgotten, return to me. *Gar sabr koni ze ghure halva sazi* —“If you are patient, you can make halvah from sour grapes”: encouraging words once offered by my long-suffering Persian teacher as I butchered verb conjugations. Now I cautiously wonder if the phrase might be apt. Are we seeing the possible—if not immediate—end of a regime that has held one of the world’s oldest civilizations hostage for nearly half a century?

But then my mind flicks to darker memories: the sadistic crackdowns and the long history of crushed uprisings. I watch Iran strike its Gulf neighbors. Another proverb surfaces: *Mar-e zakhmi khatarnak-tar ast* —“A wounded snake is more dangerous.”

Twenty years ago, I lived in the foreigners section of the student dormitories (*Kuye Daneshgah*) of Tehran University, a dense complex of concrete blocks just off Enghelab Street where arrivals from across Iran crowded into functional rooms for the duration of their studies. Like almost everywhere in the country, the regime’s violence had struck these buildings. Only a few years earlier, security forces and Basij militia had stormed them during the 1999 student protests, beating them in the corridors and throwing some off balconies. As an older student told me matter-of-factly one day: *Inja bud*. It was here.

The government as a functioning entity doesn’t really exist anymore. Things are falling apart.

Two decades on, the violence has never really stopped. In January, the regime murdered around 30,000 of its own people; weeks later, the United States and Israel began pounding its facilities, trying, finally, to end it for good. For now, Iranians can only wait. Many hope the war will not last, and that it might herald the birth of something better. In the meantime, explosions are constant. Tehran’s night skies flare with ordnance as cell phones light up with rumor. So far the strikes have largely hit regime facilities, although 153 people, including children, were killed when a [reported U.S. strike](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/03/08/world/middleeast/iran-minab-school-strike.html) hit a school in southern Iran.

As the war rolls on, I try to speak to the people who matter most in all this: Iranians. These days, checking in on those I care about is a valuable exercise in more ways than one.Only by understanding what is happening inside the country, and in the minds of its people, can we begin to judge whether this war—and the colossal gamble it represents—has any chance of succeeding.

Yesterday, I spoke to a friend in Tehran I will call Reza. “The bombing is heavy,” he told me. “And it’s frightening. But we have been frightened and terrorized for almost 50 years.”

Daily life continues—as it must, Reza said. The roads are emptier than usual. He and his family are trying to stay indoors as much as possible. “We need to buy food, and I go out for that, but nothing more. So far there are thankfully no shortages, but how long that will continue we don’t know.”

Mehdi Yahyanejad is an activist and tech entrepreneur who founded Balatarin.com, a Persian-language news-sharing social media site. He is now based in Los Angeles and also helps smuggle Starlink terminals into Iran. Yahyanejad’s efforts to keep Iranians connected are more vital than ever: The regime has imposed yet another internet blackout, which only fuels fear and paranoia. “Most people are disconnected,” he tells me. The state is in disarray. Senior officials are scattered and confused. “The government as a functioning entity doesn’t really exist anymore. Things are falling apart,” he said. “Many offices are running at about 20 percent capacity just to keep services going.”

Airstrikes have hammered Tehran, Isfahan, and other major cities, although they appear largely targeted at the regime’s coercive infrastructure: Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) and army facilities, police stations, and so on. So far, Reza tells me, people generally believe that Jerusalem and Washington are doing what they can to avoid civilian casualties. This is welcome news, not just in itself but because continuing support from at least a majority of Iranians is critical for a successful outcome to the war. Over the weekend, Israel began targeting oil storage depots. Thick black clouds billowed over cityscapes. Air pollution got bad, visible in the grime that covered many cars the morning after.

Most Iranians I have spoken to doubt there will be a full-scale invasion, though they don’t discount limited ground operations. The deeper concern is what comes after. Some fear ethnic fragmentation, Kurdish or Baluchi uprisings that could rip the country apart. For the moment, President Donald Trump seems to have backtracked on the idea of the Kurds as a form of “boots on the ground.” He told reporters aboard Air Force One: “I don’t want the Kurds to go into Iran.... The war is complicated enough as it is.”

We don’t want a civil war that destroys the country. And that would only benefit the regime anyway.

Everyone is worrying about what comes next. Over the weekend, the Assembly of Experts, the body tasked with selecting Iran’s new supreme leader, chose Mojtaba Khamenei, the former leader’s son. This was dismaying among those I speak to. “What’s the point of all this if we just get more of the same?” says a young mother that I have been in contact with since the January protests; I’ll call her Shireen. Down the road, depending on the success of the war, there is the possibility that exiled [crown prince Reza Pahlavi](https://www.thefp.com/p/reza-pahlavi-irans-reluctant-prince) could return. According to Yahyanejad, he commands wide support, but his restoration could aggravate ethnic tensions. There could also be some kind of governance by a combination of opposition figures and technocrats from within the regime, but this idea remains vague.

Above all, people fear either a shattered state that remains under sanctions, a war that drags on for months, or the unraveling of Iran itself. As Reza concludes: “We don’t want a civil war that destroys the country. And that would only benefit the regime, anyway.”

No one I am speaking to has reported any signs of mass uprising—it’s just too dangerous. “The regime is as focused on making sure we don’t try to rise up as it is on fighting the Americans and Israelis,” Shireen says.

The entire regime is now on a war footing. IRGC commanders appear on state TV to threaten any potential protesters—and their families. “Their words are working,” says Shireen. “People still hate the regime, but we don’t want our children killed. We hope that maybe it will fall. But it doesn’t look like it yet.”

Gangs of Basij enforcers, often heavily armed, roam the streets, threatening civilians and forcing them indoors. Anyone who looks like they might be thinking of protesting is threatened or beaten or worse. There are checkpoints across every city. Known protesters are scrutinized even as they move through the streets to get basic necessities.

Despite the regime’s grip on the streets, it’s known that there are serious fractures within its leadership. Since the war began, I have also been speaking to Western security officials. Almost all have a regular flow of information coming out of Iran; some have even penetrated the Iranian security services so deeply that they receive information about Iranian military decision-making almost in real time. The picture they paint of the regime is that of a system still capable of repression, but increasingly fragmented behind its facade of wartime unity. Senior figures have scattered for security reasons, communications are strained, and discipline at the top is crumbling. “Decision-making is accordingly confused,” one Western source who is across the intelligence on Irantells me.

At the same time, an internal power struggle appears to be under way. Ali Khamenei was a powerful leader who centralized authority around himself. His death has created a vacuum. The choice of his son to replace him was not a surprise—though perhaps it should have been. The Islamic Republic is a revolutionary regime that once prided itself on its opposition to hereditary rule. Yet such is the nature of power: Those who overthrow monarchs and dictators often come to resemble them.

He will have a tough job on his hands. First, Israel had already threatened to hunt down whoever succeeded Khamenei. Second, there is chaos among the regime’s different factions. “There is major infighting with the IRGC increasingly acting on its own authority and trying to take control over the regime leadership,” the same source tells me. What is more, they appear to be succeeding. “The Revolutionary Guard is making decisions without regard to the central leadership in Iran, which is angering other factions. There are many signs that they are in de facto control.” Given Mojtaba’s closeness to the corps, their position is only likely to strengthen further now.

“The regime is as focused on making sure we don’t try to rise up as it is on fighting the Americans and Israelis.”—Shireen, a young mother

A transitional leadership council has attempted to coordinate state responses, but its authority appears fragile. “It is trying to unify efforts,” my source says, “but the Revolutionary Guard leadership is not listening.”

These tensions are beginning to surface publicly. On Saturday, Iranian president Masoud Pezeshkian went on state TV [to apologize](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HW5VC08tNMw) to Iran’s gulf neighbors for its attacks on them. “I personally apologize to neighboring countries that were affected by Iran’s actions,” he said, before urging them not to join U.S.-Israeli attacks on Iran.

But he was swiftly contradicted by figures close to the IRGC. Messages circulating on [IRGC-linked Telegram channels](https://x.com/DrEliDavid/status/2030290561861562844) dismissed the president’s remarks, saying he had “made a mistake” and urging readers to ignore his words while Iranian strikes continued.

The hard-line outlet *Raja News*, closely aligned with the IRGC, [attacked](https://x.com/RZimmt/status/2030248161072001512?utm_) Pezeshkian in an editorial titled “Apologizing in the Middle of War: A Burden on the Nation’s Spirit in the Middle of War—Take the Microphone Away from Pezeshkian.” It accused him of projecting weakness and undermining Iran’s claim to legitimate self-defense. Wartime messaging, it insisted, should come only from figures who project strength—at least until a new Supreme Leader is chosen.

On Saturday, Ali Larijani, the secretary of Iran’s Supreme National Security Council and the most powerful man in Iran at the time, [told](https://x.com/IranIntl_En/status/2030384738163204441?ref_src=twsrc%5Etfw%7Ctwcamp%5Etweetembed%7Ctwterm%5E2030384738163204441%7Ctwgr%5E4e6a199efaa1ebf8bc2a67647b8882db68eac94f%7Ctwcon%5Es1_&ref_url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.iranintl.com%2Fen%2F202603079244) state TV that the Islamic Republic “will not leave \[Trump\] alone” after his killing of Khamenei.

Critically, though, my securitysources suggest that the regime’s broad propaganda efforts have not wholly succeeded. “The regime is conducting a propaganda operation to spin the narrative of U.S.-Israeli aggression.... Yet the effort is only partially successful,” says one source. “The educated class, students, and protesters are not buying it.”

Despite the Islamic Republic’s best efforts, the battle for Iran—by both external powers and Iranians inside the country—is far from over. It may take time, but I am convinced this regime will fall. As I read messages from those who have resisted it for years and watch events unfold online, another Persian expression comes to mind: *Zolm pāyedar nist.* “Oppression does not endure.”
