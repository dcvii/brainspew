Lest you think the New York Times’s decision to publish a now-debunked claim about “dog rape” in Israeli prisons is limited to Nicholas Kristof’s orbit, here is a statement by the New York Times spokesperson responding to rumors that retractions in Kristof’s piece might be coming.

The spokesperson said, “there is no truth to this” rumor and strangely points to Kristof’s Pulitzers from 1990 and 2006 as some sort of prop to deflect from accountability. What’s more, the spokesperson pointed to “victims’ own words” backed by independent studies.

I want to talk about that.

As I posted on here, Kristof’s article claiming “dog rape” actually linked to a random X user named Shaiel Ben-Ephraim. Ben-Ephraim, who himself has been accused of sexual harassment while at UCLA, in turn linked to reports from organizations linked to Hamas and the PFLP, both designated terrorist organizations. Are these the “independent studies” New York Times is referring to?

Ben-Ephraim has also expressed a deep hatred for Israel in the past. For instance on May 4, he posted “I do not support the existence of Israel.” After Kristof’s article was written, Shaiel celebrated and characterized Kristof’s report as a “massive step towards the end of Israel.”

Why did Kristof think it was appropriate for the New York Times to link to this person for its evidence of such a serious allegation?

Now let’s see if the New York Times is telling the truth about “victims’ own words” and “independent studies.”

Based on my review of this allegation, the dog rape claim originated from a 25-year-old lawyer from Gaza, who supposedly claimed to Euro-Med Human Rights Monitor that he witnessed this happening to a different person while he was detained by Israel. According to Israel, Euro-Med’s leader Ramy Abdu is one of Hamas’s main operatives so the claim warranted additional scrutiny to begin with. Interestingly, yesterday Mr. Abdu celebrated Kristof’s piece and taunted Israeli sexual assault victims of October 7th, claiming they did not give “credible testimonies.” This is a person behind a study the New York Times considers an “independent study”?

Because the claim from Euro-Med was so bizarre and unusual, experts across many publications and communities took it seriously and investigated it. And there is a reason this claim was thoroughly debunked and never made it into ANY major publication before it ended up in Kristof’s column.

What were the findings of these investigations?

First, experts said what was being claimed by Euro-Med is biologically and behaviorally impossible for an animal to do. What’s more, when that same lawyer discussed his prison experience with another anti-Israel agency (B’Tselem), the details were completely different and the lawyer never mentioned the dog claim. More, investigators couldn’t find any details of that other person’s existence. There were no medical records or forensic evidence that he exists or that any of it occurred.

None of this seemed to matter to Kristof. None of this made it into his column.

And it is a consistent theme. Other detainees Kristof spoke to gave varying versions of reports depending on who they spoke to. Their claims appeared to be fantastical and unsubstantiated too. Kristof reported the unsubstantiated versions regardless.

But then there’s also the timing.

According to Israel’s Foreign Ministry, the Civil Commission investigating Hamas’s systematic sexual violence on October 7 approached the New York Times with their findings. The New York Times reportedly said it was not interested.

“Aware of the report and its release date, the night before its release the NYT ran a shameful attack on Israel, belittling Hamas’ sexual crimes,” the Foreign Ministry added.

The New York Times denied this, but when a paper stands behind Kristof’s seemingly libelous and debunked claims, how do we know when they’re telling the truth?

In any case, the fact that the New York Times stood behind a story that clearly didn’t meet evidentiary standards of journalism at the most basic level speaks to serious and concerning problems at the paper.

If you can’t tell, the fact that the New York Times ran this story truly shocked me.
