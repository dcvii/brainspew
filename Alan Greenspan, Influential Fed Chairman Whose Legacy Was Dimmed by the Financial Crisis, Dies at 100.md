Alan Greenspan, who started life as a shy, only child of a single mother, transcended the role of central banker, [exercising extraordinary influence](https://www.wsj.com/articles/SB110072933456177155?mod=article_inline) over American finance and commerce and enjoying unparalleled global stature during a period of remarkable prosperity. But by the time of his death, at age 100, the 2008-09 global financial crisis had recast his aura of technocratic omnipotence.

Former colleagues and Nobel Laureate Milton Friedman [hailed Greenspan as the greatest central banker](https://www.wsj.com/articles/SB112527984490925254?mod=article_inline) of all time [when he finished 18½ years](https://www.wsj.com/articles/SB113867954176960734?mod=article_inline) as [Federal Reserve](https://www.wsj.com/topics/subject/federal-reserve) chairman in 2006. Just two years later, the financial crisis triggered an austere reappraisal of his record. His view that markets could effectively police themselves [became a driving force of regulatory policy](https://www.wsj.com/articles/SB110081981338978661?mod=article_inline) in the 1990s and early 2000s.

Greenspan devoted his professional life to understanding how the U.S. economy worked, first as a private-sector forecaster when America was emerging as the economic superpower following World War II. Later, as technology, global trade and financialization refashioned business, he achieved a level of fame and influence rivaled only by U.S. presidents. Greenspan, the second-longest-serving Fed chairman (just behind William McChesney Martin, 1951-1970), husbanded that power to build up the central bank’s political independence.

Greenspan was an enigma: a savvy political operator and ladies’ man who was also an introvert. He was a skilled saxophonist in a bebop jazz band who devoured books about titans of finance and industry while his bandmates smoked tobacco and marijuana between sets. An adviser to Republican presidents, he got along famously with President Bill Clinton and his senior economic advisers—all Democrats.

Greenspan is survived by his wife, the journalist Andrea Mitchell. In a statement, she said that Greenspan had died at their home on Monday from complications of Parkinson’s disease.

“He was a giant of a man who helped shape the U.S. economy for decades under presidents of both parties, but was always honest in acknowledging his mistakes,” Mitchell said. “To me he was my husband, who shaped my life from our very first date in 1984.”

### The ‘Greenspan put’

In 1959, the libertarian Greenspan warned of how central banks could allow markets to think the business cycle had been tamed, breeding complacency and risk-taking by investors. His early academic work declared the Fed one of the “historic disasters in American history,” according to Sebastian Mallaby’s biography, “The Man Who Knew.”

But as Fed chairman, he molded the central bank as a bulwark against instability. His perceived inclination to stem market panic earned a moniker, the “Greenspan put”—as if the Fed itself was a put option that insured investors against losses. The Fed cushioned markets after the stock-market crash of 1987 [and the 1998 failure](https://www.wsj.com/articles/SB911168945488412500?mod=article_inline) of Long-Term Capital Management and played a key role arranging a bailout that followed the Mexican debt crisis of 1994.

Greenspan cast a spell over Wall Street whenever he testified on Capitol Hill. With sad brown eyes, he offered drab, wordy pronouncements in a gentle voice. “Since I’ve become a central banker, I’ve learned to mumble with great incoherence,” he quipped at one of his first hearings as Fed chairman in 1987. “If I seem unduly clear to you, you must have misunderstood what I said.”

In the 1990s, he reached rock-star heights as a policymaker while the U.S. charted a long economic expansion following the end of the Cold War. He landed on the cover of Time magazine in 1999, the leading figure in what the magazine dubbed “The Committee to Save the World” after defusing financial crises in Asia and Russia.

Pressed over whether he would keep Greenspan as Fed chairman during a Republican presidential debate in 2000, Arizona Sen. John McCain summed up the national consensus: “Not only would I reappoint him, but if he died we’d prop him up and put sunglasses on him as they did in the movie ‘Weekend at Bernie’s.’ ”

### Craving escape

Alan Greenspan was born on March 6, 1926, in New York, an only child. His father, Herbert, was a self-educated stockbroker. Greenspan’s parents split when he was young, and his mother, Rose, raised him, living with her parents in a cramped apartment in the Washington Heights neighborhood. She made ends meet during the Great Depression by taking a job at a furniture store in the Bronx.

A young Alan didn’t have a close relationship with his father, who had immigrated as Haim Grunspann to Ellis Island in 1906 at age 4. But Alan solidified his dream of a bigger life after accompanying Herbert on a business trip to Chicago in the late 1930s. On the train journey through steel country, he watched with awe the huge industrial furnaces that belched smoke and fire. He began collecting train schedules but didn’t dare tell his mother why: “It was her world that I was escaping,” he wrote in his 2007 memoir.

Greenspan excelled at the clarinet and attended George Washington High School. (Henry Kissinger was a senior during his freshman year, though the two wouldn’t meet for another 30 years.) He wasn’t allowed to join the Army after doctors found a spot on his lung that looked like tuberculosis. Instead, he enrolled in music classes at Juilliard, the prestigious conservatory, and played in dance bands.

After the war, he earned an economics degree at New York University. Many students were enthralled by John Maynard Keynes, whose work was revolutionizing the field. Greenspan declared himself uninterested in economic policy and preferred focusing on technical challenges.

After taking a job at the Conference Board, a business research organization, he rummaged through libraries to unearth measures of railcar freight loading and trends in short staple cotton prices since the Civil War. “Instead of reading ‘Gone with the Wind,’ I was happy to immerse myself in ‘Copper Ore Deposits in Chile,’” he wrote.

A largely self-taught economist, he founded a successful economic forecasting firm in the 1950s. Business clients included Mobil Oil and Alcoa. In 1957, Greenspan burnished his reputation as an analytical whiz when he warned the chief executive of a large steel company that inventories were oversaturated and a recession was likely. The CEO apologized the following year for ignoring Greenspan’s correct prediction.

“Forecasting is simply a projection of how current imbalances will ultimately resolve,” Greenspan later wrote. Companies wouldn’t necessarily “take the word of a 30-year-old kid as to where the economy is going, but they might very well listen to what he thinks are the various balances here and there.”

Business success derailed his studies for an economics Ph.D. at Columbia under Arthur Burns. Greenspan bought a duplex on Long Island that he shared with his mother, as well as a 1959 Buick convertible with red leather seats and tail fins from which he blasted Bach concertos on the expressway. Over the next two decades, Burns would regularly tease Greenspan over how little progress he had made writing a thesis. (Greenspan earned his doctorate in 1977 after NYU accepted a collection of previously published articles as his thesis.)

### ‘The undertaker’ goes to Washington

Though he advised Richard Nixon’s presidential campaign in 1968, Greenspan resisted entreaties to serve in the White House. That is until advisers, chiefly Burns, prevailed on him in 1974. Burns by then had been appointed Fed chairman by Nixon. Greenspan agreed after receiving assurances that Nixon, who was hobbled by the Watergate scandal, wouldn’t resort to new wage-and-price controls. Greenspan hated the measures and had been aghast that so many economists blessed the idea.

His Senate confirmation hearing to serve as chairman of the Council of Economic Advisers, on the afternoon of Aug. 8, 1974, was a few hours before Nixon announced his resignation. During his questioning, Joe Biden, then a young senator from Delaware, applauded Nixon’s choice: “If he picks a conservative, I want him to pick a straight one and a bright one.”

Though Greenspan didn’t know Gerald Ford well, the two men developed a rapport. With high inflation ravaging the economy, Greenspan recoiled when he learned Ford’s advisers planned a grassroots movement to encourage personal savings by passing out buttons that proclaimed, “Whip Inflation Now.” He warned the White House that the whole ploy wouldn’t work: Small-business owners wouldn’t voluntarily forgo price increases because their margins were too thin.

Greenspan’s views had been heavily shaped in the 1960s when he was part of the intellectual circle that gathered every week at the Manhattan apartment of libertarian philosopher Ayn Rand. The two were introduced by a friend of Greenspan’s first wife, Joan Mitchell, an art historian from Canada. (Mitchell and Greenspan married in 1952 but divorced after less than a year.)

Rand assigned Greenspan a nickname, “the Undertaker,” given his serious manner and because he always wore a dark suit and tie. Greenspan prided himself on his reasoning ability and thought he could beat anyone in an intellectual debate. Arguing with Rand “was like starting a game of chess thinking I was good, and suddenly finding myself in checkmate,” he wrote. “I was intellectually limited until I met her.” They remained friends until her death in 1982.

### An ambiguous proposal

A bachelor for much of his life, Greenspan dated newswoman Barbara Walters in the 1970s. “He wasn’t the sort of man you would notice when he walked into the room. And often I would have to introduce him to friends more than once because they wouldn’t remember him,” Walters wrote in her autobiography.

When a gossip column publicized their relationship in 1974, President Ford forwarded it to his chief of staff with a note: “I don’t believe it.”

Walters would later joke about financial advice she said she received from Greenspan in 1977, when he cited [New York City](https://www.wsj.com/topics/place/new-york-city) ’s financial distress in counseling her against buying a Fifth Avenue apartment. The unit’s value tripled over the next decade. “Now whenever he tells me not to buy a piece of real estate, I buy it,” she teased.

Walters and Greenspan remained friends but didn’t marry, in part because she didn’t love music in the same way that he did. “That was a filter which separated how I looked at women,” Greenspan told Mallaby.

He and Andrea Mitchell began dating in 1984, bonding over a shared appreciation of music, and became a power-couple fixture at star-studded galas. Greenspan overcame his bashfulness in 1987 to ask Nancy Reagan, who frowned on unwed couples at formal events, if his invitation to a White House state dinner could include Mitchell as his guest.

Nearly 12 years after their first date, Greenspan asked Mitchell if she wanted a small wedding or a big one. The proposal was “as ambiguous as some of his testimony before government committees,” Mitchell recalled to Mallaby. Ruth Bader Ginsburg presided over [their 1997 wedding](https://www.nytimes.com/1997/04/06/style/alan-greenspan-andrea-mitchell.html).

### The Fed years

When Ronald Reagan tapped Greenspan to succeed Paul Volcker as Fed chairman in 1987, an administration official told the New York Times, “If you think that Volcker was the master of obfuscation, wait until you hear Alan.”

Investors weren’t sure what to expect and believed Greenspan might be more flexible to the White House than Volcker. The high inflation of the late 1970s had been tamed by two painful recessions in the early 1980s, and Greenspan saw his primary task as consolidating those gains. Doing so drew him into political conflict with lawmakers and President George H.W. Bush.

![President Reagan congratulated Greenspan after he was sworn in as the new Fed chairman in 1987.  ](https://images.wsj.net/im-983249?width=1260&size=1.500&pixel_ratio=3)

President Reagan congratulated Greenspan after he was sworn in as the new Fed chairman in 1987.

President Reagan congratulated Greenspan after he was sworn in as the new Fed chairman in 1987. Barry Thumma/AP

During one of his first congressional testimonies, Greenspan received an earful from senators after suggesting the Fed was going to have to cool down the labor market by raising rates. After the hearing, he offered cursory thanks to his policy adviser, Donald Kohn, for helping prepare his testimony. He then told Kohn, “You’re never getting me to say any of that about raising the unemployment rate again.” It was an example of Greenspan’s “fine political antennae,” said Kohn in an interview.

Greenspan’s reluctance to cut interest rates more aggressively after the 1990-91 recession led to an especially icy relationship with Treasury Secretary Nick Brady, who canceled their golf dates at Augusta National and weekly breakfasts. “They really put a lot of pressure on him, and he didn’t budge, near as I can tell,” said Kohn, who later became a Fed governor and vice chairman.

Years later, Bush blamed Greenspan for his 1992 election loss. “I reappointed him, and he disappointed me,” Bush said coldly. To Greenspan, the whole episode was pitiful. The U.S. economy was “really moving up in 1992, and if he couldn’t make it work, it wasn’t my fault,” Greenspan said in a 2007 interview with the Sunday Times of London.

After the election but before Bill Clinton had been sworn in, Greenspan flew to Little Rock, Ark., to meet the new president. The two hit it off immediately. A few months later, Greenspan found himself seated between first lady Hillary Clinton and Tipper Gore, the vice president’s wife, at the president’s first State of the Union address.

![Hillary Clinton, Greenspan and Tipper Gore during Bill Clinton’s address to Congress in 1993.](https://images.wsj.net/im-972984?width=700&size=1.000&pixel_ratio=3)

Hillary Clinton, Greenspan and Tipper Gore during Bill Clinton’s address to Congress in 1993.

Hillary Clinton, Greenspan and Tipper Gore during Bill Clinton’s address to Congress in 1993. Luke Frazza/AFP/Getty Images

In all, Greenspan was appointed five times by four different presidents of both parties, including Bush’s son, President George W. Bush.

“As much as anyone else, he stood for thoughtful, politically aware but not politically driven policymaking,” said Lawrence Summers, who was Bill Clinton’s Treasury secretary from 1999 to 2001.

Greenspan used his clout to dispense advice on all manner of domestic policy. After one congressional hearing where Greenspan pushed for lower capital-gains taxes, Kohn pressed him: “Did you really need to weigh in on that? That’s not our thing.”

“That’s what I’ve thought my whole life,” Greenspan responded, “and I’m not going to change my mind now and just duck the question.”

In contrast to present-day Fed leaders who frequently sidestep efforts to become embroiled in hot-button political issues by professing a desire to stay in a narrow lane of Fed policy matters, “Greenspan’s lane encompassed the whole economic highway,” said Kohn. “It is amazing to me how much he influenced national debates and set the agenda on all sorts of economic subjects.”

That led to occasional miscues. Greenspan misjudged the sustainability of the federal budget surplus in the late 1990s and later supported sweeping tax cuts that ultimately made the deficit more intractable.

### Clarity as the enemy

Greenspan delighted in his ability to keep markets and politicians on their toes by wrapping his views in complex, garbled syntax. “I spend a substantial amount of my time endeavoring to fend off questions and worry terribly I might end up being too clear,” he joked in 1995.

He would envision the next morning’s newspaper headline as he spoke to Congress, ready to turn incomprehensible if he didn’t like what he imagined reading the next day. “On occasion, when I realized I’m halfway through a sentence and I don’t want to complete that sentence, I would roam off almost irrelevantly, because I didn’t care what the transcript said,” he said in a 2009 interview with Fed staff.

Despite such inscrutability, Greenspan initiated a decadeslong glasnost at the central bank, pulling back the curtain on the Fed’s mystique. Until 1994, the Fed hadn’t announced rate changes. But that February, Greenspan told his colleagues he wanted to issue a statement to signal the beginning of a series of rate increases. After having held rates at a low level for years, announcing the start of a hiking cycle would be akin to “hitting a gong,” he said. “I think we ought to stand up and hit it.”

The communications revolution partly reflected changes in how the Fed was conducting policy. Instead of targeting growth of the money supply, it shifted to setting a short-term benchmark rate. Over time, officials concluded telling markets what they were doing might improve the transmission of their policy.

The changes also stemmed from political necessity. Concerns about secretive central-bank deliberations had given fodder to Fed critics in Congress, particularly after Greenspan disclosed in 1993 the existence of near-verbatim transcripts of its policy meetings going back decades. To preclude lawmakers considering more radical moves such as allowing video cameras to record Fed meetings, Greenspan led his colleagues to approve the release of policy-meeting transcripts with a five-year lag.

### Two bold calls

Greenspan nurtured his reputation as a policy-setting “maestro” with two bold calls in the 1990s. The first was in 1994, when the Fed hit that proverbial gong by launching a pre-emptive strike on inflation that would push the federal-funds rate target to 6%, from 3%, in one year. Bond investors faced huge losses. California’s Orange County went bankrupt. Mexico devalued its peso. But the U.S. economy avoided a downturn—one of the few times the Fed has achieved a so-called soft landing.

A few years later, Greenspan earned plaudits again for resisting pressure from colleagues to raise rates aggressively as the unemployment rate tested new 30-year lows. Conventional forecasts relied on something known as the Phillips curve, which predicts that wages and prices accelerate when the unemployment rate falls below some natural, sustainable level that can’t be directly observed.

But Greenspan was studying corporate profit margins. He marshaled data to suggest a productivity boom thanks to personal computing was under way. Strong productivity translated to declining or stable unit labor costs, he argued, which would allow the economy to tolerate more growth without higher inflation.

Greenspan’s command of statistics gave him an intuition about where the economy was going and where pressures were building up. He was an intensive user of the Fed’s workhorse economic simulations even though he frequently trashed the models when staffers included the results in briefing materials.

One staff economist briefed Greenspan on population projections for a hearing on Social Security in the early 2000s by laying out sheets of paper on a conference table so they could visualize the waves of the baby-boom generation. “He was absolutely fascinated by that level of detailed data,” said Jonathan Pingle, who is now chief U.S. economist at UBS.

During monetary policy deliberations with the other 11 members of the rate-setting Federal Open Market Committee, Greenspan spoke first and issued his recommendation. His second-in-command, the president of the New York Fed, would follow with a hearty endorsement. Everyone else fell in line. “It was really hard to disagree once the two of them went. It became very difficult to have a real policy discussion,” said Kohn.

Colleagues were afraid to cross him and didn’t often try. “If I’m person No. 8, and every person before has said, ‘Yes, sir, I support your proposal,’ and then it’s my turn, I sure as heck found it intimidating to say anything other than, ‘Yes, sir, I support your proposal too,’” said Janet Yellen, a Fed governor from 1994 to 1997 who later became Fed chairman and Treasury secretary.

### A regulatory blind spot

Greenspan’s faith that self discipline would effectively equilibrate financial markets proved to be his blind spot. “He conducted monetary policy like a maestro,” said Alan Blinder, a Princeton economics professor who served as vice chairman at the Fed under Greenspan in the 1990s. But his regulatory legacy was different. “It caused a lot of harm,” said Blinder.

Greenspan believed financial institutions had a self interest in not taking risks that would wipe out shareholders; similarly, he thought they had natural incentives to behave ethically to preserve the value of their reputations. Under Greenspan’s watch, the Fed didn’t blow the whistle on riskier lending practices in which Wall Street used financial alchemy to turn dodgy mortgages into highly rated securities.

By the early 2000s, low inflation had given rise to new challenges—namely, that their very success might lead investors to behave more recklessly. There was nothing to ensure that stable economic conditions would also maintain stability in financial markets.

“The only way to guarantee that there will not be bubbles is to have an unstable, highly inflationary monetary policy,” Greenspan said in a 2013 interview with a Fed staffer. “There are no bubbles in North Korea; there weren’t any in the Soviet Union.… So there’s a very fundamental problem with monetary policy in that, if you do too well, as I like to say, no good deed goes unpunished.”

Greenspan famously warned in 1996 that the stock market was getting very speculative—exhibiting “irrational exuberance.” The Dow Jones Industrial Average proceeded to gain more than 80% over the next three years. Still, he concluded the Fed shouldn’t try to prick bubbles with rate increases given uncertain benefits and potentially severe costs.

After the dot-com bubble burst in 2001 without leaving much of an imprint on the broader economy, Greenspan felt vindicated. “Instead of trying to contain a putative bubble by drastic actions with largely unpredictable consequences, we chose…to focus on policies ‘to mitigate the fallout when it occurs and, hopefully, ease the transition to the next expansion,’” he argued in a 2004 speech. Critics said this asymmetry—taking small steps to limit the growth of bubbles but large ones to clean it up afterward—only fueled more risk-taking.

### The looming crisis

When term limits forced Greenspan to retire in January 2006, the economy was solid, inflation low, and prices of stocks and houses buoyant. But troubles were building. When home prices stopped rising, home building turned down and delinquencies mounted on poorly underwritten mortgages. By 2008, the U.S. was in the worst financial crisis it had experienced since the Great Depression. Iconic Wall Street firms like Bear Stearns and Lehman Brothers failed, and millions of Americans lost homes in foreclosure.

In a 2007 interview with The Wall Street Journal, former Fed governor Edward Gramlich said [he had privately urged](https://www.wsj.com/articles/SB118134111823129555?mod=article_inline) Greenspan to clamp down on predatory lending around 2000. “He was opposed to it, so I didn’t really pursue it,” Gramlich said. From 2000 to the end of Greenspan’s tenure, the Fed referred to the Justice Department only three institutions for fair lending violations related to mortgages.

Fed leaders and other regulators didn’t understand how risks had been distributed across the financial system by innovations such as the securitization of credit, in which loans were pooled together and their cash flows sold off to investors. Many blamed the deregulation of and self-regulation by Wall Street, championed by Greenspan and presidents from both parties, for stripping away safeguards that might have prevented an inflated housing market from upending the financial system.

“The right question is whether Alan Greenspan, this huge guru who had tremendous influence, could have rung the bell better. And he could have,” said Kohn. “He was a bit too complacent.”

In the early 2000s, Greenspan had flagged the danger of frothy housing markets and, separately, the use of home-equity withdrawals as a means to support consumer spending. But he “didn’t see how weak the financial system would be when those markets collapsed,” said Kohn.

Greenspan tacitly acknowledged policy mistakes in a 2008 appearance before Congress.

Greenspan tacitly acknowledged policy mistakes in a 2008 appearance before Congress. Mark Wilson/Getty Images

The former Fed leader tacitly acknowledged mistakes in [a final appearance before Congress](https://www.wsj.com/articles/SB122476545437862295?mod=article_inline) in October 2008. “Those of us who have looked to the self interest of lending institutions to protect shareholders’ equity, myself especially, are in a state of shocked disbelief,” he said.

Greenspan thought he received disproportionate blame for the crisis together with undue credit for the economic boom of the late 1990s. He attributed his biggest accomplishment in sustaining low inflation to the end of the Cold War and the integration of China and other Asian economies into the global economy.

“I was praised for things I didn’t do,” [he said in a 2008 interview](https://www.wsj.com/articles/SB120760341392296107?mod=article_inline). “I am now being blamed for things that I didn’t do.”
