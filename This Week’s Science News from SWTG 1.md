### This Calculation Could Change The Periodic Table

![undefined](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!nwHo!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5fd1d43a-6e09-4e0f-8742-545ea0bcc1b3_3840x2245.png)

The periodic table is steadily expanding because physicists are producing heavier and heavier atomic nuclei. These heavy nuclei tend to be very short-lived, but a very intriguing, longstanding prediction has it that when the nuclei get heavy enough, they will actually become more stable again. The big question has been just exactly how heavy the nuclei have to be for this. Physicists are now getting close to these so-called “magic” nuclei, and in a paper that just appeared a few weeks ago, a group says they have finally figured out just why some nuclei are more stable than others.  
  
The so far heaviest element to be added to the periodic table is Oganesson, which has atomic number 118. Its only confirmed isotope, oganesson-294, has a lifetime of about a microsecond, which doesn’t seem long until you remember that most particles in the standard model decay in less than a nanosecond.  
  
Producing heavier nuclei is somewhat of a niche interest even in a niche field like nuclear physics. There are basically only three laboratories in the world working on that, one in Japan, one in Germany, and one in Russia. They produce these super heavy nuclei by slamming already large nuclei into each other and basically hoping that they’ll stick together.  
  
Oganesson was first discovered in 2002 in Russia, and is named after the Russian physicist Yuri Oganessian. It was later confirmed by the other labs and officially added to the periodic table in 2015. But meanwhile, experiments have tentatively found evidence for nuclei with atomic number 119 and 120, though these have not yet been confirmed.  
  
This is exciting because there have been some predictions saying that somewhere around atomic number 120 or so, the “island of stability” might begin — that is, the atomic nuclei might get longer lived again. Just how long they’ll live has remained rather unclear, but estimates have ranged from seconds to minutes.  
  
[The new paper now](https://journals.aps.org/prl/abstract/10.1103/8lzc-j1lx) finally gives us a good theoretical grip on just why some nuclei are more stable than others. To understand what they did, you first need to know that atomic nuclei have energy levels, just like electrons around a nucleus do. The more neutrons and protons you add to the nucleus, the more of these energy levels you fill up. It’s called the nuclear ‘shell’ model and it is similar to the electron shell model for atoms.  
  
Now, at particular values there are gaps in the energy spectrum. This is like electrons shells, which are “full” at some point. And if there is such a gap, that basically means that it is difficult for such a nucleus to decay. These nuclei are then particularly stable. The question is just where these gaps in the energy spectrum are.
