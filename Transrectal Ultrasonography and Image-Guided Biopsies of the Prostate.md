## Excerpt

Transrectal ultrasonography (TRUS) is a cornerstone in the evaluation of the prostate. The technique was first described in 1963 by Takahashi et al and was later used for prostate cancer detection by Gotoh and Nishi in 1965. However, the technique was not widely used until 1985, when 5 MHz transrectal probes became available. TRUS is now commonly used for imaging prostatic pathology, especially benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH), prostate cancer, possible prostatic abscesses, and prostatitis, as well as providing visual guidance for prostate biopsies.

BPH is extremely prevalent, with 80% of men being affected by age 70. Imaging is not required for diagnosis, which is typically based on clinical signs, symptom score, post-void residual urine volume, digital rectal examination (DRE) results, pressure/flow studies, and/or cystoscopy. However, changes in the prostate are easily recognized on TRUS, and it may be recommended for anatomical evaluation and volume measurements to help with surgical decision-making and determination of PSA density for cancer screening if any surgical intervention is planned.

Prostate cancer is the most common new cancer diagnosis in men, and the incidence is only expected to increase as the population ages. The widespread worldwide routine monitoring of prostate-specific antigen (PSA) has greatly increased the overall diagnosis rate of prostate cancer. However, these are mostly low-grade neoplasms frequently treated with active surveillance. TRUS is the most widely used imaging tool to evaluate prostate cancer, as it serves as the primary guide for prostate biopsies. About 1 million prostate biopsies are performed in the United States and 3 million worldwide every year, making it one of the most commonly performed urological procedures for men.

Prostatic abscesses appear as dark, hypoechoic cavities, frequently in the transitional zone. These abscesses are reliably diagnosed by TRUS, which also allows simultaneous aspiration and drainage.

Prostatitis is a fairly common disease that is classically diagnosed based on physical examination and laboratory findings alone. Still, with the more recent advancements of TRUS, imaging has played an increasing role in its diagnosis. In prostatitis, the prostate appears more rounded with increased vascularity, and a periurethral halo may be observed.
