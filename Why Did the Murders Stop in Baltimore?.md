The number of homicides fell to a 60-year low after city leaders finally decided to start taking violence seriously enough.

Say “Baltimore,” and people think of crime. Shows like *The Wire* and *Homicide: Life on the Street* depicted the city as gritty, gun-ridden, and plagued by gang violence. These fictional representations weren’t far off what statistics show about the city’s reality. Since the turn of the millennium, young black men have been three to four times more likely to die by homicide in Baltimore than in the nation as a whole.

And as violent as it already was, things got worse a decade ago. The 2015 riots, in response to the death of [25-year-old Freddie Gray](https://www.cbsnews.com/baltimore/news/maryland-freddie-gray-death-unrest-settlement-police-reform/) in police custody, inaugurated a retreat in Baltimore policing and a multiyear surge in street violence. In 2020, amid [a national increase](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2021/10/27/what-we-know-about-the-increase-in-u-s-murders-in-2020/) in killings, the Charm City saw homicide rates about eight times higher than the national average.

Then, in late 2022, Baltimore’s fortunes turned. Starting sometime late in the calendar year, murder rates started plummeting. The city reported [333 murders](https://www.wmar2news.com/infocus/a-closer-look-at-the-2022-murders-in-baltimore-city) that year. Last year, after three years of steady decline, there were just 133 murders in the whole city—a more than 60 percent drop. That’s [the fewest murders](https://magazine.publichealth.jhu.edu/2026/baltimores-historic-homicide-reduction) Baltimore has seen since 1965.

“It was shootings every day, every night,” said Kin Brown-Lane, a lifelong resident of Cherry Hill, one of the city’s most dangerous neighborhoods, about the situation prior to 2022. “People didn’t have their kids outside to play, because you just never knew what was going to jump off out here.”

Brown-Lane runs [Switching Lanes Adult & Youth Institute](https://switchinglanesadultandyouth.org/about), a nonprofit that gets community kids working with seniors. Now “the kids can go back outside and play,” Brown-Lane said, and Cherry Hill seniors are feeling the difference. “The most important people that you want to talk to when it comes to safety and feeling safe are the seniors,” she said. “And when you talk to the seniors, they’ll tell you the changes.”

Cleoda Walker, an 80-year resident of Cherry Hill, said that life there has “shifted for the better.” While seniors still feel nervous going out at night or to go shopping, most of them tell her that they “aren’t going to let crime stop them,” in large part because they feel like it is under control.

What has happened in Baltimore was supposed to be impossible. *The Wire* creator David Simon long said that Baltimore’s crime would never fall until the U.S. [ended its drug war](https://www.themarshallproject.org/2015/04/29/david-simon-on-baltimore-s-anguish#:~:text=The%20part%20that%20seems%20systemic,destroyed%20by%20the%20drug%20war.). Academics have regularly turned to [systemic racism](https://urbanhealth.jhu.edu/sites/default/files/2023-05/sdh-2016-summary-report.pdf) as the explanation for Baltimore’s crime problem.  
  
Yet the levels of crime Baltimore now experiences were seen as inconceivable just a few years ago. And the numbers keep falling. In April, Baltimore had four homicides, the lowest total for any single month since [at least 1970](https://www.baltimorecity.gov/mayor/news-media/press-releases/2026-05-01-mayor-brandon-m-scott-statement-on-aprils-decline-in-homicides-and-nonfatal-shootings). So far this year, [there were 38](https://app.powerbigov.us/view?r=eyJrIjoiZjcyZDY5NWYtNTBlZS00OTg0LTgyNDQtOWYwMDEyOGI5Y2M1IiwidCI6IjMxMmNiMTI2LWM2YWUtNGZjMi04MDBkLTMxOGU2NzljZTZjNyJ9), compared with 51 in the same period last year. At the current rate, Baltimore would end 2026 with fewer than 100 homicides. There were 323 just four years ago.

How did we get from a city in which the question was how high can crime rise, to one where the question is how low can it go? The answer might be linked to the [nationwide decline](https://www.thefp.com/p/the-murder-rate-is-plummeting-youll) in murder, spurred by a restoration of policing as the excesses of the George Floyd years recede. But that begs the question of what cities across the country are doing right. And it skips over how Baltimore has bucked nationwide murder trends at times, with homicides in the city staying high as violence fell elsewhere in the 1990s and 2000s.

When you look closely and talk to the people on the front lines, some strong patterns emerge, with two competing stories coming into focus. One is the return of a program focused on deterring the small fraction of offenders in Baltimore who commit the large majority of violent crimes. The other is the election of a new tough-on-crime prosecutor, who replaced a scandal-plagued “progressive.”

Which story you prefer has increasingly become a partisan matter, producing unnecessary political feuding. The bigger lesson is that intelligently targeting the most violent offenders, both through deterrence and incapacitation, can yield large, durable reductions in murder. Baltimore’s success shows that its homicide problem was, in reality, just the result of its past leaders’ failure to take violence seriously enough.

Statistically speaking, almost no one reading this article will be murdered. Out of the 3,072,666 deaths in the United States in 2024, 20,162 were homicides, Centers for Disease Control and Prevention data show. If you live outside of one of a handful of murder hot spots, your risk falls even lower. Even within these areas, you need the right ingredients for a high homicide rate: groups of young men with guns, a tendency to get into personal “beefs,” and little social oversight.

When you mix that recipe together, though, things can get insanely dangerous. In parts of some American cities, research [has shown](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2799859), young men have a higher rate of homicide death than did U.S. soldiers serving in Iraq and Afghanistan. This hyperconcentration of violence has shown up in [Washington, D.C.](https://cjcc.dc.gov/sites/default/files/dc/sites/cjcc/NICJR%20GVPA%20Summary%20Report%20%28January%202024%29.pdf); [Chicago](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2149219); [Oakland, California](https://www.oaklandca.gov/Public-Safety-Streets/Crime-Prevention/Oakland-Ceasefire-Strategy); and elsewhere. The networked nature of the violence is important. Killers and victims are often tied up in the same social networks, meaning that beef between group members can continually escalate in a cycle of retaliation.

Baltimore follows this pattern. In Baltimore’s Western District, 72 percent of murders between 2015 and 2021 [were attributable to](https://popcenter.asu.edu/sites/g/files/litvpz3631/files/baltimore_pd_reducing_gun_violence_in_baltimore_2024.pdf) a small number of men, mostly organized into gangs. The same analysis estimated that the area’s gang members accounted for just 2 percent of the district’s population but as much as 75 percent of its shootings and homicides.

That’s why Brandon Scott, then a mayoral candidate, reached out in 2019 to Anthony Braga, a criminologist now at the University of Pennsylvania. In the 1990s, Braga helped develop a strategy for reducing gun violence in Boston. The city had the standard problem: About 61 “gangs” accounted for just 1 percent of Boston’s young people but 60 percent of the homicides.

Armed with this knowledge, Braga and Harvard Kennedy School colleagues David Kennedy and Anne Piehl designed a strategy variously called [Group Violence Intervention](https://www.nlc.org/resource/reimagining-public-safety-impact-updates/boston-ma-group-violence-intervention), focused deterrence, or “Operation Ceasefire.”

They gathered up the frequent offenders, well-known to police, and had officers issue a clear message: Violence would no longer be tolerated. Community organizations offered help—with jobs, records, and so on. But the overwhelming message was that if these offenders offend again, the law will come down on them like a ton of bricks.

The strategy [worked](https://www.d.umn.edu/~jmaahs/MA%20Theory%20Articles/Braga_problem_oriented%20policing_deterrence.pdf). Youth homicides fell 63 percent, while the number of “shots fired” incidents fell 32 percent.

---

Braga said that his team was wary of bringing the program to Baltimore. The city [had tried](https://web.archive.org/web/20240104025234/https:/www.baltimoresun.com/2020/12/01/an-oft-tried-plan-to-curb-violent-crime-in-baltimore-resurfaces-city-leaders-say-better-leadership-will-bring-better-results/) ceasefire twice before. The implementation fell apart. Nevertheless, after Scott became mayor at the end of 2020, the Penn group helped his administration put together its [Group Violence Reduction Strategy](https://www.baltimorecity.gov/monse/gun-violence-intervention/gvrs) (GVRS). The program started in the Western District; it [now operates](https://www.baltimorecity.gov/mayor/news/press-release/2026-04-17-mayor-brandon-m-scott-gvrs-partners-announce-expansion-to-bpds-northern-district) across six of the city’s nine police districts.

The approach involves a detailed investigation of every shooting that happens in the city. Every week, the Baltimore Police Department and its partners review the week’s incidents. They make plans to arrest anyone who has committed a crime or violated parole. Occasionally, the program leads to tips that end in an arrest.

But mostly the focus is on deterring the cycle of escalation. GVRS has yielded about 600 arrests in its five years of operation. For every shooting, GVRS prescribes reaching out to known associates of the victim. A second weekly meeting brings together law enforcement officials with service organizations and “community moral voices”—respected neighborhood figures who can put pressure on a would-be shooter to stand down.

At one recent coordination meeting, about 20 people gathered around the table of the conference room at Baltimore’s Doxa Ministries Church Without Walls. Under the direction of Reginald Williams from the Mayor’s Office of Neighborhood Safety and Engagement, they talked through two new “referrals” associated with the victim of a recent shooting. One had a long criminal history and was on house arrest. Another, barely an adult, was himself a victim a few years earlier.

Both men will have their doors knocked on by several of the meeting’s attendees. They will be offered services—job training, tattoo removal, relocation, whatever they need to get out of the “life.” But they will also get a clear message, delivered verbally and in the form of a letter from Mayor Scott: Baltimore is watching them—and will come after them.

Brown-Lane is one of the community’s moral voices who works with GVRS. She described speaking to the brother of a young man who had been shot in a shopping center.

“His brother also was in the street, so literally nobody could get to him,” she said. “So I went to him, and because of the respect level, he talked to me. He’s in \[an intervention program\], he’s doing well. He wanted to be moved, he wanted new, he wanted different—and he really wanted it, and he got it.”

Brown-Lane reported that her neighborhood has gone for long stretches without a shooting or homicide. She thinks GVRS is to thank for it. “You can take the high road and do what you need to do, or you can go back out in the street, and there’s consequences to going back out in the streets,” she said.

News coverage sometimes emphasizes the social-services aspect of Baltimore’s program. Braga said that misses the point. “It is not a soft approach to crime,” he said. “The folks who are driving the violence are held accountable for their past violent behavior and any continuing violence that they are committing.”

Something else has changed in Baltimore: its top prosecutor.

In 2015, Baltimore elected Marilyn Mosby as its state’s attorney. She was the nation’s youngest big-city prosecutor, part of the wave of “progressive prosecutors” that swept into office. Backed by left-leaning activists, these prosecutors sought to use their discretion to unilaterally decriminalize a host of petty offenses in the name of ending mass incarceration.

Mosby almost immediately got a chance to prove her bona fides. Shortly after her election, Freddie Gray died after a “rough ride” in a police van following a misdemeanor arrest for possession of a knife. In a predominantly black city already primed by the Ferguson, Missouri, protest movement, Gray’s death lit the powder keg. The ensuing [protests and riots drove](https://www.nber.org/papers/w27324) a [spike in violence](http://socweb.soc.jhu.edu/faculty/morgan/papers/MorganPally2016.pdf) in the city that arguably did not abate until 2023. Mosby stepped in, not to put an end to the disorder but to [lead a failed attempt](https://time.com/4426433/freddie-gray-charges-dropped-baltimore-officers) to prosecute the officers involved in Gray’s death.

In 2021, her office [stopped prosecuting](https://eji.org/news/baltimore-ends-prosecution-of-drug-possession-and-other-low-level-offenses/?utm_source=chatgpt.com) drug possession, prostitution, trespassing, and “other low-level offenses” entirely. Unsurprisingly, the ensuing surge in crime made her unpopular with voters. So, too, did [her indictment](https://apnews.com/article/baltimore-prosecutor-marilyn-mosby-indicted-deca6f356290e50ae5d618f95ef8063f) for perjury and making false statements on mortgage applications. In 2022, Mosby lost reelection. (She spent a year in [home confinement](https://www.cbsnews.com/baltimore/news/appeal-denied-marilyn-mosby-perjury-mortgage-fraud-maryland) after being convicted of mortgage fraud and perjury, though the fraud conviction was overturned.)

Mosby’s replacement was Ivan Bates, a former defense attorney who had represented one of Gray’s alleged killers. On the campaign trail, Bates took Mosby to task for her corruption and the city’s crime problems, [promising to focus](https://marylandmatters.org/2018/05/11/candidates-for-baltimore-state-s-attorney-hammer-absent-incumbent) on gun violence and on building bridges between the community and law enforcement. Since taking office, Bates has made prosecuting violent offenders his top priority.

What if the new state’s attorney is what’s really driving violence down, not the mayor’s strategy?

Some residents give Bates a significant share of the credit. Arch McKown has been involved in community public-safety efforts for between 15 and 20 years in two Baltimore neighborhoods: Patterson Park and Butcher’s Hill. He said that things have gotten “a lot more quiet lately.”

“Marilyn Mosby, she was of a very progressive mindset in terms of how she was prosecuting, and she sowed quite a bit of dysfunction in the agency,” McKown said. Under Bates, McKown has seen more collaboration with the police, more willingness to process felons in possession of a gun, and a more credible threat of punishment.

Bates gives some credit to GVRS. Asked what drove the decline in homicide in Baltimore, his first word was “collaboration.” And he agreed that “GVRS has done some good things.” But, Bates added, “you cannot have GVRS be successful if you do not have successful prosecution.”

Bates said that his office has identified about 6,000 frequent, violent offenders and put between 3,000 and 3,500 of them in prison. The cooperation of federal law enforcement has helped take a number of these offenders off the streets.

“If you’re seeing your friends all going to prison, you’re going to go: ‘What? I don’t want to go to prison,’ ” Bates told The Free Press. (Pete Kiehart for )

And while GVRS helps, Bates said, it’s not the proverbial magic bullet. “The previous state’s attorney, when there was GVRS, didn’t prosecute. We had 342 murders,” Bates said. Data from his office suggests that less than 4 percent of all cases closed in 2025 were referred from GVRS—implying that the program just isn’t yielding many lockups.

That accords with GVRS’s own total of about 600 arrests over five years. But that doesn’t fully encompass the value of the program. Its goal is to *deter* criminal offenders, stopping them from offending in the first place.

GVRS also can’t work without a tough prosecutor. GVRS provides the information to authorities, the warnings to potential offenders, and the support to leave violence behind. Prosecution is the alternative. And it’s only when prosecutors get serious that the alternative can start to work.  
  
“If you’re seeing your friends all going to prison, you’re going to go: ‘What? I don’t want to go to prison,’ ” Bates said. “Now all of a sudden, that job or that program someone’s offered you before that you didn’t want to talk about—now it looks pretty appealing.”

The ambiguity behind Baltimore’s homicide drop has given much of the discussion of crime an unfortunately partisan valence. [Some right-of-center](https://freebeacon.com/crime/violent-crime-began-plummeting-in-baltimore-just-after-voters-fired-their-soros-backed-prosecutor-experts-say-thats-no-coincidence/) [news outlets](https://thedispatch.com/article/baltimore-murder-rate-decline-prosecuting-crime/), eager to downplay the program that Scott has often made sound like a social-services solution to crime, have emphasized the election of Bates. [Left-wing coverage](https://www.thetrace.org/2025/09/brandon-scott-baltimore-violence-safety) often characterizes focused deterrence as a “public health” intervention.

In a sense, it is impossible to know which made the difference. But trying to credit one or the other obscures how crucially they work together. GVRS keeps violence from escalating, reducing opportunities for offending in the first place. At the same time, locking up thousands of violent repeat offenders [will incapacitate](https://www.city-journal.org/article/build-more-prisons) them. But GVRS’s deterrence can’t work if the threat of incarceration isn’t credible. A good prosecutor is the linchpin to a successful deterrent strategy.

For decades, Baltimore allowed violence to flourish. People died, were injured, or were scared off from the city entirely. Over the past five years, the city’s government has come around to a simple conclusion: Stop tolerating violent people. When it did, the city got safer.

McKown, the public safety advocate in Patterson Park and Butcher’s Hill, said there is reason for continued optimism: “Baltimore and a number of other cities have shown that violence is not some fixed condition that people just have to accept.”

Baltimore shows other cities struggling with violence that murder is a problem that we know how to solve: Lock up offenders and deter further escalation. The only question is: Do more leaders have the will to do it?
