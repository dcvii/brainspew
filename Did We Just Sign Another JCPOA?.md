Mooch welcomes back [Darya Dolzikova](https://www.rusi.org/people/dolzikova), Senior Research Fellow with the Royal United Service Institute's Proliferation and Nuclear Policy Program, joining from London to assess where Iran's nuclear program actually stands following the MOU signed between Washington and Tehran. [The last time Dolzikova was on the channel](https://youtu.be/zJgBLUxdDkU?si=cXfx7rliJksccTYr) — roughly eleven months ago — the question was whether Operation Midnight Hammer's B-2 strikes had obliterated or merely damaged Iran's nuclear capability. A lot has happened since: Operation Epic Fury, a fragile ceasefire, an Iranian shootdown of a US Army Apache helicopter that nearly reignited full-scale combat, and now a memorandum of understanding that's been presented to the public as a diplomatic breakthrough.

Dolzikova walks through what the MOU actually contains. It’s a roughly 100-page document that lays out Iran’s nuclear obligations in considerable detail, with sanctions relief offered in return. The structure echoes the JCPOA — the 2015 nuclear deal that Trump pulled out of in 2018 — in that it envisions a gradual, phased lifting of UN Security Council resolutions, EU nuclear-related sanctions, and secondary US sanctions tied to Iran’s nuclear activities. The core problem the agreement is trying to solve is the same one the JCPOA was designed to address: Iran’s enrichment program. When the JCPOA was in force, Iran was capped at 3.67% enrichment. After Trump withdrew and reimposed sanctions, Iran progressively walked its enrichment level up — reaching 60% by the time the June strikes hit last year. That’s not weapons-grade, but it’s a short technical step away, and the stockpile accumulated at that level is the central concern any new agreement has to credibly address.

The deeper question Mooch and Dolzikova keep circling is whether any agreement can hold given the structural dynamics that undermined the JCPOA. Iran has insisted enrichment is an inalienable right regardless of its civilian energy needs — there’s no technical rationale for enriching to anywhere near current levels to run nuclear power plants. The Trump administration’s criticism of the JCPOA has always been that it was time-limited, that it allowed Iran to keep its infrastructure intact, and that the sanctions relief front-loaded the economic benefits before Iran’s compliance could be verified over the long haul. Whether the MOU addresses those structural weaknesses — or whether it’s a reset of the same cycle — is the thread that runs through the entire conversation, and Dolzikova’s read is that the details of implementation and verification will determine whether this represents a genuine resolution or another chapter in a very long standoff.
