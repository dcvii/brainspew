[Sam Harris](https://open.substack.com/users/2045807-sam-harris?utm_source=mentions) published [an essay](https://substack.com/@samharris/note/p-200794273?utm_source=notes-share-action&r=e0eqs) this month explaining why he won’t debate “critics of Israel.” (I think he principally means antizionists, but I won’t belabor the debatable difference.) The response was predictable: a chorus of bad faith actors calling him a liar and a coward, and a smaller chorus of good faith ones— [Eli Kowaz](https://open.substack.com/users/20764988-eli-kowaz?utm_source=mentions) among them—arguing that the stakes are too high to leave the table. Harris is right, Kowaz is wrong. But the more important point has nothing to do with Israel.

The question underneath the controversy is whether public intellectuals owe engagement to anyone who demands it; and how we manage our public communications in general, including the comments from onlookers. Ultimately, the answers start by understanding what we’re even talking about. Not “free speech,” not “open debate,” not “heckler’s veto.” No, we’re talking, as we often are, about matters of rights; and privileges, ones that get confused for the former.

---

## Right, Not Permission

The demand that serious thinkers debate anyone who challenges them rests on a category error. Public engagement isn’t a legal obligation, nor a civic duty. It isn’t owed to critics or hecklers the way honesty is owed to audiences. A writer’s obligation concerns readers—regarding clarity, [factualism](https://edokwin.substack.com/p/against-nonfactualism), making better arguments. Writers do not run to whoever shows up at the door claiming to deserve a hearing.

This is straightforwardly an expression of freedom of association. No one—regardless of platform size, public profile, or cultural influence—is obligated to lend their time and credibility to an exchange that serves **neither** *truth* nor *the public*. The size of your audience doesn’t matter. Having a bigger platform doesn’t convert the expectation of engagement into a duty. If anything, it *raises* the cost of *performing* **debate theater** for people whose track records disqualify them as serious voices.

The Kowaz objection—that leaving is how you lose a fight you could still win—sounds pragmatic but proves too much. If “the stakes are high” is sufficient warrant to compel engagement with anyone, there is no principled stopping point. **The stakes are always high**. Every bad faith actor in history has believed themselves to be on the right side of something urgent. Accepting this framework hands a permanent demand on your time and platform to whoever screams loudest about what’s at risk.

## Terminally Unreachable

There is a category of person for whom argument is structurally futile—not because they’re stupid, but because their commitments are load-bearing. The belief isn’t held tentatively, waiting to be updated. It’s doing identity work. It’s the foundation of their position, maybe their whole identity. Challenging it doesn’t prompt reconsideration; it triggers defense. After all, [persuasion is predetermined](https://edokwin.substack.com/p/persuasion-is-predetermined-courage).

I’ve watched this play out in my own comment threads. A reader—let’s call her Margaret—asserted that universal healthcare is moral, full stop, as though she’d stated a weather fact. When I asked her to justify it, she didn’t engage the question. She bristled at the asking. “I’ll look into it”—the most epistemically neutral response available, conceding nothing, just signaling willingness to examine—was too threatening to offer. The belief wasn’t a belief. It was a load-bearing wall. Remove it and something structurally important collapses. She couldn’t imagine it.

I watched the same pattern in a recent exchange with a commentator I’ll call Miles (cuz that’s his name), who was arguing that covering Democrats unfavorably, talking about their polling numbers, their 80/20 issues problem, and all manner of other fair criticisms, constitutes net-harmful doom-mongering. His error was structural: he couldn’t separate what the data showed from what covering the data meant about the people covering it. He assumed bad faith and engaged in pernicious motive attribution dressed up as corrective. When I used the word “tribe” analytically, he got disproportionately defensive—simultaneously claiming it was both offensive and inaccurate, two defenses that cancel each other out. He ended the exchange with a [monologue about “empathy.”](https://edokwin.substack.com/p/stop-telling-leftists-theyre-good) He was epistemically out to lunch.

Neither Margaret nor Miles is a villain. That’s the point. Sincerity doesn’t redeem epistemic capture—it deepens it. The person who knows they’re rationalizing has an off-ramp. The *true believer* who’s convinced their rationalizations are valid and necessary has foreclosed the exit. Engaging them doesn’t open the argument; it *performs* it, and does so for an audience that has already decided. **There’s no alpha in it**, as the people in New York and San Francisco like to say.

## Your Visit, Your Problem

The same confusion that drives the “debate-me” demand shows up in miniature every time a creator adjusts their comment settings and someone calls it censorship. The critics are wrong on the facts and wrong on the principle—but the wrongness is instructive. The demands and false charges? Always self-serving.

Start with the facts. Comment moderation is not new. Letters to the editor are always curated. Call-in shows have screeners. Publications have always chosen which responses to print and which to bin. The only thing that changed is that social media briefly created the illusion of a default open floor, and a generation of users mistook a ***product** design choice* for a constitutional guarantee. When platforms added native tools for moderation, things that let creators do what they were always permitted to do, the howling began. As if the internet is a government building instead of private property. As if social platforms are city parks instead of businesses or private clubs.

Josh Slocum and Stephen L. Miller, among others, have been particularly vocal about Substack’s recent [Reply Rules](https://on.substack.com/p/introducing-reply-rules). They treat these as though they represent some novel affront to free expression. They don’t. They’re a UI wrapper around a right that has always existed. Around behavior which has always been the norm. Creators screen, delete, restrict, ban/block, and otherwise moderate their commenters. They do. They always have. Creators are not the government. Creators are not throwing anyone in jail when exercising the right to freely associate or to enforce reasonable boundaries.

Now the principle. A comment section is not a public square. It’s a room in someone’s house. The owner decides the furniture, the guest list, and the rules. A well-maintained house—active moderation, clear norms, genuine engagement—is a mark of care. A neglected one, left wide open and wallpapered in graffiti, isn’t a monument to courage or integrity; it’s evidence of indifference. Leaving your comments on and ignoring them isn’t brave. It’s negligent. The creator who actually moderates, who sets Reply Rules and enforces them, who thinks about what discourse looks like in their space—that person is doing more for real conversation than the one who leaves the door unlocked and walks away. And far too many do just walk away.

That latter aspect is often missed or actively elided in these discussions. When people claim it’s “cowardly” to limit comments or ban people, what they seem to be assuming is that the alternative will be attention, maybe even engagement. A back-and-forth. Perhaps a legit meeting of minds, stress-testing of perspectives and positions.

But no, that’s folly. As people like the great and powerful [Jamie Paul](https://open.substack.com/users/1635473-jamie-paul?utm_source=mentions), [Rohan Ghostwind](https://open.substack.com/users/284109948-rohan-ghostwind?utm_source=mentions), et al have basically admitted, they leave comments open whilst turning off notifications. They’re letting their readers squawk, sure, but they’re not there. They’re ignoring you. I don’t say that to shame them, just as a descriptive fact. What exactly is a random comment worth if no one cares? Is this really a testament to “free speech”? Something that functions like a falling tree in an empty forest? No, worse. Like a fart in the wind. Who cares? What are we even talking about?

### Free Speech, Rightly Constituted

What free speech protects, properly understood, is the individual against state coercion. The government cannot silence you. A private creator on a private platform declining to host your remarks is not the government. It is, again, freedom of association—the same right that lets you choose who comes to your dinner table. The people invoking First Amendment logic in this context are either confused about what the First Amendment says or hoping you are.

And what, exactly, is being defended here? The right to leave a comment is not a civic act. It’s a visit. If the visit is substantive—if it adds something, challenges something, builds something—then it has value, and good creators will recognize that and welcome it. But the act itself confers no status. You are not a freedom fighter because you typed something into a text box. You are not persecuted because the box was removed. The self-importance required to frame comment restrictions as suppression is remarkable, and it would be funny if it weren’t so tedious.

There’s a psychological mechanism underneath the tedium worth naming. Prohibition generates desire. The creator who closes their comments—or restricts them, or filters them—suddenly makes the act of commenting feel urgent in a way it never was before. People who never had anything particular to say…magically discover they have a great deal to say, now that saying it has been complicated. This isn’t really about free expression. It’s mimetic desire reflex. The demand isn’t really about discourse. It’s about access, and about wanting anything that’s withheld.

None of this means creators are above accountability or that all moderation is well-applied. Some is capricious. Some is genuinely self-serving in ways worth criticizing. The distinction that matters is between a creator who curates deliberately—who has thought about what they want their space to be—and one who uses moderation as a shield against legitimate challenge. The former is exercising judgment. The latter is the cowardice the critics think they’re identifying. They’re rarely identifying it correctly, because it requires engaging with the actual track record of the person they’re criticizing—which is, of course, more work than a hot take about censorship.

## What Debate Culture Actually Is

Contemporary debate culture—the platform-lending, the endless back-and-forth on platforms built to reward conflict, the challenges to perform—is not a truth-seeking mechanism. It’s slop content. It’s what you get when rhetorical combat becomes a genre and the outcome is measured in engagement rather than insight. No one is enlightened by watching two people argue past each other for an hour. The format doesn’t produce understanding; it produces the simulation of understanding, with winners and losers assigned by whoever controls the framing.

Harris hasn’t withdrawn from public discourse. He’s done podcast tours with unfriendly hosts; published self-critical essays addressing issues and people across the political spectrum; and participated in conversations with people he substantively disagrees with. What he’s declined is the specific *performance* of debating people whose track records mark them as neither serious nor good faith. That’s not retreat. It’s discernment. I share his position.

The conflation of “won’t debate these specific people” with “has left the argument” is itself a bad faith move—or, charitably, a sloppy one. Debate is one unit of rhetoric, and not an especially high-value one. A well-reasoned essay, read by ten thousand people who engage it seriously, does more epistemic work than a debate watched by a hundred thousand people waiting for a dunk.

## Good Standing

Who, and what, earns engagement? People with solid track records and subject matter expertise, principally. People show their colors in how they approach disagreement before they’ve made their case. Bad questions signal bad faith: leading, accusatory, premise-laden questions designed not to understand but to trap. Good questions don’t guarantee good faith, but they’re data points. Someone asking fair, on-topic, non-accusatory questions—trying to understand before trying to attack—has at least cleared the first bar.

The civility trap runs the other direction too. As [I’ve noted before](https://edokwin.substack.com/p/manners-masking-malice-how-niceness), polished manner is not evidence of good faith. Some of the most operationally bad faith behavior I’ve encountered has arrived wrapped in measured tones, flattery, and performed open-mindedness. The tell is what they’re actually getting at or angling for, underneath all that positioning. Whether they update with evidence. Good faith interlocutors who get something wrong apologize cleanly: “I was wrong, my bad.” Bad faith ones offer non-apologies—”I’m sorry if you were offended”—or escalate to meta-complaints about your tone. The *performance* of civility is not civility. It’s often heinous cruelty.

The demand that Harris debate antizionists is, in many cases, exactly this: **a polished ask designed to extract legitimacy for a position that hasn’t earned it.** Showing up and arguing is a concession of seriousness. It signals that the other side deserves to be taken seriously. When they don’t, the signal is false. Responsible creators should decline. Doing otherwise sends exactly the wrong signal to audiences about what the epistemic and moral baselines really are.

## What Refusal Is, and What It Isn’t

Principled refusal and cowardly avoidance get conflated all too often. To the bad faith critics, they look alike from the outside. This is because bad faith actors have always demanded microphones, even as they refused to engage their own critics; this asymmetry is one of their primary tools. Half the epistemic damage of the last decade was done by people who wouldn’t engage real scrutiny while demanding everyone engage theirs. So the Harris framework has to come and close the loophole.

This closes on the track record, not genetic fallacies or rank tribalism. The person invoking the right of refusal, of free association, can still be judged. Do they have a public record of making arguments, engaging criticism seriously, updating when wrong, and applying consistent standards? If so, what difference does an open or closed comment section make? What would a debate stream or adversarial podcast show? Nothing, except making the bad actors feel better, bigger, more legitimate.

If you won’t debate someone because you can’t answer their arguments, that’s cowardice. If you won’t debate someone because their track record disqualifies them as an honest interlocutor, and cuz the exchange would produce theater rather than truth, that’s judgment. The difference is visible over time. We must pay attention.

No one is owed your platform. Not your time, not your credibility, not the legitimacy that comes from being treated *as serious* by serious people. These are yours to extend or withhold. Harris and I agree that withholding them from people who haven’t earned them isn’t a failure of intellectual courage. It’s the very exercise of it.
