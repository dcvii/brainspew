An unusual community is taking shape in a suburb of Austin: a ranch-style house surrounded by trailer parks, a robotics facility and a drone-building site.

The site, known as Proto-Town, is a 1,200-acre campus in Lockhart, Texas, a short drive from Elon Musk’s [Tesla](https://www.wsj.com/market-data/quotes/TSLA) headquarters. It serves as a business commune for young tech founders and other entrepreneurs who all live and work there.

Founders and employees live together in the house or nearby trailer parks they call man camps. Workers at the dozen firms on site often eat meals as a group. Many work on their startups from morning to night.

Proto-Town founders Josh Farahzad, 26, and Merle Nye, 28, met while attending college at Duke. They worked at a variety of startups for years, and then met John Cyrier, a longtime Texas politician.

The three launched Proto-Town in 2024 with just under six figures of funding. Last month, the project was valued at around $100 million following its first fundraising round. Venture capitalist Josh Kushner and [Coinbase](https://www.wsj.com/market-data/quotes/COIN) co-founder Fred Ehrsam are investors, the founders say.

Aspiring software companies and AI firms are still flocking to San Francisco. But more robotics, energy and defense companies are setting up shop under the blistering Texan sun. Places like Proto-Town are the latest sign that Austin and its surroundings are becoming a base for these so-called “hard tech” companies.

In their downtime, the mix of Texans, company founders and engineers who live in Proto-Town like to ride around the flatlands on dirt bikes, head into Lockhart for barbecue or drive to downtown Austin to go two-step dancing.

Startups on the site are attracted to the state’s low regulation and the build-anything atmosphere of Proto-Town and the surrounding Caldwell County.

“They’re very willing to play ball,” said Ethan Blagg, a Proto-Town member, referring to Proto-Town and the county. His startup Dynamo is trying to build massive drones to carry 10,000-pound payloads.

Joining Proto-Town is a bit like rushing a fraternity. The founders welcome prospective companies to live and work on the site for a few days, and then decide whether they are a good fit. The more mature companies pay for leases on large plots of land to test their products, while some early-stage founders pay only room and board.

Many of Proto-Town’s occupants mirror the attitudes of a newer generation of founders who believe in a build-at-all-cost approach. They cite China’s manufacturing strength as an inspiration, and share aspirations to build an “American Shenzhen.”

Last fall, Daniel Weddle, 41, left his home of Bloomington, Ind., where he ran an event venue, to move to Proto-Town. He lives now with his partner and two dogs in Proto-Town’s trailer park.

His company, Terran, is testing a machine to build 3D-printed homes out of dirt and clay. Every morning, he lugs a five-gallon water tank into his trailer, and he eats lunch with a group of other founders on the site.

“I don’t have to shop for groceries, I don’t have to drive anywhere, my dogs don’t need leashes,” he said. “It’s a very simple life.”

Proto-Town traces its origins back to 2022, when Lockhart was a front-runner to host a $100 billion semiconductor facility for chip maker Micron. But Micron opted instead to build in Clay, N.Y. Lockhart, population about 20,000, was still reeling from that loss when the Proto-Town founders arrived.

“We’re going to build a city,” Farahzad recalled thinking at the time.

But first, he had to sell local politicians on his vision. Hoppy Haden, the county’s judge, said he was bewildered by their new-city proposal at first.

“I thought they were kind of crazy,” he said. “They look a bit older than my grandkids.”

The founders won over Haden and other politicians. The partners leased their first of three parcels of barren flatland for around $25,000 a month, anchored by a dilapidated ranch house. It was infested with so many scorpions that anyone entering the bathroom at night carried a flashlight.

Proto-Town’s brain trust came up with an unconventional recruitment strategy. They bought a decommissioned school bus, painted it bright red, and outfitted it with models of the proposed town, and Farahzad and Nye drove it around the country.

Over the next two years, startups began populating Proto-Town. Hard-tech industries such as manufacturing and defense are booming in Austin, giving projects like Proto-Town a much-needed tailwind. That includes a new [Samsung](#phrase-auto-company?ref=COMPANY%7C005930.SE;onlineSignificance=passing-mention) multibillion-dollar semiconductor facility, based around 30 miles northeast from Austin, and aerospace company Firefly.

In March, nuclear technology company Oklo said it would create a test reactor in Proto-Town. The company wants to increase the production of isotopes, which are used in cancer therapy and manufacturing applications, but are largely imported from outside the U.S.

Bedrock Robotics Chief Executive Boris Sofman visited the site last year, looking for land to test the company’s autonomous construction machinery, including excavators to dig ditches without human control. Bedrock was based around San Francisco with a few small test sites, but the team needed more room.

Now, up to 20 Bedrock employees work in Proto-Town, where the company has 40 acres. Other engineers based in San Francisco remotely connect to the machines in Texas to run tests.

Bedrock was drawn to Austin because of the nearby talent from SpaceX and Tesla, and it has become a subcontractor for other startups in the town.

“There’s a great ecosystem here,” Sofman said. “Getting that level of flexibility is really hard.”
