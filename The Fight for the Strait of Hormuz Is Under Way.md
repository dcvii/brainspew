After weeks of drift and wishful thinking, the United States appears to have begun the effort to reopen one of the world’s most critical shipping lanes.

Winston Churchill [may never](https://winstonchurchill.hillsdale.edu/americans-will-always-right-thing/) actually have said that “Americans can always be trusted to do the right thing, once all other possibilities have been exhausted.” But the aphorism has stuck in public memory for a reason. Its logic appears to apply perfectly to the Donald Trump administration’s attitude toward Iran’s closure of the Strait of Hormuz, where at last the United States appears to be in the beginning stages of forcing things open. Intense Iranian retaliation on Monday, including strikes on the United Arab Emirates and attacks targeting the U.S. Navy and shipping in the strait, suggests that this effort may come at the cost of ending the tenuous ceasefire in place since April 7. But it will be worth it—the current stalemate was economically untenable in the long run.

On Sunday, President Trump [announced that](https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/116512555123589170) the United States will “guide” the ships of other countries trapped in the Persian Gulf out via the strait. He dubbed the exercise “Project Freedom”—not an “operation,” mind you, but a “project”—language designed to avoid trouble with Congress and the War Power Resolution’s 60-day clock.

Nevertheless, according to the president, efforts to interfere with this “humanitarian” mission will be “dealt with forcefully.” Central Command followed up with its [own statement](https://x.com/CENTCOM/status/2051075238352355404?s=20), asserting that the “defensive mission” would be supported by “guided-missile destroyers, over 100 land and sea-based aircraft, multi-domain unmanned platforms, and 15,000 service members.”

---

Both the president and the Central Command notably did not describe the effort as an escort operation, but, on Monday morning, Central Command [announced that](https://x.com/CENTCOM/status/2051274596570050755?s=20) an unspecified number of American guided-missile destroyers had transited the Strait of Hormuz into the Persian Gulf (the first such report since shortly after the ceasefire was announced in April), while two American-flagged commercial vessels that had been trapped in the gulf since the start of the war had made it safely out. Iran, for its part,attacked shipping in the strait and claimed to have struck American naval vessels with missiles. Central Command denied that any American ships had been struck, but said that six Iranian boats had been sunk while interfering with shipping. Perhaps most escalatory of all, Iran attacked targets across the United Arab Emirates.

The upshot is this: Two months after the closure of the strait by Iran, the United States has taken what appears to be a substantive but still limited step in the direction of forcing it open. Iran has retaliated energetically. President Trump will have to decide if this means the ceasefire is over—but even if he does not, to gain the upper hand in the ongoing standoff with what remains of the Iranian regime, he will need to continue the work of clearing the strait—ensuring traffic passes safely in both directions and punishing Iranian interference—even at the risk of further Iranian retaliation.

---

It was always going to [take time](https://www.thefp.com/p/how-to-reopen-the-strait-of-hormuz) to set the military conditions for reopening the strait, but what has been perplexing is the repeatedly expressed wishes of President Trump that the status quo for maritime traffic be restored by someone other than the U.S. Throughout March, the president noted that the closure was more a problem for other countries than for America. That may be true—but it is still a major problem for America. To take just one example beyond the elevated price of gasoline (which will absolutely go even higher the longer the strait is closed), America gets roughly 10 percent of [its aluminum](https://www.wsj.com/business/autos/auto-industry-car-makers-aluminum-prices-supply-c6fbe348) from the Persian Gulf. It would be oversimplifying things to say that the purchasers of Ford trucks are the MAGA base—but in the aggregate they probably didn’t turn out to vote for Kamala Harris.

Still, two weeks into the war, the president [said that](https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/trump-says-many-countries-will-send-warships-keep-strait-hormuz-open-2026-03-14/?utm_source=chatgpt.com) “the Countries of the World that receive Oil through the Hormuz Strait must take care of that passage” with help, but not leadership, from the United States. As the weeks went on, the rhetorical focus shifted to the strait reopening organically: “When this conflict is over, the strait will open up naturally,” the president said in a televised address at the start of April. We were left to infer that this referred to a negotiated outcome with the Iranians, to their backing down in the face of overwhelming American threats to destroy their economy, or possibly to the destruction of Iranian military capabilities.

Two months after the closure of the strait by Iran, the United States has taken what appears to be a substantive but still limited step in the direction of forcing it open.

None of these scenarios came to pass—the strait did not, in fact, reopen itself. The president threatened Iran’s power plants and bridges—indeed, [the destruction](https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/116363336033995961) of Iranian “civilization”—if the strait was not reopened. But Iranian civilization survived, and a ceasefire took hold on April 7, with the president claiming that Iran had agreed to reopen the strait as a part of the deal. They had not, and did not. The president allowed the two-week deadline for the expiration of the ceasefire to pass without action, and set no further deadlines—though he did impose what will in time be a painful blockade of Iran’s ports, leading to the conditions of stalemate and the game of economic chicken that persisted until last night.

Has the [Battle of Hormuz](https://www.thefp.com/p/the-battle-of-hormuz-approaches) begun? Just as Iran was highly unlikely ever to agree to reopen the strait itself without receiving concessions unacceptable to the Trump administration, so it is unlikely to accept this “humanitarian” and “defensive” effort without even further retaliation.

The possibility of the outright end of the ceasefire is very real, in which case the region will need to brace for further rounds of missile and drone attacks from Iran—but the Islamic Republic will also need to brace for what could be a devastating round of strikes on economic and leadership targets by the United States and Israel.

The risk is worth taking. Whatever the pain imposed by the American blockade of Iranian ports, the current leadership of Iran is unlikely to be persuaded to accept American demands regarding its nuclear program and the strait in time for the massive global (and domestic!) economic damage caused by Iran’s closure of the strait to be avoided. The leverage they enjoy through closing the strait needs to be eliminated. [Mr. President](https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/116351998782539414), as you once put it yourself, “Open the Fuckin’ Strait.”
