### A former teammate's account.

In 2012 I was playing professional baseball for the Marlins, specifically the Jupiter Hammerheads in Jupiter, Florida. I was a closer. I wore number 16. I had been wearing it since high school, where I struck out 16 batters in a game often enough that the number became mine. I went on to play college baseball, win a national championship, get drafted a time or two etc. under the same number.

José Fernández joined the Marlins that year. The first thing you need to know about José is that Major League Baseball listed him at 6’3”. He was not 6’3”. He was 6’0”. They spread his weight across three imaginary inches to make it look right on paper. In person he was a tree trunk. Two hundred pounds of legs and forty pounds of an adult toddler’s upper body. Built to drive off a rubber, not to fit a prototype.

We both pitched under Joe Coleman, who struck out Mickey Mantle four times in a single game. I say this so you understand the level. Joe Coleman does not use tobacco normally, he chucks kayak grape into his mouth and then whatever happens happens. He also attributes Hank Aarons success to rampant abuse of “greenies” - a story for another time.

José was kind at first. He bought everyone lunch. His whole family came to every game: grandmother, mother, cousins, all of them, sitting in the sweltering Florida heat no matter how small the game, supervising their kid in this American machine. It reminded me of Carlos Correa’s family over in the Astros organization, whose entire extended family was present at all times, as if minor league baseball were a thing that required witnesses to prove your bloodline had produced anything of worth.

José liked warming up with me. He thought my slider was good. It was. It was straight filth. My grip eliminated the traditional dot so the pitch comes out of your hand looking like a two-seam fastball, my fastball, and then it isn’t. I had the strongest grip strength in the entire Marlins organization. I won a shirt for it during spring training. It was embarrassing but it is of note that the pitch could not be thrown with the same conviction by anyone else’s hand.

I had shown that slider to two people in my life. Burch Smith (Padres), my old best friend who owes me his entire career for getting him into college baseball & Andrew Heaney (Angels), my road roommate for the 2012 season, my warm-up partner, my good friend from college, a very good man. That is the complete list. Baseball fans will understand what this means. At this level of the game a pitch is not a technique. It is a private weapon. You show it to people you trust with your career.

José only really threw a cutter, and a slurve-type pitch that always mutated into a curveball that lacked bite (this is a very difficult combination to throw for a young pitcher). He needed a real slider. He asked me in the locker room. He had a way of asking that was not quite asking. He would bump against me after a shower, hushed, glancing around to make sure no one was watching whenever he needed anything. He made sure no one could see him do it because asking would mean admitting he wanted to learn something from a lower draft pick, and he was a first-round draft pick, and first-round picks do not learn from lower draft picks. I understood this. He showered in a towel, or sliding shorts. I am more comfortable naked than in clothes, and it was not uncommon to see me walking around naked - being totally naked in front of reporters, through the dining area, playing ping pong etc. All of this was new to him.

There came a day where he stood there and stared at me for the ten minutes I showered, working up the nerve. I am from Texas in all ways, I am used to it. A man only stares at another man while he’s naked if he wants something from him. I pursed my lips and let thick Copenhagen spit roll down my lips and chin in the shower and fall down to careen down off of what he was staring at, giving him a show. He blushed profusely, I remember this because of his tan Cuban skin turning bright red.

He bumped into me again while I was drying off. I showed him. I thought: this is a first-round talent. And I would hope for the same. So, when he did his signature “bump into you gently, I have a question” I showed him the grip to my slider in the locker room with his hair still wet as he stood next to me staring down at a baseball enclosed in my grip that I held right in front of my endowment for dramatic effect. Out of the kindness of my heart. My pride and joy. My failsafe when times get hard. My secret weapon. I handed it to a kid whose grandmother was sitting in the stands proud that her family wasn’t - relatively speaking, meaningless.

*Two weeks later José Fernández became an asshole to anyone who was not a star or present big leaguer.*

I was injured by then. Rib chondritis from pitching, and I was already throwing with a broken hand: broken on Vince Wheeland’s (Astros) face at Oklahoma State, where I was team captain and punched him square in his bowling ball of a head for refusing to do conditioning after he tried to bow up to me. So I was out there flinging my arm at ninety to a hundred miles per hour hoping my hand stayed together, ribs lighting up every time I breathed.

José came to me after a game and told me to give him my number.

Fuck no, I said.

He said it belongs on someone useful.

I see, I said.

When I had my jersey in my locker, while I showered, he took it. Just took it. Held it and said it was his. He really wanted 16. I realized I was dealing with a child. When I went to grab it back he shoved me.

*Terrible idea.*

We cussed each other out, in Spanish, English, maybe some French. I was about to break my hand on his face the same way I’d broken it on Wheeland’s. And then for reasons I do not know I went quiet. I thought about what terrifies a Cuban more than anything in that moment. And I somberly stated that I curse 16.

José lost his mind. *Diablo. Coño.* The whole vocabulary coupled with a panicked look and desperation.

I walked off. Fuming, displaying placidness.

---

The next time I saw José Fernández was spring training, 2013.

I was walking past one of the fields on the third-base side, right along the fence. José was on the mound wearing my number. He was facing Mike Stanton.

I heard the thud before I understood it. Flesh, and then a whimper. A ninety-five-mile-per-hour fastball to the face. Twenty million dollars of damage in a single pitch. Stanton went down. José squatted on the mound and put his hands to his head and cried. I was a dugout’s distance away, staring at him.

I kept walking. People crowded in. I needed a shower.

---

I went to Greensboro for the 2013 season and pitched a phenomenal second half of the year. José went to Miami and won the National League Rookie of the Year. We both wore 16. The same number on two jerseys, one of them cursed or not cursed depending on what you believe.

I got tired of the game. Not the pitching. The fake war. The war men that are too afraid to go to war fight in a safe little stadium, pretending to be warriors. I also got tired of the rest of it. The premadonnas. The groupies waiting outside the stadium to get hopefully impregnated and tethered to a rising star’s bank account after every game. The affairs and the filth, constant, all the time. I retired to go become a Green Beret. I don’t relive baseball much. I had a degree to finish, and far more education to pursue, which I valued more than the game, the Army was poised, and did give me all of this and more for the price of blood.

---

On September 25, 2016, a thirty-two-foot boat called *Kaught Looking* hit the Government Cut north rock jetty off Miami Beach at sixty-five miles per hour at three in the morning. José Fernández was at the helm. His blood alcohol was twice the legal limit. He had cocaine in his system. He was twenty-four years old. They found him under the overturned hull, pinned between the t-top and a boulder.

I was at Fort Rucker, Alabama. Flight school. We got thirty minutes of phone access once a week. I learned about it from a text message during that window. SportsCenter confirmed it.

Sucks.

The Miami Marlins retired number 16 the following day. First number the franchise had ever retired. No Marlin will wear it again.

*I’m happy they retired my number.*
