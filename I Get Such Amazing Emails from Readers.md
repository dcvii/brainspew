### Here are some of them

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!HHDD!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F27069ff0-70d4-470b-80a4-240458ddaf08_2400x800.jpeg)

I hear stories from readers—some of them sad, but many bring me great joy. Below is a very inspiring email I received earlier this week.

So many of us are trying to live more mindfully. And it’s hard to do nowadays. We rely on technology, but it has turned on us. It’s intrusive and manipulative—isolating its users, while promoting passivity and addictive behavior.

I’m trying to help people live better lives in this hostile environment. That’s the main purpose behind *The Honest Broker*. When I get an email like this, it makes me feel like it’s not all in vain.

I’m sharing this with permission—but leaving out the author’s name.

> Dear Ted,
> 
> I am writing to express my gratitude for your work and for being an Honest Broker. In an attempt to illustrate my thanks, I’d like to briefly describe the wonderful effect you have had on my life.
> 
> Since discovering your Substack in 2021-2022, I have begun to turn my life around by making significant improvements to my quality of life and, subsequently, the lives of those around me. This is in no small part due to the influence of your books and articles.
> 
> The feeling I frequently get when I read your writing is along the lines of “He gets it,” or “Someone finally said it,” and “This needed to be said.” I would describe this to a friend as “Ted is able to express in words things that I feel and know to be true but am unable to describe myself.” Maybe that’s just the mark of a greater intellect or of a talented writer in general. But, to me personally, it is as if I had an understanding friend. This is something I needed and came to me at a time when I felt I had none.
> 
> For the past several years you have served as a much needed role model. I greatly admire your world view and relate to your experience as a musician finding your way. In 2022-2023, while reading *Music to Raise the Dead*, a copy of *From Ritual to Romance* jumped out at me from the racks at a thrift store. Around the same time, I picked up a copy of *The Golden Bough* and *Battle for the Mind* by W. Sargent as well as *Solitary Confinement* by Christopher Burney. These ideas were swirling about in my head, as I was also drinking heavily as usual, when a line from *The Honest Broker* jumped out at me. One of your friends had recently gotten sober, and you mentioned that it had been “deserving of your highest praise.” When I read that line, some willingness appeared within me that was not traceable before. At that moment I considered seeking help for my drinking problem.
> 
> As I write this, I have been sober for 2 years and 10 months. I have been wanting to write this letter to you for about as long. In lieu of going into more detail, I want to thank you again for your amazing body of work that influenced me, gained my trust, and continues to give me hope.
> 
> I have so many questions I would like to ask, but I feel selfish writing them here. I am sure you will continue to inspire myself and others with your writing, and for that I am content.

This serves as eloquent testimony for the real purpose of arts and humanities, and why I’m such a fervent advocate for them. You don’t pursue them to pass tests or drop names at a cocktail party—but to live a wise life, a mindful life, a good life.

There are people everywhere who are doing just that. Their stories aren’t told in the media or made into motion pictures, but they are far better role models than movie stars or pop singers.

This email came from a conservatory-trained pianist at an early stage in her career:

> If you can share, I’d be really interested to know: You probably get many requests to participate in various projects—how do you decide which projects to say yes to and which to decline?

My response:

> First, I always try to give priority to my ‘big picture’ long term projects—even though they may not bring in any income at the time. This can cause a lot of pain and suffering in the short term, and external circumstances can force our hands at times. But if we don’t fight for our long term goals, who will?
> 
> For a while, I had a rule that I would always spent at least one day per week on projects that were my own choice, not something anyone hired me to do. Over time I shifted even more of my focus and energy to my pet projects. (Nowadays I turn down almost everything that doesn’t fit with my own objectives—but I didn’t get to that degree of autonomy overnight.) We all need to be able to pay our bills, so sometimes there are limits on our ability to manage your own daily schedule.
> 
> But you should spent a significant amount of time thinking about where you want to be in 5, 10, 20 years. And then make sure you are taking steps in that direction.
> 
> There were long periods in my life when I had very little control over my schedule. But even in those difficult days, I found some time every week to chase my dreams. That might mean pursuing them on weekends or late at night, but I refused to give up on them. Many of those dreams did became realities—but only because I never abandoned them, and kept building my pathway to them week after week.
> 
> Probably the key difference between me and most other writers is: (1) I go to great lengths to avoid projects ‘assigned’ to me by other people, maybe almost to an extreme, (2) I am always thinking about what I want to do as part of my long term vocational plan, and (3) I make decisions that others tell me are stupid, because I’m willing to walk away from a significant short term opportunity. But I know I need to do this if I hope to achieve the higher goals I’ve set for myself.
> 
> I hope this helps.

This next question came from an esteemed music writer: He wanted to know how I had “defied the odds” in my relationship with Stan Getz, who is frequently portrayed as an irritable and irascible individual—and he mentions that he had heard this from many sources, including Getz’s ex-wife Monica.

Here’s my response.

> Stan is not fairly portrayed—he had good times and bad times. I saw both, but I prefer to remember and talk about the positives. Zoot Sims came closest to the truth when he said (in an oft-quoted comment) that Stan Getz is a “nice bunch of guys.” So I don’t think I really defied the odds, although I may have seen more of the positive side than most.
> 
> At the time I met him, Stan was going to AA meetings, trying to get his life together, and making friends among the Stanford faculty. It was the first time in his life he had genuine friends, he remarked at one point. There were setbacks and dark moods—often very dark. But the positive side of his personality was sweet and almost childlike.
> 
> At times you absolutely had to call him *charming* —a word rarely applied to Getz nowadays, but a label he genuinely earned. Even Monica must have seen that at some point or she wouldn’t have married him. So don’t believe all the hype about the evil Stan Getz. Like Shaft—and all of us—he was a complicated man.

A reader of my book *Healing Songs* asked me how he can become an actual practitioner of healing music. I responded as follows:

> In *Healing Songs*, I wrote about the history of healing music. But in order to become a *practitioner,* you need to find a teacher—or probably several teachers. If musical healing were my chosen vocation, I would track down practitioners in different aspects of musical intervention (chanting, singing bowls, Monroe Institute, binaural beats, drum circles, Cymatics, Tomatis method, Nordoff-Robbins, 8D music, etc.) and pay them for lessons. Or, even better, volunteer to work as an apprentice.
> 
> Of course, there are already degree programs in music therapy. They are useful, too—but they leave out some of the most important information. The fields of study I mention above have not been completely legitimized (or even partially legitimized in some cases), so they won’t be taught in most classrooms. But each one has some kernels of wisdom worth learning. Hence, if I were planning a career in therapeutic music, I might bypass the credentialing programs completely. Or maybe I’d get the credential, but also spend several years learning alternative practices.
> 
> If you knew both—music therapy and alternative aural healing traditions—you have a powerful combination, with expertise few could match. That could be the foundation for an amazing vocation.
> 
> But the bottom line is that you can’t learn this stuff from a book. You find mentors and teachers, and you need to be ready to devote years to learning from them.

A reader with background in the visual arts asked me why these get so little attention in the current day.

My response:

> What's happened to visual arts is especially insidious. Instagram photos and social media images have replaced painting and sculpture in the public sphere. People still crave visual art, but they are given AI slop and meme photos instead.
> 
> The problem is that this will spread elsewhere in the culture. It’s already happening.

Here’s another question from the reader who asked above on how I decide on specific projects: “What do you think someone like me, a classically-trained musician with an interest in "being useful" to the world (I know there are many ways to interpret "being useful), might consider doing in the music world?”

My response:

> I've always believed that the universe is constantly showing us ways where we can make a difference—but we tend to block these out, so we rarely even notice what it's saying to us.
> 
> I know many musicians who have found extraordinary ways to have a positive impact on the world. One friend of mine goes into hospices and plays harp music for the dying–she is almost like an angel in that regard. I know others who travel on spiritual or healing pathways through music. Others build entire communities around song. And, of course, there are countless ways to change lives through music education.
> 
> In fact, if I were starting all over, I would consider devoting an entire career to finding ways to teach music that are more engaging and inspiring than today's pedagogy. For most beginning music students, lessons are a drudgery, but it shouldn't be that way. The encounter with your music teacher should be a highlight of your week, but how rarely that is the case.
> 
> I'm sure you will find the right path. You are certainly asking the right questions. Good luck on your journey.

The next email came from a young reader who needs to decide on a vocation. He is considering a career as a college teacher, but worries that universities now feel dead and stultifying. He asked me what he should do.

Here’s my response:

> When I was younger, I thought it would be great to teach at a university—and I even did it for a few years, and enjoyed it. But the world has changed. Universities are now mostly centers of group think, feudalistic hierarchies, and intense bureaucracies. If I talk to ten people there, nine of them will typically express the identical opinion in almost the exact same words—you’d think you had stepped into some cult or fringe religion where dogma is embraced as revealed truth.
> 
> I think the tenure process contributes to this—which is strange because tenure was invented to do the opposite. But it’s now more like hazing to get into a secret society where, once you’re admitted, rigid rules are imposed with ruthless uniformity. But the bureaucratization of admins is an even worse problem. It has turned a previously fresh and independent vocation into the exact opposite.
> 
> But let me be more realistic. If I were younger, I might consider occasional opportunistic stints in teaching—show up as a guest lecturer for a few days or weeks or months. That could still be fun, and have an impact. I’m often invited as a guest to various campuses, and almost always have a good time. But I would never trust those institutions as a longterm home for a creative, free-wheeling vocation. In the current moment, it’s better to work outside of the legacy institutions.
> 
> But I’m just saying what’s right for me—others might have a completely different view. For people who know how to ‘work the system’ and rise in a feudalistic bureaucracy, a university might be a great environment.

One reader wrote to give me advice, and it was a good tip—on how to manipulate the streaming platforms in order to find more interesting music. So I’m sharing it with you.

> I created a new schema for discovering music. I create a new playlist every month. I start with a strange, obscure song that I come across randomly (non-algorithmically) and I use that song as an algortithm-seed. I listen, add what I like, and then let the algorithm grow from there. After each month, I have a playlist full of fun, unexpected music.

A reader, age 46, wrote me about his to learn how to play Bach, especially *The Well-Tempered Clavier*, on the piano. He’s willing to devote the next decade to this, but worries whether he can make reasonable progress as a late-learner in music. He asked what advice I had for practicing and developing keyboard technique.

I responded as follows:

> If you're willing to invest a thousand hours into Bach, I'm sure you will see noteworthy results. And if you manage to put in three or four thousand hours, it will have a transformative impact on your life.
> 
> My son is a good example. When his college sent him home for lockdown, he started playing Bach on the piano every day. He has now been doing this for more than five years, and has made great progress. But he had to put in the hours.
> 
> You're young enough to make this happen. It took me roughly 5-10 years to learn how to play jazz piano. It will take my son the same amount of time to become a skilled performer of Bach. And you can do the same. Just pace yourself while maintaining focus and discipline.
> 
> For finger dexterity, I relied on Hanon. But my son doesn't do any exercises—he just plays Bach, and believes (probably rightly) that this music is itself a method of improving finger independence and strength.
> 
> The challenge is really time management. I know from experience how busy a person is in their forties—in some ways it's the hardest decade of our lives—but if you can devote the time, the results will happen. And even if you delayed this project until your 50s, it's still an achievable goal.

Another reader asked my advice on whether to launch a teaching initiative on Substack.

> Your question reminds me of people who ask whether they should become professional musicians. My reaction is typically cautious, and that often surprises them. They assume that my love of music will lead me to enlist others into the vocation. But even the question they ask strikes me as a little funny.
> 
> People usually don't *choose* these things—they simply embrace them as part of their destiny. You become a musician (or dancer or poet or painter or whatever) because you are so excited by these pursuits you don't really feel you have a choice.
> 
> Writing is like that, whether on Substack or in other settings. You don't choose it; it chooses you. Anyone who *chooses* to be a writer has probably made a mistake.
> 
> So when I hear from people who are considering Substack, I try to gauge how committed they are. Many have a hope that just launching on the platform will give their writing careers a big boost, and solve a hundred other problems along the way. But the blunt truth is that writing on Substack is not much different from writing elsewhere—you get results after lots and lots of work. Or, in some instances, even the hard work never pays off. The platform itself can help in some ways, but it's more of a tool than a solution.
> 
> On the other hand, if the idea of launching a new writing and teaching venture on Substack makes you feel passionate and committed and full of energy and excitement, it’s probably the right place for you.
> 
> So in this case, you should look inside yourself for an answer, not to me. In any event good luck on your life's endeavors.

After someone mocked Edward Gibbon (1737-1794), author of the massive *Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire*, I mentioned that he deserves credit as of the great prose stylists in English, and added that I had learned some writing tricks from reading him. I was pressed for details on this, so I responded as follows:

> I’ve read a couple thousand pages of Gibbon, and mostly for the prose style. So I would need a whole essay to talk about what I learned from this project. But I will call attention to his skill in moving from the particular to the general in a single sentence, as well as his nonpareil skill with modifiers. (The use of ‘nonpareil’ there is an example.)
> 
> But the thing I liked most about Mr. G was a particular kind of subordinate clause, usually inserted in the middle of a sentence, in which he simultaneously provides both a comparison and a contrast. I would need to go back and review his work to find examples, but they’re everywhere. It’s something along the lines of: “The Scythians, who matched the valor of the Imperial troops while lacking the perspicacity to emulate their stolid legionary discipline, were defeated over the course of three sanguinary hours....etc. etc.”
> 
> Do you see what he does there? I’ve never encountered another author who uses this kind of clause so deftly—and it not only flows nicely, and sweetly breaks up the sentence, but conveys a lot of information very quickly.
> 
> He probably got this from reading so much Latin prose. His sentences feel as solid as the Roman Pantheon—where, somehow, the cement still holds everything together after two thousand years.

By the way, that passage ending in ‘three sanguinary hours’ was not actually written by Gibbon. It’s my imitation of his style. If you read enough of it, you start emulating it.
