Willard Van Orman Quine, one of the most influential philosophers of the twentieth century, challenged long-standing assumptions about truth and meaning, like many philosophers who had drawn a distinction between two kinds of truth. Take, for example, the sentences “All bachelors are unmarried males” and “All vixens are female foxes.” Both are true, but why? It is tempting to say that they are true because of what the words bachelor and vixen mean. These sentences are true in virtue of meaning: the expression bachelor has the same meaning as unmarried male, so “all bachelors are unmarried males” is guaranteed to be true simply by the meanings of the words. Likewise, “all vixens are female foxes” is true for the same reason. Because these truths rely solely on meaning, there is no way that experience could falsify them. Philosophers call such truths analytic truths.  
  
Analytic truths are contrasted with synthetic truths, which are true because they describe how the world happens to be. For example, “All bachelors live in the vicinity of the Earth” is also true, but not in virtue of meaning. Its truth depends on the facts of the world: if bachelors did not live on Earth, the sentence would be false. Historically, philosophers from Kant to Hume and Leibniz recognized this distinction. Kant in particular emphasized the difference, although he did not claim that all a priori necessary truths are analytic. By the mid-twentieth century, there was broad consensus that analytic and synthetic truths were categorically different.  
  
Quine, however, presented a powerful argument challenging the analytic–synthetic distinction, which had a profound impact on philosophy. He questioned what it means for a statement to be “true by virtue of meaning alone.” If “all bachelors are unmarried males” is analytic, it must be true just because of the meanings of the words, not because of any empirical facts. But how do we define “meaning” rigorously? Attempts to define analytic truth in terms of synonymy, definition, or necessity fail because the terms end up referring to each other in a circular way. There is no independent, non-circular way to identify what makes a truth analytic.  
  
Quine also argued that our beliefs about the world form a web, and that statements face the test of experience not individually but collectively. As he wrote, “Our statements about the external world face the tribunal of sense experience, not individually, but only as a corporate body.” To illustrate, suppose someone believes the Earth is flat. Evidence appears that challenges this: a sailor claims to have sailed around the world and returned to the same place, and observers note that ships disappear hull-first over the horizon, suggesting the sea curves. These pieces of evidence may cast doubt on the flat-Earth theory, but they are not automatically decisive. One might doubt the sailor or try to explain the ship observation through unusual behavior of light. As more evidence accumulates, the belief might eventually be abandoned, but it could always be preserved by making adjustments elsewhere in the web of beliefs.  
  
According to Quine, beliefs traditionally called analytic are simply those that are most deeply entrenched in this web. They are the beliefs we are slowest to revise, but, in principle, they are not immune to revision. Even supposedly necessary truths can be reconsidered if the rest of the web requires adjustment. Some have even suggested that developments in quantum mechanics might demand revisions to classical logic, illustrating the potential revisability of even the most entrenched beliefs.  
  
Quine further illustrated the problem of defining analyticity with a helpful analogy. Suppose he defines a wibble as a collection of woobles, a wooble as an adult zoofa, and a zoofa as “things that, when fully grown, make up wibbles.” One has defined the terms, but still has no idea what a wibble actually is. Analytic truth is similar: defining it in terms of other notions like synonymy, meaning, or necessity does not clarify what it is. One might suggest that examples—“all bachelors are unmarried” or “all vixens are female foxes”—can explain analytic truth, but Quine points out that this is question-begging. Why assume these statements are unrefutable? We might struggle to imagine circumstances under which they would be false, but struggling to imagine does not mean such circumstances cannot exist. Since all statements are verifiable to experience collectively, even those traditionally called analytic are revisable. To use these examples to define analytic truth would be circular.  
  
As a result of Quine’s arguments, the notion of analyticity has largely, though not entirely, fallen out of philosophical favor. His work demonstrates that the distinction between analytic and synthetic truths is far less clear-cut than previously thought, and that what philosophers once considered truths guaranteed by meaning alone are better understood as highly entrenched beliefs within a complex, interdependent web of knowledge.
