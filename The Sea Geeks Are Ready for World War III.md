***Ships are [burning](https://www.reuters.com/world/cargo-ship-hit-by-projectile-strait-hormuz-crew-evacuates-2026-03-11/) in the Strait of Hormuz. There are [rumors](https://www.reuters.com/world/middle-east/us-energy-secretary-deletes-post-about-navy-escorting-vessel-through-strait-2026-03-10/) that the Iranians are laying underwater mines. An American submarine has torpedoed an enemy warship for the [first time](https://edition.cnn.com/2026/03/05/world/video/pentagon-us-submarine-strikes-iranian-warship-indian-ocean-watson-hk-digvid#:~:text=Link%20Copied!&text=time%20since%20WWII-,Link%20Copied!,international%20naval%20conference%20in%20India.) since the Second World War. Oceans are now battlefields.***

***But can America triumph in this arena? We’re not making enough ships—and the ones we have are out of date. Last April, President Donald Trump signed an executive order to “restore America’s maritime dominance”—but the bureaucracy of the “Big Five” defense contractors is making it hard to do that.***

***Enter: the ocean-trepreneurs.***

***An Indian-born NASA-trained engineer who’s building flying boats for the Navy. A former Amazon employee who’s making self-piloting seaplanes. An Irish guy who’s making underwater drones that, he says, could help take out the Iranian mines rumored to be planted in the Persian Gulf. Suzy Weiss spoke to them in San Francisco, where the great Pacific Ocean meets the West’s best technicians.***

***You’re not going to want to miss this.***

SAN FRANCISCO — It all started with the fighter jets doing tricks over the bay. It was San Francisco Fleet Week, the annual fall festival when the Navy show squadron known as the Blue Angels storms into the Bay Area to show off their precision and prowess, and to keep public morale high.

“It was the coolest thing ever,” said David Zagaynov. He was watching in 2023, with his roommates. They were all software engineers at Amazon and Lockheed Martin, and spent their workdays locked behind computer screens. But as they watched planes with names like “Super Hornet” flipping around above the Pacific, he said, “We were like: ‘ *Why can’t we build planes?’* ”

I met Zagaynov just over two years later, in the San Francisco warehouse of the company they started after this epiphany. It’s called Poseidon Aerospace, and it makes unmanned seaplanes. As we perched on the side of something that looks like a massive, oddly shaped hot tub—but is actually one half of a fuselage—Zagaynov told me nowadays he “wouldn’t be caught dead” building software. “If I spend so much of my time and effort building something and pouring my life into it, I want it to be something meaningful,” he said, “and not just, like, back-end optimization for some small business to operate with 1-percent greater efficiency.”

Poseidon’s seaplanes are named after birds—there’s a Seagull, a Heron, an Egret—and they can be used to ferry food to islands in an archipelago, or supplies to an oil rig. (Aaron Wojack for *The Free Press)*

While many of America’s greatest minds are obsessed with creating artificial intelligence that will augment white-collar jobs—building ways to do online banking better or making online agents that can talk to other online agents—Poseidon, which [raised $11 million in November](https://www.poseidonaero.com/news/poseidon-aerospace-announces-11m-fundraise-to-build-unmanned-cargo-aircraft), is producing machines that can do things in the real world. The seaplanes are named after birds—there’s a Seagull, a Heron, an Egret—and they can be used to ferry food to islands in an archipelago, or supplies to an oil rig. Or even weapons to an aircraft carrier. (The planes are remotely operated, via Starlink.) The government could theoretically store and launch drones from a Poseidon plane that floats miles offshore. In fact, the company’s website has an [entire section](https://www.poseidonaero.com/defense) dedicated to “POSEIDON DEFENSE,” which includes a map that shows how a plane in the sea around the southern tip of Japan could reach Taiwan, Guam, and our allies in South Korea. It is overlaid with text that reads: “BRINGING AMERICA’S ARSENAL TO OUR ADVERSARIES’ DOORSTEP.”

When I met Zagaynov, he told me that for the past few decades, “We’ve been fighting a lot of sand wars in the Middle East, and not a lot of water wars.” But since then, recent developments in the Persian Gulf show that—to quote Zagaynov—“The sea is hot right now.” Last week, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth announced that the U.S. military had used a torpedo to [sink an Iranian warship](https://www.cnn.com/2026/03/04/politics/us-submarine-iran-warship#:~:text=Off,force%20also%20joined%20rescue%20efforts.%E2%80%9D) off the coast of Sri Lanka, for the first time since the Second World War. (One of 30 Iranian ships we’ve sunk since the beginning of the war.) At the same time, shipping vessels traveling through the Red Sea are on high alert—the Iranian-backed Houthis have a history of attacking commercial vessels in transit there—while the Strait of Hormuz is at a standstill, jeopardizing the flow of oil from the Gulf to the rest of the world. The result is economic unease, and a new awareness that our loose grip on the waterways of the world makes us fragile.

This was true even before this latest conflict, though. In the last couple years, both Russia and China have taken to [severing underwater cables](https://www.forbes.com/sites/jillgoldenziel/2025/02/13/law-doesnt-protect-undersea-cables-russia-and-china-know-it/) —in the Baltic Sea and the Taiwan Strait respectively, creating chaos for the companies that maintain them and the economies that rely on them. Nearly all of the internet is reliant on this infrastructure. The Caribbean, where President Donald Trump has [bombed speedboats](https://www.thefp.com/p/killing-narco-speedboat-survivors) allegedly owned by cartels, and deployed a naval fleet, has become a front line in a heated-up war on drugs. And a few months ago, the Chinese military ran drills with their so-called “ [shadow navy,](https://www.reuters.com/graphics/USA-CHINA/TAIWAN-INVASION/zjpqdekmlvx/)” a fleet of civilian ships that experts saw as a kind of dry run for an invasion of Taiwan.

The naval historian [Alfred Thayer Mahan wrote](https://www.digitalhistory.uh.edu/disp_textbook.cfm?smtID=3&psid=4044) that “whoever rules the waves rules the world”—and if that’s the case, then America, despite its splashy recent wins in the Gulf, is in big trouble. China [has the largest navy in the world](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/RL33153) with over 370 battle-force ships, while the U.S. Navy only has about 290. Chinese shipyards produce new military vessels at a significantly faster rate, and that gap widens when you include commercial vessels; China pumped out about 1,000 large commercial ships in 2023 while the U.S. made less than 10. Ship stock is low, and well-laid plans to remedy that seem to [always fall apart](https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2025/12/11/opinion/editorials/us-military-industry-waste.html); they’re billions over budget and years delayed, wrecked by the bureaucracy of the “Big Five” defense contractors, shoddy supply chains, and a shortage of tradesmen to actually build them. Hence why, last April, Trump signed an [executive order](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/04/restoring-americas-maritime-dominance/) to “restore America’s maritime dominance,” to reshore shipbuilding, and to increase our commercial fleet.

Enter: the start-up founders who are dreaming up vessels that are faster, better, and cheaper to make—in the hopes of getting a piece of the so-called “ [blue economy](https://www.lse.ac.uk/granthaminstitute/explainers/what-is-the-blue-economy/).” It’s not just about making America battle ready; it’s about making the U.S. less vulnerable, in an increasingly unstable world. Think: [underwater data centers](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/networkocean) to power the AI that’s essential for maintaining the global edge, or deep-sea mining start-ups that extract industrial metals like iron and cobalt that lay thousands of meters below sea level. The founders of these companies consider their projects the spiritual successors to the Apollo missions—sea, they say, is the new space—or perhaps it’s the Manhattan Project.

Poseidon, which raised $11 million in November, is producing machines that can do things in the real world. (Aaron Wojack for *The Free Press)*

Whatever it is, there’s a sense of urgency about it. Zagaynov hopes to have the plane we’re sitting on up in the air, and landing on water, in the next couple of months. Nearby, there’s a toaster oven and a hair dryer for drying resin, evidence of what Zagaynov describes as the “cowboy engineering” the company uses to move as fast as possible. “You could spend thousands of dollars on the ‘right tool’ from some random vendor in the middle of America,” he said, “or you can just get that thing done.”

While the Pentagon [wrangles with Anthropic](https://www.theatlantic.com/technology/2026/03/pentagon-anthropic-dispute/686307/) —and [negotiates with OpenAI](https://thehill.com/policy/technology/5774970-openai-pentagon-caitlin-kalinowski/) —about how to draft artificial intelligence into warfare, the ocean perhaps seems like a strange obsession for a tech bro: It’s real, and rough. Dario Amodei and Sam Altman ponder the ethics, the philosophy, and the code; engineers like Zagaynov are wrestling with the elements, physics, and ecology—the dreaded moisture and salt water that can instantly render fancy robots with cutting-edge software into hunks of sinking metal. “Things go wrong. If you’re flying something, and it’s 1,000 miles away, you can’t just [call triple A](https://northeast.aaa.com/membership/join/?solicit_cd=11745&cmpid=int_pd_googl_mxx-acq-33poff-2free-rdsd&ef_id=CjwKCAjwyMnNBhBNEiwA-Kcgu3CdfkP4Cei6XtSVkTJLm0N-NUkW1hgUTfswnaJrWvOpNXDOAptTZBoC8MoQAvD_BwE:G:s&s_kwcid=AL!13316!3!799416471986!e!!g!!aaa%20roadside%20assistance!20967675016!163694630131&gad_source=1&gad_campaignid=20967675016&gclid=CjwKCAjwyMnNBhBNEiwA-Kcgu3CdfkP4Cei6XtSVkTJLm0N-NUkW1hgUTfswnaJrWvOpNXDOAptTZBoC8MoQAvD_BwE) to come help you,” Zagaynov said.

For these founders, it’s not just about making America battle ready; it’s about making the U.S. less vulnerable, in an increasingly unstable world.

But a grin spread over his face as he told me about the first time one of his water planes flew, in March of last year. He and his co-founders had loaded the prototype onto a U-Haul truck and drove two and half hours south to the San Luis Reservoir. (“We got a lot of looks from people, like ‘What the hell are these guys doing?’ ”) They got it into the water, taxied, and tested the connection to their remote system. Then, he told me: “We ripped it.”

The plane flew; the team wept. The feeling, he said, was “ecstatic.”

I checked in with him this week—since the U.S. launched missiles at Iran, setting off a conflict that’s engulfed the Middle East—and Zagaynov spoke as if Poseidon’s moment had come. Planes like his become critical, he said, when airports are under attack or destroyed. All they need for a runway is water, which means they’ll always have somewhere to land.

“You can’t bomb the ocean out of existence.”

“I genuinely want America to win,” says Sampriti Bhattacharyya. “And it’s not about outnumbering China, it’s about out-innovating them.”

Bhattacharyya is the founder of Navier, a Bay Area-based start-up that she described as a “next-generation American maritime company.” It makes what are, essentially, flying boats. They run on electricity, and can hit up to 30 knots using hydrofoil technology—which are effectively underwater wings that lift the hull four feet above the water. Navier’s vessels come in three sizes—30, 80, and 120 feet—and because they use electricity, Bhattacharyya told me, they are both cheaper to run and have better stamina than the average navy vessel. “You want boats that can go in the South China Sea, can go to the Red Sea, and do multiple mission days without refueling,” Bhattacharyya said.

“I genuinely want America to win,” says Navier founder Sampriti Bhattacharyya. (Courtesy of Navier)

“We already have our system deployed in U.S. Navy vessels today,” she added. Bhattacharyya has licensed her hydrofoil system to the defense contractor Leidos. And Navier, like Poseidon, has a “DEFENSE” section of its website, which describes why their vessels are perfect in a war zone: They’re more stable, since they sit above the waves, so it’s easier to hit a target if you’re launching a weapon from the deck. “One of our customers has systems for taking down drones, shooting things,” she told me. “The boat is so stable, they can go through rough waters and can take down drones when they’re tracking enemy boats.” The website also describes how “near-zero slamming”—which is when a speedboat violently bounces on water—“reduces crew fatigue” and “electric propulsion eliminates engine noise and vibration,” which makes the boats stealthier.

Bhattacharyya, who grew up in India, didn’t take her first plane ride until she was 20, when she came to America for college with $200 and a dream to [work at NASA](https://www.nasa.gov/) —which she did, as an aerospace engineer. (“I was inspired by the Apollo mission,” she said.) After a few years working on spaceships, she went to get her [PhD at MIT](https://www.mit.edu/), where she worked on a prototype for an underwater drone that could monitor nuclear reactors. She was living in Massachusetts in 2014 when Malaysia Airlines Flight 370, carrying 227 passengers and 12 crew, went missing. Bhattacharyya couldn’t believe it. “You’re telling me a Boeing 777 got lost in the ocean, a massive airplane with so many people, and you can’t find it?” Around that time, she recalled, there was talk of colonizing Mars—and yet no one could explain what happened to a commercial flight that dropped into the sea. That’s when she really fell into the “maritime rabbit hole.”

When it comes to water work, she thinks Silicon Valley needs a mind-set shift. “Everyone thinks it’s a software solution, like, ‘Let’s add some AI hype and raise a billion dollars around it,’ ” she said; it’s as if people have forgotten that we need the best *hardware* in the world. But Bhattacharyya sees the tide turning. “Under this administration, people are thinking: *Hey, let’s go back to building*,” she said.

The sea is attractive to Silicon Valley disruptors for the same reason it’s awash with conspiracy theories—including those of the [UFO](https://nypost.com/2025/10/26/us-news/ufo-tracker-shows-thousands-of-eerie-underwater-objects-lurking-along-us-coasts/) variety: It’s massive. The ocean makes up over 70 percent of the Earth’s surface, and the part that falls within U.S. jurisdiction—the [Exclusive Economic Zone](https://www.gc.noaa.gov/documents/2011/012711_gcil_maritime_eez_map.pdf), as it’s called—covers a larger area than all 50 states combined. The farther you get out there, the fewer regulations there are, and regulators.

“I want people to imagine this new frontier,” Bhattacharyya said. “The future of food, energy, and everything is here.”

And what America needs, she said, is an “Apollo mission, but for the sea.”

According to Rob Lehman, the race is on. The blue economy has gone from a “market with only a few players,” he said, to “a blood-red ocean with new entrants coming into the space, it seems, every day.” Lehman is a former Marine and a co-founder of Saronic Technologies, which makes autonomous boats packed with sensors and cameras. Founded in 2022 in Austin, Texas, Saronic already has 1,300 employees, a [$7.5 billion valuation](https://www.theinformation.com/articles/autonomous-warship-startup-saronic-raising-7-5-billion-valuation) —and a nearly $400 million contract with the U.S. Navy. According to Lehman, Saronic is delivering their Corsair vessel—which is 24 feet long and has a 1,000-pound payload—to our maritime forces at a “high volume.” He said the fact that they serve both the government and commercial clients allows them to “mash the gas pedal.”

Lehman told me that Saronic recently [invested $300 million](https://www.opportunitylouisiana.gov/news/saronic-technologies-investing-300-million-to-expand-franklin-shipyard-creating-3200-jobs-in-louisianas-bayou-region) in the Franklin Shipyard in Louisiana, and they’re shopping for a site for “Port Alpha,” which Lehman told me will be the “largest shipyard in the Western Hemisphere and potentially the world.” In other words: His company is going to keep accelerating—because, as he said, self-piloting vehicles are the future of warfare. “Shame on us if we’re continuing to put people in harm’s way when there are robots that can do it on their behalf,” he said. It was a sentiment echoed by several ocean-trepeneurs I met.

The blue economy has gone from a “market with only a few players,” Lehman said, to “a blood-red ocean with new entrants coming into the space, it seems, every day.”

This week, Iran [threatened to place underwater mines](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/iran-mines-strait-of-hormuz/) in the Strait of Hormuz. Soon after, Trump posted on Truth Social, “If Iran has put out any mines in the Hormuz Strait, and we have no reports of them doing so, we want them removed, IMMEDIATELY!”

“Nobody wants to see American troops put in harm’s way,” says Will O’Brien, co-founder of Ulysses Maritime Technologies. (Aaron Wojack for *The Free Press*)

“You’re not going to want to send divers down there, and nobody wants to see American troops put in harm’s way,” said Will O’Brien, explaining that this is the “textbook example” of how his company can help America win. He’s the co-founder of Ulysses Maritime Technologies, which makes underwater drones that can trawl the seafloor for mines—or possibly even prevent them from being laid. “What we could do is patrol. We can put our robot to sleep on the seabed. It could listen, and if it needed to, defend against a threat,” he said. It can also protect deep-sea cables. “When people think *underwater* and *defense* they think: *torpedoes*. Things that go—and do—boom. That’s actually not it,” O’Brien said. “The underwater remand is gathering data.” Meaning: mapping the seabed, having eyes and ears on whatever’s going on below the surface.

The Ulysses founding team, who I met in California, includes two Irishmen and a Scot, who all moved to America two years ago. They’re all in their 20s, and work out of a warehouse in San Francisco, a 10-minute walk from the bay. Already, they have contracts with an offshore wind developer that uses their robots to inspect underwater infrastructure, among other government partnerships. While I spoke to O’Brien, one of Ulysses’s underwater drones—which was about four feet long, with a black carbon-fiber hull outfitted with cameras and sensors—was marooned on a worktable festooned with tubes and tools and also an Xbox controller, used to remotely pilot the thing.

Could it end illegal fishing? “Yeah, we can do that,” said O’Brien. Stop piracy and protect ocean borders? “Our vehicle can do that, and our system can do that.” He hopes the craft—“It’s a sea cop,” O’Brien said—will one day patrol the [Exclusive Economic Zone](https://www.gc.noaa.gov/documents/2011/012711_gcil_maritime_eez_map.pdf). Or, even sooner, be deployed to the Middle East to deal with those Iranian mines. “We’re ready to be activated,” he said.

The Ulysses founding team, who I met in California, includes two Irishmen and a Scot, who all moved to America two years ago. (Aaron Wojack for *The Free Press*)

O’Brien sees a parallel between his underwater drones and the airborne ones that have helped Ukraine’s relatively small army fend off Russia’s much larger one. The wars of the next decades will be won, he suggested, by simple bits of technology. In the Persian Gulf, very expensive missiles are being deployed by, say, the United Arab Emirates to shoot down relatively cheap drones; and two or three Iranian soldiers in a small boat can lay a mine. “Small mines worth thousands of dollars can cause billions of dollars of trade disruption, potentially altering the world economy,” said O’Brien. “You need to meet these small, cheap, plentiful devices with other small, cheap, plentiful devices.”

“You’re moving away from fighter jets to 10,000 aerial drones,” he added. “You’re moving from big, expensive ships and submarines to a load of Ulysses surface vehicles and underwater vehicles.”

Ulysses Maritime Technologies makes underwater drones that can trawl the seafloor for mines. (Aaron Wojack for *The Free Press*)

When I asked what makes Zagaynov so obsessed with the sea, he said: “It’s something left to conquer.” Like those who set out to discover the New World, the new seafarers,funded handsomely with venture dollars and obsessed by speed, are racing back to the violent waves not only for money but for glory. They believe in making America not only safe but strong—and that means having American vessels atop and below the surface, always ready to strike.

When I suggest that they’re pirates, the swaggering boys of Ulysses push back. *Pirates* are anarchists; they prefer *cowboys*. “We’re bringing some order to the Wild West,” O’Brien said, “taking out some outlaws.”
