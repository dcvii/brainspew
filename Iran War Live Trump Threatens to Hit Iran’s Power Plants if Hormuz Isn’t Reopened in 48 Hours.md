All crew are safe.### [Power Infrastructure Can Be Lawfully Targeted in Certain Circumstances, Military Experts Say](https://www.wsj.com/livecoverage/iran-us-israel-war-updates-2026/card/power-infrastructure-can-be-lawfully-targeted-in-certain-circumstances-military-experts-say-wVSOxc7KGyVZcClHdiWu)

While power plants are generally considered to be civilian infrastructure, current and former U.S. military officials say that an adversary’s electrical infrastructure can sometimes qualify as a legitimate military target.

Under the Law of Armed Conflict, if striking power infrastructure makes a concrete contribution to a military operation and the potential harm to civilians is minimized, it can be permissible, they say.

“Expected civilian harm–including second order effects from loss of electricity–cannot be excessive relative to the anticipated military advantage,” said David Deptula, a retired Air Force Lieutenant General who helped direct attacks during the 1991 Desert Storm campaign to evict Iraqi forces from Kuwait. “The choice between temporary disruption and permanent destruction can influence this analysis.”

The primary legal justification for attacking Iraq’s electrical network in 1991 was to disrupt its military’s command and control, said Michael Schmitt, professor emeritus at the U.S. Naval War College and a retired Air Force lieutenant colonel who served as a judge advocate general. The generals sought to do this by targeting power lines and avoiding strikes on generators, though this guidance was not always carried out in practice by U.S. aircrews.

Each case has to be assessed on its own merits. Among the considerations, Schmitt said, is whether there are other ways to achieve the same military effect, including cyber attacks. President Trump did not address any of the legal issues when he threatened in a social media post Saturday to bomb the biggest power plant in Iran.

“Military officers that are conducting the analysis of the target will do a very granular assessment of the strike,” Schmitt said.

“You cannot bomb a power station to send a message. You have to be able to articulate a ‘definite military advantage,’ not a political advantage,” he added. “Disrupting enemy morale or changing the thinking of leadership by destroying civilian objects–that’s not a thing.”

President Trump’s [threat to target Iran’s “power plants”](https://www.wsj.com/livecoverage/iran-us-israel-war-updates-2026/card/trump-threatens-to-obliterate-iran-s-power-plants-if-it-doesn-t-open-hormuz-strait-ZLzqUBs1JUhKGS1M76xc) recalls the 1991 Desert Storm campaign to evict Saddam Hussein’s troops from Kuwait. The U.S. and its allies carried out hundreds of air strikes against Iraq’s electrical system and oil installations.

Those attacks were designed to produce the maximum military effect with the least damage, according to U.S. military directives at the time. The electrical grid around Baghdad was targeted with special filament-carrying cruise missiles to short out the power lines. Pilots were instructed to avoid bombing generator halls, which U.S. intelligence estimated would take years to rebuild. Instead, the nearby switching yards that transmitted the electricity from the plants were to be struck.

“Electrical targets will be targeted to minimize recuperation time. At electrical production/transformer stations the objectives will be the transformer/switching yards and the central buildings in these yards,” said one U.S. military directive that was quoted in “The Generals’ War,” a history of that conflict. “Boilers and generators will not be aimpoints.”

Some exceptions were made. An electrical generator near the southern oil-producing hub of Basra was struck because U.S. commanders worried it could provide power that would aid the Iraqi military’s defense of Kuwait. Others were hit because the targeting guidance wasn’t always passed down to lower levels in the chain of command.

The Harvard University School of Public Health concluded after the war that destruction of the electrical plants had hampered efforts to purify water and contributed to infant mortality. But the public health crisis was also aggravated by Saddam Hussein’s reluctance to comply with United Nations terms for lifting a trade embargo. That said, some of the plants were rebuilt more quickly than anticipated.

Better and more precise munitions exist today. But the U.S. Central Command hasn’t explained how it might strike Iran’s power infrastructure.
