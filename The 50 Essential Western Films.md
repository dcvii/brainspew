I never expected to write this article.

That’s because— [as I’ve explained elsewhere](https://www.honest-broker.com/p/what-i-learned-from-binge-watching) —I’ve never been a fan of cowboy movies. I avoided them even as a youngster and, in later years, never really gave them a chance.

They were filled with tired formulas, repeating the same simple stories over and over. Man shoots man. Man loves woman. Man rides horse. End of story.

Or so I thought.

I now know I was wrong about all that. The best western films are filled with surprising and contradictory tales, much like the myths of ancient times—where deities can act godlike one day, and disrupt everything the next with some unexpected transgression.

The western, when shaped by a visionary filmmaker, is just like that. It’s a wide open playing field with no rules whatsoever. So you can play it all almost any way.

The comparison with myth is relevant. I finally came to embrace movies about the Old West because, I gradually realized, they define the grand American mythos, whether we like it or not. We have inherited these stories, and need to make sense of them—because they tell us things about ourselves that we won’t learn any other way.

At least that’s what I finally decided. And that’s why I recently immersed myself in these films—binge-watching them over the course of several months.

I’ve now emerged from this rich experience with a guide for you. Below is the first installment of my survey of the 50 essential western films.

I chose these films based on their mythic splendor and cinematic excellence, but also with an eye for movies that shook me up—breaking new ground in brash new ways. In aggregate they represent a picture of American life as rich as anything you will find in any other creative idiom.

I’m sharing these in chronological order. So I’m starting with a film from 1903—and we will gradually make our way toward the current day.

### The Great Train Robbery (1903)

Thomas Edison gets credit for lightbulbs and recordings, but he also helped launch the western genre—without ever leaving New Jersey. *The Great Train Robbery*, released by Edison Films in 1903, lasts just 13 minutes, but that proved sufficient to thrill audiences and establish the commercial viability for films about gun-toting desperadoes in cowboy hats.

![](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fgv6GhAaUNc)

The highlight of the movie is a single 20-second scene, disconnected to the plot. It shows an outlaw firing his gun straight into the camera—and has been borrowed and parodied everywhere from *[Chisum](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-pYe0ORQmbI)* to *[Tombstone](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ZI_m7b83wfE)* to *[Goodfellas](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jIoNkifiBms)*. The Wild West may have been disappearing in 1903, but the Western film was just starting.

### The Daughter of Dawn (1920)

*The Daughter of Dawn* features an entire cast of Native Americans—some three hundred members of the Comanche and Kiowa tribes. The movie was made during the course of three months in 1920 in the Wichita Mountains of Oklahoma, and presents tribal life in a mostly unfiltered way.

Actors provided their own costumes and props, even the teepees. The scenes of dancing and hunting are realistic, and the romantic centerpiece of the film (a love triangle) defies all the prevailing Hollywood stereotypes about violent Native Americans.

As such, The *Daughter of Dawn* is a good reminder that counter-narratives to the formulaic western are almost as old as the genre itself. We’re fortune that this 83-minute film, long thought to be lost, was rediscovered by a private investigator in 2005, and now is preserved in the National Film Registry at the Library of Congress.

### Greed (1924)

I long assumed that the anti-western didn’t emerge until 1960s, when bold directors (Leone, Peckinpah, Corbucci, etc.) defied the simplistic assumptions of the genre and portrayed the Old West in all its stark reality—good, bad, and ugly mixed together, almost like the Nietzschean will to power on saddleback. But already in 1924, director Erich von Stroheim somehow filmed a western totally liberated from romance and hero-worship—a kind of tale Zola or Balzac might have constructed if they had lived in the American frontier.

The result was the most ambitious film of the era. But we will never know quite how ambitious—because the original nine-hour version of *Greed* did not survive. The film now exists in truncated versions, but even these convey much of the grandeur of the filmmaker’s original conception.

In its current form, the story focuses on a married couple who destroy each other through selfishness and avarice. The husband (played by Gibson Gowland, who is compelling at every turn, but never had another starring role in his career) eventually murders his wife (ZaSu Pitts), and tries to hide from the authorities as a gold miner in Death Valley.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!aAMe!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9d168b60-f68a-41e1-b32f-a2c745c71d52_958x720.jpeg)

ZaSu Pitts and Gibson Gowland in Greed.

Making the film was also deadly. Von Stroheim filmed in Death Valley during July and August, 1923, when the temperature got as high as 123 degrees. One crew member died of heatstroke and fourteen became too ill to continue working. Even without knowing this, the audience feels the desolation in these closing scenes—which are as hard-hitting as anything you will see in the most nihilistic westerns of later years.

### Tumbleweeds (1925)

Before John Wayne, the hottest cowboy in movies was William S. Hart. If he looks comfortable in the saddle, it’s because he was born in 1864, when your daily commute had four hoofs. In 1915, theater owners voted him as the most popular film star in the United States, but he never survived the transition to talking movies—his declamatory Shakespearean delivery didn’t fit his cowboy looks. So his final starring role happened in *Tumbleweeds* from 1925, produced with his own money.

This movie shares the real story of the West, namely a battle over land rights. The setting is Cherokee Strip, but the Cherokees don’t have any clout in this film (and only a few seconds of screen time)—so the real battle is between cattle ranchers and the newly arrived homesteaders. It’s easy to critique this as shallow history, but you have to feel sympathy for the cowboys and their cast of thousands (of cattle). They will get evicted and watch tepid suburbia eventually take their place. Confronting this sad fate, Hart laments: “This is the end of the West.”

And *that* was more than a hundred years ago.

### Go West (1925)

Buster Keaton didn’t invent the western comedy, but *Go West* was the most ambitious of the early efforts to satirize cowboy life. Keaton tries his hand at all the usual tasks—cattle branding, bronco busting, dairy farming, roping, and (of course) gambling at the saloon. He even demonstrates some skill as a cow whisperer. But this fails to prevent a stampede—through 1920s urbanized Los Angeles—at the film’s climax.

“I actually turned ‘em loose here in Los Angeles in the Santa Fe depot in the freight yards,” Keaton later recalled, “and brought ‘em up Seventh Street to Broadway...And we put cowboys off every side street to stop people in automobiles from coming into it.” The result is one of. the most striking confrontations of Old West and New West ever put on the screen—but perhaps anticipating [this scene from a later movie about early Los Angeles](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=e_bE9cpwC1s).

### Cimarron (1931)

Only four western films have won the Oscar for Best Picture (all of them included in this survey), and it started with *Cimarron* in 1931. In fact, *Cimarron* was nominated in every category for which it was eligible at the Academy Awards that year.

You can immediately see why this film received so many honors (although it lost money at the box office). The story, based on Edna Ferber’s novel of the same name, raises the western above the level of mass entertainment, and takes a forty-year perspective on the settlement of Oklahoma—covering everything from land grants to oil drilling. The protagonist Yancey Cravat, played by the charismatic Richard Dix, is a dreamer who upholds Native American rights and dignity of outcasts. But he’s a failure as a husband and family man.

This was a very progressive movie for its day, although some tropes have not aged as well. Even so, it’s still one of the most ambitious westerns ever made.

### Harlem on the Prairie (1937)

Black cowboys really did ride the range in the Old West, but I doubt they were anything like those featured in *Harlem on the Prairie* (1937)—marketed as the first “all-colored” western film. Lead actor Herb Jeffrey (late changed to Jefferies) starts singing from the moment we meet him, and delivers two sweet songs in his velvet baritone before the first shootout. His whole posse is made up of singers—a quartet that also worked together as the Four Tones in real life.

![Movie poster](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!aj8O!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa2e57137-c04d-473d-aa6a-124ec5b64282_750x1118.jpeg)

Movie poster

You won’t find better doo-wop music in any other western, and Jeffrey could have built an entire career as a singing cowboy. Instead he joined the Duke Ellington band a short while later, and enjoyed a huge hit with “Flamingo.” But during his brief stint in Hollywood he helped break down the race barrier in genre films.

### Way Out West (1937)

The formulas of the genre are already starting to feel tired and invite humorous take-offs. Laurel and Hardy step in to do that, and deliver one of their two best feature films (the other is *Sons of the Desert*). *Way Out West* belongs on any short list of the finest comedies of the decade.

The hapless duo face various desperadoes in Brushwood Gulch, as they attempt to retrieve the deed to a gold mine. There’s much to admire here, but I must call special attention to Laurel and Hardy’s unexpectedly deft song-and-dance work, which will live up to the standards of even the toughest saloon audience.

![](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LXCwlO2jnYU)

### Stagecoach (1939)

If you watch too many westerns, you start taking John Wayne for granted. But here in his breakout performance, Wayne grabs the audience’s attention with an endearing youth and innocence that would soon be gone—the Duke aged rapidly—and showcases the 6 foot 4 inch frame he had used to good effect as an offensive tackle on the USC football team.

![John Wayne](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!_Ze-!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7aad2411-2ce5-4c03-91bf-d34aa8dcb006_616x468.jpeg)

John Wayne

Director John Ford took a chance on this B-movie actor, and cowboy films would never be the same. But even a traditional western like this brings surprises—for example, Wayne’s protection of a prostitute, who is sent packing by city elders. We already see here the growing moral ambiguity of the genre, and it will continue to get murkier over the next thirty years.

Does the western celebrate the outsider or insider, the rule-maker or the rule-breaker, the sheriff or the gunslinger? This is the moment when we all started wondering.

### My Darling Clementine (1946)

Real-life cowboy heroWyatt Earp spent his final days in Southern California, hanging out with movie stars and consulting on film projects. Hollywood repaid the favor posthumously by turning Earp’s life into more than a dozen movies. On one film set, Earp met the future director John Ford, then a prop boy. Decades later Ford made the most endearing of all the Earp films, *My Darling Clementine*.

There’s not much true history in this film. But Earp’s favorite song really was “My Darling Clementine”—a mining song, not a cowboy tune, I’ll point out. Here it serves as the inspiration for a unlikely love triangle in Tombstone, Arizona. Clementine Carter (played by Cathy Downs) pursues her fickle lover Doc Holliday (Victor Mature) but ends up courted by Earp (Henry Fonda). The two men waver over their allegiances, unsure whether they are rivals or co-conspirators

This sets in motion one of the most delicate westerns ever filmed. Ford lingers over sweet scenes—a community square dance, a church gathering, a dramatic performance by a visiting Shakespearean actor, and other uncharacteristic fare for the genre. Of course, everything culminates in the famous shootout at OK Corral. But even the onscreen violence can’t dispel the charm of this homage to Western romance—which is as well constructed and beautifully shot as any of Ford’s films.

### The Treasure of the Sierra Madre (1948)

The antihero would later emerge as the defining western character of the 1960s. But Humphrey Bogart defined the character type back in this classic 1948 movie, rightly praised as one of Hollywood’s finest moments.

Prospectors battle for gold, but this inevitably leads them to battle each other in a world purged of the western’s typical idealistic formulas. Twenty years later, Sergio Leone would tell a similar story with *The Good, the Bad, and the Ugly*, but the blueprint is here in full, courtesy of director John Huston.

![Humphrey Bogart](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!27tJ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fcb74f813-9073-402a-89dd-7cde2cc7854e_750x375.jpeg)

Humphrey Bogart

He won two Oscars (directing and screenwriting), and was nominated for Best Picture too (losing out to Laurence Olivier’s *Hamlet—* perhaps an even older prototype for the antihero). Many are puzzled by the Academy’s neglect of Bogart, who didn’t even earn a nomination, but delivers one of the defining performances of his career. But some compensation was made when, forty years later, Daniel Day-Lewis received an Oscar for *There Will Be Blood*, and cited this earlier role as inspiration.

### Red River (1948)

This is a classic western with a familiar story of cattle drivers on the open road—but with a new twist. The shadow of generational conflict enters into the genre, anticipating the dominant theme of so many films over the next two decades.

John Wayne wasn’t even forty when he made this movie, but he is already the living symbol of aging patriarchal authority. He looks like he is fifty—there was clearly already a lot of mileage on those spurs. But Wayne adapts skillfully to this new kind of role. It puts him on a collision course with young Montgomery Clift, who refuses to bow down to the older generation.

Director Howard Hawk was at the peak of his career when he made this film—he had just released *The Big Sleep* and would film *A Star Is Born* next. *Red River* stands proudly alongside those works with its dramatic tension and rich psychological conflicts; but it also includes some of the most impressive scenes of stampedes and cattle-driving you will ever see. if livestock could win Oscars, these cows would ride high as a prize-winning herd.

### Winchester 73 (1950)

John Ford and John Wayne set the gold standard as the ultimate western director-and-actor combo, but in the early 1950s the silver medal went to Anthony Mann and Jimmy Stewart, who collaborated on eight films. *Winchester 73* marked a turning point for Stewart, not only because the role required uncharacteristic brutality—which he somehow pulled off while retaining his likability—but also due to his success in negotiating a share of box office revenues. That was a Hollywood first.

The film opens with a shooting contest in Dodge City, with the best marksman winning a rare “one in a thousand” rifle, the Winchester 73. Stewart gets the gun, but can’t hold on to it—and neither can any of its subsequent owners. It is a cursed symbol of power, much like those mythic rings, from Wagner to Tolkien, that are seized at great risk. During the course of the movie, eight people own this weapon, for better or (mostly) worse. The story is artfully constructed with a fabulistic spin rare for this genre.

### High Noon (1952)

In *The Sopranos*, Tony Soprano keeps asking where Gary Cooper has gone—it’s like Paul Simon’s quest for the missing Joe DiMaggio. *Hey, hey, hey.* But in Tony’s case, he is looking for a version of Gary Cooper with a very specific address: Main Street at noon. That’s where the sheriff must face down outlaws in this stark, understated film.

Cooper is the only man in town who takes his responsibilities seriously—everyone else runs and hide. I’d love this story just for that (I must be like Mr. Soprano), and *High Noon* is one of my favorite films in any genre.But I especially admire its tight construction, archetypal resonance, and relentless pace, which unfolds almost in real time. If Sophocles wrote cowboy stories he would have come up with something like this.

### Hondo (1953)

How do you update the western genre in the year 1953? That’s easy—you film it in 3D. So director John Farrow brought his expensive high tech cameras into the desert lands of Chihuahua to film a dramatic story of love and adventure in Apache territory.

It’s too bad that the 3D cameras kept malfunctioning in the dust and heat. And when *Hondo* was finally released, few theaters showed it in the new format. But it was still a success in 2D, mostly because of John Wayne—who delivers one of the more nuanced roles of his career as a mixed-race cowboy who wants to maintain good relations with both US cavalry and Native Americans.

### Shane (1953)

Shane is a gunslinger, played by Alan Ladd, who seeks a more peaceful way of life among homesteaders. But he is drawn into a brutal conflict between these newly arrived farmers and a domineering rancher who wants to remove them from his grazing land.

That’s a familiar story, told in many other western films. But this movie has charmed millions of viewers because of a young farm boy, played by Brandon deWilde, who serves as the emotional center of the film. This lad idolizes Shane, and would like to be a gunslinger himself. In a way, he’s a stand-in for the kind of audience who watches films of this sort—but seeing this fascination with violence in such a young, innocent boy is unsettling in the extreme.

The myth of the Wild West is already starting to unravel here—preparing us for the heartless antiheroes and outright villains of the next decade. But it took a child to pave the way.

### Johnny Guitar (1954)

“Johnny Guitar is one of the cinema’s great operatic works,” asserts director Martin Scorsese—so passion infuses every scene from beginning to end. Perhaps it helped that the two leading female stars, Joan Crawford and Mercedes McCambridge, fought a behind-the-scenes feud that echoed their on-screen rivalry—due to the fact that the former had once dated the latter’s husband.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!nRQ2!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F883d0353-04cb-4411-9287-d7bf6cf273a5_1024x799.jpeg)

“People didn’t know what to make of it,” Scorsese continues, “so they either ignored it or laughed at it.” The film deviates markedly from the conventions of Western dramas. America wasn’t ready for a woman as the toughest hombre in a Wild West town, but Crawford is absolutely convincing in the role. And her titular sidekick, played by Sterling Hayden, is even more unusual as a gunslinger who abandons his six-shooter for the six strings on his guitar.

The film may have had a bigger impact in Europe, where director Nicholas Ray became a darling of French Wave filmmakers Godard and Truffaut. The latter described *Johnny Guitar* as a kind of Beauty and the Beast story. You can see how art house directors latched on to the personal quirks of the characters and their world-weary disillusionment.

In some ways this is more a story of the Far West today than anything that actually existed in the nineteenth century. Even the hope in redemption from new technology (here the railroad line) has an uncanny feeling of contemporaneity.
