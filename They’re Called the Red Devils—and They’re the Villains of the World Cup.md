INGLEWOOD, Calif.— [Belgium](https://www.wsj.com/topics/place/belgium) had just scored its fourth goal in a World Cup beatdown of the U.S. national team this week when its players saw one more way to twist the knife.

Eliminating the host nation and silencing tens of thousands of American fans wasn’t enough. In order to celebrate the goal—and hammer home their point—the Belgians broke into what’s known as the “Trump Dance,” the arm-pumping, hip-swaying move popularized by the president.

The jab was hardly subtle. President Trump had personally intervened in an effort to lift the suspension on American striker Folarin Balogun before the match. Even in the locker room, they weren’t done. They did the same dance again, this time while blasting “YMCA,” a song frequently played at Trump rallies. And the heckling continued on social media.

“Overturn this,” the team posted.

Belgium didn’t necessarily win many friends in the U.S., but it did give American fans an obvious team to root against. Belgium now takes on Spain at Sofi Stadium on Friday for a spot in the semifinals, and this country known for chocolate, waffles, and Tintin has embraced the unlikely role of World Cup villain.

In back-to-back games now, the Belgians have done more than just win—they’ve also riled up their opponents in the process.

With a population of under 12 million, Belgium wasn’t expected to be a major factor at this tournament. After a third-place finish at the 2018 World Cup, the team known as the Red Devils didn’t make it out of the group stage in Qatar. The players once hailed as the country’s golden generation were older, slower, and looking at their primes in the rearview mirror.

That was only reinforced when they crawled through the group stage here with draws in their first two matches against Egypt and Iran. They only clinched a berth to the knockout rounds after beating New Zealand in their third match.

Belgium again faced near elimination when it trailed Senegal 2-0 in the round of 32. But with goals in the 86th and 89th minutes, they stormed back to tie it up. Then, in stoppage time after 30 minutes of extra time, they were awarded a controversial penalty kick that sealed the game.

Despite being rescued with a video review that dragged on for seven minutes, the Belgians weren’t particularly graceful in victory. Manager Rudi Garcia was slammed for comments he made in which he said Senegal was one of “those teams” that he expected to fold late in the match.

Critics felt Garcia was making unfair generalizations about African countries. Garcia later wrote on social media that he was referring to any team that might be unfamiliar with holding a lead and it would have applied to teams from other continents.

Next came the charged contest against the Americans. The day before the game, Balogun stunningly had a one-game suspension overturned by FIFA. The situation was particularly contentious because Trump had personally lobbied FIFA president Gianni Infantino to allow America’s leading goal scorer to play.

FIFA’s insistence that the judiciary body that made the decision was independent did nothing to assuage Belgium’s soccer federation, which said it was astonished by the move. Before the game, the Belgians’ appeal failed and the country said it wouldn’t rule out taking additional action if Balogun played.

Balogun started at the top of the U.S. attack, but it didn’t make much of a difference. From the opening minute, Belgium absolutely dominated the Americans and squelched every shred of optimism the U.S. had generated among fans about their promising start to the tournament.

Still, Belgium wasn’t ready to drop the matter even after the players were done dancing in the locker room. The day after bouncing the U.S., the federation said it was still advocating for a review of the procedures that let Balogun play.

“The RBFA will continue to pursue the ongoing FIFA matter off the pitch,” the Royal Belgian Football Association said. “Now, together, our full focus turns to Friday’s quarterfinal against Spain.”
