PALM BEACH, Florida — Howard Kessler drew a box on a piece of paper and scribbled lines shooting out from all four sides. “When I dealt with banks, in one meeting, I remember this guy said, ‘Look, Howard, I’m in here. You’re all over there!’ ” Kessler told me. “I think outside of the box. I think differently.”

Kessler’s way of thinking helped make him an architect of [affinity credit cards](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/a/affinity-card.asp) back in the 1980s. Sponsored by airlines, colleges, department stores, unions, and other organizations, affinity cards paved the way for airline frequent-flyer programs, credit card perks, and the rise of a cashless society. He became a billionaire—and [bought a mansion](https://archive.ph/QLhOj) here for $30 million in 2001.

No one had ever paid so much for a home in Palm Beach, including Kessler’s friend, Donald Trump. “Donald thought I was an idiot for paying that much,” Kessler said as we sat in the main house that overlooks the ocean, not far from his two swimming pools, a green clay tennis court, a greenhouse, gazebo, and guesthouse. “It’s worth 10 times more now,” he added.

It would be no surprise if you have never heard of Kessler, 78, who told me when we met that he had never been interviewed by a journalist. I was at his lavish home to discuss the extraordinary role Kessler has played in shaping one of the more surprising policy decisions by his longtime friend, now president of the United States. Kessler is the reason why Trump, who does not drink or smoke, signed an [executive order](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/12/increasing-medical-marijuana-and-cannabidiol-research/) in December that set the stage to loosen restrictions on marijuana.

The president’s move bucked decades of [Republican dogma](https://www.thefp.com/p/the-abstainer-in-chief-gets-friendly) on marijuana and opened the floodgates for medical research. Kessler, driven by a personal passion for medical cannabis, spent almost a decade trying to win over Trump. And it worked.

“This was never a priority for Biden or any president except Trump,” said Kevin Sabet, the president and CEO of Smart Approaches to Marijuana, an anti-cannabis group. “The drive with this president is 95 percent—if not 100 percent—about his friendship with Howard Kessler,” added Sabet, who served as a White House drug control policy adviser to George W. Bush, Barack Obama, and Bill Clinton.

If the Justice Department follows through on the president’s order to update the legal status of marijuana, it would be moved from a category for drugs like heroin and ecstasy, deemed to have “high potential for abuse” and “no accepted medical use,” to a category for substances with “moderate to low potential for physical and psychological dependence.” That includes drugs like ketamine and Tylenol with codeine that are seen as having accepted medical uses.

Kessler does not fit neatly into the box of wealthy people trying to bend the president’s ear, including on regulatory changes that would make their companies more profitable.

Marijuana rescheduling also is expected to eliminate tax burdens for many cannabis companies, a windfall for those who lobbied Trump.

I wanted to interview Kessler because his successful advocacy is different from how business often gets done in Trump’s Washington. He does not fit neatly into the box of wealthy people trying to [bend the president’s ear](https://www.thefp.com/p/how-to-win-a-pardon-from-trump), including on regulatory changes that would make their companies more profitable. In contrast, Kessler has no marijuana-related business interests.

He told me that his support for looser regulations is the passion project of his life, and a way to help fix the American healthcare system. As he tells it, rescheduling would unlock the power of Medicare and Medicaid to cover cannabinoid-based therapies for seniors. Kessler believes that could open a path to delivering faster, more effective clinical trials for everything from psychedelics to cancer treatments.

“My end goal is to change healthcare,” he told me. “I want to show I changed the world a second time.”

Kessler made his fortune after he helped come up with the idea of affinity credit cards. Georgetown University was one of the earliest adopters, its name printed on cards issued by Maryland National Bank, who Kessler partnered with on the effort. The credit card operation was spun off into a company called MBNA Corporation, became the world’s largest issuer of independent credit cards in the world, and was sold in 2006 to what is now Bank of America Corporation for $35 billion.

His success translated to powerful political connections. Kessler cultivated a friendship with the Clintons and stayed overnight at the White House. In 2000, Kessler was one of five wealthy Bostonians who Bill Clinton sought out during a trip to [discuss fundraising](https://www.capecodtimes.com/story/news/2000/08/06/kessler-has-history-political-largesse/51009981007/) for his presidential library. Kessler is also a longtime [campaign donor](https://www.fec.gov/data/receipts/individual-contributions/?contributor_name=howard+kessler&contributor_zip=02116&contributor_zip=33480) to Democrats, spending millions of dollars since the late 1990s in support of the Clintons, Barack Obama, and Biden in 2024. (Some of his money also has gone to Republicans.)

Before reshaping drug policy, Kessler revolutionized the banking industry by creating affinity credit cards for universities, airlines, and sports teams. (Saul Martinez for *The Free Press*)

In Florida, when Trump purchased the Mar-a-Lago estate and turned it into a private club in the mid-1990s, Kessler joined as a member. Trump often asked: “How’s my buddy doing?” One day, Kessler surprised Trump by asking if he even knew Kessler’s name, he told me.

The next evening, Kessler was dining at The Breakers, a luxury resort in Palm Beach, when he got a tap on the shoulder from someone sitting behind him. It was Trump. He said: “Hi, Howard, how are you doing?”

“It was the first real encounter we had,” Kessler told me. According to Kessler, Trump said that what he appreciated about Kessler “was the length of time I was married to my wife,” adding that Trump “appreciates a long-term relationship.”

Kessler and Trump grew close, and even closer in 2006, when Trump faced a backlash for erecting a flag on an 80-foot pole in front of Mar-a-Lago without a permit. The town also said its height and location violated ordinances.

Trump called Kessler for help, and the credit card mogul brokered a peace deal that included moving and shortening the flagpole, as well as a [$100,000 pledge](https://archive.ph/PWQMK) by Trump to charity. “The town was against him,” Kessler said. “I made it easy for both sides.”

“He’s been a good friend,” Kessler added, “and we spent so much time together in those days, and he was in a different world.”

Kessler’s fascination with medical cannabis and how it could help seniors began in 2014. Kessler’s son convinced him that it could help people suffering from pain. Many of Kessler’s friends said the same thing. He started telling Trump about it—and found out that the future president heard similar stories during rounds of golf.

“I went to Aspen with some of my Palm Beach friends, and they took me to dispensaries,” Kessler said. “I was amazed by how many older people were there trying to buy this stuff.”

After Trump won the presidency in 2016, Kessler met with Trump’s son-in-law, Jared Kushner, and showed him a film about the benefits of cannabidiol (CBD) for seniors. Kushner and other Trump administration officials weren’t receptive and seemed confused, Kessler recalled. When he used the words *endocannabinoid system*, he lost Trump. (It is a [cell-signaling network](https://www.health.harvard.edu/blog/the-endocannabinoid-system-essential-and-mysterious-202108112569) that regulates appetite, sleep, pain, memory, and mood.) “I knew I had to stop because he wouldn’t understand it,” Kessler told me.

A longtime Democratic donor, Kessler's financial success translated to powerful bipartisan connections, including a close friendship with Bill and Hillary Clinton. (Saul Martinez for *The Free Press*)

In 2019, Kessler founded the Commonwealth Project, which describes itself as a “national movement to transform health and longevity for people aged 65 and older by [restoring balance](https://commonwealthproject.org/a-new-standard-for-senior-care/) to the body’s most overlooked regulatory system: The Endocannabinoid System.” The organization supports a “payer-provider system” for cannabinoid-based therapies through programs like Medicaid Advantage. Kessler believes this model could generate roughly $60 billion in annual savings for the federal government.

The Commonwealth Project is a subsidiary of Kessler’s company, HJK Medical Enterprises, but generates no revenue, according to people close to Kessler. All of the money that he puts into the project is used to support cannabis research, the people said.

The Biden administration generated cautious optimism in the cannabis industry and among other cannabis advocates. On the campaign trail, Biden said he supported medical cannabis but had concerns that it was a “ [gateway drug](https://www.washingtonpost.com/nation/2019/11/18/joe-biden-marijuana-gateway-drug-legalization/).” While he was president, his unease became an insurmountable hurdle. “He had a phobia,” Kessler said, believing that Biden’s resistance stemmed from the president seeing his son Hunter struggle with drug addiction.

A person familiar with the rescheduling process during the Biden administration said there was “institutional discomfort with any type of relaxation of drug laws for cannabis.”

“It really had to do with a personal relationship” with President Trump, Kessler said.

In 2024, Kessler was diagnosed with cancer. He used cannabis occasionally for pain, and his experience with chemotherapy made him believe that others who are sick could benefit from an alternative medicine. When Trump won reelection, he seemed more receptive to cannabis reform, according to Kessler and other people familiar with the matter. Trump called Kessler often to see how he was handling the chemotherapy.

Kessler and hisadviser Lynn Honderd met with Vince Haley, now Trump’s director of the Domestic Policy Council, to discuss rescheduling, but Haley was skeptical. Kessler got a more encouraging response from his neighbor in Palm Beach: Mehmet Oz, the television host who became Trump’s chief of the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services. Oz is now overseeing implementation of a pilot program under which Medicare would pay for some CBD and hemp-derived products.

Kessler also wasn’t shy about calling Trump’s chief of staff, Susie Wiles. Before taking the job, Wiles worked for Republican lobbying firm Ballard Partners, which represents cannabis interests.

The cannabis industry also sought help from well-connected Republicans to make its case. They included former Trump advisers Kellyanne Conway, who consults for marijuana business interests, and Alex Bruesewitz, who did consulting work for a pro-cannabis [political action committee](https://www.thefp.com/p/maga-and-marijuana-might-become-best).

The cannabis industry heavily lobbied the Trump administration, but Kessler says the president’s historic executive order ultimately came down to a long-term personal friendship. (Saul Martinez for *The Free Press*)

Kim Rivers, the CEO of Trulieve Cannabis, attended pro-Trump fundraisers and advocated for rescheduling with White House officials, people familiar with the matter told me. Trulieve also donated to Trump’s inauguration fund. Wendy Bronfein, the co-founder of cannabis company Curio Wellness, met with Trump in December at the Kennedy Center Honors gala. Bronfein told me that she discussed the importance of rescheduling to open up new “therapeutic access for people and research.” Trump said that he was working on it.

Kessler told me that Trump “was lobbied to death, and people paid money,” but that isn’t why the president issued the executive order. “They got a direct financial benefit,” Kessler said. “I haven’t focused on that. I’ve been focusing on a solution that could change the world.”

The political dynamics of Trump’s executive order were fraught. House Speaker Mike Johnson, a Louisiana Republican, urged the president against it and believed it could be dangerous to the public, a House staff member told me. White House adviser Stephen Miller seemed disinterested when Bronfein approached him at the Kennedy Center. “I don’t know that he loved that conversation,” Bronfein told me.

Trump went ahead with the order anyway. It authorized a pilot program to reimburse Medicare patients for products that include CBD. Kessler believes that the order also paves the way for key medical research long blocked by Republicans, who “spent all these years creating a stigma,” he said. “This could potentially extend quality of life for years for people.”

In our conversation, Kessler emphasized medical uses of cannabis and his belief that it could benefit seniors. He wants Trump to focus on that, rather than recreational uses of marijuana. A person familiar with rescheduling said it will be “a long process” and that litigation is expected, including from the group Smart Approaches to Marijuana—which retained former [Trump attorney general](https://www.marijuanamoment.net/anti-marijuana-group-hires-trumps-former-attorney-general-for-lawsuit-to-block-rescheduling-move-directed-by-president/) Bill Barr as a lawyer earlier this year. He declined to comment. Kevin Sabet, the group’s president, told me that everything is “on the table for us.”

The day Trump issued the executive order, Kessler was in attendance at the White House ceremony. The president thanked him and asked Kessler how he was feeling. “Great,” he replied.

“It looks like you’re doing great,” Trump said. “I’m proud of you. I want to emphasize that the order I am about to sign is not the legalization, or it doesn’t legalize marijuana in any way, shape, or form.” Oz [hailed Kessler](https://www.democrats.senate.gov/newsroom/trump-transcripts/transcript-president-trump-signs-an-executive-order-to-ease-cannabis-rules-121825) as a “mutual friend” who has “been a passionate advocate for avoiding narcotics, especially in seniors and particularly seniors suffering from cancer.”

“God bless you for being a pain in our sides,” Oz said.

After the signing, Kessler’s phone rang. It was his friend, the American president. “It worked out great!” Trump told him.

Looking back now, Kessler told me at his mansion in Palm Beach, “you couldn’t resonate with anybody” on cannabis during Trump’s first presidency. This time around, “because of our relationship and my commitment, he did the executive order,” Kessler said.

“It really had to do with a personal relationship,” he said. “He’s amazed at what I did with credit cards and understands it changed the world.”
