Matt Mahan Is a Normie Democrat. Could He Be California’s Next Governor?

He may not have the name recognition, but with Eric Swalwell out of the race, it’s Mahan’s time to shine.

When I finally got San Jose mayor Matt Mahan on the phone early Friday evening, he was in a rental car zigzagging through the west side of Los Angeles, and Artemis II was about to splash down off the coast of San Diego, two hours south.

Mahan, one of 10 candidates in California’s gubernatorial race, was back in the city to do some television hits and meet and greets, and he was sounding appropriately somber: A few hours before, the *[San Francisco Chronicle](https://www.sfchronicle.com/politics/article/eric-swalwell-allegations-22198271.php)* and then [CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2026/04/10/us/eric-swalwell-sexual-misconduct-allegations-invs) had reported on several women accusing Representative Eric Swalwell, the front-runner in the race, of sexually assaulting or harassing them. One woman had accused the congressman of rape.

Swalwell denied the allegations, calling them “ [absolutely false](https://x.com/RepSwalwell/status/2042800069334962405?s=20),” but the long knives were out. There were a lot of votes on the line. And campaign cash. And endorsements.

“Eric Swalwell is symptomatic of an insider, unaccountable politics that doesn’t think it needs to answer to the people,” Mahan told me after his team hastily assembled a press conference to address the Swalwell allegations.

Now, with Swalwell suspending his campaign Sunday night, “the whole race was fundamentally reset,” a Democratic strategist in LA told me.

And Matt Mahan, who had been polling in the [low single digits](https://www.270towin.com/2026-governor-polls/california) despite a good bit of big-name support and early fundraising, suddenly had a real chance.

“He’s the normie candidate,” the Democratic strategist said. For voters who don’t think about politics a lot, and who still believe in capitalism and worry about buying their first home and a gallon of gas, “the only place they can really land is Mahan,” he said, echoing consultants, campaign aides, and bundlers I spoke with over the weekend.

The question is whether the campaign cash comes in, Mahan supporters told me.

There is a good-government group in the Bay Area, Govern for California, [raising $35 million](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/04/07/us/elections/california-governor-mahan-donor-silicon-valley.html) for Mahan, but he needed more than that. And that money was being raised “in escrow”—meaning donors get their donations back if the group doesn’t hit its target.

The Democratic consultant was perplexed by all the donors who had yet to commit, or who hadn’t maxed out.

“I won’t put any gas in the car, but why can’t it go 100 miles an hour?” he said.

I first met Mahan a few weeks earlier, at Carney’s, the hamburger joint in a yellow train car on Sunset Boulevard. He had flown down to LA early that morning to raise money for his then-fledgling gubernatorial campaign.

“This used to be the *place*,” a burger flipper told one of Mahan’s aides when the mayor and his entourage turned up.

In the 1980s,Carney’s at lunchtime in the middle of the week was a scene: rock ‘n’ rollers, actors, agents, talent managers. Dudes with wifebeaters and ripped jeans and long hair, girls in stilettos, girls on roller skates. Porsches and Lamborghinis in the parking lot.

---

Now it was more subdued. The city, like so much of California, was still figuring out what it was supposed to be next.

Mahan, 43, was the only candidate in the race who seemed to grasp that California’s identity—not just its economy or infrastructure—was on the line.

He’s a Democrat, meaning he can win statewide, and he grew up in Watsonville, a mostly Hispanic town an hour south of San Jose. His father served in the 101st Airborne, and then he was a letter carrier, a union man. Mahan excelled at his Jesuit high school and got into Harvard University, where he lived down the hall from Mark Zuckerberg. (They’ve stayed in touch “intermittently.”) After graduation, he taught public school, and he was in the tech start-up space for several years. He seems to grasp that his party, which controls the machinery of government in Sacramento and in all the state’s big cities, had torpedoed the California Dream—the sense of infinite possibility.

“We need Democratic leaders in California who are willing to say no to their friends,” Mahan told me. He meant the unions—the teachers, the correctional officers, the health workers, the janitors, the hundreds of thousands of state and county bureaucrats—plus the environmentalists, the homeless industrial complex, the plaintiffs’ bar. Everyone who has made it insanely expensive to build in a state that once did nothing but create new things.

Until Friday, there were only four truly viable candidates in the June 2 primary, including Mahan, who called the other Democratic contenders “the same old, same old.”

That includes billionaire hedge-funder Tom Steyer, who had been [endorsed by Jane Fonda](https://x.com/TomSteyer/status/2042606165742248166?s=20) —“Who wouldn’t want Hanoi Jane in their corner?” a Democratic donor joked—and who, I’ve been told, liked to say that his wealth made him incorruptible.

“Any billionaire who says they can’t be bought—it’s just not true,” David Crane, who runs Govern for California, told me. “You desperately need the endorsement of recognizable organizations that are populist-sounding, like the nurses, to get anybody to pay attention to you.”

---

Then there’s former representative Katie Porter, whom a former donor called “smart,” “a performance artist,” and “completely lacking in any leadership skills.” (Porter is best known, perhaps, [for yelling](https://www.youtube.com/shorts/BwVU9gid_pA) at one of her aides to get out of her “fucking shot” during a video recording.)

And then there was Swalwell, whose candidacy is no more.

Alas, the vast majority of California voters have never heard of Matt Mahan, and with early voting starting May 23—less than six weeks away—and only a fraction of the electorate paying attention, name ID is everything.

He’s not a fixture on MS NOW, like Swalwell, and he doesn’t have the bottomless coffers to match Steyer, who has spent more than $100 million on his campaign. And he lacks the connections, the visibility, that Porter picked up when she ran for and won her House seat during the great blue wave of 2018, taking on Donald Trump.

One of Mahan’s advantages is that he seems to think that his job is his job, not the next one. He’s not, in other words, a platform surfer surfing his way up up up. To do that, you have to make concessions, lots of them, big-time, until you no longer resemble you. This was the point a Democratic strategist in San Francisco made—about former California governor Gray Davis, Gavin Newsom, and Barack Obama. By the time Obama was Mahan’s age, he was already a U.S. senator and just two years away from launching his presidential bid. “If you want to be president, you’ll give in to the California Teachers Association and the correctional officers, or your career will be over,” the strategist said.

“Ultimately,” David Crane said of Mahan, “he cares about something greater than himself”—that was why he was backing him.

Barry Porter, an investment banker who has raised tons of cash for Democratic presidential candidates over the years, told me that shortly after Rick Caruso bowed out of the gubernatorial race, the developer told a group in LA that “if you like me, you’re going to *love* Matt.”

Porter said that he first met Mahan about three weeks ago at a dinner. The mayor started giving his stump speech, talking about how he had reined in crime, installed $85,000 modular units to house the homeless (versus [somewhere around $1 million](https://www.citywatchla.com/los-angeles/29866-what-does-it-cost-to-house-a-homeless-person) in LA), made government more accountable, and fostered a pro-business climate.

“Mahan is high approval once people find out about him. Now is the moment where that happens. In start-ups we call that ‘fund it now!’ ” —Garry Tan

“Two minutes in, a buddy of mine to my right had a sort of *Jerry Maguire* moment,” Porter said. “He was like, ‘He got me at hello,’ followed by, ‘We gotta help him.’ ”

The tech titans, fed up with so-called progressives and their hostility to technological progress, felt the same.

Garry Tan, the CEO of the start-up incubator Y Combinator, said Mahan’s election would precipitate a “ [boom loop](https://garryslist.org/posts/matt-mahan-for-governor-california-s-last-chance) ” that would jolt the state’s economy. He [has donated](https://localnewsmatters.org/2026/02/14/matt-mahan-billionaire-tech-donors-california-governor/) $78,400, the most you can legally give to the campaign. Sohas Google co-founder Sergey Brin; Palantir co-founder Joe Lonsdale; David Baszucki, the creator of the gaming platform Roblox; and [Rick Caruso](https://www.thefp.com/p/saving-california-its-just-not-that), the billionaire developer who had himself flirted with a gubernatorial run.

“I think Steyer, if he implemented his open campaign promises and does whatever the hard left wants, would probably destroy California,” Tan messaged me. “Porter and the other Dem machine names are high name recognition, low ceiling. People know their names. They just don’t want to vote for them.”

Then, he said, there’s Mahan. “Mahan is high approval once people find out about him. Now is the moment where that happens. In start-ups we call that ‘fund it now!’ Those are the unicorns.”

Never before have the stakes felt so stratospheric.

Either California continues down the path of the “performative politician,” as Mahan put it—always advertising one’s fealty to the luxury beliefs: the gender fluidity, the climate radicalism, the post-October 7 antisemitism on campus—or it embraces a more serious politics. Either it succumbs to the plummeting housing starts and plummeting schools (now No. 38 in the nation) and the graffiti and homelessness and excrement and the exodus of talent and capital—or not.

“It feels like this is our last chance,” one of Mahan’s supporters told me—a smidge overwrought, but only a smidge.

Democrats, Mahan said, have “become consumed by virtue signaling and catering to highly organized interests, often, you know, in place of actually making government deliver results.”

“We’ve become a party that’s enamored of jargon and so-called systems thinking,” he said. “We’ve spent too much in college dealing in abstractions, honestly.”

Then, he added: “We have to be *for* something. It really worries me that so much of our headspace on the left is really focused on what we’re against.”

“We’ve become a party that’s enamored of jargon and so-called systems thinking. We’ve spent too much in college dealing in abstractions, honestly.” —Matt Mahan

He meant the fixation on Trump, which is what Democrats talk about when they don’t want to talk about whatever they’re doing (or not doing).

The question was whether the Democratic Party could become a normal party again, one that’s made up of voting blocs with often overlapping, incongruous demands—a coalition, a big tent.

David Crane said: “The center cannot hold when teachers are underpaid and forced to strike even though state spending on schools has exploded, people aren’t getting healthier even though state spending on healthcare has exploded, nobody can afford anything, unemployment is the highest in the country, economic mobility for the lower- and middle-income classes has stalled, the University of California and California State systems go hungry while prison guards are paid twice the national average, and more.”

One of Mahan’s bundlers told me: “We’re driving some of our most innovative and successful people out of the state. I know probably a couple dozen people who have left, all of whom were big taxpayers, all of whom had hired a lot of people who they took with them out of the state.”

Progressives, in California, in New York, in all the big cities, Mahan said, echoing something I’d heard from his fellow Democratic reformer [Daniel Lurie](https://www.thefp.com/p/daniel-lurie-wants-to-save-san-francisco-progressive-politics-mayor-california), the San Francisco mayor, need to prove that everything they touch doesn’t go to shit—unless they want more of Trump. (He didn’t put it exactly like that, but that was the sentiment.)

“We need to prove that blue cities and states have a constructive alternative to the future,” Mahan said.

Now, as he starts to tick up in the polls, Mahan is coming in for the inevitable drubbing.

Tony Thurmond, the head of California’s public schools and another one of Mahan’s gubernatorial rivals, [argued Mahan](https://www.instagram.com/reel/DWR0gXmkiKn/) is owned by tech moguls who would replace jobs with artificial intelligence. Steyer has [said Mahan](https://www.tomsteyer.com/press/steyer-mahan-s-donors-show-who-he-s-fighting-for) is being bankrolled by people like Steyer—billionaires.

It didn’t help when Mahan came out against a proposed one-time 5-percent tax on billionaires.

“My mom always told my sisters and me, ‘Don’t cut off your nose to spite your face,’ ” [Mahan wrote on X](https://x.com/MattMahanSJ/status/2008230742309126449?s=20) in January.

“And as I think about the ramifications of the so-called Billionaire Tax, I can’t help but hear her voice. We need a rising economic tide to lift all boats, not a political plan that will sink California’s innovation economy.”

---

The post, part of a five-part thread, asked why government needed more taxpayer money without having come up with a “meaningful plan” for spending the money it already has. “Waste, fraud and abuse are real in the healthcare system—and throughout state government.”

He seemed to be tapping into the zeitgeist. There was the investigation into the ongoing [hospice fraud](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HqYBq_74Fg0); the state’s nonexistent, multibillion-dollar [high-speed rail](https://www.instagram.com/reel/DWy96txEQgl/); the boatloads of Covid money that just... disappeared. A recent *City Journal* report [estimated](https://www.city-journal.org/article/gavin-newsom-california-fraud) that $180 billion have been stolen under Newsom’s watch. (The mostly nonpartisan site *CalMatters* countered that much of that fraud actually amounts to “ [poor management](https://calmatters.org/commentary/2026/04/gavin-newsom-empire-fraud-california/).” But still.)

Good government is Mahan’s path to the governorship—or so he hopes.

“I don’t want to give away too much of our campaign strategy, but there’s a big chunk of the Democratic Party in California that is not happy with the direction of the state, and looking for change,” he said over lunch. There are also Republican-leaning voters who don’t love their options—former Fox personality Steve Hilton and Riverside County sheriff Chad Bianco—and know full well that no Republican can win in a state that Trump lost by more than 20 points in 2024, he said. “And, importantly, there’s about 20 percent of the electorate in the middle who are truly independent voters. Nobody’s talking to *them*.”

He was encouraged, he said, that his opponents’ support was “very soft.”

“The one in four voters who have been following this race has not been enthusiastic about what they’re hearing,” he said.

“We’re doing town halls, having events, and we’re filling rooms,” he said. “So people are clearly looking for an alternative.”

Mahan, it should be noted, doesn’t look like a governor.

He’s not broad-shouldered. He doesn’t have a big head (his head is actually a little narrow). He doesn’t smile as easily as the baby-kissers, the glad-handers. He doesn’t make the kind of intense eye contact that is, by now, a cliché on the stump. He’s crisp, well-mannered, exceedingly polite. He doesn’t vacillate, the way so many of the pols do, between the saccharine and the combative—piling on the chocolate sauce or going mano a mano.

But he appears to know how to run for office—including step one: insisting you don’t really want to run for office.

“I have a great life,” Mahan said between fries. His wife, Silvia Scandar Mahan, had been a lawyer until she became president and CEO at a Jesuit high school. “We’re very happy where we are. Our kids are in school. They love their soccer teams.”

He decided to run, he said, because he’s “concerned about the future of our state.” But running for governor of the most populous state in the country, the state that used to define the American psyche, the freedom, the edge, the search for self, Hollywood, Haight-Ashbury, the mountains, the coast, the borderlessness of it all—“it’s an insane thing to do,” Mahan said. “Right?”

He had been hoping to debate his fellow gubernatorial aspirants at the University of Southern California last month—to introduce himself to voters, to take on the accusations that he’s the billionaires’ puppet. That was the conundrum he faced: proving to voters that the billionaires like him not because he’s their lackey, but because he knows how to make things grow.

“There’s a big chunk of the Democratic Party in California that is not happy with the direction of the state and looking for change.” —Matt Mahan

“The kids are so radical now, I think they think they’re the same,” the Mahan bundler said.

But the debate had [blown up](https://www.latimes.com/california/story/2026-03-23/california-leaders-call-to-boycott-debate-if-other-candidates-not-included).

Six of the candidates were supposed to have been on the debate stage: Swalwell, Steyer, Porter, Mahan, and the two Republicans.

All the leading nonwhite candidates were excluded, because they were polling in the single digits and, unlike Mahan, trailed in fundraising.

But that didn’t stop them from screaming racism. [Steyer](https://www.instagram.com/reels/DWUJIgGD4If/), [Swalwell](https://www.facebook.com/EricMSwalwell/posts/debates-are-a-fundamental-part-of-our-democratic-process/1505178940969343/), and [Porter](https://x.com/katieporterca/status/2035861704643404130) distanced themselves from the debate, questioning the methodology. (There was nothing wrong with the methodology.) In the case of the Latino and black and Asian candidates, it was all about stoking Latino anger. In the case of the white candidates, it was about not offending non-white and especially Latino voters, who now comprise [more than 30 percent](https://latino.ucla.edu/press/ucla-lppi-new-brief-highlighting-latino-eligible-voters-in-california/) of California’s electorate.

On March 23, Democratic leaders in the state legislature sent USC President Beong-Soo Kim [a letter](https://www.scribd.com/document/1016604713/Letter-to-USC-President-Kim-Final-032326) “demanding” that he open the debate to all “leading candidates,” suggesting Mahan had been included because Caruso, who sits on USC’s board, is backing him. That night, Kim called off the debate.

Now, Mahan’s Silicon Valley donors are getting antsy, worried that their great last hope will flub it, that he’s not bloodthirsty in the same way as Newsom, who would do anything for a vote and is often compared to the fictional serial killer Patrick Bateman. One of his supporters texted me that he worried Mahan is “too nice to win.”

Of course, there had been the last 72 hours—and Mahan, along with all the other gubernatorial hopefuls, plunging their stakes into Swalwell’s back. Was that not evidence that Mahan was adequately saber-toothed? I asked one of his donors.

“We’ll see,” he said. “We’ll hope.”

There was an irony about Mahan riding the corpse of the Swalwell campaign, was there not? I asked. The mayor had made his name denouncing performative politics, and now he was cashing in on all the Democrats and influencers who were making a show of trashing Swalwell, announcing to the whole wide world that they wanted nothing to do with him. There have yet to be any investigations; most of the accusations are anonymous. But still, better to be sure no one suspects they don’t #BelieveAllWomen.

“I guess,” the donor said. Then, he added: “I don’t care.”
