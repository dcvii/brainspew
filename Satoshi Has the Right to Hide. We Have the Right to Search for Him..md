Do we have a right to know who Satoshi is? That is, the Satoshi Nakamoto who created Bitcoin in 2008 and mysteriously disappeared. He announced that he was leaving Bitcoin development in 2011, leaving no further trace except for two emails of uncertain authenticity that may have been sent in 2014 and 2015.

A *[New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/04/08/business/bitcoin-satoshi-nakamoto-identity-adam-back.html)* [investigation](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/04/08/business/bitcoin-satoshi-nakamoto-identity-adam-back.html) by John Carreyrou published last week is the latest effort to unmask Satoshi’s true identity. Based on evidence that ranges from an early interest in anonymous digital cash to hints in his writing style, the *Times* article concludes that Satoshi is British crypto CEO Adam Back. Two years ago, an HBO special, *[Money Electric: The Bitcoin Mystery](https://www.hbomax.com/movies/money-electric-the-bitcoin-mystery/90e45730-bcb5-4525-aaea-44425a77c531)*, identified Satoshi as Canadian programmer Peter Todd, but as possibly working with Adam Back, giving the nod to Todd on the programming side. To be clear, [Back denies the claims](https://www.coindesk.com/markets/2026/04/08/adam-back-denies-he-s-satoshi-nakamoto-after-nyt-report-claims-he-s-bitcoin-s-creator), [as does Todd](https://edition.cnn.com/2024/10/08/investing/satoshi-nakamoto-identity/index.html).

The identity of Satoshi isn’t just the greatest mystery of the crypto world, it’s one of the great mysteries of the world, period. So it’s no surprise that historians, journalists, crypto enthusiasts, and casual crypto watchers (I include myself in that latter category), are eager to know who Satoshi really is. In 2014, *Newsweek* identified him as an unemployed Japanese American programmer, in a scoop that [quickly fell apart](https://www.newsweek.com/2014/03/14/face-behind-bitcoin-247957.html). *Wired* pinned him as Craig Wright, an Australian cryptographer, who [turned out](https://www.wired.com/2015/12/bitcoins-creator-satoshi-nakamoto-is-probably-this-unknown-australian-genius/) to be an imposter. There have been many other guesses over the years.

Currently the total outstanding value of Bitcoin is well over a trillion dollars. No other individual has created such a valuable financial asset. There is an entire crypto universe of other assets to boot, including the now-prominent stablecoins, tied to the value of the dollar. Adding to the mystery is that Satoshi’s personal Bitcoin stash, valued at over $70 billion, has just sat untouched since Bitcoin was first created. It seems as if someone is declining the opportunity to live as a mega-billionaire.

But do we really have the right to dig into Satoshi’s identity and background? Privacy is at the very core of Bitcoin: Satoshi, whoever it turns out to be, believed strongly in the right to make anonymous transactions. And it seems Satoshi valued his own privacy a great deal, given how hard he has worked to keep his identity a secret. Back, who denies being Satoshi, [has said](https://x.com/adam3us/status/2041816020776611935?s=20) that it is better for Bitcoin that its creator should remain unidentified. Bitcoin and its associated technologies are used widely, no matter who Satoshi might turn out to be. What is the public interest in unmasking him?

Naming Satoshi could damage that person’s life in some significant ways. If the world believes you might be worth $70 billion or more, at the very least you will be deluged with requests for funds. You will not be able to go out in public without being identified and asked for photos or an autograph. Some of the world’s security services might start spying on you, and paparazzi might flock to your trail.

It’s possible, of course, that Satoshi—or any team that might have gone by the pseudonym—has lost the key to access the funds. That’s a possibility that the HBO documentary suggested. It would explain why the wealth has not been touched. It also would make those individuals look like fools and laughingstocks, exposing them to public humiliation without the recompense of the billions.

Still, I will vote for investigating who Satoshi is. I see the value of allowing room for curiosity, and of humans knowing their history, as higher than that of one person’s privacy, at least in this case. Satoshi had a right to do what he did, but others also have a right to use their own investigative efforts to find out as much as they can about the person who created what has become an important part of the financial system.

Keep in mind that Satoshi’s actions in creating Bitcoin have been highly controversial. Many people, such as [Paul Krugman,](https://paulkrugman.substack.com/p/crypto-is-for-criming) feel that Bitcoin has made the world a worse place by enabling black market transactions and by helping cyberhackers collect and keep their payments. Bitcoin has been a tool for Iran [to sell weapons](https://www.ft.com/content/d1ceb1a4-3493-4776-ae22-c94d76dc478f?syn-25a6b1a6=1), and Bitcoin theft has helped drive [North Korea’s nuclear program](https://www.bbc.com/news/world-asia-60281129). In fact, right now Iran is demanding Bitcoin payments for Hormuz tolls.

My overall evaluation is more positive than Krugman’s, but that is not the point. If the quality of an innovation is debatable, it seems legitimate to want to know from where and from whom it came. The motives and plans of the creator seem like fair game, too. By putting Bitcoin out there, Satoshi made himself a public figure, whether he was looking for that status or not.

Let’s say that I dumped some iron filings into the ocean, or engaged in some form of legal geoengineering, with the goal of reversing harmful climate change. Do I have the right to anonymity for such an action? I do not think so. If reporters can trace my steps, I think they should try and do so. That is true even if my actions created a net benefit for mankind, and it is all the more true if those actions turn out to be harmful. Identifying me could well lead me to some harm, but the question here is not a purely utilitarian calculus. Societies should value knowing the truth very highly, even if not everyone ends up better off.

So do I think Adam Back is Satoshi? Both yes and no. I do not believe Back was the genius who wrote the code and turned the idea of Bitcoin into a reality. Back does not seem like the proper personality or cognitive type for such a feat; basically, he does not come across to me as nerdy enough. The code innovator might be Todd, or any number of other individuals Back had contact with. And thus you might, as the HBO team did, identify Todd as the true creator.

Nonetheless, I believe that Back was the ringleader, organizer, and marketer of a small team that created and promoted Bitcoin. That is why Carreyrou found so much circumstantial evidence that linked Back to the wording, preoccupations, and even punctuation practices of Satoshi. Yet there is no smoking gun in the code itself; in fact, the nature of the code seems to [point away from Back](https://x.com/robertgraham/status/2042039637984596459?s=46).

But there is an additional and, to me, psychologically weighty reason why I believe Back is involved, and on which I agree with Carreyrou. Carreyrou writes that he set off on the story after seeing Back’s demeanor in the HBO documentary—tensing up, shifty eyes, jerky movements—when he hears his own name in a list of Satoshi suspects. I also watched the documentary with great care, and had a similar impression at a different part of the program, when Back and Todd are confronted with the possibility that they might have worked together on Bitcoin. If you believe in “poker tells,” to my eyes they look and react as if they have been caught out, not accused unjustly. Carreyrou’s observations were similar in his final in-person interview with Back.

I have had two credible (to me) sources confirm my impressions, although there also I am making further personal judgments, not pretending to have proof. And so our assessments of Satoshi, and also Adam Back, and Peter Todd, will remain very human ones. Satoshi could identify himself, and could prove his identity by moving part of his Bitcoin stash. Otherwise, the question is not resolvable through fully objective means, at least not yet, and so we must choose whom we wish to trust, and what signals to rely on. That is perhaps the biggest Satoshi lesson of them all.
