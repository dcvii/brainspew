Recently as I snacked on Chicken McNuggets (new honey hot sauce, mmm…) I found myself reflecting on the weird relationship the public has with the fast-food chain. Particularly, the strange discord between the public’s scorn regarding the healthiness of McDonald’s food and the massive industrial dominance McDonald’s has in the cheap food market. Insulting the health benefits (or lack thereof) of McDonald’s food is nothing new. Back in the 80’s rumors flew that their burger meat was kangaroo rather than beef. Why this was “bad” is now a mystery to me…it turns out kangaroo meat is delicious [1](https://grimoiremanor.substack.com/p/the-mcdonalds-paradox#footnote-1-190430932) and rather expensive to import, but that was how it was.

Documentaries demonstrated how McNuggets were made from gelled chicken goo reassembled to look vaguely like chicken meat (has that stopped me from loving them? No). Indeed, one of my favorite jokes about them is referring to them as “McDeadlets” [2](https://grimoiremanor.substack.com/p/the-mcdonalds-paradox#footnote-2-190430932).

Perhaps most infamous was the 2004 documentary *Supersize Me* which seemed to suggest that a 30-day diet of only McDonald’s food could cause liver damage and other negative health outcomes. The veracity of the documentary’s claims is now in serious doubt, particular as Morgan Spurlock, the producer and test subject, has [admitted](https://www.wsj.com/articles/a-big-mac-attack-or-a-false-alarm-1527114255) abusing alcohol during the same period. Nonetheless, at the time it sparked a huge national conversation about fast food eating.

And yet…people keep eating it. I don’t eat fast food as much as when I was younger, but I still like it from time to time. And I can vividly remember those images of pink chicken paste that become McNuggets while biting into one and think, “Well, it may not be pretty but tasty miracles of science seldom are.”

McDonald’s is doing [just fine](https://www.mmcginvest.com/post/mcdonald-s-corporation-under-the-golden-arches-a-financial-juggernaut) financially, you’ll be glad to know, despite all the criticism. That’s not to say the public relations issues haven’t sometimes stung. After Supersize Me, McDonald’s dropped their Supersize options. I’m not sure why the revelation that eating more high calorie, high salt food would be worse that consuming less would be a scandalous shock to the public, but alas. And in the 2000s, bowing to public outrage over rising obesity, McDonald’s started offering healthier options like salads and apple dippers for the little varmints.

The result was predictable: After all the fuss, [almost nobody](https://www.distractify.com/p/does-mcdonalds-still-have-salads?utm_source=chatgpt.com) bought them. The healthy options were quietly killed off the menu entirely during covid19, ostensibly to speed up drive-through operations, though they were never brought back [3](https://grimoiremanor.substack.com/p/the-mcdonalds-paradox#footnote-3-190430932). Now, McDonald’s is riding high, and Supersize Me is the source of doubt. Spurlock never released his eating logs and other experimenters actually [lost weight](https://www.al.com/entertainment/2015/08/meet_the_science_teacher_who_l.html) eating McDonald’s only for long periods of time. The revelations about his abusing alcohol pretty much torpedo any causal claims in the film.

So, what happened? Why did the public seem to demand healthy food options, then not buy them when they had the chance?

The issue is complex but probably boils down to several issues. First, people often taken public stances for social credit, even though their private behavior doesn’t match those public stances [4](https://grimoiremanor.substack.com/p/the-mcdonalds-paradox#footnote-4-190430932). Second, people can sometimes convince themselves they want morally “good” things, but don’t like them once they actually get them [5](https://grimoiremanor.substack.com/p/the-mcdonalds-paradox#footnote-5-190430932). Three, people prefer to attribute their bad behavior to external entities rather than take personal responsibility.

In that sense, McDonalds is an easy target. It’s low-brow, cheap and naughty. People want it, but they’re embarrassed by that. So, they blame McDonalds for conducting some kind of evil sorcery for giving them exactly the product they want. The hypocrisy is only made obvious when McDonalds tries to offer something healthy and the public are like, “What the crap is this…leaves? If I wanted a salad, I’ve already got frou-frou places to go to get that.”

People like to pretend they are above all that. “How could anyone even think of eating a McDeadlet?” we loudly claim while quietly wondering if we should get our next order with the new Creamy Chili sauce or stick to the tried-and-true honey mustard.

A lot of these social panics target entities with a similar profile:

> · The thing as perceived as low-brow. McDonalds rather than a fancy bistro (restaurant eating in general is loaded with salt and calories it’s worth noting), video games rather than books (though books themselves were once…and often still are…the target [6](https://grimoiremanor.substack.com/p/the-mcdonalds-paradox#footnote-6-190430932)), etc..
> 
> · The thing is popular with kids, racial minorities, or the poor (or, in previous eras, women).
> 
> · Using the thing is perceived as naughty, tied to some negative outcome that may or may not exist.
> 
> · There’s often a comparison to some halcyon prior era that likely never existed where everyone ate healthy, no one ever had high blood pressure or got cancer, the kids were all happy, it was always 72 degrees and sunny, yet all the plants were green, etc.
> 
> · People happily ignore that “fixing” the issue would likely price a significant percentage of the population out. Healthy restaurants (relatively speaking) already exist; they’re just far more expensive on average. We already have healthier fast(ish) food chains like Cava or Sweetgreen or even Panera…just they’re a lot more expensive than McDonalds.

McDonalds continues to flourish for a simple reason: people want cheap, fast, naughtily delicious food that *they already know isn’t healthy*. It’s not a health spa, it’s a guilty pleasure. Wave your fist at the sky all you want, but the public have spoken with their wallets. Besides, does anything quite taste like childhood like a McDonalds hamburger?

In general, I think McDonalds does have one thing right: we need to stop blaming big companies for our naughty choices. We’ve been doing that for decades, and it’s helped exactly 0. I’m libertarian enough to be highly suspicious of the government trying to intercede in our health choices and stop us from eating Big Macs, drinking soda, playing video games, etc. Maybe smoking cigarettes was an unusually potent one-off, but I firmly don’t believe that should be the model for how we let the government dictate most health behaviors.

Letting people know the health risks of their behavioral choices is good: it gives people the knowledge they deserve to have when making those choices. Beyond that, we don’t need a government nannying us like children throughout our lives. So enjoy that quarter pounder and live (or die) with the consequences.
