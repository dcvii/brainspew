***After President Trump’s weekend talk of an agreement with Iran, the past 36 hours have seen a flurry of military activity in the Middle East. On Monday, the United States launched a series of “self-defense” strikes on sites in southern Iran, sinking two boats that were laying mines. The move prompted Iran’s Ministry of Foreign Affairs to vow retaliation for what it called a “grave violation” of the ceasefire in a war now nearing its fourth month. And all of this is only the latest twist in a conflict that seems to change shape with every passing moment—often because of a social media post by the president himself.***

***What are we to make of it all? To bring clarity on where the war is—and what to expect next—we turn to historian and Free Press columnist Niall Ferguson. —The Editors***

> Like all our fellow citizens, Rieux... was torn between conflicting fears and confidence. When a war breaks out, people say, “It’s too stupid; it can’t last long.” But though a war may be “too stupid,” that doesn’t prevent it lasting. Stupidity has a knack of getting its way; as we should see if we were not always so much wrapped up in ourselves. —Albert Camus, *[The Plague](https://bookshop.org/a/93116/9781400042555)*, part I, chapter 5

Since February 28, we have experienced the geopolitical equivalent of a roller-coaster ride on magic mushrooms. We hurtled from Operation Epic Fury to Operation Economic Fury to Project Freedom to no fury at all, and not much freedom of navigation either. We sped from the impending obliteration of Iranian civilization to “a Memorandum of Understanding pertaining to PEACE... largely negotiated, subject to finalization.” If you have not experienced the psychological equivalent of whiplash in the last 12 weeks, you have not been paying attention. How many nearly-peace nearly-deals have we nearly had? Four? Five?

On Sunday, there was once again media speculation that a deal to end the war was close to being done. According to [Barak Ravid](https://www.axios.com/2026/05/24/iran-deal-strait-hormuz-sanctions-nuclear) of *Axios*, quoting an unnamed “U.S. official,” Donald Trump’s nearly-but-not-quite-finalized, still-could-fall-apart draft memorandum of understanding (MOU) had seven key elements:

1. There would be a 60-day ceasefire extension.
2. During that period, the Strait of Hormuz would be reopened with Iran agreeing to levy no tolls and to clear mines.
3. Also during that period, the U.S. would issue sanctions waivers to enable Iran to sell its oil.
4. The faster the Iranians cleared the mines and let shipping resume, the faster the U.S. blockade would be lifted, on the basis of “relief for performance.”
5. At the same time, negotiations would be held on curbing Iran’s nuclear program. According to Ravid’s source, the draft MOU included “commitments from Iran to never pursue nuclear weapons and to negotiate over a suspension of its uranium enrichment program and the removal of its stockpile of highly enriched uranium.”
6. If the negotiations progressed successfully, Iranian funds would be unfrozen and sanctions permanently lifted.
7. The war between Israel and Hezbollah in Lebanon would also end.

There are two ways of interpreting this nascent deal, and either could be right, depending on the small print and how things actually go over the summer. Either, as Iranian American commentator [Patrick Bet-David](https://x.com/patrickbetdavid/status/2058387299470037460?s=46&t=iZh6EpZ9fah6fJLKeAFlaw) insisted, Trump has succeeded where George W. Bush, Barack Obama, and Joe Biden failed, ending Iran’s nuclear program with a devastating blitzkrieg. Or, as [Robert Kagan](https://www.theatlantic.com/international/2026/05/trump-surrender-iran-endgame/687252/?gift=hVZeG3M9DnxL4CekrWGK3z7P5ziNahM4p4Y6XS3ozvg) argued in *The Atlantic*, the Iranian terms “are those of a victor” and Trump’s acceptance of these terms is “a tacit admission of defeat.”

Signs that a deal is imminent include the fact that Israel’s prime minister, Benjamin Netanyahu, has [not shot it down](https://x.com/netanyahu/status/2058546285834080466?s=46) in flames. More importantly, as Trump’s Truth Social post on Saturday made clear, he spent that day in the Oval Office on calls with the leaders of Saudi Arabia, the United Arab Emirates (UAE), Qatar, Turkey, Egypt, Jordan, and Bahrain, not forgetting Pakistan’s field marshal Asim Munir, who appears to have been the key intermediary. If all these states are content with the terms of the draft memorandum, that reflects a major advance. Up until this point, the Emiratis seemed as reluctant to compromise with Iran as the Israelis. It is also encouraging that Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf, Iran’s parliamentary speaker and lead negotiator, and Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi traveled to Qatar at the start of this week.[News of the Day](https://www.thefp.com/forum/news-of-the-day-7c2/post/265432225?utm_source=comment_embed)

Signs that a deal is *not* imminent include the events of Monday. Despite the ceasefire, U.S. planes sank two Revolutionary Guard speedboats in the Strait of Hormuz. Iran responded by launching surface-to-air missiles at the planes, prompting American strikes on the missile launchers. Also on Monday, Israel struck more than 70 Hezbollah sites across Lebanon. And, of course, Trump changed his tune on Truth. “The negotiations are proceeding in an orderly and constructive manner,” he wrote [on Sunday](https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/116629952973301768), “and I have informed my representatives not to rush into a deal.” The [next day](https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/116635193825443617), he declared: “It will only be a Great Deal for all or, no Deal at all—Back to the Battlefront and shooting, but bigger and stronger than ever before.”

The devil is always in the detail in such matters. No judgment can be anything but provisional until three questions are answered:

1. What exactly will be the future governance of the Strait of Hormuz? If the Institute for the Study of War [is right](https://understandingwar.org/research/middle-east/iran-must-not-rule-the-strait-of-hormuz/), the Iranians are seeking to establish a “Persian Gulf Strait Authority” and to force oil-importing countries to sign transit agreements with them or face either high tolls or exclusion. That would be a major improvement in Iran’s strategic position and the end of a half-century of effective American control of the strait.
2. What exactly are the concessions the Iranians are prepared to make about their nuclear program? If they [really are](https://www.newsnationnow.com/world/deal-with-iran-largely-negotiated-subject-to-finalization-trump/) making “commitments... to never pursue nuclear weapons and to negotiate over a suspension of \[their\] uranium enrichment program and the removal of \[their\] stockpile of highly enriched uranium,” that would indeed represent a major improvement on the 2015 Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action—which only temporarily limited Iran’s nuclear program—and a victory for Trump (and Netanyahu).
3. Is there any substance to Trump’s [latest suggestion](https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/116635193825443617) that the Abraham Accords could be expanded to include Saudi Arabia and Qatar, and even Egypt, Jordan, and Turkey—perhaps even Iran (“it would be an Honor to have them also be part of this unparalleled World Coalition”)? That would be a stunning diplomatic coup, though the fact that Trump says he is “mandatorily requesting” it somehow makes me skeptical.

We shall see. At some point we get off the roller coaster. At some point the mushrooms wear off.

The [one thing I was confident about](https://www.thefp.com/p/niall-ferguson-khamenei-may-be-gone) when the United States and Israel went to war against Iran three months ago was that war would be short. I felt sure of this mainly because I could not imagine President Donald Trump getting embroiled in a protracted conflict—a potential “forever war”—least of all in the Middle East. So I felt sure that Gulf War III would be closer in duration to Gulf War I (six weeks) than to Gulf War II (eight years).

President Trump and Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth brief reporters on the Iran war at the White House on April 6, 2026. (Tom Williams via Getty Images)

In the first phase of the conflict, a barrage of U.S. and Israeli air strikes inflicted what seemed like a devastating decapitation strike on the Iranian regime, killing Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei and other key figures in its leadership, and then substantially disarming their heirs by destroying a large number of its missile and drone launchers.

In the second phase, Iran retaliated by closing the strait and then launching retaliatory strikes, not so much against the U.S. and Israel as against Iran’s Persian Gulf neighbors. So effective were these countermoves that Trump was compelled to agree to a ceasefire.

We then entered phase three: the war of the blockades, in which each side hoped that the consequences of the closure of the strait would force the other to capitulate.

Between TACO (Trump Always Chickens Out) and NACHO (Not a Chance Hormuz Opens), there has been a lot of facile analysis out there. The reality all along has been quite different. For one thing, Trump has oscillated between military action and olive branches, sometimes “chickening out,” but not always. And, of course, the strait has to be reopened at some point. The hard question has been not if, but when?

Let’s take a step backward to get some historical perspective. To listen to many European leaders, and some in the Gulf, Trump’s conduct has been unprecedented in its recklessness. But the most conventional thing Donald Trump has done in his entire life has been to go to war in the Middle East. And he has gone about this war in an entirely familiar way.

Like president after president since 1950, he has found it easy to get into a war, harder to get out. Like president after president since 1969, he has hoped that airpower and/or economic sanctions would avoid the risks of having boots on the ground. The classic pattern of U.S. interventions is nearly always “Fire, Aim, Ready.” The classic pattern of American diplomacy is nearly always to regard talking and fighting as alternatives to one another, rather than complements.

In substance, what we have seen in the past three months is not so very different from Richard Nixon [trying in 1972](https://www.nixonfoundation.org/2017/09/address-nation-vietnam-april-26-1972/) to persuade Hanoi to give him “peace with honor,” meaning a “ [decent interval](https://millercenter.org/the-presidency/educational-resources/nixon-kissinger-and-the-decent-interval) ” between the U.S. departure from South Vietnam and its annexation by the North. The cycle is entirely familiar: bomb—await collapse of foe—negotiate—find that foe is good at negotiation—threaten—negotiate—bomb... and so on.

What is distinctive is Trump’s unfiltered social-media style, which is quite unlike the traditional way modern presidents have addressed the nation in times of crisis. Whereas previous presidents, from John F. Kennedy to Joe Biden, preferred formal television addresses, occasional and carefully prepared press conferences, and off-the-record briefings to favored journalists, Trump uses social media a lot, often dozens of times a day, and also has informal exchanges on the record with reporters, as well as regular sit-down interviews.

The whiplash arises not just from the frequency of Trump’s communications but even more from their bewildering swings from extravagant threats of obliteration to evocations of peace in our time. Because Trump has the peculiar game-theoretical tactic of bluffing roughly half the time and meaning it the other half, it’s not TACO or NACHO—it’s more like TAMALES: Trump Alternately Mogs and Loses to Enemy Statesmen.

Back in January, as the Iranian regime set about brutally repressing mass protests, Trump posted: “If Iran shots \[*sic*\] and violently kills peaceful protesters... the United States of America will come to their rescue. We are locked and loaded and ready to go.” Nothing happened until the end of February, though this was mainly for logistical reasons, as it took time to assemble a strike force large enough for the contemplated action.

On [March 3](https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/116166926920657651), as it became clear that the Iranians had succeeded in halting most traffic through the strait, Trump announced that he had “ordered the United States Development Finance Corporation (DFC) to provide, at a very reasonable price, political risk insurance and guarantees for the Financial Security of ALL Maritime Trade... traveling through the Gulf.” He added that, “If necessary, the United States Navy will begin escorting tankers through the Strait of Hormuz, as soon as possible. No matter what, the United States will ensure the FREE FLOW of ENERGY to the WORLD.”

It goes without saying that this did not happen. It is May 26 and the Strait is still largely closed.

On [March 21](https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/116269822349947644), Trump thundered: “If Iran doesn’t FULLY OPEN, WITHOUT THREAT, the Strait of Hormuz, within 48 HOURS from this exact point in time, the United States of America will hit and obliterate their various POWER PLANTS, STARTING WITH THE BIGGEST ONE FIRST!”

Like president after president since 1950, Trump has found it easy to get into a war, harder to get out.

Two days later, he claimed that Iranian representatives were desperately seeking peace negotiations, writing, “They called, I didn’t call. They want to make a deal.” On [March 30](https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/116317880658472708), he was “in serious discussions with A NEW, AND MORE REASONABLE, REGIME to end our Military Operations in Iran. Great progress has been made.” But, he went on, “if for any reason a deal is not shortly reached, which it probably will be, and if the Hormuz Strait is not immediately ‘Open for Business,’ we will conclude our lovely ‘stay’ in Iran by blowing up and completely obliterating all of their Electric Generating Plants, Oil Wells and Kharg Island (and possibly all desalinization plants!), which we have purposefully not yet ‘touched.’ ” We held our breaths.

On [April 1](https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/116329512466946656), Trump claimed that “Iran’s New Regime President, much less Radicalized and far more intelligent than his predecessors, has just asked the United States of America for a CEASEFIRE!” But—before we could exhale—he added: “We will consider \[that request\] when Hormuz Strait is open, free, and clear. Until then, we are blasting Iran into oblivion or, as they say, back to the Stone Ages!!!”

Trump’s amped up his threats over the next seven days, reaching a bloodcurdling climax on [April 7](https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/116363336033995961):

> A whole civilization will die tonight, never to be brought back again. I don’t want that to happen, but it probably will. However, now that we have Complete and Total Regime Change, where different, smarter, and less radicalized minds prevail, maybe something revolutionarily wonderful can happen, WHO KNOWS? We will find out tonight, one of the most important moments in the long and complex history of the World. 47 years of extortion, corruption, and death, will finally end. God Bless the Great People of Iran!

A retired American admiral with experience of war in the Middle East at the highest level recently told me that he had inferred from Trump’s language at that time that the president might be considering nuclear strikes. But not even conventional strikes happened. On the evening of [April 8](https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/116365796713313030), Trump posted that the Pakistani government had requested that he “hold off the destructive force being sent tonight to Iran, and subject to the Islamic Republic of Iran agreeing to the COMPLETE, IMMEDIATE, and SAFE OPENING of the Strait of Hormuz,” he had agreed “to suspend the bombing and attack of Iran for a period of two weeks.” A lasting peace deal, he now said, was imminent: “Almost all of the various points of past contention have been agreed to between the United States and Iran, but a two week period will allow the Agreement to be finalized and consummated.”

Those who think peace is at hand this week should remember how they felt when they read those words six weeks ago. I hardly need to tell you that peace was not at hand. On [April 12](https://www.opb.org/article/2026/04/12/trump-claims-u-s-will-close-strait-of-hormuz-after-iran-peace-talks-collapse/), Trump revealed in two lengthy posts that negotiations in Islamabad had drawn a blank because “IRAN IS UNWILLING TO GIVE UP ITS NUCLEAR AMBITIONS!” It was at that point that he announced the U.S. blockade of the strait.

April 17 was the truthiest day of the war. Between 9:55 a.m. and 10:40 a.m., Trump posted eight times. First he claimed: “The U.S.A. will get all Nuclear ‘Dust,’ created by our great B2 Bombers - No money will exchange hands in any way, shape, or form.” Then he inveighed against NATO. Then he thanked Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Qatar. Then he announced that “Iran, with the help of the U.S.A., has removed, or is removing, all sea mines!” He promised to make Lebanon great again. He thanked Pakistan. He called it “A GREAT AND BRILLIANT DAY FOR THE WORLD!” He said, “Iran has agreed to never close the Strait of Hormuz again. It will no longer be used as a weapon against the World!”

[That same day,](https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/116420275523158052) Trump declared that the strait was “COMPLETELY OPEN AND READY FOR BUSINESS AND FULL PASSAGE, BUT THE NAVAL BLOCKADE WILL REMAIN IN FULL FORCE AND EFFECT AS IT PERTAINS TO IRAN, ONLY, UNTIL SUCH TIME AS OUR TRANSACTION WITH IRAN IS 100% COMPLETE. THIS PROCESS SHOULD GO VERY QUICKLY IN THAT MOST OF THE POINTS ARE ALREADY NEGOTIATED.” On [April 20](https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/116437457191403164), he added: “If Iran’s new leaders (Regime Change!) are smart, Iran can have a great and prosperous future!”

I could go on. Over the subsequent weeks, Trump oscillated between threatening that “lots of bombs” would “start going off” (April 20) and announcing “great progress... toward a Complete and Final Agreement with Representatives of Iran” (May 5). This was the same post that announced that Project Freedom, announced the day before, had been “paused for a short period.” On May 17, Trump posted a barrage of puerile AI-generated images symbolizing American victory. The next day, following a weekend warning that “the Clock is Ticking... or there won’t be anything left of them,” Trump revealed he had scheduled a massive, full-scale military assault for May 19. Shortly afterward, however, he posted that he had instructed the military to “NOT \[do\] the scheduled attack,” claiming that the leaders of Saudi Arabia, Qatar, and the UAE had requested the postponement.

Which brings us nearly up to date.

The point of reminding you of all this verbiage is simple. Never in the field of human conflict has so much drivel been spouted by just one individual to so many avid readers. And the irony is that this barrage of “truth” has concealed the one genuinely important decision that Trump has taken throughout the conflict.

That decision was taken about six or seven weeks ago, I would guess, probably around the time of the multi-post Truth storm of April 2-5. It was the decision not to proceed with a forcible military reopening of the Strait. My understanding is that a plan to achieve that objective was presented to the president by the chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, Dan Caine, and the commander of U.S. Central Command, Brad Cooper. It would have involved the deployment of thousands of special forces operators and Marines to Iranian territory, as well as the forcible opening of the Strait by Navy destroyers.

In my view, it was the only option available to the president to avoid the prolonged battle of the blockades that has since unfolded. The reason he turned it down was the classic reason so many American presidents have recoiled from deploying “boots on the ground.” He dreaded the body bags and the coffins. He feared the stricken destroyer. Above all, he feared the media coverage and the popular revulsion. He was, in other words, like many another president before him.

As Camus said, when a war breaks out, as when a plague begins, we cannot believe something so awful can last long. “It’s too stupid,” we say. But stupidity does indeed have a knack of getting its way. And just as Trump’s “truths” are mostly stupidities, it is we who read them as if they are true who are—truly—stupid.
