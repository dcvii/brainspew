WASHINGTON—The Trump administration is reclassifying marijuana as a less dangerous drug, a significant policy shift that could make it much easier to buy and sell pot and reward investors in the multibillion-dollar cannabis industry.

Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche signed an order Thursday that moves marijuana out of the government’s most restrictive drug classification. Marijuana has been classified as a Schedule I drug since 1970, alongside LSD and heroin. The action downgrades marijuana to Schedule III, making it obtainable with a prescription.

The move marks the greatest softening of federal cannabis policy since U.S. states began legalizing recreational marijuana in 2012. It sets up a faster process by which state licensed-marijuana producers and distributors can register with the U.S. Drug Enforcement Administration and also makes clear that researchers won’t be punished for obtaining pot products for use in their work. The order also for the first time allows state-licensed medical marijuana companies to deduct business expenses on their federal taxes.

“The rescheduling action allows for research on the safety and efficacy of this substance, ultimately providing patients with better care and doctors with more reliable information,” Blanche said.

President Trump, a teetotaler who eschews alcohol and drugs, in December told his administration to push forward on easing marijuana restrictions, after an [aggressive lobbying push](https://www.wsj.com/politics/policy/how-trump-became-the-unlikely-champion-of-easing-marijuana-restrictions-54ce34f8?mod=article_inline) by advocates including a cannabis CEO, a Florida sheriff and a Mar-a-Lago member, The Wall Street Journal previously reported.

The order marks one of the marijuana industry’s biggest victories, eliminating tax burdens that have made profitability an uphill battle for many [cannabis companies](https://www.wsj.com/finance/investing/pot-stocks-risky-bet-the-irs-will-chill-out-f447fb85?mod=article_inline). Cannabis executives say it will help normalize the business environment for marijuana sellers and improve access for buyers.

The new government policy could affect everything from marijuana cigarettes to pot-infused beverages and gummies. It came over the objection of many Republicans, including 22 senators who signed a letter last year urging Trump to keep the status quo.

But even as a Schedule III drug, marijuana remains illegal under federal law without other changes by Congress.

More than half of states have legalized marijuana for some purpose over the past decade, and many Democrats and some Republicans have taken a more tolerant view of its use.

The Justice Department could still prosecute marijuana offenders, though it has taken a largely hands-off approach to the industry in states where it has been legalized. Officials in recent years have also said they don’t view prosecuting federal marijuana crimes as the best use of their limited resources.

The rescheduling decision comes over long-held concerns among some DEA officials who still believe that marijuana’s [medicinal benefits remain unproven](https://www.wsj.com/politics/policy/biden-push-to-ease-marijuana-restrictions-sparks-tensions-051759f7?mod=article_inline) and that it is prone to abuse. Namely, they are concerned about modern cannabis strains that can be many times more potent than those that were common years ago, and they believe more research is needed.

The agency refused [to reclassify marijuana](https://www.wsj.com/articles/u-s-refuses-to-reclassify-marijuana-but-will-support-more-research-1470924635?mod=article_inline) in 2016. The Biden administration in 2024 proposed downgrading marijuana, yielding 43,000 formal public comments. The DEA was still in the review process when Trump returned to the White House.

Blanche’s order relies on a provision of federal law that allows the attorney general to make the determination for how drugs are classified.
