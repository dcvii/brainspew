### An environmental scan of recent developments

Greetings from spring in the mid-Atlantic, as winter and summer wrestle for preeminence. That’s an apt background for today’s topic, checking in on recent political and policy developments in AI. Let’s scan the past several months.

We’ll start with international uses and geopolitical aspects AI, then shift to national and subnational cases with a special focus on the United States. We’ll add notes on copyright developments and conclude with some reflections. I admit that these categories are artificial and you’ll probably see some stories and points blur between them.

If you’re new to this newsletter, welcome! This is one of [my scanner reports](https://aiandacademia.substack.com/t/scanner), which are examples of what futurists call [horizon scanning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizon_scanning), research into the present and recent past to look for signals of potential futures. On this Substack I scan various domains where I see AI having an impact, documenting each instance. I focus on technology, of course ([for example](https://aiandacademia.substack.com/p/ai-developments-in-high-summer-2025)), but also scan [government and politics](https://aiandacademia.substack.com/p/a-scan-of-ai-and-politics), [economics](https://aiandacademia.substack.com/p/the-ai-economy-keeps-thundering-along), and [education](https://aiandacademia.substack.com/p/higher-education-grapples-with-ai), this newsletter’s ultimate focus.

It’s not all scanning here at AI and Academia! I also write other kinds of issues; [check the archive](https://aiandacademia.substack.com/archive) for examples.

## 1: AI, geopolitics, and strategy

Global competition over AI seems to be persisting and perhaps becoming a popular cause. [A RAND survey](https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RRA4363-1.html) found a majority of Americans thought it important that their country compete with China on AI. Some details: “Men rated the importance of U.S. dominance more highly than women did. Those with a high school diploma or less rated the importance lower than other respondents, and ratings also tended to increase with respondent age.” In contrast [the Club of Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Club_of_Rome) issued [an open letter](https://www.clubofrome.org/ai-open-letter/) calling not for international competition over AI but for global collaboration on it in the form of “a special session of the UN General Assembly specifically on Artificial General Intelligence.”

That competition over AI between the United States and China continued. The American National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) [published a report](https://www.nist.gov/system/files/documents/2025/09/30/CAISI_Evaluation_of_DeepSeek_AI_Models.pdf) warning that the Chinese Deepseek AI “may pose a risk to application developers, to consumers, and to U.S. national security.” At a Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Protection Subcommittee of the House Committee on Homeland Security hearing speakers [warned](https://www.scmp.com/news/us/politics/article/3346942/us-sounds-alarm-over-chinas-humanoid-robots-amid-security-concerns) of competitive threats from Chinese AI-backed robots. NVidia’s CEO Jensen Huang [repeatedly referred](https://qz.com/nvidia-jensen-huang-china-ai-comments) to the two-nation competition as very close. For example, “China is nanoseconds behind America in AI.”

Related to this contest is the American decision to [send AI specialists abroad](https://www.engadget.com/ai/the-us-will-send-tech-corps-members-to-foreign-countries-in-its-latest-push-for-ai-dominance-191916940.html?guccounter=1&guce_referrer=aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuZ29vZ2xlLmNvbS8&guce_referrer_sig=AQAAAARFfy2s8qh0SDoZMKXN1ONBDEJnzgH0UdKyHoZHTELBrLDm6N-v2-x46IeQJ-IA2iHLqeKyka4PpyROhClfp4sqt63Ld6cAb4a6yqNGCWjguxWWrYrpFCMIQv0cfraZn5r87csjJB8YHX8vW323EK9pj_uUgDhXZCXtZRegfeGR) as part of the long-running Peace Corps. The Tech Corps “will recruit STEM graduates or those with professional experience in the artificial intelligence sector and send them to participating host countries.” I do note that the Corps’ website is down, and can only point you to the Internet Archive’s [most recent archived version](https://web.archive.org/web/20260308222104/www.peacecorps.gov/tech/).

Meanwhile, AI for politicking continues to grow. Some politicians embrace the tech for communications. President Trump's administration of using AI to [generate a steady stream](https://www.politico.com/news/2025/12/15/the-white-houses-unabashed-embrace-of-ai-00690885) of images, memes, and videos. In contrast, some prominent American Republicans [oppose](https://www.nonzero.org/p/the-intra-maga-fight-over-ai) AI in general, presumably refusing to use it for strategic purposes.

Candidates, operatives, and supporters are using the technology for elections. A candidate running for a state office in America [staged a debate](https://www.wusa9.com/article/news/politics/elections/virginia-lieutenant-governor-candidate-debates-ai/65-0fe240ac-34c7-4ee1-88c1-cad809bf33f1?emci=8940f076-6daf-f011-8e61-6045bded8ba4&emdi=2148646f-ffaf-f011-8e61-6045bded8ba4&ceid=358923) with an AI version of his opponent. Democrat Ghazala Hashmi declined to debate Republican John Reid, to Reid had an AI trained on Hashmi’s public record answer for her:

![](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BWe1PbGIZw4)

Opponents to Irish presidential candidate [Catherine Connolly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catherine_Connolly) created and [posted a deepfake](https://www.politico.eu/article/deepfake-video-of-irish-presidential-candidate-rocks-campaign/) video of her, where her digital representation announced she was withdrawing from the race. This didn’t weaken Connolly’s campaign, as she won by a large margin. On the European continent two conservative Dutch parliamentarians [used AI](https://www.politico.eu/article/elections-europe-ai-deepfakes-social-media/) to create deepfake images of their opponent; their party’s leader later apologized.

Governments have also been using AI for security and military purposes. Before the Iran war started the head of Israel’s National Cyber Directorate (INCD) [forecast](https://www.jpost.com/defense-and-tech/article-884683) a future conflict wherein AIs organized massive cyberattacks on enemies. As I write this it seems likely that the United States and Israel (at least) have been using AI in the Iran war, but it’s not clear exactly how. AI-assisted targeting seems likely. That INCD leader, Karadi, described how “Iran used cyber weapons to try to target every citizen in Israel multiple times during the 12-day June war;” potentially Iran is doing something similar now. Another world power, China, recently announced a system whereby a soldier would use AI to help control a personal swarm of up to 200 drones:

> According to the *[South China Morning Post](https://www.scmp.com/news/china/military/article/3340972/1-soldier-200-drones-china-showcases-rapid-launch-and-agility-swarm-warfare-tactics)*, the drones are launched from the Swarm I land vehicle, A.K.A the High Mobility Swarm Weapon System, which can simultaneously launch 48 fixed-wing drones that work together. Each unit can then autonomously communicate with each other, allowing the entire swarm to fly in precise formation and divide tasks among themselves, like conducting multi-target reconnaissance and strike operations.

One of the leading founders of contemporary AI, [Yoshua Bengio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yoshua_Bengio), [recommended](https://www.ft.com/content/181f6706-1b23-4691-96bd-db3ae2e3e6bf) that governments worldwide compel AI companies to purchase “liability insurance to cover existential risks.” Otherwise they would lack incentives to proceed safely.

## 2: AI at the national, provincial, or state level

Now let’s focus on individual nations and subnational polities for their approaches to AI in terms of law, governance, policy.

**China** [The new Five Year Plan](https://www.china-briefing.com/news/chinas-15th-five-year-plan-key-insights-for-foreign-investors/) includes [a strong emphasis on AI](https://www.aspistrategist.org.au/key-takeaways-from-chinas-new-five-year-plan/). That includes:

> large scale commercial application of AI, infrastructure projects on hyperscale intelligent computing clusters, and improved AI governance. Targeted future industries include quantum technology, embodied AI, brain–computer interfaces and 6G.

The Chinese government is taking various steps to maintain political control over AI. [According to a Wall Street Journal account](https://www.wsj.com/tech/ai/china-is-worried-ai-threatens-party-ruleand-is-trying-to-tame-it-bfdcda2d) the state has removed some content and works closely with AI companies. Additionally, “\[a\]ll AI-generated texts, videos and images must be explicitly labeled and traceable, making it easier to track and punish anyone spreading undesirable content.” Beijing also [promulgated](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c8dydlmenvro) new [rules](https://www.cac.gov.cn/2025-12/27/c_1768571207311996.htm) for controlling AI content for children.

**Vietnam** The first southeast Asian nation to regulate AI [issued rules](https://www.france24.com/en/live-news/20260228-vietnam-ai-law-takes-effect-first-in-southeast-asia) which took effect March 1. That state now mandates that AI businesses “clearly label AI-generated content such as deepfakes that cannot readily be differentiated from reality” as well as telling users “when they are interacting with an artificial rather than human agent.”

**South Korea** Seoul [issued](https://www.dickinson.edu/site/custom_scripts/dc_faculty_profile_index.php?fac=webbe) the AI Basic Act which assigns companies the duty to give the government “a mandatory prior notice requirement for ‘high-impact AI’ affecting fundamental human rights.” South Korea is positioning this law in reaction to European Union delays in implementing their AI Act.

**Japan** A new political party, [Team Mirai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Team_Mirai) (チームみらい;; “Team Future”), won national legislature seats and is [advocating](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/02/22/world/asia/japan-ai-politics-election.html) AI to support governmental operations. [One project](https://www.itmedia.co.jp/news/articles/2510/16/news097.html) uses AI to make policy development more accessible. One key strategy: Mirai argues that “savings achieved through A.I. could be used to lower contributions to pension and health care plans for working-class families.”

**United Arab Emirates** The UAE announced it [fended off](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-22/uae-says-it-foiled-a-wave-of-cyberattacks-on-vital-sectors) an AI-driven cyberattack.

**European Union** The European Parliament [worked on revising](https://www.trendingtopics.eu/eu-parliament-votes-to-delay-ai-act-deadlines-and-ban-nudifier-apps/) its AI Act. Proposals advanced technical policy implementation dates to late 2027 and 2028, while adding a ban for nudifier applications.

**Canada** The University of Toronto’s Munk School [published a white paper](https://aicompetitiveness.ca/assets/Sovereign-by-Design-Full-Report-2026.pdf) calling on Canada to take strategic steps to secure its AI sovereignty: “the question is how to structure dependencies to preserve choice, reduce foreign leverage, and ensure that Canadian data and infrastructure remain governed by Canadian laws and values.”

The ombudsperson for British Columbia [warned](https://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/british-columbia/bc-ombudsperson-crisis-driven-complaints-9.6983861) that some residents could lose access to government services if the province relied on AI to provide them.

## 3: The United States on AI

The CFO of America-based OpenAI, Sarah Friar, [floated](https://www.marketwatch.com/story/how-will-openai-cover-its-heavy-spending-it-now-suggests-maybe-the-government-could-help-e1ca64f5) the idea of governmental backup for their venture, then walked back the concept. CEO Sam Altman stated he didn’t want federal support [on X](https://x.com/sama/status/1986514377470845007):

President Trump [announced](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/11/launching-the-genesis-mission/) the [Genesis Mission](https://genesis.energy.gov/), an effort whereby the Department of Energy leads scientific innovations anchored on federal resources and AI. DoE will build out supporting infrastructure while [collaborating](https://www.energy.gov/articles/energy-department-announces-collaboration-agreements-24-organizations-advance-genesis) with businesses and laboratories.

Trump also published a [“National AI Legislative Framework”](https://www.whitehouse.gov/articles/2026/03/president-donald-j-trump-unveils-national-ai-legislative-framework/) which is a template for a potential Congressional bill. It includes points on making AI companies pay for data center electrical power, some kind of compromise between creative work owners and AI training needs, and blocking AI from censoring political speech. There are also calls for Congress to reduce barriers to innovation (not clear what that means) and to expand workforce training. One key detail is that such legislation would supersede state laws, which is [irking](https://www.wsj.com/politics/policy/ai-state-federal-data-center-energy-9753e3ce) state utility regulators.

The United States military has been [struggling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthropic%E2%80%93United_States_Department_of_Defense_dispute) with the AI firm Anthropic. The Department of ~~Defense~~ War [deemed](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/03/17/technology/anthropic-pentagon-national-security-risk.html) the technology a national security risk and declared Anthropic a supply chain risk, which would cripple the company’s business. The company then [sued](https://www.anthropic.com/news/where-stand-department-war) the administration. As Matteo Wong [observes](https://www.theatlantic.com/technology/2026/03/pentagon-anthropic-dispute/686307/), at stake are fundamental questions about government-AI relations. Also in play are public attitude towards AI. One sign of this is that Claude downloads from Apple’s app store [surged](https://www.axios.com/2026/03/01/anthropic-claude-chatgpt-app-downloads-pentagon) to the leading position, passing ChatGPT, after the break became public, so much so that the service [went down](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-02/anthropic-s-claude-chatbot-goes-down-for-thousands-of-users) for a time.

Another branch of the federal government, the Department of Homeland Security, [ordered](https://www.forbes.com/sites/thomasbrewster/2025/10/20/openai-ordered-to-unmask-writer-of-prompts/) OpenAI to produce some user prompts for security purposes.

America might decide on an unusual way to supply electrical power for AI-supporting data centers. A Texas company, [HGP Intelligent Energy](https://hgpenergy.com/), has [pitched](https://www.forbes.com/sites/petersuciu/2025/12/29/nimitz-class-supercarrier-nuclear-reactors-could-power-ai-data-centers/) the government on using nuclear reactors from decommissioned submarines and aircraft carriers as power sources.

Another American enterprise, [Americans for Prosperity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Americans_for_Prosperity), is urging the Trump administration to expand its support for AI development. In particular the group wants data centers powered by fossil fuels, an unsurprising argument given its Koch brothers funding. In contrast, there is rising opposition to AI aimed at the data center build out which Democrats [might be able to channel](https://www.politico.com/news/magazine/2025/12/28/ai-job-losses-populism-democrats-bernie-sanders-00706680). On the left Bernie Sanders has been [calling](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2026/feb/21/ai-revolution-bernie-sanders-warning) for [stronger](https://youtu.be/dthbi4lzO58?si=wYrwd9WUNpA1uQ5Z) AI regulation, even [talking with Claude about it](https://youtu.be/h3AtWdeu_G0?si=6OJWEvyU9kVXrzMJ):

![](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=h3AtWdeu_G0)

Democratic senator Amy Klobuchar also [called](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/12/09/opinion/klobuchar-trump-ai.html) for strong federal AI regulations.

There are also some Republican anti-AI folks, as noted above, like senator [Josh Hawley](https://www.axios.com/2025/10/14/hawley-draft-ai-chatbot-bill) and outgoing Florida governor [Ron DeSantis](https://www.politico.com/news/2025/12/27/we-have-to-reject-that-with-every-fiber-of-our-being-desantis-emerges-as-a-chief-ai-skeptic-00704333). (Interesting to see Newsmax offer a fairly uncritical [account](https://www.newsmax.com/newsfront/desantis-ai-florida/2025/12/28/id/1239916/).)

At the Congressional level AI companies are [raising](https://www.wsj.com/tech/ai/tech-titans-amass-multimillion-dollar-war-chests-to-fight-ai-regulation-88c600e1) hundreds of millions of dollars for lobbying against regulations they dislike. I was struck by this analysis and strategy:

> OpenAI co-founder, Greg Brockman… and his wife, Anna Brockman…said they have observed "two extremist camps" in the AI regulatory debate and that "neither feels right." "We believe in AI centrism, which supports thoughtful regulation toward the goal of unlocking AI's potential to improve quality of life for every person (and every animal)," they said. The couple said they want to see federal AI regulation that promotes innovation, defends systems from misuse and protects "the privacy of conversations with AI." Beyond that, they said, "Most developers and open-source models, and almost all deployments of today's technology, should have minimal additional regulatory burden."

Meanwhile, a bunch of Senators and Representatives [fired off AI legislation](https://www.aasa.org/resources/blog/wide-array-of-ai-legislation-introduced) in late 2025. Three focused on education, including protecting children from AI abuse.

(They continue the modern Congressional habit of naming laws with cute acronyms: Learning Innovation and Empowerment (LIFE), Recommending Artificial Intelligence and Standards in Education (RAISE), Guidelines for User Age-Verification and Responsible Dialogue (GUARD))

More action occurs at the state level. California is [investigating](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/01/14/california-to-investigate-elon-musks-grok-over-sexualized-images-00728508) Grok.ai for generating sexualized images of children. Amazon [sued](https://edocs.puc.state.or.us/efdocs/HAA/um2410haa341355036.pdf) an Oregon utility for not providing the electrical power they asked for. The Rockefeller Foundation [launched](https://www.rockefellerfoundation.org/news/rockefeller-foundation-and-center-for-civic-futures-launch-ai-readiness-project-to-build-state-capacity-for-responsible-ai/) an effort to prepare state and local governmental capacity for dealing with AI.

## 4: Copyright

Struggles over AI and copyright continued.

A group of writers [proposed](https://techcrunch.com/2025/12/17/adobe-hit-with-proposed-class-action-accused-of-misusing-authors-work-in-ai-training/) a class action lawsuit against Adobe for training its SlimLM tool on their work. The New York Times [sued](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/12/05/technology/new-york-times-perplexity-ai-lawsuit.html) Perplexity for copyright violations:

> The suit accuses Perplexity of violating The Times’s copyrights in several ways, most notably when the start-up’s search engine retrieves information from a website or database and uses that information to generate a piece of text and to respond to queries from internet users. That would not be a fair use of that material, the suit claimed, because Perplexity grabbed large chunks of the publication’s content — in some cases, entire articles — and provided information that directly competed with what The Times offered its readers.

An artist who uses AI to create images [sued](https://www.deseret.com/entertainment/2024/10/08/ai-generated-art-copyright-rejection/) the federal Copyright Office to win copyright protection for his works. The office has previously refused to do so.

Meta and a group of news companies [agreed](https://www.axios.com/2025/12/05/meta-ai-deals-news-publishers) to allow Meta’s AI to use some of their content.

The British government previously proposed a change to copyright law allowing an exception for AI training, but is now [considering](https://www.thebookseller.com/news/government-scraps-copyright-exception-as-preferred-option-for-ai-training-in-win-for-creative-industries) scrapping it. That country’s Competition and Markets Authority [argued](https://apnews.com/article/google-uk-britain-tech-online-regulation-f2bf8545f3b987aa1900a829c0d01390) that Google should let content creators opt out of having their materials scraped for AI training purposes. In response, Google [offered](https://blog.google/products-and-platforms/products/search/search-ai-features-controls/) more user controls.

Also in the intellectual property world, a British software firm, The Foundry Visionmongers, [sued](https://www.reuters.com/legal/litigation/adobe-sued-trademark-infringement-over-foundry-ai-tool-2026-03-12/) Adobe for trademark infringe in its Firefly Foundry application.

## Some reflections from a futurist angle

What can we learn from these political developments, looking ahead?

The Iran war is starting to impact and demonstrate AI capabilities. It’s still unclear at the moment ([here’s a post on how I track the war](https://bryanalexander.org/digital-literacy/how-i-follow-the-iran-war/)), but it seems that at least some combatants are using the technology in various ways. The Straits of Hormuz closure may hit chip making as it cuts the flow of oil and also helium to Asian makers. As the war continues we may see intersections between its politics and those around AI: some supporting Israel and the US *and* AI, while others oppose all three, for example. To the extent the conflict involves cyberwar we should anticipate an AI role on that front.

AI sovereignty may develop as an organizing principle for various polities. It’s an institutionally conservative term, one which frames AI within classic political goals. Again, geopolitics may drive this on to the extent that nations want to evade the American AI tech stack, or the Chinese.

There is also continuity on AI and politics. I find myself repeating some points from previous newsletters. Politicians and governments are increasingly using AI as tools of statecraft and electioneering, although it’s not clear deepfakes have had a decisive impact on any election so far. The US-China AI competition continues; I hope we get some good analysis of the new Five Year Plan. Depending on how the Iran war turns out, we might see US-China relations impacted, which should impact AI.

In American politics we’re seeing traditional political structures in play over AI. We have the federalism struggle over which level of government should regulate the technology. The Trump administration continues to embrace AI, from the Tech Corps to Genesis. And a red versus blue framing seems to be settling in, although with some noteworthy Republican defections. Indeed, as I’ve been noting for a while, the culture wars are embracing AI. That partially explains the gender divide RAND found.

The Pentagon’s break with Anthropic cast Claude as a polarized actor, especially as many users raced to download it. Perhaps conservatives will make a point of shunning Claude in favor of ChatGPT and others. Maybe Democrats will in turn pick up Claude, or at least the ones who don’t reject generative AI overall. There’s a broader question at stake concerning government’s role in regulating and using this new technology, and the autonomy of AI enterprises.

Data centers are increasingly controversial and political and they are settling into preexisting politics as well with utility politics and federalism. I’ve seen local signs of that in Virginia, where there’s a massive data center build out. Our local state legislator has said that data center complaints are by far the issue they hear the most about.

I am curious to see if the Brockmans’ “AI centrism” term has legs. Given American political polarization, the center is a tricky place to define, much less hold.

Copyright dynamics continue to unfold. There are openings for regulation in many nations plus opportunities for lawsuits.

Overall, the AI and politics/policy world is one of continued expansion, development, divides, and churn.

…and I’ll stop there. This post is already longer than I planned. Now over to you all. What are you seeing on these themes?
