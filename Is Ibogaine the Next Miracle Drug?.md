American addicts and traumatized veterans are flocking to Mexico to ingest a powerful hallucinogen. But the president just signed an executive order that might make it legal to do so on American soil.

There’s a whole host of scientists and lobbyists who believe in ibogaine’s potential—not only for veterans struggling with PTSD, but also for opioid addicts and others. (Illustration by The Free Press, image via Getty)

Chase Rowan used to jump out of planes for a living. At the age of 21, he became an Army Ranger, trained to parachute behind enemy lines to capture or kill insurgent fighters. He went on his first combat tour, to northern Iraq, in 2005.

“There’s a lot of times you think you’re not going to come back,” Rowan told me. “But you do it for your buddy sitting next to you. And because you don’t want to be that little bitch.”

He completed over 150 combat missions in hostile territory without a hitch. But two months after he got back from Iraq, while he was working at Fort Benning Army base in Georgia, an airborne training exercise went horribly wrong.

It was the middle of the night. The plane was 800 feet up. Rowan jumped, as he had so many times before—and his parachute didn’t fully deploy. His body was going 60 miles an hour when it hit the runway.

Miraculously, Rowan survived. But he had broken several bones and sustained a traumatic brain injury. In a Veterans Affairs hospital, a doctor gave Rowan a Percocet pill for the first time, and so began a lifetime of addiction.

---

For the first year after his injury, Rowan struggled with chronic headaches and struggled to keep his balance while standing or walking. But even after the symptoms subsided, Rowan continued to ingest any opioid he could get his hands on: oxycodone, heroin, and fentanyl. In the past decade and a half, he’s crashed two cars while high and cycled in and out of rehab. In 2010, in the middle of his first child’s birth, he snuck out of the maternity ward to go get a fix. When his wife finally left him with their two kids in early 2023, Rowan admitted, “It was long overdue.”

A few months later, Rowan said, he was ready to end his life.

In April 2023, Rowan told me, he was sitting on his living room couch, beside a small stack of suicide notes, watching Eminem music videos. He was planning to hang himself from a pull-up bar the next day, when something caught his eye in the suggested videos tab: a clip from *The Joe Rogan Experience*. Rogan was interviewing U.S. Marine Dakota Meyer—Medal of Honor winner, savior of 36 lives in Afghanistan—about an experimental post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) treatment that, [Meyer said](https://youtu.be/d-FVySWRRy0?si=ZMBgY-zGqobP5f0I&t=2148), made him feel like “my soul had gone through a workout, just an ass-kicking workout,” and cured him of weekly anxiety attacks. It was then that Rowan heard a word he had never heard before: *ibogaine*.

“It gave me my life back,” Meyer said to Rogan in the interview.

When Rowan heard those words, he froze. He paused the interview. Then he played it over again. And again.

“It was God, really, telling me, ‘Chase, this is it. This is for you. This is what you need,’ ” Rowan said.

Four months later, he was on his way to Mexico to receive ibogaine treatment.

Ibogaine is derived from the [root of iboga](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/neuroscience/iboga), a plant indigenous to Africa historically used in tribal healing ceremonies. When ingested, the substance causes intense, vivid hallucinations that can last for hours, with many users [reporting feelings](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30967101/) of peace and spiritual renewal in the aftermath of taking it. The experience is not without risk—ibogaine can [cause cardiac arrest](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4837967/) if administered improperly—and in the U.S., it’s been illegal since the Controlled Substances Act declared it a Schedule I drug in 1970.

But that could be about to change. On April 18, [in the Oval Office](https://www.youtube.com/live/8xrI-_qo64E), President Donald Trump signed an [executive order](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2026/04/accelerating-medical-treatments-for-serious-mental-illness/) instructing the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) to fast-track a review of ibogaine, open a pathway for seriously ill patients to legally access it, and allocate $50 million of federal funding for psychedelic research.

Joe Rogan was there too, standing behind the president as he signed—he had texted Trump earlier this month about ibogaine’s potential to treat opioid addiction, he said, and received a reply that not even *Saturday Night Live* could spoof: “Sounds great. Do you want FDA approval? Let’s do it.”

Joe Rogan was standing behind the president as he signed the executive order. (Jim WATSON / AFP via Getty Images)

But it’s not only Rogan who has got America to this point. There’s a whole host of scientists and lobbyists who believe in ibogaine’s potential—not only for veterans struggling with PTSD, but also for opioid addicts and those with treatment-resistant depression. It also has he support of Health and Human Services secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr., who has long championed psychedelic research while [expressing skepticism](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fFpk0HAIVYA&t=5655s) of more established pharmaceutical treatments for mental health issues, including selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) and benzodiazepines.

We don’t know exactly how—or even definitively if—ibogaine works. But we live in an era when a new drug can go from unknown to household name in a few years because of just how effective it seems to be: Think of Ozempic. In speaking to nearly a dozen people who have taken it—as well as advocates for and against legalizing it—the power of first-person testimonies, on podcasts and social media, is undeniable. The opioid addict who almost died of cardiac arrest during a psychedelic trip but woke up with zero desire to use. The traumatized Marine who despaired of ever getting better, until one night changed his life. The daring neuropsychiatrist who bet his academic reputation on the proposition that a little-known psychedelic root could heal the most broken minds. The story of ibogaine is the story of human beings fighting against the very worst kinds of internal suffering, praying for salvation—and finding it.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!SymI!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Faf9c2297-30dd-4189-bc29-828a4bd4482f_1320x30.png)

If you meet anyone in America who’s tried ibogaine treatment, they probably went to Mexico—where the psychedelic is legal, and unregulated.

Rowan was one of those people. After he saw the Rogan episode on ibogaine, he said, he “became obsessed.” Every day for the next four months, he listened to podcasts and read whatever literature he could find on the substance. A friend pointed him toward The Mission Within, a clinic in Tijuana. The cost of treatment came to just under $7,000, he said, but he discovered he could get a grant from a nonprofit that funds brain injury treatment for veterans. He started saving up to cover the travel costs and in the meantime went cold turkey on everything he was addicted to, because he’d read that taking ibogaine while still in active withdrawal [can increase the risk](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/17425255.2021.1944099) of cardiac arrest during treatment. “I ended up being sober for four and a half months, white-knuckling it,” he told me.

Rowan told me he went to The Mission Within in August 2023, staying for four days that changed his life.

On the night of his arrival, he said, he lay on a comfortable bed in a softly lit room as clinicians hooked him up to multiple medical machines, including a heart monitor. There was a bucket in case of nausea, an occasional side effect of ibogaine therapy. He took the ibogaine in pill form, and fell asleep soon after. The ensuing trip lasted four or five hours, he said, and included the feeling of leaving his body and seeing “a movie of me from above, watching 10 years of my addiction, hurting my wife and kids.”

“That was the first time I understood what my addiction was doing,” he said. “I started crying. I threw up in the bucket.”

The next day, Rowan said, he felt hopeful but physically exhausted—what patients at the clinic call “the gray day.” On the third day, he took [5-MeO-DMT](https://jheor.org/post/2542-from-toad-toxin-to-medicine-the-promise-of-5-meo-dmt), a psychedelic derived from toad venom, which he says showed him a brief vision of heaven and a God who still had plans for him. The following morning, he headed back to the U.S.—and was arrested at the border on an old warrant for an unpaid speeding ticket.

After he got home, Rowan said, his old compulsions were gone; he’s been sober ever since, he said, and he’s back in touch with his wife and children. This year, he plans to move back in with them. When he thinks of drugs now, Rowan told me, “All I think of is poison. All I see is my family’s pain.”

Nearly everyone I spoke to about ibogaine went out of their way to assure me it is not a magic bullet, and that building good habits after treatment is just as important as the treatment itself, if not more so. Some started meditating and journaling; others forged deeper connections to faith and family. “I have two new habits,” said Rowan. “I pray to God, and I do push-ups every day.”

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!SymI!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Faf9c2297-30dd-4189-bc29-828a4bd4482f_1320x30.png)

Put simply, some scientists believe ibogaine acts as a reset button for the brain, allowing patients to break bad habits and negative thought patterns. Unlike most medications for addiction, which target a single receptor, ibogaine [appears to flood](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4382526/) multiple systems at once, blocking the brain’s opioid and nicotine receptors while also [raising serotonin levels](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0006899398005277) in a way similar to SSRIs.

But until two years ago, most of the available clinical data on ibogaine came from government-funded research from the early 1990s, when a federal pathway to medicalization seemed possible. The FDA even [approved a clinical trial](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4888945/) for ibogaine in 1993, though it never came to fruition: After a [young woman’s death](https://akjournals.com/view/journals/2054/1/1/article-p20.xml#:~:text=One%20fatality%20was%20reported:%20a%2024-year-old%20female,1993%2C%20died%2019%20hr%20after%20ibogaine%20administration.) in an ibogaine study in the Netherlands that same year and a [spate of disputes](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1752928X13001959) over the drug’s patent, the trial was discontinued before it could start.

Since then, much of the most innovative work on ibogaine treatment has been done in private clinics outside the U.S., by companies like Ambio Life Sciences—which is based in Tijuana, runs seven specialized clinics, and says it is the world’s leading ibogaine provider by patient volume. A spokesperson told me business is booming: Ambio has treated more than 3,500 patients, including 1,500 veterans and first responders, since opening its first location in 2021, and currently treats over 160 patients a month across all locations, up from around 20 per month just five years ago. (Ninety-two per cent of patients, the spokesperson said, are American.) It’s not cheap: Treatment starts at $7,350 and can reach up to $30,000. Nevertheless, the spokesperson told me, “demand has risen significantly over the last year.” (And it’s not just veterans with PTSD or civilians dealing with serious addiction: This year, Ambio has also seen a “massive influx” of applications from people suffering from Parkinson’s disease and multiple sclerosis, Jonathan Dickinson, its co-founder and CEO, told me.)

The boom is, in all probability, because Ambio’s Tijuana clinic was recently the setting of a study that got a lot of media attention. In January 2024, a team of scientists led by Stanford University neuroscientist Nolan Williams published the results of an [observational study](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-023-02705-w) in *Nature Medicine*, which focused on 30 special operations veterans, most of whom were experiencing severe psychiatric symptoms stemming from traumatic brain injuries. All of them had independently booked themselves in for ibogaine treatment at the Tijuana clinic, where Williams and his colleagues assessed each participant, before and after treatment, on measures of PTSD, anxiety, depression, and cognitive function. Their findings were stunning: After treatment with ibogaine and magnesium, white matter in the brain—the connective neural tissue enabling functions like focus, learning, and problem-solving— [thickened after a month](https://www.cell.com/iscience/fulltext/S2589-0042\(26\)00496-7), while areas responsible for emotional regulation grew larger.

Marcus Capone is a veteran who struggled with a brain injury until he tried ibogaine treatment. (Daphne Kanas for The Free Press )

The Stanford study made Williams the face of ibogaine within the scientific community—and eventually, in the mainstream media. Last April, he gave a [TED Talk](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2pPPJbnZOtQ) about ibogaine, in which he called it “one of the most amazing drugs on the planet.” He was quoted in prominent features in, among other outlets, *[The](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/03/05/health/ibogaine-psychedelic-opioid-addiction.html)* *[New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/03/05/health/ibogaine-psychedelic-opioid-addiction.html)*. Then, in October 2025, Williams died by suicide. In [a eulogy](https://med.stanford.edu/news/all-news/2025/12/nolan-williams-obituary.html) published by Stanford, a colleague said: “We all wonder what he would have invented next year or 10 years from now and what a difference these contributions would have made.”

While reporting this article, I reached out multiple times to the [Stanford Brain Stimulation Lab](https://bsl.stanford.edu/), and directly to the scientists who worked there with Williams, all of whom referred me to other colleagues or declined to be interviewed. But I was able to speak to Dr. Michael Ostacher, who works in addiction medicine research at Stanford and was a colleague and mentor of Williams.

Ostacher told me he thinks Williams was an “amazing psychiatrist and clinician” who “was driven by a real wish to cure people with psychiatric illness.” But he also expressed skepticism about the study that arguably mainstreamed ibogaine. In exchange for participation, Ostacher pointed out, the patients received three fully funded trips to Palo Alto, California, to get brain scans, as well as a trip to Mexico. At the Ambio clinic, subjects also had access to sweat lodges, Reiki, and other holistic therapies in addition to the ibogaine treatment itself. It’s hard to disentangle the effects of the drug from the effects of what was essentially a wellness retreat. The study’s sample size—30 men, all of whom were veterans—was tiny and homogenous. And there was no control group: all 30 veterans received ibogaine, and none were given a placebo.

“People who take ibogaine or many of these psychedelic drugs in adequate doses have clearly profound experiences,” said Ostacher. “The question is whether or not it’s those profound experiences that improve the psychiatric condition, or the effect of participating in something that you have some belief in before you do it.” In other words, if you go into a treatment thinking, *This drug is going to heal me*, it’s more likely to.

All the veterans who took part in the study had their travel and accommodation costs covered by a nonprofit named Veterans Exploring Treatment Solutions (VETS), which was set up by ex-Navy SEAL Marcus Capone and his wife, Amber. Marcus left the military in 2013 after struggling with a brain injury likely triggered by years of repeated blast exposure; his civilian life was governed by mood swings and depression, and he had a near-permanent “thousand-yard stare,” according to Amber. By 2017, he was thinking about suicide—until Amber heard, through a SEAL friend, about a clinic in Mexico offering ibogaine treatment, and convinced her skeptical husband to go. Within days, he felt like a new person; he stopped drinking, and he said he stopped relying on his prescriptions. The couple were so grateful for the treatment that in 2019 they set up VETS, which provides grant funding for around 300 veterans a year to seek ibogaine treatment. In 2025 alone, they said, it received 2,151 applications, approving only 13 percent.

The couple have also been vocal advocates for researching the clinical potential of psychedelics—and on Saturday, Amber and Marcus were [in the room](https://www.facebook.com/VeteransExploringTreatmentSolutions/videos/a-historic-moment-witnessed-firsthandour-co-founders-marcus-capone-and-amber-cap/1654140265833590/) when Trump signed the executive order announcing just that. “It was a really surreal moment,” Marcus told me, adding that it felt like a “a huge milestone” in the quest to make sure people “have access to modern, innovative treatments.”

While ibogaine’s successes are increasingly well documented, some of its most catastrophic failures remain shrouded in mystery. In 2022, a 49-year-old man [died shortly after treatment](https://www.rollingstone.com/culture/culture-features/ibogaine-pychedelic-benefits-dangers-death-1235079364/) at Beond, a celebrity-endorsed clinic in Cancún, Mexico. The man’s ex-wife told *Rolling Stone* he had been taking large amounts of fentanyl up until the day he arrived at the clinic—something that, according to experts, significantly raises the risk of cardiac arrest during ibogaine treatment—but an autopsy was never conducted to establish a definite cause of death.

To date, there have been 33 recorded ibogaine-related deaths, with one confirmed fatality taking place on American soil. Andy Haman, 54, was an actor and competitive weightlifter who [had a seizure and died](https://www.cbsnews.com/colorado/news/ibogaine-ameen-alai-death-andy-haman-psychedelic-treatment/) after ingesting ibogaine in a friend’s basement in Broomfield, Colorado, in 2021. A coroner’s report marked ibogaine overdose as the cause of death, but it also noted that Haman had an enlarged heart and restricted arterial blood flow and may have been suffering from sepsis at the time of treatment.

Juliana Mulligan narrowly escaped death during ibogaine treatment. She suffered a series of six cardiac arrests in 2011 at a now-defunct Guatemalan ibogaine center, where a clinic worker gave her a double dose by accident. Mulligan was at the clinic to treat her opioid addiction, she said, and she does not recall the cardiac events—only waking up from her treatment feeling like a weight had been lifted off of her.

“All of the guilt and shame that drove me to use, all of the heaviness of my addiction, was gone,” she said. “I remember thinking immediately: *This is the future of substance-use disorder treatment*.”

A doctor told her what had happened to her body while her mind had been tripping—and for the rest of her stay, Mulligan told me, the clinic staff referred to her as “ *seis milagros*,” or six miracles. But the experience didn’t make Mulligan an ibogaine critic. Quite the contrary. She was so struck by the effectiveness of the treatment, she said, that she thought, “ *I need to figure out how to make this safer.*”

---

“It was like, ‘Okay the cardiac stuff happened, and that was completely avoidable. I just went to an unsafe place,’ ” Mulligan said.

She has since worked at multiple ibogaine clinics abroad, and now operates her own business, Inner Vision Ibogaine, in Brooklyn, New York, which offers counseling for those considering ibogaine treatment, coaching for patients recovering from treatment, and consulting for ibogaine clinic workers and proprietors. Mulligan does not offer ibogaine treatment herself, though she told me she is “cautiously optimistic” that it will soon be legally available on U.S. soil.

“There’s a window of opportunity here to build something that is safe and ethical, and I want to do my best to make that happen,” Mulligan said. “I hope to be involved in overseeing the training and setting the standards and writing the protocols.”

Another American who’d like to help bring legal ibogaine to the U.S. is Bryan Hubbard. Last year, he co-founded the nonprofit Americans for Ibogaine, in order, he told me, “to demand the medicalization of ibogaine within the United States for anyone who would choose to receive that therapy within a time horizon of six years.” (He told me big donors to the group include the venture capitalist Steve Jurvetson and his wife, Genevieve, and former BigCommerce CEO Brent Bellm.)

Hubbard discovered ibogaine after he lost family members to the opioid epidemic, and left his career as a lawyer to lobby the Kentucky state government to fund research into psychedelic treatments for addiction and PTSD. He has since become the driving force behind what he calls a “dual-track” approach to the medicalization of ibogaine: on the one hand, pushing the FDA and Drug Enforcement Administration for federal approval, and on the other, organizing states to fund and permit clinical research.

Last year, he worked with former Texas governor and Americans for Ibogaine co-founder Rick Perry, who has received ibogaine treatment, to lobby the state legislature to pass [SB 2308](https://capitol.texas.gov/BillLookup/History.aspx?LegSess=89R&Bill=SB2308), which allotted [$50 million in matching funds](https://spectrumlocalnews.com/tx/south-texas-el-paso/news/2026/03/18/rick-perry-pushes-for-legalizing-ibogaine-to-aid-veterans) dedicated to ibogaine research. Since then, appropriations from the bill have funded [a proposed clinical trial](https://www.texastribune.org/2026/03/31/texas-ibogaine-research-clinical-trials-psychedelics/) from a consortium of Texas universities that aims to build upon the Stanford study’s findings—though the trial’s path to fruition remains uncertain after the state failed to find a private drug company partner, as required by the legislation.

Since early 2025, lawmakers in 19 states [have introduced or passed legislation](https://www.americansforibogaine.org/legislation/) to fund research into clinical applications of ibogaine. “The objective of Americans for Ibogaine is to bring six to 12 additional states into partnership with Texas to create the unstoppable external force that will crash through an ossified federal wall to bring this into reality,” Hubbard told me in November.

This past weekend, those federal walls fell faster than anyone anticipated. Trump’s executive order established a pathway that FDA commissioner Marty Makary said could compress drug approval timelines from over a year to a matter of weeks. The FDA also announced it had issued its first-ever clearance for an ibogaine clinical study to be conducted on American soil.

Among those beaming and nodding along as the president prepared to sign was a tall, bearded man I recognized as Hubbard. I called him this week to get his reaction. “I’m on cloud nine,” he said.
