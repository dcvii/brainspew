On Valentine’s Day last year, I received an intriguing email from Jake Silverstein, Editor-in-Chief of the *New York Times Magazine.*

He wrote:

> The *New York Times Magazine* is working on a project to define the 25 Greatest Living American Songwriters, and we’re seeking your input. The list will form the backbone of a special issue of the magazine to be published later this year….We are inviting you to submit your nominations.

I sat down and made a list of worthy candidates. The first half dozen names came easily enough, but then it got harder. After I reached number nine, I wanted to stop. But I eventually added six more names before submitting my list.

The *New York Times* asked if it could publish my ballot. I refused—I decided that I would share it instead with subscribers to *The Honest Broker*. I also turned down an invitation to write about the songwriters I picked. Here, too, I decided that anything i had to say about these artists would take place on Substack.

And then…

And then nothing happened.

My deadline for submitting names was February 28, 2025—more than a year ago. As it turned out, one of the songwriters I picked died a few months later. My list was already outdated.

When we reached the end of 2025, I assumed that the NY Times had abandoned this project. So imagine my surprise when [they finally published the results yesterday](https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2026/magazine/greatest-american-songwriters-alive.html) —more than 14 months after soliciting my ballot. The only thing that had changed in the interim was the *Times’* decision to expand the final list to 30 names.

Of the 30 songwriters on the *New York Times* list, only five were on my ballot. But some of my picks were considered ineligible based on dubious criteria (see below).

You can find the results of the Times survey at this link.

Let’s jump into my picks, and you can offer opinions in the comments.

For a start, the top four names seemed obvious to me. Here they are.

#### Joni Mitchell

#### Stevie Wonder

#### Brian Wilson

#### Bob Dylan

I grew up in the same neighborhood as Brian Wilson, and [his songs are so closely intertwined with my early life,](https://www.honest-broker.com/p/brian-wilson-is-my-brick) that I can’t even pretend to objectivity here. But I’m certain that I would have voted for him even without this personal connection.

As it turned out, none of that mattered. Wilson died on June 11, 2025, almost four months after I submitted my ballot. So he was excluded from the survey results published yesterday.

Joni Mitchell was also excluded, but for a less plausible reason. The *Times* declared that Mitchell is not an American songwriter—they have assigned her to Canada. This strikes me as wrongheaded for multiple reasons. For a start, the term *American* can be accurately applied to the Americas, and the last time I looked at a map, Canada was still located in North America.

But I also believe that our identity can be defined by our choices, and not just our DNA. When artists move to a new locale and reside there for many years (or even the rest of their lives), that becomes part of their identity too. But, frankly, there are a lot of things in current identity politics that I find rigid and arbitrary, and this is just one of them.

In any event, Mitchell got ditched from the *Times* ranking. That’s their loss—I’m keeping her on mine.

Stevie Wonder was also an obvious name for my list, and might be the artist whose work most resonates with me as a jazz musician. Over the years, I’ve performed dozens of his songs, and I admire their construction. In all honesty, I don’t think he matches Joni Mitchell or Bob Dylan as a lyricist, but that’s a tiny quibble—the aptly-named Wonder belongs on any Mount Rushmore of living American songwriters.

That leaves Bob Dylan. And here I must make an unusual admission. I’ve never been an obsessive Dylan fan. I’ve met Dylan fanatics, and I just don’t possess the same level of passion that animates them. I’ve rarely had much interest in performing his songs.

But even with that reservation, I can’t deny his greatness. In some ways, he is the flip side of Stevie Wonder—Dylan’s harmonic grasp and instrumental skills don’t match Wonder’s, but his lyrics are works of genius. I celebrated when he won the Nobel Prize in literature, and am confident that his music will survive and earn praise for generations to come. So he, too, belongs on my Mount Rushmore.

---

By the way, this is a good point to say that I would have added two more names to this Mount Rushmore list—raising it from 4 to 6 honorees, and bringing my total to 17. But these two artists died not long before the ballots went out. So I left them off my submission, but let me add their names here as a token of appreciation and admiration.

#### Burt Bacharach (with lyricist Hal David)—ineligible because deceased 2023.

#### Stephen Sondheim—ineligible because deceased 2021.

---

I could have ended my list at this point. But there were two other names that absolutely had to be on my ballot. They are also Rushmore worthy—or let’s call them *Rushmore adjacent.*

#### Paul Simon

#### Carole King

Both of them have achieved greatness in the highest degree. Here, too, I’ve studied and performed their music—so I know it (and love it) from the inside out.

I’m happy to report that they both showed up on the final *NY Times* list.

---

The next three names also felt obvious to me, but apparently the other voters disagreed. None of them made it into the final list of *Times* honorees.

#### Jimmy Webb

#### David Byrne

#### Randy Newman

The only reason to exclude them might be quantity (of hit songs), certainly not quality. Each of them—Webb, Byrne, Newman—has composed songs so brilliant that I am envious. If I taught a class on songwriting, we would study their work in class.

---

If you’re counting, you see that I now had nine names for my ballot. I thought I should at least submit ten songwriters. But at this point, I struggled to identify a tenth musician whose work would merit inclusion alongside Dylan, Wonder, etc.

With some reluctance, I added a tenth name.

#### Billy Joel

I’ve met people who mock Billy Joel. He is the first person on my list so far who is definitely *un-cool*. But I’ve never been able to understand the animosity. Joel is a very skilled songwriter, both for music and lyrics. I probably wouldn’t buy a pricey ticket for a Billy Joel concert, but I can’t deny his artistry.

Frankly, the fact that he is ridiculed by hipsters makes me want to include him all the more. But it didn’t do any good. He got left off the *Times* list.

---

So, there I had ten names. And I probably should have stopped there. But I decided to add five more—some of them eccentric, and unlikely to make the final cut. Here they are:

#### John Fogerty

#### Neil Young

#### Stephin Merritt

#### Herbie Hancock

#### Caroline Shaw

Some comments on these final picks.

- Neil Young was born in Canada, so he was (again) determined ineligible by the *Times* identity police*.*
- I rank Fogerty a couple steps below the Paul Simons and Carole Kings of this world, but he still has put together an extraordinary oeuvre.
- Herbie Hancock was unlikely to get many votes, because he is an instrumentalist (of his [vocoderized singing](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GHhD4PD75zY), the less said the better)—but I firmly believe that great songs don’t always require lyrics. So he was a worthy candidate. (I might have included Pat Metheny for the same reason.)
- Stephin Merritt was chosen more on the basis of my personal affinity for his songs, and I never expected him to make the final list. But he did!
- Caroline Shaw is the least well known artist on my list, and also the youngest. But I wanted to include a composer of classical songs, and I believe that she has already demonstrated greatness in her work.

I stopped at that point. I wasn’t happy about excluding Tom Waits—who would have been next on my list. But you’ve got to draw the line somewhere.

So who did I miss? Let me know in the comments.
