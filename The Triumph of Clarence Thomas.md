Congratulations to [Clarence Thomas](https://www.wsj.com/topics/person/clarence-thomas), who this week will become the second-longest-serving Supreme Court Justice in history. Longevity is its own reward, but more important is that the 77-year-old Justice has served long enough to see his originalist view of the Constitution remake American law.

This wasn’t what most legal experts expected when President George H.W. Bush elevated Judge Thomas from the D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals at age 43 in 1991. The left tried to destroy him personally at his confirmation hearing, but the judge memorably fought back and gained his seat.

When Justice Thomas joined the Court, replacing Thurgood Marshall, it still tilted left on the major questions of American law. Republican Presidents had tried to move the Court away from the results-before-law jurisprudence that dominated the 20th-century Court. But they were frustrated as their nominees adopted the views of the “living Constitution” left.

It got worse when Bill Clinton replaced Byron White with Ruth Bader Ginsburg. The Court’s conservatives, including Justice Thomas and Antonin Scalia, were often in frustrated dissent. But those dissents have often proved to be the foundation for the current Court’s majority opinions.

The latest example arrived last week in the voting-rights case, *Louisiana v. Callais*. In *Holder v. Hall* in 1994, an otherwise routine vote-dilution case, Justice Thomas took the opportunity to write a concurrence that dissected the Court’s *Thornburg v. Gingles* (1986) ruling that had paved the way for widespread racial gerrymanders.

Justice Thomas wrote that “few devices could be better designed to exacerbate racial tensions than the consciously segregated districting system currently being constructed in the name of the Voting Rights Act.”

Joined by Scalia, Justice Thomas added that “Our drive to segregate political districts by race can only serve to deepen racial divisions by destroying any need for voters or candidates to build bridges between racial groups or to form voting coalitions.”

Last week his constitutional ship came in. Justice Samuel Alito’s 6-3 majority opinion didn’t overturn *Gingles*, but it declared racial gerrymanders to be illegal in all but the rarest circumstances. Justice Thomas’s brief concurrence repeated his views in *Holder v. Hall*, which Justice Alito also cited.

Justice Thomas has put his stamp on other areas of the law, notably gun rights in *Bruen* in 2022 and executive overreach in regulating bump stocks in *Garland v. Cargill* (2024). His concurrence in *Students for Fair Admissions* (2023) is another vindication of his long-time view, often in dissent, that the Constitution is color-blind. He has also made his mark with other Justices in correcting the bias against religious free expression that was a wrong Court turn in the last century.

On Thursday Justice Thomas will pass Justice John Paul Stevens (1975-2010) in longevity. If he remains until May 20, 2028, he would pass Justice William O. Douglas (1939 to 1975). Presumably Justice Thomas has more years left to serve, and more opportunities to restore the Constitution’s proper understanding.
