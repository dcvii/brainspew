***Looming over every development, negotiation, and controversy in the ongoing war with Iran is a persistent question: Could this be the prelude to World War III?***

***It’s not an unreasonable concern. The closure of the Strait of Hormuz is squeezing the global economy. Iran’s nuclear ambitions remain at the center of high-stakes negotiations. All the while, China—reportedly aiding Iran in targeting U.S. military sites—watches closely, waiting for the right moment to launch an attack it has spent decades preparing for: an invasion of Taiwan.***

***This would mark the biggest crisis of this century—the moment when tensions between the United States and its foremost rival finally come to a head, and the fate of an island at the center of the world’s most critical high-tech supply chains hangs in the balance. And it is a scenario for which, according to the author of today’s Big Read, we have no plan.***

***Eyck Freymann is the author of a new book, [Defending Taiwan](https://bookshop.org/a/93116/9780197823842), and he offers a sweeping examination of this looming conflict, and what the United States must do to prevent it. Today’s essay, adapted from the book, drops us into the opening hours of a Taiwan crisis—and explains why our failure to learn from the Iran war may already be pushing us closer to global economic disaster than we realize. —The Editors***

The strike comes without warning. A night watchman streaming television dramas on his smartphone tries to refresh the page. On the seafloor beneath the Taiwan Strait, underwater drones and frogmen are severing the dozens of undersea cables connecting Taiwan to the outside world. A university student scrolling social media assumes there is something wrong with the dormitory Wi-Fi. The bandwidth keeps slowing and slowing, until nothing will load at all. Then cyberattacks start to cripple the power plants that service downtown Taipei. Neon signs and streetlights go dark. Taipei’s broad boulevards are now illuminated only by headlights of passing cars and the moonlight.

Five minutes later, the explosions begin. A salvo of missiles screeches across the night sky and strikes the residences of Taiwan’s leaders: the president and vice president, the premier and vice premier, the heads of the armed forces and intelligence services. In a blink, Taiwan’s democratically elected government has been decapitated.

Hundreds more missiles rain down on naval bases, air defense batteries, the tarmac at the Ching Chuan Kang Air Base, command posts and radar beacons, telecom infrastructure. Special forces and sleeper agents from the mainland begin hanging red posters on streetlamps declaring that Taiwan’s defeat is certain, and resistance futile. Hundreds of coast guard vessels and China-flagged merchant ships change course toward the median line. Dozens of helicopters take off from bases in China, heading toward Taiwan.

The president of the United States, visiting a school in Atlanta, is 8,000 miles away when the news breaks. Secret Service agents rush the president into a command vehicle. The secretaries of state, defense, and treasury, the national security adviser, and the chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff are dialed in. At hundreds of facilities across the Indo-Pacific, U.S. military forces mobilize to their highest state of readiness. Air Force One is wheels up.

As reports of explosions and blackouts in Taipei flood social media, global financial markets enter free fall. Within hours, Apple alone loses nearly $1 trillion in market value. Investors abandon developing economies and flock to safe havens like gold and Swiss francs. Liquidity in the global banking system begins to evaporate, leaving many firms unable to meet overnight obligations. Meanwhile, speculators stockpile rare earths, pharmaceuticals, and other exports from China, anticipating that supplies will soon be cut off. Insurers suspend coverage for cargo traveling through the East and South China Seas. Airlines across Asia cancel flights. The financial system, overwhelmed by uncertainty, starts to fracture.

The president has key decisions to make, and most of them aren’t about the military. Taiwan’s semiconductor fabrication plants produce more than 90 percent of the world’s most advanced chips. What will be the impact on the global economy if production stops? Are U.S. allies prepared to fight? And what is the plan for the financial panic that has already begun? Should the U.S. retaliate economically against China even if it would make that panic worse?

---

In this moment of crisis, POTUS would convene the Joint Chiefs of Staff, followed by urgent calls to the New York Stock Exchange, Federal Reserve Board of Governors, and the Treasury secretary. A halt in trading would likely be the first step to stem panic and prevent a run on the banking system. Yet such measures could only slow, not stop, the financial reckoning. The Federal Reserve would face extraordinary challenges in balancing its dual mandate of price stability and full employment. Slashing interest rates to stabilize financial markets and preserve jobs could exacerbate inflation, which would be poised to skyrocket amid supply-chain disruptions and resource shortages.

This is just in the first hours.

More than a month into America’s war with Iran, this scenario should not sound like fiction.

On February 27, the Strait of Hormuz was the world’s most important energy choke point and one of its most secure. Roughly one-fifth of global oil supply passed through it every day. Tankers transited routinely. Insurance rates were stable. The possibility that the strait might close was discussed in think tanks and priced into risk models, but it belonged to the category of events that informed people acknowledged as a possibility without really believing.

The next day, the United States and Israel killed Iran’s supreme leader. Within hours, the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps declared the strait closed and began attacking commercial vessels. Since then, tanker traffic has dropped to nearly zero. The International Energy Agency has declared it the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market.

Now, after ceasefire talks in Pakistan ended without a deal, the United States has begun blockading Iranian ports and partially blockading the strait itself, aiming to squeeze the Iranian regime.

But even if they came to an agreement to open the strait tomorrow, the Strait of Hormuz would not be the same waterway it was on February 27. Every shipping company, every insurer, every energy importer would now know that Iran is willing and able to hold the global economy hostage. That knowledge does not expire. Insurance premiums would stay elevated. Trillions of dollars of investment would move accordingly. The Gulf States, who watched Iranian drones and missiles strike their territory, would draw their own conclusions about American promises and Iranian capabilities. You cannot just undo economic coercion at this scale. Once the threshold is crossed, the world reorganizes around the new reality. If the United States backs down under economic duress, it will be inviting Iran to hold the Strait of Hormuz hostage again in the future.

This point about irreversibility applies to Taiwan with far greater force.

The Taiwan Strait does not just carry oil. It carries the circulatory system of the digital economy, including chips on which every advanced weapons system depends. If the United States and China found themselves in a serious confrontation over Taiwan, the economic disruption might begin before any shots were fired. Or, if the conflict started suddenly, insurance markets, shipping routes, and capital flows would all react simultaneously and far more violently than they did after Donald Trump struck Iran. There is no emergency stockpile of chips; no equivalent of releasing 400 million barrels of oil.

Financial markets are highly sophisticated readers of public officials.

And the military fight—if it came to one—would be even more perilous. An amphibious invasion of Taiwan would be the most complex military operation in modern history. It would require the People’s Liberation Army (PLA) to execute large-scale, multi-domain warfare across land, sea, air, cyber, and space. Taiwan, using its geography, advanced technology, and asymmetric defenses, would aim to delay and disrupt any invasion until international reinforcements could arrive. Taipei is nearly as densely populated as New York City, but its streets are much narrower. PLA military analysts liken this scenario to “battling rats in a china shop.”

Escalation would be likely. If China tried to starve Taiwan into submission via a blockade, the island—in conjunction with allies including the U.S.—could respond with extensive strikes on mainland targets, large-scale mine warfare, and escalation into cyberspace and outer space. Both sides would likely target each other’s satellites, disrupting communications, surveillance, and precision targeting. If the crisis escalated, the use of tactical nuclear weapons could not be ruled out.

Against this backdrop, the question of what the United States would actually do is not academic. It is the whole thing. And the answer, at the moment, is: Nobody knows.

In December 2023, the bipartisan House Select Committee on the Chinese Communist Party published an [alarming report](https://chinaselectcommittee.house.gov/sites/evo-subsites/selectcommitteeontheccp.house.gov/files/evo-media-document/reset-prevent-build-scc-report.pdf) concluding that “the United States lacks a contingency plan for the economic and financial impacts of conflict” with China.

And yet, the U.S. government still has not communicated any credible economic contingency plan. Even if Washington refrained from full-scale economic warfare to decouple with China, an invasion and blockade of Taiwan would disrupt supply chains, leading to rapid, broad-based, and potentially sustained shortages of key goods, as well as inflation and mass layoffs. The effects on developing economies would be profound. Capital would flee from assets perceived as risky—particularly in countries like South Korea and Malaysia that are deeply dependent on electronics and other supply chains involving trade with both sides.

These supply chains, once disrupted, might take many years to heal. Replacement of semiconductor manufacturing equipment or facilities damaged during a conflict could take years. Even if Taiwan’s fate were quickly resolved in either direction and a ceasefire achieved, the United States and its major developed country allies would probably seek to permanently curtail their dependence on imports from China. There would be no return to the world before.

If the international trading system fractured—and the risk is real—skepticism toward free trade, already growing in the United States, would intensify. Many other countries might follow suit. Countries could then cite national security exemptions to justify virtually any protectionist trade or industrial policy. If many countries did this at the same time in a crisis, international trade could collapse, as it did during the Great Depression. The result could be a prolonged global economic cataclysm that would degrade U.S. prosperity and economic security for decades.

How can Washington effectively communicate its resolve during such a brinkmanship crisis if it visibly lacks a coherent plan? Why would U.S. allies follow the United States to the brink if they believe that falling over the brink would mean economic calamity for themselves?

---

For now, fortunately, deterrence is holding in the Taiwan Strait. During 13 years in power, Xi Jinping has avoided fomenting any crisis that could seriously risk war with the United States.

But deterrence can hold only if the deterring side is credibly prepared to accept the full consequences of the new situation, not just its response to day one.

Financial markets are highly sophisticated readers of public officials. They react to news at lightning speed. If the U.S. government is caught unprepared for a financial shock and markets discern that Washington has no plan, they may start to move in ways that Washington will struggle to control.

Xi needs to believe that moving against Taiwan would set in motion a cascade he cannot control or reverse. Showing him that requires more than fancy military hardware. It requires a political, diplomatic, and economic agenda for dealing with the ensuing crisis. It requires working with allies. It requires a vision for the future of the global economy that would follow a rupture—since there would be no going back.

The Iran war has demonstrated, vividly, the consequences of failing to do so. Trump’s decapitation strike on Iran’s leadership was a decisive military act. Everything since—the economic chaos, the allied improvisation, the absence of any plan for what comes next—has been a demonstration of what happens when you haven’t thought past the first move.

It did not have to go this way. The United States has spent decades planning how to fight a war over Taiwan, but it has spent almost no time thinking about the broader world that would come into being the day deterrence failed. The Iran war is showing us, in real time, what that failure of imagination looks like. The question is whether Washington will treat it as a warning, or a learning opportunity with great bearing on the lurking crisis that matters most.
