Elbridge Colby rose in Washington by warning against foreign wars, and he now leads defense policy. The Iran war is testing his influence and resolve.

On March 3, Elbridge Colby found himself in the unusual position of having to defend a war he has spent most of his career warning against. The top Pentagon strategist appeared before a [Senate committee](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=f8uB1NcAbNI&t=6280s) only three days after President Donald Trump announced the launch of Operation Epic Fury over the skies of Iran.

When Senator Elizabeth Warren asked him how the Iran war was “America First,” Colby [didn’t miss a beat.](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/03/03/iran-strikes-arent-an-endless-war-pentagon-policy-chief-argues-00808690) “I would say America First and peace-through-strength are served by rolling back, as the military campaign is designed to do, the threats posed by Iran’s very large and growing missile and one-way attack drone program, its navy, and of course, ensuring that it doesn’t have a nuclear weapon.”

For most senior Trump administration officials, testifying to the merits of the president’s war would be unremarkable. But Colby, known as Bridge to his friends, is a different story. The sandy-haired 46-year-old policy intellectual made his name in and out of government by warning about the perils of overstretched U.S. military commitments.

His job in the second Trump presidency as the under secretary of defense for policy seemed like a perfect match. Colby was serving a commander in chief who campaigned against the Middle Eastern wars of his predecessors. Yet the Iran war is a reminder that Trump’s worldview cannot be reduced to campaign slogans. For years, Colby has tried to persuade Washington to focus on the growing threat from China and shift away from Europe and the Middle East. And yet here he is, serving in an administration that has launched another war far away from America’s chief adversary.

In recent years Colby has emerged, along with former Trump State Department and National Security Council official [Michael Anton](https://www.thefp.com/p/magas-machiavelli-the-quiet-rise-of-michael-anton), as a leading foreign policy theorist within the MAGA movement. He has also mentored a generation of younger thinkers and analysts, many of whom claimed national security jobs in the second Trump administration, to the exclusion of foreign-policy hawks.

For years, Colby has tried to persuade Washington to focus on the growing threat from China and shift away from Europe and the Middle East.

The anti-war MAGA populists hoped that Colby would shield the president from the reflexive bellicosity of the Washington establishment. Tucker Carlson said this best when he introduced Colby on his podcast after Trump’s 2024 election victory. “I think you’re one of the very few people with deep experience in national security who shares the president’s priorities on national security, which is amazing,” [Carlson said](https://youtu.be/PtsGqGc-Iuw?si=Tsl4fjP37X5T_Vlb&t=52). “There aren’t too many. You’re, I would say, the leader of them.” In a [2023 profile](https://www.politico.com/news/magazine/2023/04/11/tucker-carlson-eldridge-colby-00090211) of Colby for *Politico*, *National Interest* editor Jacob Heilbrunn praised his Washington career as a “long march against the neocons” who continue to dominate foreign policy inside the Republican Party.

The problem for Carlson and his faction of MAGA is that Trump himself agrees with the old guard on Iran. That tension emerged a year ago during Colby’s nomination hearing. Republican senator Tom Cotton [grilled Colby](https://youtu.be/pk5q5uE7peg?si=vUUc8OSre0WzjKJv) about his past remarks saying that a nuclear Iran could be contained. Colby was careful back then. He said that in light of Iran’s development of long-range ballistic missiles, “a nuclear-armed Iran... would pose an existential danger to the United States.” That was enough for Cotton at the time. But Colby was on probation. Cotton got him to pledge, on the record, to provide the president with credible military options to address Iran’s nuclear program.

The perception inside Washington in the early months of the second Trump presidency was that Colby and the Pentagon bureaucracy would slow-walk military plans for Iran. The hawks may still have sway in Congress, but the populist restrainers were taking over the national security state.

That’s not what happened. Instead, the military provided Trump with war plans for Iran, and the president approved them. It was left to Democrats this March to throw Colby’s old words back in his face once the second Iran war began. When Virginia senator Tim Kaine asked him “Have we learned nothing from 25 years of war in the Middle East?” he was almost quoting directly from Colby’s post-election appearance on Carlson’s podcast. “Haven’t we learned the lesson over the last 25 years about the ill-advised nature of very significant conflicts in the Middle East that don’t have clear goals and clear connection to American interests?” [Colby mused at the time](https://youtu.be/PtsGqGc-Iuw?si=ubiYEHyWRpe2nkRM&t=514). “Didn’t we run this experiment a couple of times?”

---

Today, Colby sings a very different tune. In an interview at the Pentagon earlier this month, he told me that Operation Epic Fury has been successful. “The United States has severely degraded the capabilities of what is a noxious and aggressive Iranian government,” he said. “One of the impressive things is that no country has supported Iran. Even their putative friends have not come to their aid.” This was a dig at China, which did the bare minimum to help their ally. Yes, China apparently shipped Iran some [precursor chemicals](https://www.washingtonpost.com/investigations/2026/03/07/laden-iranian-ships-depart-chinese-port-tied-key-military-chemicals/) for missile fuel and in 2024 sold Iran a [spy satellite](https://www.abc.net.au/news/2026-04-06/us-intelligence-chinese-satellite-imagery-middle-east/106508322) that has helped Iran’s targeting of U.S. bases during the war, but little overall.

Nevertheless, the war has also exposed a problem that Colby has been warning about for years. America does not make enough [missiles and interceptors](https://www.csis.org/analysis/last-rounds-status-key-munitions-iran-war-ceasefire) for long wars, especially if it intends to amass a force large enough in the Pacific to deter China from taking Taiwan. One Colby ally in Washington told me, “The war is proving Bridge was right about the limitations of our military. It sucks for him that he can’t take the credit for being right.”

*The Free Press* conducted a dozen interviews with Colby’s friends and rivals inside the Trump administration and Congress as well as friends who have known him throughout his career. What emerges is a man caught between the contradictions of Trump’s second-term foreign policy. “The president is not with him when it comes to Iran,” one administration official told me. At the same time, Colby’s push to get European allies to take more responsibility for defending the continent from Russia, in what he has called NATO 3.0, is a centerpiece of the president’s foreign policy. “Colby may not get his policy on China or the Middle East,” this official said, “but his legacy will be felt in Europe.”

On paper, Colby looks like a strange fit for the MAGA movement. He is a throwback to an old Yankee elite that Trump demolished in 2016 when he defeated Jeb Bush, the last presidential candidate of the Bush dynasty that had recently dominated the Republican Party. His grandfather, William Colby, was the Central Intelligence Agency’s 10th director and the head of the [Phoenix Program](https://history.state.gov/historicaldocuments/frus1969-76v06/d74) in the Vietnam War, which targeted Vietcong leaders for assassination. His father, Jonathan Colby, made his career as an investment banker, including as a senior adviser to the Carlyle Group, the fund that employed former president George H.W. Bush from the late 1990s until the early 2000s.

Bridge Colby graduated in 1998 from the Groton School, the Massachusetts prep school that produced 20th-century titans of American statecraft such as Franklin Roosevelt and Dean Acheson. He went on to Harvard and earned a law degree from Yale. In an administration filled with big media personalities like his Pentagon boss, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth, Colby stands out. His demeanor is Brahmin; one cannot imagine him [posting AI video memes](https://www.cpr.org/2026/05/06/did-fbi-director-kash-patel-use-ai-to-rip-off-the-beastie-boys/) on his social media account or challenging senior officers to [push-up contests](https://www.foxnews.com/politics/rfk-jr-hegseth-face-off-impressive-pullup-pushup-pentagon-showdown).

Ross Douthat, a *New York Times* columnist who befriended Colby at Harvard and later lived with him in Washington, described Colby to me as “a representative of a defunct elite in a way that is different from Jake Sullivan,” the wunderkind national security adviser to President Joe Biden. “When populists recoil against the deep state, or when critics of U.S. foreign policy talk about the blob, they are talking about a foreign policy elite that developed in the post–Cold War era that had liberal hawk and neoconservative wings that took American hegemony for granted.”

Colby, Douthat said, is reminiscent of a previous elite: The Cold War mandarins could be prone to bouts of hubris, such as the Vietnam War, but also operated in a bipolar world, constrained by a great power rival in the Soviet Union.

Colby’s grandfather, William, exemplified both the hubris and prudence of America’s Cold War foreign policy elite. Despite his misadventures in Vietnam, he later cooperated with [Senator Frank Church’s investigation](https://nsarchive2.gwu.edu/NSAEBB/NSAEBB522-Church-Committee-Faced-White-House-Attempts-to-Curb-CIA-Probe/) into the American deep state in 1975, the only full-scale reckoning for the CIA and Federal Bureau of Investigation during the Cold War. He infuriated the Ford White House and many fellow spooks by providing Congress with the “ [Family Jewels](https://nsarchive2.gwu.edu/NSAEBB/NSAEBB222/),” a 700-page report that detailed the agency’s assassination plots, spying on the U.S. anti-war movement, and experiments with unwitting prisoners and mental patients to research the effects of LSD, among other things. Ford’s chief of staff at the time, Donald Rumsfeld, begged Colby to stonewall Congress. Henry Kissinger, then secretary of state, [privately said of the elder Colby](https://nsarchive2.gwu.edu/NSAEBB/NSAEBB526-Court-Ordered-Release-of-Kissinger-Telcons/): “You accuse him of a traffic violation, and he confesses murder.”

William Colby’s post-government life was relatively quiet. But his death was suspicious. His biographer, [Randall Woods](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=X2r4RdKZ7Bs), has speculated he may have been murdered. Colby’s body was found in the Wicomico River near his vacation home in Rock Point, Maryland, in 1996, several days after he went missing. The [medical examiner found](https://www.chicagotribune.com/1996/05/12/colby-may-have-suffered-heart-attack-before-drowning/) he may have suffered a heart attack or stroke before drowning, but Elbridge’s uncle Carl Colby [released a documentary](https://firstrunfeatures.com/themannobodyknew/) in 2011 that speculated that the death was a suicide. In that film, former national security adviser Brent Scowcroft describes William Colby as a “tortured soul” because of lingering guilt over his role in sharing the CIA’s secrets with Congress.

Bridge Colby and his father, Jonathan, in 2011 took issue with Scowcroft in a [letter to the editor](https://www.washingtonpost.com/opinions/the-william-colby-we-knew/2011/11/29/gIQAxbtJMO_story.html) of *The Washington Post*. They wrote that the elder Colby’s “policies of cooperation with Congress and of accountability to the Constitution were driven by a combination of principle and pragmatic political calculation. Whether he made the right calls is for others to decide, but to say that he was a ‘tortured soul’ is to misunderstand and caricature what he was doing.”

An irony is that Colby’s approach to grand strategy more closely hews to Scowcroft’s vision of the world than that of his grandfather. William Colby was a true believer when it came to Vietnam. He was not just a policymaker but a practitioner of the brutal counterinsurgency strategy that failed to bring free markets and democracy to that country.

His grandson echoed Scowcroft’s realism when he first came to Washington in 2002. This was a time when the foreign policy discussion was still shaped by an idealism born out of America’s Cold War victory. It was not enough for President George W. Bush to destroy al-Qaeda; the ultimate goal of U.S. foreign policy was to end tyranny in the world and spread democracy, as Bush said in his [second inaugural address](https://www.presidency.ucsb.edu/documents/inaugural-address-13).

Colby was out of step with that idealism. When he lived across the hall from Douthat at Harvard, he often offered the minority view among the hawks in their late-night debates. Douthat, in a [2021 column](https://www.nytimes.com/2021/09/11/opinion/china-america-foreign-policy.html), said Colby would channel “the realist foreign-policy thinkers he admired, predicting quagmire, destabilization, and defeat.” Today Douthat, who now aligns with Colby’s foreign policy, considers his friend to have been prescient.

As Colby climbed the ranks in Washington, he developed a deep knowledge of the hard power realities of the U.S. military. His writings in this period focused on the limited resources for the U.S. military at a time when many strategists focused instead on America’s unmatched power compared to rival militaries.

Colby argues that America cannot afford its current military footprint in the Middle East and Europe and still expect to deter China from launching wars to establish hegemony in Asia.

Colby was a Republican in college as he is today. He volunteered for George W. Bush’s 2000 campaign in 1999 which, before 9/11, promised a more humble foreign policy. As an undergraduate he opposed the [1999 air war](https://www.nato.int/en/what-we-do/operations-and-missions/kosovo-air-campaign-march-june-1999) that stopped Serbia’s strongman Slobodan Milošević from cleansing Kosovo of Albanians.

During the Barack Obama administration, Bridge was on the team that negotiated the nuclear arms control treaty with Russia known as [New START](https://www.state.gov/new-start-treaty). And he was a finalist in 2015 to be foreign policy director for Jeb Bush’s presidential campaign. According to *The Wall Street Journal*, Colby’s name was [removed from consideration](https://www.wsj.com/articles/BL-WB-54364) after the political side of the campaign came across some of his foreign-policy writings. A [2010 blog post](https://foreignpolicy.com/2010/11/12/thinking-responsibly-why-containing-tehran-is-both-plausible-and-practical/) for *Foreign Policy* magazine, for example, argued that a nuclear Iran could be contained.

The setback turned out to be an opportunity. Colby avoided the stain of being on a losing campaign. In 2017, the first Trump administration tapped him to be a deputy assistant secretary at the Pentagon, where he helped draft the 2018 National Defense Strategy. In the Trump years, Colby managed to earn the trust of other Republican restrainers. After testifying before the Senate Armed Services Committee in 2019, he impressed freshman Republican senator Josh Hawley with his emphasis on sharing the defense burden among U.S. allies and his focus on China. The two have had a close relationship since.

Colby also forged a relationship with J.D. Vance after Vance won a Senate seat in 2022. The future vice president brought Colby to a Senate Republican lunch in 2023, where Colby made the case against unlimited support for Ukraine to repel the Russian invasion of the year before. He made enemies at that lunch, including then–Senate Majority Leader Mitch McConnell.

That lunch from 2023 served in some ways as the opening round of a fight that continues today. The GOP old guard wants America to remain a global power in Europe, the Middle East, and Asia. The populist wing of the GOP argues for a more restrained U.S. role in the world so that resources can focus on securing the homeland.

Even though Colby is often associated with the restrainers, he is more accurately described as a “prioritizer,” a term coined in 2022 by foreign policy analysts [Jeremy Shapiro and Majda Ruge](https://ecfr.eu/article/polarised-power-the-three-republican-tribes-that-could-define-americas-relationship-with-the-world/) to describe one of the three foreign policy schools inside the GOP coalition. Unlike his party’s restrainers, Colby does not argue for a broad American retreat from the world. Indeed, he has been a top advocate in Congress for the president’s new $1.5 trillion defense budget.

Instead, Colby argues that America cannot afford its current military footprint in the Middle East and Europe and still expect to deter China from launching a sequence of wars to establish hegemony in Asia. He makes this case in his [2021 book](https://bookshop.org/a/93116/9780300268027), *The Strategy of Denial: American Defense in an Age of Great Power Conflict*. The book caused a sensation in Washington, attracting both fierce critics and defenders. Its core thesis is that America should build an “anti-hegemonic coalition” among China’s regional rivals, like Japan and India, that would amass enough force to effectively deny China a reasonable military option to assert military dominance in Asia. At the same time, he makes a persuasive case that any U.S.-Sino war would have disastrous consequences for both countries.

Colby today is in a strange position. As his March testimony before Congress demonstrated, he is by no means loved by Democrats. But he is also distrusted by many powerful Republicans.

On March 5, for example, Colby was grilled by the Republican chairman of the House Armed Services Committee, Rep. Mike Rogers, who accused Colby of usurping the White House’s authority last year when he issued guidance that led the military to withdraw a brigade from Romania. “We made repeated requests to be consulted on any potential U.S. force posture changes,” Rogers said. “Despite those requests last October, just two weeks before the decision to withdraw the U.S. brigade from Romania, you told me during a meeting in your office at the Pentagon that you were not aware of any such ongoing discussions. I am concerned you were not being truthful.”

Colby disputed the characterization and pledged his honesty to Rogers. “Let me reassure you emphatically that I am a loyal lieutenant of the president, and we do not do anything in the Department of War that is not aligned with the president. I am not in the chain of command, sir.”

The president himself has been inconsistent on the overall question of U.S. forces in Europe. He has urged Europeans to spend more for their self-defense. But earlier this month he appeared to [reverse a Pentagon decision](https://www.wsj.com/politics/national-security/trump-says-poland-will-get-an-extra-5-000-u-s-troops-d71d6d6e?st=mQvt31) to delay the rotation of a 5,000-man armored brigade to Poland.

Republican hawks in Congress “wouldn’t dare attack the president or Hegseth,” one Colby ally in the Trump administration told me. “So they go after Bridge.”

Colby was also blamed in 2025 for holding up a [shipment of weapons](https://www.politico.com/news/2025/07/02/ukraine-weapons-freeze-elbridge-colby-00438156) to Ukraine because U.S. stocks of the munitions were low. Other Pentagon officials told me that Colby was unfairly blamed for the pause in arms shipments. Nevertheless, the origin of the pause was obscure, and it seemed to align with Colby’s priorities.

Colby, who was confirmed last year for his job, has cleared the hurdle of Congress but some of his staffers have not. Nominations for two of his protégés— [Alex Velez-Green](https://www.congress.gov/nomination/119th-congress/345/17) and [Austin Dahmer](https://www.congress.gov/nomination/119th-congress/246/5) —have been held up by Republicans on the Senate Armed Services Committee since their names were sent to Congress last year. Both nominations were sent back to the White House in January. “This is how the committee is telling Bridge they are unhappy,” one Senate GOP aide told me. Dahmer and Velez-Green are being punished, other Senate staffers tell me, because Colby is their mentor.

In this respect Colby has been a lightning rod. Republican hawks in Congress “wouldn’t dare attack the president or Hegseth,” one Colby ally in the Trump administration told me. “So they go after Bridge.”

Colby faced similar intense scrutiny last summer during a review of the arms agreement forged by the Biden administration between Australia, the UK, and the U.S., known as AUKUS. Colby met with Australian and British ambassadors as part of the review, and those meetings did not go well. The *[Financial Times](https://www.ft.com/content/41e272e4-5b25-47ee-807c-2b57c1316fe4?syn-25a6b1a6=1)* also reported that Colby sought commitments from Japan and Australia regarding what the two countries would do to aid a U.S. military campaign if war broke out between China and Taiwan. The Japanese and Australians balked. They worried that the U.S. commitment to sell Australia the nuclear submarines now had new strings attached.

The AUKUS kerfuffle was one factor that prompted an August 8 memo to the heads of all government departments and agencies from White House chief of staff Susie Wiles and acting National Security Adviser Marco Rubio, according to two administration officials. “The National Security Council serves as the White House’s primary coordinator for all interactions, engagements, or agreements involving foreign governments or foreign equities,” it said, according to a copy obtained by *The Free Press*. “Agencies must clear the purpose and scope of any proposed call, conversation, meeting, or trip with the NSC prior to engagement.”

Colby himself declined to comment on the record on the matter. But he stressed that he remained a loyal Trump lieutenant. “The president has very rightly called for increasing U.S. defense spending,” he told me. “This is critical if we’re going to be able to achieve the goals laid out in the National Security and Defense Strategies.”

For now, this year’s [National Defense Strategy](https://media.defense.gov/2026/Jan/23/2003864773/-1/-1/0/2026-NATIONAL-DEFENSE-STRATEGY.PDF), which Colby’s office helped to draft, has been his most significant contribution to U.S. foreign policy. Yet in many ways, the vision laid out in that document has already been overtaken by the launch of Operation Epic Fury, which the strategy neither urged nor anticipated. It stressed the success of last June’s Iran war and touted the “significant opportunities,” moving forward, to help America’s allies in the Middle East defend themselves. There was no mention of the need for a second Iran war.

Perhaps the lesson is that Trump’s foreign policy cannot fit neatly into the categories preferred by policy intellectuals like Colby. Trump can sound like a neoconservative, as when he urged the Iranian people to rise up against their oppressors, and then he can sound like a cold-eyed realist, praising Pakistan’s military strongman for hosting Iran-U.S. negotiations in Islamabad.

Colby was supposed to bring a populist sensibility to Trump’s second administration. A year and a half in, he is the loyal lieutenant to a president who has launched a war Colby has spent his career trying to prevent.
