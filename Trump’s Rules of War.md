In five years as president, Donald Trump has established his own unique and changeable set of rules about when and how to use American military power. The current confrontation with Iran is proceeding based on some new guidelines, which may not sit well with many of his supporters. In the last four days, Trump has broken two of his own rules about foreign and defense policy, maintained two (so far), and ended a longtime American taboo.

First, Trump’s previous military actions complied with the “one and done” rule. This meant no “endless wars” and instead, very brief military actions that were over when they were announced: the killing of Iranian terrorist leader [Qasem Soleimani](https://www.nytimes.com/2020/01/02/world/middleeast/qassem-soleimani-iraq-iran-attack.html) in his first term, the Midnight Hammer attack on Iranian nuclear sites last year, and the capture of Venezuelan strongman Nicolás Maduro. Now, instead of another brief attack on nuclear sites or missile launchers, Trump has undertaken what could be a multi-week effort.

While not the endless wars he still derides, this is the first time he has done more than a one-day strike. The political downside is obvious, and the assaults on him have been fierce from MAGA influencers who feel betrayed, from leftists who see any U.S. strike as imperialism, and from an almost united Democratic Party. Trump says he does not care and that, for one thing, he defines what’s MAGA and what is not. He may get away with this if the war goes well and is short, but he has accepted the political risks with eyes wide open.

Second, Trump’s previous uses of military power followed a “no U.S. casualties” rule. In the current Iran campaign, he accepted real risks and predicted casualties.

“My administration is taking every possible step to minimize the risk to U.S. personnel in the region,” he said in a [recorded statement](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/world/read-trumps-full-statement-on-iran-attack) as the air assault began. “Even so, and I do not make this statement lightly, the Iranian regime seeks to kill. The lives of courageous American heroes may be lost, and we may have casualties.”

The Trump who would never contemplate risking American lives and engaging in more than single-day assaults is gone.

He accepted this risk—this probability—with eyes wide open. The willingness to endure American deaths in action he ordered is something entirely new for Trump.

These two rule-breaking decisions will be of some interest to Xi Jinping as he assesses whether to attack Taiwan. The Trump who would never contemplate risking American lives and engaging in more than single-day assaults is gone.

The taboo Trump has broken: killing a foreign head of state. While Israeli bombs killed Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, the information on his location was [provided by the U.S.](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2026/mar/01/how-israeli-sleight-and-us-might-led-to-the-assassination-of-ali-khamenei) with the intention of having him killed.

In December 1981, President Ronald Reagan signed [Executive Order 12333](https://www.archives.gov/federal-register/codification/executive-order/12333.html), a lengthy description of responsibilities within the U.S. intelligence community that includes this line: “No person employed by or acting on behalf of the United States Government shall engage in, or conspire to engage in, assassination.”

The ban was not new, having [first been promulgated](https://www.britannica.com/event/Executive-Order-11905) by President Gerald Ford in 1976. But *assassination* was never defined—and is not easy to define. Killing an enemy leader during an armed conflict is obviously different from a peacetime assassination done for political reasons. Still, the United States has [never killed](https://www.cp24.com/news/world/2026/03/03/has-the-us-ever-assassinated-a-world-leader-before/) a head of state before, and Trump has set a precedent: An enemy leader is a legitimate target, whatever his office or title.

There are two rules about the use of the U.S. military that Trump has maintained throughout his presidency, and the first is “no U.S. ground troops.” What separates even this much larger effort in Iran from the endless wars in Iraq and Afghanistan is, as of today, not only the time element but also that there are no American boots on the ground.

Note that Trump has not ruled out ground troops. “I don’t have the yips with respect to boots on the ground,” [he told the press](https://nypost.com/2026/03/02/us-news/trump-wont-rule-out-sending-us-troops-into-iran-if-necessary-tells-the-post-i-dont-care-about-polling/) Monday. “Every president says, ‘There will be no boots on the ground.’ I don’t say it. I say ‘probably don’t need them,’ \[or\] ‘if they were necessary.’ ”

He is wise not to rule things out, just as he is wise to say we will strike Iran as long as is necessary, week after week. To do otherwise would be foolish, closing off options and giving the Iranian regime a strong sense that they can just hold on, wait Trump out, and survive.

But would he really contemplate boots on the ground? It’s not hard to believe that he would approve a brief commando raid, given what he has approved already: Troops landed briefly in Venezuela, and that commando raid could have gone wrong (and almost did when one helicopter was hit and its pilot badly injured). But the risks to American troops inside Iran would be greater, and the likely blast from critics at home would be loud—even if the raid were successful. So “no boots on the ground” is still the rule for Trump, though sometimes rules are made to be broken.

Finally, the great bugaboo for Trump and MAGA: no nation-building! That rule has not been broken. During his visit to Saudi Arabia last May, [Trump said](https://www.realclearpolitics.com/video/2025/05/13/trump_to_saudi_arabia_the_nation_builders_wrecked_far_more_nations_than_they_built.html) “In the end, the so-called nation-builders wrecked far more nations than they built,” and he derided the “Western intervention\[alists\]... giving you lectures on how to live and govern your own affairs.”

In fact, the whole issue of nation-building arises only when the U.S. has conquered and occupied a country. Otherwise, we lack the power to “build”—to determine how politics develops. In Germany and Japan after World War II, in the Philippines when it was an American possession, and in a few other cases, the U.S. did have that power, as it did in Afghanistan and Iraq in the years after 9/11.

The truth is that Trump is rewriting his own rules day by day, to the disgust of isolationists and antisemites like Tucker Carlson, many MAGA luminaries, and possibly his own vice president.

In Venezuela, Trump is seeking an alternative way to deal with a hostile regime: Don’t occupy, and instead weaken the regime, depose the leader, and see if something can be worked out that will produce a government that is not hostile to the U.S. Trump has not used the word *democracy* as a goal of his Venezuela policy. Why not? Because that was never his goal, though it remains the likely outcome. He may be indifferent to liberty in Venezuela, but Venezuelans aren’t.

In Iran, the war aims beyond strictly military goals (like ending the nuclear weapons program, sinking the navy, stopping the buildup of ballistic missiles) are vague. Again, Trump never says *democracy*, but he has [told Iranians](https://www.yahoo.com/news/articles/trump-tells-iranians-hour-freedom-084335189.html) that “the hour of your freedom is at hand.... When we are finished, take over your government. It will be yours to take. This will be probably your only chance for generations.”

But what will Trump’s reaction be if they do try to “take over” their government, and once again are mowed down by the thousands? Will Trump stand by and watch? Will he again say [what he did in January](https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/115888317758045915): “Iranian Patriots, KEEP PROTESTING—TAKE OVER YOUR INSTITUTIONS!!!.... HELP IS ON ITS WAY,” only to delay his fire for months? Or will he offer real help, for example by attacking the forces killing unarmed Iranian protesters? Maybe we don’t do nation-building, but do help Iranians who want to do their own.

Is that too close to “nation-building?” The truth is that Trump is rewriting his own rules day by day, to the disgust of isolationists and antisemites like Tucker Carlson, many MAGA luminaries, and possibly [his own vice president](https://www.thefp.com/p/jd-vances-iran-dilemma).

No one would have predicted the day after the killing of Soleimani that Trump would end up where he is now. But there is a pattern here. Upon returning to office, it took only five months for him to get to Midnight Hammer, six more to take action against Venezuela, and only two more months between the day of Maduro’s capture and the bombing of Iran. Add to that calendar the end of the “one and done” and “no casualties” rules, and Donald Trump’s evolving rules of war seem far more adventurous than his campaign speeches let on. He owes Dick Cheney a big apology.
