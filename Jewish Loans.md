Here's a chart of interest rates over seven centuries.  
  
But something stands out: Where are all the Jewish loans?  
  
Jews have had a reputation for making loans for centuries, but this analysis explicitly omits them. The reason is simple: Jewish loans were different.

In the premodern world, religious restrictions on moneylending abounded.  
  
Since states were weak and Christians had no ability to charge usurious rates, Christian finance was handicapped. So the enterprise came to be dominated by Jews, who weren't similarly restricted.

Rulers were not unaware of this. In fact, they regularly made attempts to attract Jews to their towns and cities.  
  
Sometimes town representatives would even beseech their lords to let them bring Jews to their towns because, TL;DR: 'think of the poor people!'

But once Jews were permitted into towns, the scale of their lending wasn't kept to the poor who needed a means of obtaining quick cash.  
  
Quite the contrary: large percentages of the population borrowed from Jews!

Another way we know Jewish moneylending was common is the fact that just as there's a Chancellor of the Exchequer today, there used to also just be an Exchequer of the Jews.

The Exchequer of the Jewry had a vested interest in the continuance and development of the Jewish monopoly on moneylending in England.  
  
The presence of Jewish credit was what allowed the king to credibly transact with parties outside of his feudal realm, so they were protected:

Jewish moneylending was needed for markets to work, it was desired by traders and rulers alike, and it was commonly used by all strata of the population.  
  
So how high were the rates? Well, that depended on where you lived and the charters in place. Charters were commonplace:

Charters regulated what Jews were allowed to do across Europe, and a major thing they regulated was moneylending.  
  
For example, in Tirol, the charter enacted by Friedrich IV placed the maximum rate at 64.5%.

Rates varied by the town, and some rates even had progressive ceilings.

Rates were likely higher in northern than southern Europe.  
  
This makes sense, because in northern Europe, there were fewer Lombards who could substitute for Jewish moneylenders.

From various accounts, it seems Jews charged much higher rates than the practically inter-governmental rates rulers sometimes secured.  
  
The interest rate felt by non-governmental entities, non-nobles, small loan recipients, etc. was likely far higher than what's in that chart.
