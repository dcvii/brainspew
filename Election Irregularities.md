**Claims I confirmed as accurate:**

The Orange County episode is real. The DOJ sued the OC Registrar (Robert Page) in June 2025 over the removal records for 17 non-citizen registrants identified since 2020. Sixteen self-reported that they were not citizens; one, a Canadian legal resident, pleaded guilty in 2024 to casting votes in the 2016 primary and general elections and got a year of informal probation. One correction to the document's framing, though: the dispute is *not* that the county refused to identify the 17. The county turned over names, birthdates, and addresses but redacted Social Security numbers, driver's license numbers, and signature scans. The live question is whether the county must hand over that unredacted personal data, with a trial date that was set for March 2026. The DOJ side also argues 17 isn't the full universe — so the document's tidy "already purged, handled administratively" version loses a genuine ongoing tension. [aol](https://www.aol.com/news/justice-department-sues-orange-county-035015597.html)[aol](https://www.aol.com/news/justice-department-sues-orange-county-035015597.html)

The Arizona MVD database error is real and was larger than the document implies. An initial ~98,000 voters grew to roughly 218,000 affected by a coding error that mislabeled people with pre-1996 licenses as having provided documentary proof of citizenship. Critically, the document's characterization is correct: this was an administrative classification problem, not fraud. These were longtime registered voters who had attested citizenship under penalty of perjury, and the Arizona Supreme Court let them vote a full ballot in 2024. [Arizona Mirror](https://azmirror.com/briefs/number-of-voters-affected-by-mvd-citizenship-proof-glitch-grows-to-218000/)[Democracy Docket](https://www.democracydocket.com/news-alerts/arizona-data-error-could-disenfranchise-nearly-100000-voters/)

The Bridgeport, Connecticut case checks out — and again the document understates it. There were criminal charges (nine people total across 2025), but more significantly, a state Superior Court judge overturned Ganim's 2023 primary victory and ordered a new primary, citing "shocking" evidence of "blatant" ballot harvesting. So this was an overturned election, not merely some charges filed. [Connecticut Public](https://www.ctpublic.org/news/2025-02-21/bridgeport-ct-primary-election-charges)

The Diebold/Princeton claim is precisely accurate. The 2006 study by Feldman, Halderman, and Felten found that an attacker with one minute of physical access to an AccuVote-TS machine or its memory card could install malicious code that steals votes undetectably, modifying all records, logs, and counters to match the fraudulent count. They also demonstrated a self-propagating voting-machine virus. The document's key nuance — a demonstrated vulnerability is not proof a specific election was changed — is exactly the right distinction. [Princeton Center for Information Technology Policy](https://citp.princeton.edu/our-work/publications/security-analysis-diebold-accuvote-ts-voting-machine)

The San Francisco non-citizen voting claim is correct and adds useful scale context. A California appeals court upheld the program in 2023 (Lacy v. City and County of San Francisco), allowing non-citizen parents/guardians to vote in school board elections only. The telling detail: actual participation has been tiny — 59 voters in 2018, only two in 2019, around 300 in a 2022 recall. That reinforces the "vanishingly rare" theme even where non-citizen voting is *legal*. [Rennepubliclawgroup](https://rennepubliclawgroup.com/court-of-appeal-upholds-san-franciscos-noncitizen-voting-program/)[The Oaklandside](https://oaklandside.org/2024/11/01/measure-s-noncitizen-voting-oakland-school-board-election/)
