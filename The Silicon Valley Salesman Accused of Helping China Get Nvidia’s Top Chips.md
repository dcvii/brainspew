“We’re continuing to push the boundaries of innovation,” Super Micro wrote on X next to a picture of Huang shaking hands with Super Micro’s CEO, as Liaw beamed nearby.

Two days after the post, Liaw [was under arrest](https://www.wsj.com/tech/super-micro-computer-employees-arrested-for-alleged-sales-to-china-45a2bd73?mod=article_inline).

The U.S. charges against him involve the very product that warmed the mood at the conference: Super Micro servers containing high-powered Nvidia artificial-intelligence processors. Federal prosecutors say Liaw, 71, helped Chinese customers get $2.5 billion of those servers in violation of U.S. export-control laws.

The indictment puts Liaw and Super Micro in the middle of the tech war between the U.S. and China, which are racing to develop the world’s most advanced AI tools.

Nvidia’s Huang has argued—including to President Trump—that he should be allowed to sell America’s best innovations in China. While Trump has [agreed partway](https://www.wsj.com/tech/nvidia-china-exports-h2000-chips-5943aa48?mod=article_inline), his administration has kept Biden-era [export bans on Nvidia’s top-of-the-line chips](https://www.wsj.com/world/china/trump-nvidia-china-chip-exports-51e00415?mod=article_inline).

Huang said in May 2025 that “there’s no evidence of any AI chip diversion.” He cited the large size of Nvidia’s Grace Blackwell systems, some of which weigh nearly two tons, and efforts by countries and companies to ensure compliance.

Yet in the month before Huang’s comment, Liaw was allegedly busy with a scheme to evade those efforts.

At the time, Super Micro’s compliance department had paused shipments of servers to an Asian company that, according to prosecutors, was used as a pass-through to hide the servers’ ultimate destination in China.

The indictment says Liaw advised his associates to craft an email with “strong and persuasive reasons to convince” the compliance department. A few days later, one of Liaw’s co-defendants texted Liaw and another colleague, “Heheheh, Gooood news for everyone!” The email had worked.

The indictment says Liaw sprang into action, messaging his contact at the Asian pass-through company. “We will speed up the shipment right away! Please issue the B200 \[purchase order\] right away!” he wrote, referring to chips from Nvidia’s Blackwell line that are prized by Chinese developers for their ability to train AI models.

Over the next few weeks, the company ordered $510 million worth of servers, all of which were diverted to China, the indictment says.

Prosecutors also described elaborate efforts to avoid detection. Executives including Liaw allegedly corresponded about bringing in 100 people, including forklift operators, and arranging meals and a 20-person shuttle bus to help stage dummy servers in warehouses before auditors came through.

Prosecutors published photos showing one of Liaw’s deputies with an assistant who they said used a hair dryer to remove and affix labels and serial-number stickers to shipping boxes. The goal was to persuade inspectors, including one from the U.S. Commerce Department, that they were looking at genuine Super Micro servers with Nvidia chips inside.

Liaw’s team took photographs of the staged servers to send to one of Super Micro’s compliance auditors, who was off-site enjoying entertainment paid for by the Asian pass-through company, the indictment says.

After prosecutors unsealed the indictment Thursday night, Super Micro’s shares fell by one-third on Friday, erasing more than $6 billion in market value. Liaw, a U.S. citizen originally from Taiwan whose full name is Yih-Shyan Liaw, resigned from the board.

Super Micro said it was cooperating with federal investigators, and it hired a chief compliance officer on Friday. It declined to comment further. Neither Super Micro nor Nvidia has been accused of wrongdoing.

“Strict compliance is a top priority for Nvidia,” a spokesman for the chip giant said. “We continue to work closely with our customers and the government on compliance programs as export regulations have expanded.”

Last summer, David Sacks, the White House AI czar, played down the possibility of chips being diverted to China, saying it was “very easy to basically verify” their location.

Some analysts said the indictment suggested the contrary.

The details of the indictment are “a reminder of how rampant this problem is,” said Dean Ball, a former Trump administration official who worked on technology and national security. “There are people in the White House who are trying to downplay the notion of smuggling, and then smuggling is going on at the exact same time.”

Chips will continue to be diverted to China “as long as there are export controls,” Ball said.

Super Micro has had a long partnership with Nvidia, putting the chip company’s products into its servers. Super Micro’s name and logo were plastered over the exhibition floor at Nvidia’s tech conference this past week in San Jose, Calif., where Huang touted a forecast of [$1 trillion in chip sales](https://www.wsj.com/tech/ai/nvidias-ceo-projects-1-trillion-in-ai-chip-sales-as-new-computing-era-begins-671b369d?mod=article_inline).

“If Jensen wants to make his name completely clean, he wants to distance himself and distance the company from Super Micro,” said Mark Newman, an analyst with Bernstein who covers Super Micro.

Super Micro’s sales practices were the subject of a Securities and Exchange Commission investigation published in 2020. From 2015 to 2017, the company tried to inflate quarterly sales, including by asking distributors to accept goods before the end of a quarter even if customers weren’t ready yet to receive the items, the SEC found.

Liaw, who wasn’t named in the SEC’s investigation, stepped down in 2018 alongside the company’s former finance chief, who was charged with [accounting violations](https://www.wsj.com/livecoverage/stock-market-today-dow-sp-500-nasdaq-03-20-2026/card/indictment-of-super-micro-co-founder-has-a-long-backstory-heard-on-the-street-XnKJRVPv2RFeFMvjcoTZ?mod=article_inline). Super Micro was delisted for nearly two years owing to delayed financial filings following the accounting probe. It paid $17.5 million to settle with the SEC.

Liaw was soon brought back and promoted to head of worldwide sales, and he rejoined the board in 2023. Ernst & Young resigned as Super Micro’s accounting firm in 2024, citing governance issues. As a result, the company’s rehiring practices were probed as part of an internal investigation.

“I would argue they did not do a good job in rehiring someone who they knew was risky,” Newman, the analyst, said of Super Micro CEO Charles Liang and the board.
