Everyone is arguing about whether to have children. Almost no one is talking about what you get when you do.

***Happy Mother’s Day from all of us here at The Free Press! To mark the occasion, we’re publishing an essay by our newest columnist, [Caitlin Flanagan](https://www.thefp.com/w/caitlin-flanagan), that tackles what has become one of the most contentious questions of our time: Why are so many young women ambivalent about motherhood? We also, in yesterday’s Weekend Press, ran a piece by Larissa Phillips about [the joy of having adult kids](https://www.thefp.com/p/the-joy-of-adult-kids?r=4crwli), and a piece by Danielle Crittenden about [the heartbreak of losing one](https://www.thefp.com/p/when-i-lost-my-daughter). Each of these essays captures something so often missing from the so-called Mommy Wars: the mystical, almost inexpressible love of a mother for the child she birthed into existence. We hope you enjoy them. —The Editors***

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!NmZe!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3589af18-d8ad-4c89-8ed6-3bb5bab252f2_1320x30.png)

The biggest event in the history of women—bigger, even, than suffrage—was the invention of the birth control pill, which became available in the United States in 1960. For the first time, women had the ability to decide if, when, and how many children they were going to have. When that was combined with the first stirrings of modern feminism, women were finally able to do what men had been doing through the ages: break into factions and tear each other apart. The Mommy Wars will bury us all.

The Mommy Wars were originally a project of the 1980s and centered on a question still at the heart of the current iteration: If a woman can afford to stay home with her children, should she do it? At this point, [about 70 percent](https://usafacts.org/articles/how-many-mothers-are-in-the-labor-force/) of American mothers either work or are looking for work, many of them because they cannot afford not to, which would seem to make the question moot. Even *Working Mother* magazine (est. 1979) threw in the towel a few years ago, because how many things can a career woman do with a rotisserie chicken? Over the past two decades, however, a much more consequential division between young women has quietly and then loudly emerged: the one between mothers and women who are “childfree by choice,” as they often put it.

I know many such women, and you probably do too. If so, you can attest that they don’t seem torn up by the decision. In fact, they made it in exactly the way men beg women to make decisions: logically and on the merits. They know that having a baby would unmoor them from many things they cherish about their lives: the freedom to do whatever they want, untethered by round-the-clock responsibility. They have hopes and dreams about their futures, and they want their earnings to go toward fulfilling them, not toward parenthood. Most of all, they know that babies are a lot of work and that women do most of it. They want to sleep on their own schedules, not on baby time.

---

I want to correct each and every one of these bonkers assertions, but... how? Not only are these young women right, they don’t know the half of it. When my twin sons were infants, I would literally dream about sleeping. Once, when they were down for their morning nap, I walked purposefully down the hall, like a woman headed to work or maybe more like a dreamer, turned into my bedroom, and put on my pajamas. I was just zipping up my bathrobe when I realized it wasn’t *my* bedtime; it was 10:30 in the morning and there were hours to go before I slept. In many ways the whole experience was a shocking parade of indignities. In one hideous episode, the boys—maybe 18 months old—contracted some kind of gastroenteritis that struck just as we were wheels up on a flight from the Midwest to California. We went through the diapers before the “fasten seatbelt” sign was turned off.

Those events turned into funny stories soon after they happened, because... who cares about the miserable moments? They were my sons, and what in the world could be better than having those two little boys around? I knew having children would be the best part of my life, and it *was* the best part of my life. Just as I knew that the time when they were little would run through my hands like water and never come back again. If those boys were on Rotten Tomatoes, I would give them a 100. No notes. Met and exceeded all expectations.

But for millions of young women, it’s as though the intense joy that only motherhood can provide has been wiped off the balance books entirely, leaving behind only the stark truth of the fasten-seatbelt sign and the sleepwalking mother. Lately I’ve been asking myself: Did someone blow the whistle on babies?

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ALyy!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F84930b20-656b-495c-a3e9-4656ab20f3e0_1320x30.png)

There are a lot of American women—and women in other parts of the developed world—who don’t want babies. For a country to maintain its population, women must have an average of 2.1 children each. In 1990, American women had 2.08 children; in 2024 the fertility rate was the lowest it’s ever been: 1.63. In Italy—where babies have always been the kings and queens of all they survey—it’s 1.2; in Australia—which has also reached a record low—it’s [projected to hit](https://www.sbs.com.au/news/article/how-is-australias-population-changing/5a2uxjpbt) 1.4 this year. And [in South Korea](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2026/feb/25/south-korea-birth-rate-increase-rise-echo-boomers) —which has one of the lowest fertility rates in the world—it’s 0.80. What unifies the phenomena across countries is the fact that the drop began relatively recently and has been unrelenting in its downward trend.

Leaders around the world are very worked up about all this. They have a list of dire consequences that will befall the world if the population continues to decline. Disappointingly, the main one seems to be worker shortages. There is a lot of pious talk about how our old folk—our precious elders! The waning of the boomer ages!—will not have enough workers to care for them. But aren’t our old folk already beta testing the robots that will keep them company, listen to their endless stories, and call 911 when they go dark?

However, the people who make the robots are concerned about the falling birth rate, and that’s understandable. What if you created hell but there was no one left to burn in it? Peter Thiel has investigated the idea of lowering the cost of IVF treatment through medical arbitrage, potentially starting a golden age of “global fertility travel.” (“Honey, what would you say to 14 days in Phnom Penh?”) In 2022, he invested in a company [developing an app](https://techcrunch.com/2022/08/23/28-seed-thiel-capital/) that would—at last!—teach women about menstruation, the first step of which is entering the date of their last periods. ElonMusk is concerned that we will not have enough people to protect the “infrastructure,” that public good on which the chthonic world of dot.com depends.He’s also fearful that without a steady population, “civilization will die.” What civilization, exactly? Hot Cheetos, OnlyFans, and illiteracy?

I knew having children would be the best part of my life, and it *was* the best part of my life.

Because population demographics are based on the behavior of women and not of men, world leaders are working double time to persuade more of them to serve the state with their wombs. In the developed world, enticements have included bonuses, extremely long, paid parental leave, housing stipends, and debt forgiveness. [In Hungary](https://www.elfac.org/hungary-prime-minister-viktor-orban-announced-a-seven-point-family-protection-action-plan/), parents can qualify for significant reductions on the price of a seven-seater van. Imagine the happy Hungarian woman, popping her six children into her seven-seater van and saying “I couldn’t do *this* in a hatchback!”

In America, something called “the pronatalist movement” has emerged, its followers encouraging women to have as many children as possible. The most famous practitioners of the philosophy are a couple of world-class weirdos named [Malcolm and Simone Collins](https://www.thefp.com/p/i-want-people-to-have-more-kids), who left the fields of venture capitalism and tech to go live in rural Pennsylvania, multiply, and preach the good word on what they call “techno-puritanism.” They say that all of their decisions are “data driven,” including the one to [use corporal punishment](https://www.businessinsider.com/pronatalist-parents-dad-slapped-toddler-face-2024-5) on their children and to have pre-implantation embryos genetically tested for quality. So far they have five children, all of them aged six or under, who play in the background of many of the couple’s endless, strange TikToks.

Meanwhile, the current administration has put their best men on the job. DonaldTrump has declared himself the “ [fertilization president](https://people.com/donald-trump-nicknames-himself-fertilization-president-womens-history-month-11704347) ” and says that he’s eager for a new baby boom (forgetting the conditions that preceded the last one, among them the surrender of Germany and victory over Japan). He has considered [$5,000 bonuses](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/trump-baby-bonus-5000-5k-2025-white-house/) for each birth, but— *la plus ça change* —he hasn’t cut any checks. He’s also been presented—by, who else, the Collinses—with the idea of creating a National Medal of Motherhood for women who have six or more children, but bringing women with that many children to the White House sounds like a Wile E. Coyote–style ICE operation. One certain way to increase a country’s population is through immigration, but that’s not the direction we’re going.

“There is no human emotion as powerful as the love between mother and child,” writes Caitlin Flanagan. (Ed Lallo via Getty Images)

J.D. Vance (the fertilization vice president?) has warned us about unmarried cat ladies and announced, “I want more babies in America.” Thinking and acting locally, he and Usha Vance [have announced](https://x.com/SLOTUS/status/2013712959608946721) that they are expecting a fourth baby in July. Apparently Usha Vance told him he could either run for vice president or have a fourth child, but as he said to an NBC reporter: “Ladies and gentlemen, I am persuasive, because I got both.” In 2023, Vance [told the country](https://www.usatoday.com/story/news/politics/elections/2024/09/26/jd-vance-car-seats/75390401007/) that “there’s evidence that the car seat rules that we’ve imposed... have driven down the number of babies born in this country by over 100,000.” That “evidence” comes from a corker of a white paper called “ [Car Seats as Contraception](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3665046),” which claims that women tailor their family size to how many car seats their vehicles can fit, and that many sedans can’t accommodate three of them. It’s a perfect Republican equation: It’s not the climate, it’s the regulation.

None of it has worked. It seems the problem is more complicated than simple policy: The Nordic countries are rightly hailed as having the most expansive social welfare programs and the greatest gender equity and the most family-friendly policies in the world, but they’re busy depopulating themselves off the map. ([Finland](https://stat.fi/en/publication/cmfkupzc2jvng07w0rtr0p5xb): 1.30; [Norway](https://www.ssb.no/en/befolkning/fodte-og-dode/statistikk/fodte): 1.48; [Sweden](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/SPDYNTFRTINSWE): 1.43.)

Young women often say that a main reason they won’t have children is climate change and the devastating and perhaps unsurvivable future those children would be born into. In 2019, Prince Harry said he and Meghan Markle had decided they would only have two children for this exact reason—but at that point she was 37 and had only had her first, which seemed like a more relevant point. I can understand wanting an answer that shuts people up. Tell people that you’re not planning to have children and you’re in danger of the harpies and their dark prediction of “You’re going to regret that!”

Climate, however, is becoming a less universally accepted rationale. “I unequivocally reject, scientifically and personally, the notion that children are somehow doomed to an unhappy life,” Kate Marvel, a climate scientist at Columbia, told Ezra Klein in his widely circulated *New York Times* essay “ [Your Kids Are Not Doomed](https://www.nytimes.com/2022/06/05/opinion/climate-change-should-you-have-kids.html).”

Who cares about the miserable moments? They were my sons.

This issue of depopulation has only recently come to the attention of the left. In 2024, Jessica Grose of *The New York Times* told readers to “ [Stop Panicking About the Birthrate](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/08/07/opinion/birthrate-child-tax-credit.html),” although she admitted that low fertility does come with problems, one of which is that “as a society, we need future workers to support the economy and pay into programs such as Social Security.” MAGA wants babies to turn the capitalist wheel; the liberal left wants babies to keep the entitlements funded.

By the following year, Grose wasn’t as sanguine about the population drop, admitting, in a piece called “ [Don’t Let Natalists Define What’s Family-Friendly](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/05/21/opinion/pronatalist-trump.html),” that there doesn’t seem to be any “public policy lever” that can solve the problem, a bone-chilling pronouncement for any leftist. Down but not out, she offers some “bespoke” suggestions: “Maybe in a state like South Dakota a family-friendly policy would include tweaking the car seat law or improving the quality of rural roads, but in New York City, Los Angeles, or Chicago, local government could incentivize [building apartments](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2024-10-31/from-brooklyn-to-santa-monica-courtyards-make-apartments-more-kid-friendly) with courtyards.” The courtyards sound nice, but letting red-state babies fly through windshields seems counterproductive.

Perhaps it should go without saying, but the reason conservatives are defining what’s family-friendly is that the other camp isn’t especially friendly about families. “All of my friends who have children are in hell,” Chappell Roan [recently said](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kzLrHTEvVfE&t=172s) on *Call Her Daddy*. The organ of the anti-natalist left is without doubt *New York* magazine, whose writers force themselves to slog through the stroller-clogged paths of Prospect Park to report on why nobody’s having babies. Earlier this year, it published what is currently the apogee of the form, a piece called “ [Stories from Real Women Who Regret Having Children](https://www.thecut.com/article/women-regret-having-children.html).” Publication of the inevitable “Stories from Women Who Drop Their Children at Dog Rescues and Have Never Felt Better” is not far off.

In [another](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/09/16/opinion/anastasia-berg-falling-birth-rates-liberals.html) *[Times](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/09/16/opinion/anastasia-berg-falling-birth-rates-liberals.html)* [piece](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/09/16/opinion/anastasia-berg-falling-birth-rates-liberals.html), University of California, Irvine philosophy professor Anastasia Berg lays it out straight: “Today, having children has become the kind of thing that you need to justify to yourself and to others.” However, for there to be any human future at all, “some people have to be having children” (or, as Michael Jackson put it, “You wanna be startin’ somethin’ ”)and “if you want those children to share in the values that you yourself hold, you probably want some of those people to be the kind of people that you yourself are.” You can’t argue with that: Do you want a future where no one at all is interested in global international feminism? Who’s going to decolonize knowledge if you don’t go through hell for 18 years raising someone who will do it?

The people I worry about are the young women who want babies, or who are interested in babies, but all they get is the acrid howl of the Mommy Wars. But if you need a justification, how’s this for one: The reason women choose to have babies is that they know, on as deep a level as it’s possible to know anything, there is no human emotion as powerful as the love between mother and child. Across time and place, it’s recognized as the strongest—by far—bond between people.

Women don’t owe anyone a baby, and they’re not “selfish” if they plan a different life for themselves. They would be selfish if they had a child and put their own needs above his, but they’re not selfish not to have one. Being a mother is an experience that will hand you plenty of sleepwalking mornings and endless nights. But you can listen to a mother cataloging all of her complaints and then—in a very different tone, and with no awareness that she’s just undermined the argument—she will suddenly slip into one of the great unifying topics of motherhood: that her children are growing up too fast, that every morning when she lifts him up from his crib he seems a tiny bit bigger, that the photographs of just a month ago already look so different from the photographs of this morning.

---

Here’s the absolute truth of the thing: One day you drop your children off for the first day of kindergarten, and by the time you drive back to pick them up, they’re finishing sixth grade.

My children’s elementary school had a graduation ceremony at the end of sixth grade. It was held in the morning, and everyone came back at night for a big party and dinner. For some reason I put myself on the cleanup committee. By the time that finished up, the boys and I were some of the last people at school.

One of the boys was standing by the front door, and when he looked out the window, he saw that the playground—the big playground topped with a slightly bouncy green surface, the place where every day began and ended—was completely empty.

“Let’s walk around it!” he said. He had no sense of a valedictory. He just wanted us to see what it was like in the nighttime when we had it to ourselves. For some reason the moon was intensely bright, which isn’t often the case in LA.

We did, and I was filled with the kind of emotion that hits you so powerfully and so unexpectedly that you think you might break down crying. He was in high spirits because he didn’t yet know the story of life, didn’t understand that doors are always closing and parts of yourself are left behind them forever.

We walked around the playground that one time and then we found his brother, and the three of us got in the car and drove home and never went back there again.
