A Jewish pickpocket from London's East End got shipped off to Canada at 16 because his family had genuinely run out of ideas. He'd broken windows at night so a glazier could repair them in the morning. He'd been in and out of reform school. He was, by most accounts, a disaster. A parents nightmare.

His name was Moishe Cohen, later known as Morris "Two-Gun" Cohen.

By the time he died, the Chinese government paid for his headstone. It was inscribed in English, Hebrew, and Chinese by the widow of China's first president.

In between those two points, his life was more like something a out of a movie...and he wasn't necessarily playing the good guy.

He landed in Canada, picked up dice, cards, and gunslinging from a cowhand named Bobby, and decided that real estate was a more respectable hustle than pickpocketing.

He also developed a fierce loyalty to the Chinese immigrant railroad workers around him, men who were treated as disposable and reminded him, in ways he never had to explain out loud, of Jews he'd known in London.

That loyalty brought him face to face with Dr. Sun Yat-sen, the revolutionary who would become China's first president. When two thugs attacked an elderly Chinese man in a cafe, Moishe threw them out. The elderly man was Sun. The friendship that followed changed everything.

He became Sun's bodyguard, arms buyer, and aide-de-camp. He negotiated weapons deals in Yiddish with a Chinese warlord who'd learned the language while studying abroad. He rose to the rank of general in the Nationalist Army, reportedly the first Jew and almost certainly the first Westerner to hold that position. He took a bullet in his right arm during a skirmish, decided that relying on one functional hand was a liability he couldn't afford, and started carrying two pistols everywhere he went. One in the shoulder holster, one on the hip.

That's where the name came from.

When the Japanese seized Hong Kong in 1941, Cohen stayed behind to get Madame Sun and her sister onto one of the last planes out. Then he waited, because there was nothing else to do. He was arrested immediately, hauled into the infamous Stanley Internment Camp, beaten repeatedly, and dropped sixty pounds over two years of captivity. He told people later that at his lowest point, lying in that prison, he found himself reciting the Shema. The prayer he remembered from a childhood that felt like it belonged to someone else entirely.

He survived. A Red Cross prisoner exchange got him out in 1943.

In 1947, the United Nations was voting on whether to even allow a debate about partitioning Palestine into a Jewish state and an Arab state. Five members of the Security Council. One veto would end the conversation before it started. The US, the Soviet Union, and France were in favor. Britain wasn't going to veto.

That left the Republic of China, and by all accounts, China was headed toward a no.

Zionist leaders in San Francisco tried to meet with the Chinese delegation. They were turned away.

Someone made a phone call to Montreal.

Cohen flew to San Francisco, reached into his coat, and pulled out a letter he'd been carrying for 27 years. He sat down with General Wu Tieching, the head of the Chinese UN delegation. Cohen hadn't just known Wu. He had appointed Wu a general. The letter was from Sun Yat-sen, written in 1920, expressing his personal support for the Zionist cause and the restoration of a Jewish homeland.

China abstained. The vote passed.

In 1948, Israel was born.

Both Chinas, still at war with each other, sent representatives to his funeral. His Manchester tombstone carries his name in three languages. The Chinese inscription was written by Soong Ching-ling, Sun's widow and Vice Chairman of the People's Republic of China. She identified him by the name the Chinese had always used for him, Mah Kun, meaning "clenched fist."

He wasn't a simple man. He stretched the truth when it suited him, loved a good party, and once had his family sit shiva for him after a rumor spread that he'd been executed in China. He turned up in London two months later with no explanation.

But when the moment arrived that would determine whether the Jewish people would have a state, Morris "Two-Gun" Cohen was in the room carrying Sun's letter and calling in a debt that went back decades, and knew exactly what to do.
