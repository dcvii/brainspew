Growing hostilities between [Saudi Arabia](https://www.wsj.com/topics/place/saudi-arabia) and Yemen’s Iran-backed Houthi militia are raising the threat to a crucial oil-export route that the kingdom has been using to work around the blockage in the Strait of Hormuz.

A pipeline routing oil west across the country to a port on the Red Sea has allowed it to keep up export volumes during the U.S.’s war with [Iran](https://www.wsj.com/topics/place/iran). From there, its tankers sail through the chokepoint at Bab al-Mandeb and out to its main markets in Asia, stabilizing prices as shipping in the Persian Gulf has been bottled up by Iranian attacks.

So far, Bab al-Mandeb has been [kept open](https://www.wsj.com/world/middle-east/irans-houthi-allies-lie-in-wait-on-another-key-oil-route-the-red-sea-9c0c38f5?mod=article_inline) by Saudi diplomacy with the Houthis, whose intermittent attacks crippled Red Sea shipping on and off over the past three years. But a 2022 truce underlying those efforts is breaking down.

The Houthis [fired drones and missiles](https://www.wsj.com/world/middle-east/new-saudi-yemen-fighting-threatens-to-pull-houthis-into-iran-war-6d7aa26e?mod=article_inline) at a civilian airport in southwestern Saudi Arabia after the militant group accused the kingdom of bombing an airport runway in the Houthi-controlled capital of San’a this week to prevent an Iranian plane from landing.

Then on Thursday, the Houthis’ reclusive leader doubled down, lashing out at Saudi Arabia in an angry televised speech. “The real equation is this: San’a airport for Riyadh airport,” Abdul Malik al-Houthi said. “The equation is [airports](https://www.wsj.com/topics/industry/airports) for airports, seaports for seaports, and blockade for blockade.”

The militant leader threatened to target Saudi oil facilities if Riyadh, which led a coalition against the Houthi insurgency more than a decade ago, goes back to war in [Yemen](https://www.wsj.com/topics/place/yemen). He also called on his supporters to rally on Friday to protest restrictions on free movement in and out of the country, which the Saudis and their Yemeni allies have imposed for years. The Houthis are expected to test Saudi resolve again by trying to land another Iranian plane in San’a soon.

While al-Houthi said little to indicate an imminent return to attacks against ships transiting the Red Sea, officials and analysts assess that the regional dynamics could still lead the militants back to taking shots at ships passing by Yemeni shores.

“The situation in the Red Sea is once again deteriorating,” Kaja Kallas, the European Commission’s head of foreign and security policy, said Friday during a visit to Djibouti, which supports Western efforts to guard the Red Sea. “Recent Houthi missile attacks against Saudi Arabia, breaking a four-year truce, are a warning that instability on land quickly becomes insecurity at sea.”

The last Houthi shutdown of traffic drew [an American military response](https://www.wsj.com/world/middle-east/navy-houthis-maritime-war-5517a127?mod=article_inline) in 2025 that battered the group. The attacks nevertheless continued sporadically and snarled shipping for months.

This time, any disruption would happen while transit through the Strait of Hormuz is getting choked off by [Iranian attacks](https://www.wsj.com/world/middle-east/the-battle-for-hormuz-trump-shifts-into-dangerous-new-phase-in-iran-war-eb19979a?mod=article_inline). Consulting firm Capital Economics predicts that a prolonged, simultaneous closure of the waterways—or significant damage to Saudi pipeline or port infrastructure—could push the global economy into a recession.

The war with Iran and its stranglehold on Hormuz have taken millions of barrels of oil a day off the market. Global consumers have made up for the gap by pulling down inventories, but they will eventually run low.

Workarounds have bought some time. Saudi Arabia has used the pipeline running across the Arabian Peninsula to export about four million barrels a day of crude oil via the Red Sea, keeping its overall exports around 4.6 million barrels a day. That is down from 7.3 million barrels a day before the war, but the gap would be a lot wider without the Red Sea barrels.

Analysts say the Houthis feel strategically emboldened by a perception that Iran has outmaneuvered the U.S. and see an opportunity to gain an advantage by pressing the Saudis, who have strong incentives to keep Yemen out of the regional conflict.

“The Houthis smell this, they know this,” said April Longley Alley, a former senior political adviser to the United Nations’ special envoy to Yemen. “They see an opportunity to up the ante, and they’re going to take it.”

The two sides now appear closer to resuming at least low-level hostilities than at any time since [reaching a truce in 2022](https://www.wsj.com/world/middle-east/yemen-parties-agree-to-two-month-truce-11648849524?mod=article_inline) that ended virtually all cross-border attacks following seven years of fighting.

The agreement was meant to trigger steps including an easing of Saudi restrictions on fuel entering Yemen’s Red Sea port of Hodeidah and a resumption of commercial flights in and out of San’a, but there has been little progress.

The U.S. supported the Saudi-led campaign to intervene in Yemen’s civil war against the Houthis starting in 2015. Analysts say Washington will support Saudi Arabia but has its hands full with Iran and will try to avoid another direct engagement in Yemen.

U.S. and Saudi officials spoke earlier this week about Yemen, and the State Department approved the sale of some $2 billion worth of weapons to Saudi Arabia, including more than 20,000 kits for precision-guided munitions.

The immediate flashpoint for further violence is likely to remain San’a airport, which holds symbolic value for both sides. Saudi Arabia worries that loosening restrictions will allow Iran to resupply the Houthis with weapons and military advisers.

Analysts say the Houthis, under pressure at home from skyrocketing prices and dwindling supplies of basic necessities, are motivated to force open the air route.

“For them this is a domestically driven calculation to get a better deal that gets them more resources, more autonomy, more freedom to consolidate their control in Yemen and in the Red Sea basin,” said Alley, who is now a senior fellow at the Washington Institute think tank.

The Houthis have long bristled at Saudi-imposed restrictions on movement in and out of Yemen. [The funeral](https://www.wsj.com/world/middle-east/iran-isnt-just-eulogizing-a-leader-its-sending-a-message-221c97e5?mod=article_inline) this month of slain Iranian leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei gave them an opportunity to test Riyadh’s appetite for fresh confrontation.

A Houthi delegation flew to Tehran two weeks ago, drawing protests from the head of Yemen’s internationally recognized government, who lives in exile in Riyadh. The delegation’s return this week was diverted to another Houthi-controlled city after San’a airport was bombed.

If another flight is attempted and Riyadh strikes San’a airport, the Houthis are likely to ratchet up their response, which in turn could prompt the Saudis to retaliate more harshly despite their eagerness to avoid exposing their homeland to counterattacks.

Mohammed al-Basha, founder of Basha Report, a Washington-based risk-advisory firm, said the Houthis publicly updated their rules of engagement this week, saying Saudi Arabia risks renewed conflict if it tries to stop new flights from Iran.

“Abdul Malik does things gradually,” he said of the Houthi leader. “It’s an escalation ladder. He doesn’t go full in. He doesn’t want full war.”

One major factor that drove Saudi Arabia to agree to the 2022 truce was Houthi drone and missile strikes on the kingdom’s cities, including Riyadh, which jeopardized the ambitious economic development plans of Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman.

Renewed Houthi attacks now would likely be even more damaging. Saudi air defenses have improved, but so too have the Houthis’ offensive capabilities, and the kingdom’s development efforts have been [weighed down by cost overruns](https://www.wsj.com/world/middle-east/saudi-arabia-futuristic-vision-reality-3245a8b5?mod=article_inline) and the political risk brought on by the war with Iran.
