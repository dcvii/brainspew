Happy American Independence Weekend and Semiquincentennial (250th) Celebration, y’all. To be clear, what’s being celebrated each year on July 4th is neither the founding of the country per se, nor the winning of the war, nor the establishment of the government. Quite literally and simply, we’re celebrating the Declaration of Independence—known as the unanimous Declaration of the thirteen united States of America in the original printing. A founding document, but not the only one.

On July 4, 1776, the Declaration was made and announced unanimously by the Second Continental Congress, who were convened at Pennsylvania State House, later renamed Independence Hall. These delegates became known as the nation's [Founding Fathers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Founding_Fathers_of_the_United_States). The Declaration explains why the United States were—though we would now say “is”—to be regarded as independent sovereign state-provinces under a new federated local structure; no longer subject to British colonial rule.

In honor of that, we’re gonna take a closer look at the ten men most widely considered to be the essential names on the sometimes unwieldly list or umbrella of “Founding Fathers.” We’re also gonna unpack their legacies with a critical eye, including their more unsavory aspects. For this reason, I’ll be included a metric I’ve devised just for this purpose: the Slavery Score. More on that in a bit.

## Who Were the Founders, Really?

When historians talk about “the Founding Fathers,” they’re referring to a specific subset of the roughly 200-plus delegates who participated in the Continental Congresses and Constitutional Convention between 1774 and 1787. Not all of them get equal billing. Some showed up for a few months, signed their names, and went home. Others shaped the entire ideological and legal architecture of the nation.

The ten we’re examining here are the ones that every serious historical accounting includes—the ones you genuinely cannot tell the story of American independence and early governance without. They’re the irreducible core, the names that appear on the Declaration, on the Constitution, in the Federalist Papers, and in the foundational diplomatic agreements that made the United States a recognized sovereign nation.

As well, these men were products of their time—a time when slavery was not just legal but economically central to entire regions, when women had no political rights, when other sociocultural minorities or “lessers” faced real persecution, and when the idea of universal human dignity was radical enough to be dangerous. Most of them did not fully live up to the lofty values they signed onto. Most of them were wealthy (comparatively speaking), landed gentry. Some got rich from slavery, from profiteering, even from the war and the founding or expansion of the nation itself

A few tried vehemently to distance themselves from the ills of their time, as well as to encourage the better angels of their countrymen. But nearly all of them benefited from the emerging status quo (slavery, limited voting rights, and so on), whether directly or indirectly. We can acknowledge their genuine accomplishments—the intellectual courage required to declare independence, the political ingenuity that created a functional federal system, the rhetorical power of their ideals—without pretending they were all equally upstanding moral exemplars.

## Rating Systems

Part of my irreverence is gonna come through most especially with my use of metrics. One is the **Slavery Score**, a number designed to measure each Founder’s relationship to slavery across multiple dimensions. The other is their Indispensibility Grade, a letter indicating how unique, impactful, and truly foundational they were to this country.

One the first, I’ll say upfront that I’m trying to assess whether they enslaved people (and how many); whether they freed people during their lifetime, or in their will; whether they *actively promoted* or opposed the institution; and whether they used their political power to advance abolition or merely expressed private misgivings. The scale runs from 1 to 9. A score of 1 represents an absolute abolitionist who never enslaved anyone. A score of 9 represents a proto-Confederate who actively defended and promoted slavery as a positive good. (Admittedly, none of the core ten score a 9, tho some of their comtemparies obviously would have.)

Most of our ten land somewhere in the middle. Enslaving people, expressing discomfort in their private writings, taking minimal or no action to dismantle the system, and dying with enslaved people still in their possession. The score exists to make the moral math visible. It is one major dimension, perhaps the biggest dimension in some accounts, of where the rubber met the road with their principles.

Slavery was a human universal for millennia, and I don’t wish to hide the ball on that or over-emphasize it as some rare, American sin. However, I think it would be just as dishonest to write off the practice, particularly the chattel (livestock-like) form it took from the Enlightment onwards, as just “how things were.” The great tragedy of American slavery in particular is that the Founders often knew it wrong was, knew it conflicted with their stated values (the ones they founded this country on), and still did it anyway. That’s less a nuanced, complicated “product of one’s times” and more a self-aware sinner who prayed for absolution whilst condemning himself and others.

### Slavery Score Scale

- **1**: Absolute abolitionist, zero slaves owned, campaigned to end the institution
- **2-3**: Anti-slavery stance, non-slaver or early freedom-giver
- **4-5**: Enslaved people but shifted toward abolition, took some action (freed in will, founded abolition societies)
- **6-7**: Enslaved people, condemned in words, did little in deeds, died still enslaving
- **8-9**: Enslaved people, promoted slavery actively, paved way for the Confederacy

---

As for the **Indispensability Grade**, it measures how uniquely a Founder shaped the nation’s origins and early trajectory. It asks: could the republic have taken its essential form without this person, or did they contribute something singular that altered the course irreplaceably?

The scale runs from A to D. An **A** **grade** represents someone the founding simply doesn’t work without—the load-bearing walls of the republic. **Grade B** is critical and important but fulfilling a role that someone else could taken on, albeit B’s are often more elegant or operate with different emphasis. A **grade C** is significant and contributed meaningfully but wasn’t essential to the basic architecture. And finally, a **D grade** signifies the barest minimum needed to be considered a founder.

## John Adams

John Adams’ actual contributions to the nation came through diplomacy, where he was arguably the founding generation’s most effective operative. He spent a decade abroad serving Congress as minister to France, Holland, and Britain. In 1783, working alongside Benjamin Franklin and John Jay, he negotiated the Treaty of Paris, securing generous terms from Britain that included full recognition of American independence and territory stretching to the Mississippi. Both Adams and Jay distrusted Franklin’s deference to the French Foreign Minister, so they negotiated a separate peace with Britain directly—a bold move that secured better terms and equilibrium between the three countries, with America cemented as nobody’s puppet or proxy.

The tragedy was that Adams was never suited for the personal side of politics, despite his intellect and principle. He was brilliant but blunt, educated but tactless, principled but thin-skinned. He alienated colleagues in France and Congress through his combative style. He had few people he could genuinely converse with. His aloofness and refusal to engage in political compromise—virtues in principle—made him isolated and ineffective as president. He lost reelection in 1800 to Jefferson and spent 25 years in retirement, angry about it. He was proud that his son John Quincy became president; the Adams family was the first were father and son both became president. Yet the elder Adams never quite forgave history for not viewing his generously.

**Slavery Score: 2**

Never enslaved anyone, opposed slavery throughout his life on principle, refused to support immediate abolition but endorsed gradual change. His only weakness was political—his failure to push harder for immediate change. But his personal ethics never bent to accommodate the system, unlike his contemporaries.

**Indispensability Grade: B**

Adams was critical but not singular. His principles were fierce, yet his actual governance as president was hampered by his temperament, and his most important contributions were diplomatic functions another capable figure could have performed. He shaped early American foreign policy, but he didn’t invent the republic’s structure the way Madison did or define its ideological purpose the way Jefferson did.

## Benjamin Franklin

Benjamin Franklin presents a puzzle that Americans have never quite wanted to solve. He was, by almost any measure, the most polymathic figure of the founding generation—printer, writer, scientist, diplomat, inventor, statesman. He signed more key documents than anyone else. He was the only founding father to achieve genuine international celebrity, celebrated in Paris as a philosophical sage while still writing bawdy letters home. Yet he arrived at prominence almost by accident, never pursued the heights of power even when offered, and left a legacy so full of contradiction that it reads like it belongs to at least two different men.

Before he was famous, Franklin was a man without serious position. He was a printer and a writer. He published a newspaper, an almanack full of witticisms, and printed paper currency for the colonies. In semi-retirement, he took up new hobbies in his 40s, proving lightning was electrical via a kite in a thunderstorm. Suddenly, he became the most famous American in the world. Yet his personal life was a wreck of casual betrayal. He fathered an illegitimate son with an unknown woman, brought the boy into his household, and his common-law wife—Deborah Read, whom he’d proposed to at 17 before abandoning her for London—raised the child without complaint.

He was restlessly unfaithful throughout their decades together. He frequented brothels throughout his life, and advised friends on how to sleep with more women. His sexual appetites were described by contemporaries in language that would not appear in print for centuries. He was a man who viewed women primarily as objects for seduction, whose approach to sexuality combined entitlement with contempt. One infamous story described him throwing a young couple out of his home because the bride refused his advances whilst the husband was away.

On slavery, Franklin’s arc is more redemptive but still incomplete. From at least 1735 through the 1750s, he owned at least seven enslaved people. His Pennsylvania Gazette ran slave sale advertisements and bounties for escaped slaves. But by the late 1750s, he began publishing anti-slavery pieces by Quaker writers like Benjamin Lay, writing privately against the institution, and shifting his personal position. During his eight years in France (1776-1785), he was surrounded by abolitionist thinkers and intellectuals who reinforced his evolving conscience. When he returned to America, he became publicly vocal. At 81, in 1787, he accepted the presidency of the Pennsylvania Society for Promoting the Abolition of Slavery. Three years later, at 84, weeks before his death, he petitioned Congress to ban slavery.

The petition was fierce: Congress had the power under the general welfare clause to act, he argued. Slavery contradicted the very Constitution that had just been ratified. Pro-slavery congressmen attacked him viciously, called him senile. The Senate took no action. But Franklin had used his final public act to push the question of slavery into the national discourse at the founding moment. He died two months later. Yet even his abolitionism was complicated. He never freed a single enslaved person during his lifetime, though he made one condition in his will; his slave Bob would be emancipated by his heirs. He helped shape what America could be, even if he couldn’t live up to those ideals himself.

He also, crucially, refused the heights of power. He was offered the governorship of Pennsylvania and turned it down. He served as President of Pennsylvania (an office practically equivalent to governor) for three years but stepped down as soon as his constitutional limit was reached. After the Revolution, he was more celebrated than Washington. He could have seized more authority. He chose not to. For a man so full of appetite in other domains, his restraint around political power is notable.

**Slavery Score: 4**

Better than most men of his era, and even many on this list. But his abolitionism is sometimes overstated to the point of hagiography. Franklin was a man of his times in ways simultaneously good and bad, just as he was also exceptional by that same token. It is worth remembering the full extents and limits of both.

**Indispensability Grade: B**

Franklin was critical to American success but not singular. He became the most celebrated American personality in Europe, helping build our national character in the minds of those overseas. His scientific credibility gave him influence no other American had. Yet he was not structural to the founding the way Washington, Jefferson, or Madison were. He was influential, but he was not the peak.

## Alexander Hamilton

Alexander Hamilton was the most brilliant and most arrogant founding father—a lethal combination that made him indispensable as a technician and impossible as a politician. He came from nothing (born illegitimate in the Caribbean, orphaned young, apprenticed to a merchant clerk at 14) and clawed his way to Washington’s side through sheer intellect and relentless ambition. Washington trusted him in a way he trusted almost no one else, and Hamilton’s first instinct after the Revolution ended was not to rest but to build something permanent: a financial system that would bind the nation together through debt, currency, and credit.

His financial architecture was genuinely visionary. He assumed the states’ Revolutionary War debts and consolidated them under federal authority—a move that deliberately bound wealthy creditors to the national government through self-interest. He created the Bank of the United States, modeled on the Bank of England, to standardize currency and manage federal funds. He proposed tariffs and excise taxes to create federal revenue streams. He envisioned an industrial economy competing globally, not an agrarian republic of farmers and virtuous small-holders. His reports to Congress were monumental achievements of systematic thinking. He had the mind of a futurist and the temperament of a tyrant.

Here’s the problem: he couldn’t stop being a contrarian. Hamilton was vain, combative, tactless, and constitutionally unable to back down from a fight. He feuded with Adams, humiliated Burr, maneuvered against Jefferson, and treated most of Congress with barely concealed contempt. He believed *self-interest* was the primary mover of human action—and he acted accordingly, using his Treasury position to dispense patronage, build political networks, and advance his agenda regardless of democratic niceties. He believed in a strong executive bordering on monarchy. He distrusted democracy itself. When given the chance to suggest a form of government at the Constitutional Convention, he proposed something closer to an elected king.

Hamilton did maintain a high-minded opposition to slavery, and it was far stronger than most of his peers, but it had limits. He likely briefly owned slaves himself—an 1796 account entry mentions purchasing two enslaved people. He was a founding member of the New York Manumission Society, yet he attended meetings infrequently and seemed to view the society primarily as a networking opportunity for the wealthy elite. His moral conviction wasn’t false so much as utilitarian, a cynicism that ran through much of his political and social life, quite unfortunately.

By the 1790s, Hamilton had effectively become Washington’s prime minister, controlling Treasury, foreign policy through manipulation of his boss, and the development of the entire financial apparatus of the new government. When Washington left office, there was talk that Hamilton might run for president. Intellectually, he had the capacity. Practically, he had already disqualified himself through his personality. He had made too many enemies. Congress distrusted him. The South feared his centralizing tendencies. Adams, who became president, resented him bitterly. Even Hamilton’s allies found him exhausting.

Hamilton was the kind of brilliant man who wins arguments and loses elections because he couldn’t *stop* fighting or let his opponents save face in defeat. He left government in 1795, spent the rest of his life writing pamphlets and litigating, and died in a duel with Aaron Burr in 1804—a death that was almost inevitable given his inability to back down from a confrontation or admit error.

**Indispensability Grade: B**

**Slavery Score: 3**

## Patrick Henry

Patrick Henry needs introduction because, despite being called “the voice of revolution” by his own era, he’s been largely erased from the popular memory of the founding. This is partly historiographical accident and partly his own fault. Why he’s essential to the canonical list: Henry gave the founding generation something they desperately needed in 1775—permission to fight. The Continental Congress was deadlocked between those who wanted independence and those still hoping for reconciliation with Britain. Moderates were afraid of what armed rebellion would cost.

Then Henry stood in St. John’s Church in Richmond on March 23, 1775, and delivered a speech so electrifying that it swung the Virginia Convention toward arming for war. The exact words were reconstructed decades later by his biographer William Wirt (based on recollections by a man who was there), which means we can’t be certain of the precise phrasing. Purportedly, this was where the words “Give me liberty or give me death” originated. Whatever the transcription, the effect was immediate and real: the convention adopted his amendments by a narrow margin. Less than a month later, the first shots were fired at Lexington and Concord. Henry’s initial words had made the Revolution inevitable, first in Virginia; and then, beyond him, throughout America.

What’s less famous: Henry spent the rest of his political career opposing almost everything the founding generation built. He refused to attend the Constitutional Convention, allegedly saying he “smelt a rat.” He feared the Constitution created a presidency that would be monarchy in all but name. He lost that fight—barely—and then spent the 1790s working to limit federal power and promote states’ rights, laying ideological groundwork that would eventually justify Southern secession. To his allies in the North, he became proof that the South couldn’t be trusted. To Jeffersonians, he was a provincial obstructionist. To posterity, he became almost invisible.

Thomas Jefferson, who outlived Henry by 25 years, spent decades bad-mouthing him to everyone who would listen. Jefferson was still criticizing Henry to guests at Monticello in 1824, into his 80s and mere years before his own death. Since Jefferson got to control the historical narrative through his own voluminous papers and correspondence, and since Henry left almost nothing in writing (he was a speaker, not a writer), Jefferson’s acidic assessments stuck. Modern historians have tried to resurrect Henry, but the damage was done. We remember one speech, not the man.

On slavery, Henry was characteristically contradictory. He owned enslaved people from age 18 onward and accumulated roughly 80 over his lifetime. He professed slavery was wrong—calling it a “lamentable evil” and “inconsistent with the Bible”—and wrote in 1773 that he couldn’t justify it even to himself. “I am drawn along by the general inconvenience of living without them,” he confessed. Yet the number of slaves he owned increased over time, especially after his second marriage in 1777 added a dozen more to his holdings. By the time he died in 1799, he owned 67 enslaved people. He never freed a single person during his lifetime. His will distributed them among his heirs with no condition of emancipation.

**Slavery Score: 8**

**Indispensability Grade: C**

## Thomas Jefferson

Thomas Jefferson is the American Founder most internally inconsistent, most intellectually brilliant, and most destructive through that very brilliance. Before he reached the presidency he was a dilettante, bad with money, a man coasting on inherited wealth and political positions without real accomplishment. His record as governor of Virginia was so weak that he left office under a cloud. He accomplished nothing of real legislative value before ascending to the presidency. He was a violinist, a wine collector, an architectural enthusiast, a scientist who tinkered at problems without solving them. He was charming and endlessly curious but had no proven capacity to actually run anything.

His marriage to Martha Wayles Skelton appears genuinely to have been happy. By most accounts, she was as skilled at piano as he was at violin. But Martha died in 1782, a decade into their marriage, leaving Jefferson a widower at 39. By some point around five years later, Jefferson made sexual overtures to Sally Hemings, the house-slave who had accompanied his daughter Maria. Hemings was in her very early teens, he was in his mid-40s. The affair, such that one could call it that, was either started or heightened in Paris, France when Jefferson was ambassador.

Under French law, Hemings could have claimed freedom. Instead, he negotiated her return to Virginia, with promises of a good life at Monticello and eventual freedom for her (their) children. What happened in those years in Paris something dark: a man who had lost his wife finding comfort in a woman who, per reports, physically resembled Martha Jefferson; a teen girl who was his legal possession, who had no real choice but to comply, regardless of what she may have felt. The relationship lasted 38 years. She bore him six children. He freed none of them during his lifetime, despite promises. He instead kept them as house slaves until their late teens or 21st birthdays, then made allowances for them to “escape” with no pursuits or recriminations. His will added that Sally’s children be freed after his death, for whatever that was worth.

As a businessman and investor, Jefferson was atrocious. His debts were catastrophic and mostly self-inflicted. Jefferson inherited about 135 enslaved people and 11,000 acres from his father-in-law John Wayles when he married Martha. He also inherited Wayles’ debts. But that was just the beginning. He was, frankly, a compulsive spender with zero sense of financial reality. His mansion Monticello was a perpetual money pit of renovation and architectural refinement. He acquired 6,000 books—an extraordinary library but ruinously expensive. He collected fine European wines at rates equal to a full 40% of his presidential salary. He co-signed loans for friends, some in the tens of thousands of dollars; and in 1818 one such friend died without repaying, leaving Jefferson responsible. He lived on credit his entire life, using his enslaved people and western lands as collateral.

Becoming president was a momentous achievement, and it did sharpen his political instincts greatly. He was a sound statesman, a deft leader, and a master of political transactions, including expansions of the United States territories and the ushering in of new states into the union. He even made reducing the national debt his stated priority. But despite all this, he could not control his personal spending. By the end of his second term in 1809, he estimated himself deeper in debt than when he began. He died with roughly $107,000 in unpaid debts—over a million dollars in today’s money. His grandson spent 50 years of his life paying it off.

The core contradiction was this: Jefferson hated debt philosophically. He believed it corrupted freedom, whether attached to individuals or nations. Just like he hated slavery, which he railed against in his writings despite running arguably the largest slave plantation of any man on today’s list. At his core, Jefferson’s optimistic nature masked chaos. He was hopelessly delusional about the actual profitability of his plantations, as he was about his own capacity to follow the better angels of his own nature. On his deathbed, he expected a lottery ticket to save everything. **Jefferson’s capacity to live with contradiction was, as one biographer put it, “** ***epic*****.”**

As for Native Americans, Jefferson’s record was worse than callous—in was **corrupt** and, again, *highly* contradictory. He wrote that Native peoples were equals in mind and body, which was genuinely progressive for the time. He was intellectually fascinated by their languages, cultures, and origins. He displayed Native artifacts from the Lewis and Clark Expedition in his home. But as a political actor, his fascination curdled into something far worse. He saw Native lands as an obstruction to his vision of American expansion. And he did whatever was necessary to quell them.

Using the very financial pitfalls he’d experienced himself as a guide, Jefferson crafted a deliberate debt-trap policy designed to dispossess indigenous tribes. Near the end of his presidency, as resistance grew, he offered tribes only two choices: assimilation or obliteration. He drove westward expansion and set in motion the removal policies that would culminate in the Trail of Tears under his successors. The man who was fascinated by Native peoples as intellectual objects helped destroy them as living populations.

**Slavery Score: 7**

**Indispensability Grade: A**

## James Madison

James Madison was the architect—the man who thought most systematically and comprehensively about how to build a republic that would actually work. Before the Constitutional Convention, while other delegates were still uncertain about what needed changing, Madison spent months in Montpelier’s library studying centuries of political philosophy, analyzing the failures of past republics, and drafting what came to be known as the Virginia Plan. It was a comprehensive framework for a three-branch government with a bicameral legislature, with checks and balances, designed so that each branch could constrain the others through reciprocal ambition.

When the Virginia delegation arrived in Philadelphia in May 1787, Madison had already set the intellectual terms of debate. His role went beyond presenting a plan. He attended every day of the convention, took meticulous notes (the only comprehensive record we have of the debates), and worked the floor constantly—not through oratory or charisma but through relentless intellectual argument. A Georgia delegate noted that Madison “always comes forward the best informed man of any point in debate.” He was small and frail, physically unimpressive, and he never dominated through force of personality. He dominated through the weight of having actually thought through the problems nobody else had time to think through.

When compromises needed to be made, he understood the constitutional implications of each concession in a way nobody else did. After the convention, while others rested, Madison co-wrote the Federalist Papers with Hamilton and Jay—his contributions, particularly Federalist No. 10 and No. 51, remain the definitive explanation of American constitutionalism. He then pushed for the Bill of Rights, which he initially opposed as unnecessary but came to see as essential for ratification. He drafted it, guided it through Congress, and in the process refined the Virginia Plan’s structure to include explicit protections for individual liberty.

For a man of his intellectual power, Madison had remarkable flexibility—he could see when he was wrong and could change his position without the ego that would have required him to defend the indefensible. He wrote extensively about how slavery was incompatible with republican principles. He called it a moral evil. He worried about slave insurrections and the danger it posed to Virginia’s stability.

Yet Madison enslaved over 100 people at Montpelier and freed exactly zero of them during his lifetime. He inherited the plantation from his father in 1801, along with about 100 enslaved workers, and he held them for the remaining thirty-five years of his life. He considered colonizing freed slaves in Africa—an unworkable fantasy that let him avoid actual emancipation. Facing financial pressure as farming income declined, he sold sixteen enslaved people to a cousin in Louisiana who owned sugar and cotton plantations, breaking up families to cover his debts.

Madison’s presidency (1809-1817) was consumed with foreign crises and war with Britain. He was competent but not transformative. His true legacy was the Constitution itself—a document that has lasted longer than almost any other written constitution in the world. Yet that document was built on land cleared by enslaved people, maintained by the wealth that enslaved people generated, and signed by a man who knew slavery was wrong and did nothing to free those he held.

**Slavery Score: 6**

**Indispensability Grade: A**

Remove Madison and the Constitution doesn’t exist in recognizable form. He didn’t write it alone—nobody could have—but he designed the framework that every other delegate worked within. Without him, the Constitutional Convention likely produces a weaker document that doesn’t survive the first serious test.

## George Mason

George Mason drafted the Virginia Declaration of Rights in 1776—the document that established the principle that “all men are by nature equally free and independent” and first protected freedom of press, religious tolerance, trial by jury, and prohibition of unreasonable searches. Those words reverberated through the Declaration of Independence, the Bill of Rights, and future governing bodies inspired by America. He was one of five most vocal speakers at the Constitutional Convention, deeply respected by his peers. Yet he walked out without signing the Constitution.

Mason refused to sign for two reasons: the absence of a bill of rights and the Constitution’s failure to abolish slavery. He said he would “sooner chop off his right hand” than sign it as written. His complaint that “there is no Declaration of Rights” filled Anti-Federalist newspapers and became the most powerful argument against ratification. Some historians have called the omission “the greatest blunder” in Philadelphia. Mason then led the Anti-Federalist opposition in Virginia’s ratification convention, nearly derailing the entire project. Washington was furious—Mason’s refusal likely cost him their decades-old friendship.

Yet Mason enslaved dozens of people and opposed the foreign slave trade not from principle but from pragmatism—he thought ending the trade wouldn’t disrupt vested interests, and he wanted to keep enslaved people already in the country from being sold away. He never freed anyone during his lifetime. The ideological power of his Declaration of Rights created language that would eventually be used to abolish slavery, but he wasn’t willing to act on his own stated principles.

Madison noted that Mason believed a bill of rights could be drafted in hours. It wasn’t—it took four years and a ratification crisis. When Madison finally introduced the Bill of Rights in 1791, it was drawn primarily from Mason’s Virginia Declaration. Mason was satisfied, but he died a year later without signing anything.

**Indispensability Grade: C**

**Slavery Score: 6**

## Roger Sherman

Roger Sherman signed all four great founding documents (Continental Association, Declaration, Articles of Confederation, Constitution) and was the only person to do so. At 66, he was the second-oldest delegate at the convention after Franklin. He was terse, a poor speaker, and a man who made no written record of his thoughts. Yet he made 160 motions or seconds during the convention—nearly as many as Madison.

He solved the fundamental crisis that nearly broke the proceedings: the conflict between large and small states over representation. His Connecticut Compromise (also called the Great Compromise) created the bicameral Congress that still governs America—the House with proportional representation, the Senate with two seats per state. Without this compromise, the convention likely collapses and nothing.

Sherman opposed slavery intellectually and wanted only free inhabitants counted toward representation. He pushed for the language that restricted the slave trade and eventually allowed Congress to ban it in 1808. Like Madison, he worked on the now derided Three-Fifths Compromise, treating enslaved people as partial entities which gave slaveholding states more representatives than they would have had counting only free inhabitants, yet less than slavers were pushing for.

Sherman died in 1793, shortly after the Constitution was ratified. He left almost no papers—mostly mundane financial documents—which is partly why he’s been overshadowed by more voluble figures. But the structure of Congress itself is his legacy. His personal life was modest and respectable enough not to need much ink.

**Indispensability Grade: B**

The Great Compromise was essential. Remove Sherman and the Convention likely fractures. Yet he didn't design the foundational architecture the way Madison did. He solved a specific crisis that was preventing agreement on Madison's framework.

**Slavery Score: 3**

Never owned slaves, opposed slavery on principle, actively worked against its expansion while pragmatically compromising for constitutional unity, helped pass Connecticut's gradual abolition law. The score reflects genuine opposition backed by personal refusal to benefit from the institution, even as political necessity forced compromises that unintentionally strengthened slaveholder power.

## George Washington

George Washington was, by many every measures, truly exemplary. Both for his day and in retrospect. His leadership relied on resilience, a calm demeanor, and strategic prowess coupled with discipline and a long-term vision, and his leadership earned the US military victories in the Battle of Trenton and the Siege of Yorktown and, eventually, independence from the British Empire. Arriving in Cambridge, Massachusetts to take formal field command on July 2, 1775, Washington held command until independence was won, resigning his commission to the Continental Congress in Annapolis, Maryland, two days before Christmas in 1783.

At every stage of his military and politial careers, he accepted power reluctantly and gave up it before he was asked to. His sense of honor and self-restraint was legendary. As was Washington’s ability to employ unconventional tactics, such as surprise attacks and night operations, which distinguished his military leadership. He learned from early setbacks to become one of the war’s genuinely indispensable figures. The army survived in the freezing cold at Valley Forge because Washington shared in the deprivation alongside his troops.

His anti-authoritarian stance actually shaped the nation. After being elected the first President of the newly ratified United States federal government, Washington accepted re-election only once and refused to run again. He feared that if he remained in office for too long, it would set a dangerous precedent that could encourage future presidents to hold onto power for life, undermining the principles of republican governance. His voluntary action to serve only two terms has been described as one of the nation’s great acts of statesmanship.

He modeled himself on [Lucius Quinctius Cincinnatus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Lucius-Quinctius-Cincinnatus), the Roman who surrendered power when crisis ended. By leaving the presidency after two terms, Washington hoped to establish a tradition of voluntary retirement that would discourage the kind of political entrenchment and authoritarianism he had fought against during the American Revolution. This precedent went unchallenged until President Franklin Delano Roosevelt won a third term in the 1940 election. For 150 years, an unwritten rule held, because George Washington had decided that power was something you relinquished. He was offered a crown, informally, and he refused. That refusal echoes still.

On slavery, Washington’s private evolution is better documented than his public performance, and the gap between them is precisely where his moral failure lives. Washington became privately regretful about it, but continued the practice into his death. Throughout the 1780s and 1790s, Washington stated privately that he no longer wanted to be a slaveowner, that he did not want to buy and sell slaves or separate enslaved families, and that he supported a plan for gradual abolition in the United States. This sounds good until you ask the obvious question: why not just free them? The answer is that Washington prioritized his own financial security over the freedom of the enslaved population under his control, and he had spent a lifetime acquiring an impressive estate, and he was extremely reluctant to give it up except on his terms.

As a political figure, Washington also prioritized national unity over the abolition of slavery. He never made a public statement against slavery. He never used his extraordinary moral authority—the one thing that could have mattered—to push Congress toward abolition. He even circumvented laws that would have freed his enslaved workers if they did manage to escape to neighboring states.

What he did do was still remarkable for his era, at the end: his will provided for the eventual emancipation of the enslaved population owned by him, one of the few slave-owning founders to do so. He freed all his slaves by 1781 in his will—though his wife Martha was instructed to wait until her own death—and stipulated that all the freed people under the age of twenty-five must be educated. He also insisted that Black people had a right to live on American soil rather than face forced exile.

**Slavery Score: 5**

Washington enslaved hundreds, freed 123 in his will (the only such large-scale bequest among the slaveholding founders), and expressed private moral opposition he never publicly advanced. He had the power to act and didn’t. The score reflects a man genuinely conflicted but unwilling to sacrifice his wealth or political capital on the thing he claimed to believe was wrong. The anti-authoritarian self-sacrifice he showed toward power itself makes the moral cowardice on slavery sharper—not softer.

**Indispensability Grade: A**

Washington was a figure without whom the country collapses. He held the Continental Army together through Valley Forge when it would have disbanded. He refused to become a military dictator when offered a crown instead of a limited executive office. He set the precedent that the presidency was a temporary office, not a throne, by voluntarily stepping down after two terms—a decision that shaped American governance more profoundly than any constitutional clause. He didn’t write the Constitution or articulate the nation’s ideological purpose, but he was the load-bearing wall that held everything up long enough for those institutions to take root.

## James Wilson

James Wilson is another forgotten founder—a man of genuine intellectual brilliance who signed both the Declaration of Independence and the Constitution, served on the Supreme Court, and left his fingerprints on the structure of the presidency and the judiciary in ways most Americans have never heard of.

He was the principal architect of the Committee on Detail, which filled in the constitutional framework that Madison outlined. He designed the electoral college system after months of wrestling with the problem of how to elect a president. He pushed for direct popular election in both houses of Congress at a time when that was genuinely radical. He understood human equality and its implications better than most of his peers—he was the only delegate to link the Declaration’s language about “all men are created equal” explicitly to the Constitution during the ratification debates. Yet Wilson is barely remembered.

Part of this is bad luck: unlike other founders, his surviving papers are mostly mundane financial documents and contracts, not the political writings that shaped history. Part of this is that his life ended badly. Crippling debts from wild land speculation consumed his final years. He spent the end of his life fleeing creditors, even as a Supreme Court Justice. He died in 1798 at 55, broken by financial ruin while still serving on the bench.

On slavery, Wilson opposed it. He had the intellectual clarity to see it was wrong. But during the convention, he stayed relatively quiet on the issue, fearing it would break apart the fragile coalition needed for ratification. He proposed and advocated for the Three-Fifths Compromise—but he did so as an anti-slavery measure designed to give the South less representation than it wanted, not to dehumanize enslaved people. He argued that counting slaves at three-fifths would penalize slaveholding states by denying them full political power in exchange for their enslaved population. He also pushed hard for the language in the Constitution that allowed Congress to prohibit the slave trade after 1808, calling it “laying the foundation for banishing slavery out of this country.” In the Pennsylvania ratification debates, he took satisfaction in what had been achieved: “If there was no other lovely feature in the Constitution but this one, it would diffuse a beauty over its whole countenance.”

Yet Wilson enslaved a man named Thomas Purcell for 26 years. He freed him in 1794 at his wife’s request. It is a gap in his conscience and his record, but far less gross or extreme than most of his contemporaries. A contradiction, like others in his life, but a limited and remediated one in the grand scheme.

**Indispensability Grade: C**

**Slavery Score: 4**

## Closing

Fundamentally, I wrote this piece to unpack the legacies of several important men. Some names you already knew, a few you likely didn’t. Many you might have assumed were less complicated, or less self-aware, than we sometimes like to give them credit for in hindsight. I tried to strip the mythology from the men without denying the significance and the lasting impact. They were, all of them, imperfect. But they were also the building blocks of our core documents and the country itself.

I have my favorites amongst them, but tried not to let that overshadow my work here. I have my criticisms, but likewise refused to let rote recrimination be the order of the day. Hagiography and cruel denigration are equally bad because they hamper our ability to see clearly. Worse yet, many who claim to ride this line well end up taking one side of it still. I’d like to think I’ve done better than most on that score.

These men, these literal and metaphorical Fathers, are ones we all owe something to, but what that something *is* …we so often get wrong: we owe them recognition, yes; we owe them remembrance (even and especially the names which history sometimes forgets); we owe them a measure of patience and understanding; and yes, despite what some may claim or pretend, we owe them fair review, even firm criticism. Just like we owe the same of this nation as a whole.

*Huzzah for America, Long live the Republic.*
