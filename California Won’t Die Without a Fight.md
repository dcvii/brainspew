One-party rule won’t last forever. Tuesday’s primaries showed that a large share of Californians are desperate for an alternative to the state’s progressive governance.

***In Tuesday’s primaries in California, the outsiders were on the rise: Republican Steve Hilton vaulted to the top of the governor’s race with nearly 28 percent of the vote, ahead of expected front-runner Xavier Becerra. And in Los Angeles, former reality star Spencer Pratt looks set to finish second in the mayor’s race, edging out far-left city councilmember Nithya Raman and setting up a fight with incumbent Karen Bass. What do the results say about the state of the Golden State? That’s the subject of today’s Big Read, by Peter Savodnik.***

In California’s jungle primary, it’s the Revenge of the Bourgeoisie.

With 62 percent of the statewide vote counted as of Wednesday afternoon, Republican Steve Hilton leads the pack of would-be governors, and with 69 percent of the city vote tabulated, Los Angeles mayoral hopeful Spencer Pratt is closing in on Mayor Karen Bass and outpacing über-progressive Nithya Raman.

The message from a growing and very vocal contingent of voters is crystal clear: They want the crazy to stop.

The homeless encampments, the meth heads, the ancient infrastructure, the plummeting test scores, the stratospheric cost of everything—they’ve had enough. They hate that this place that they love, which was once the epicenter of the bold and the new, feels like a rusting, bloated, zombified shadow of its former self.

---

“It’s not just Republicans,” Hilton told me a few hours before the election returns started coming in last night. He was in Huntington Beach, just south of LA. “It’s a lot of Democrats and independents. Something’s happening in California. People are done with it. They’re done with the chaos and the crime and the squalor. There’s a level of rage that is very, very palpable.”

And they want a return to the old swagger, the old Ray-Ban cool, the California that imagined big and built big and knew that the rest of the world was dying to come here.

A little before midnight, on Tuesday, Pratt’s campaign [posted a photo](https://www.instagram.com/p/DZHP3HvDkL2/?img_index=1) of him and his wife, Heidi Montag, on Instagram that flicked at that old California: He was in an all-black, jacket-and-jeans getup; she was in an all-white jacket and miniskirt, with her long, blond mane. There was a security detail and an SUV, and she was wearing a small, gold cross. Both Pratt and Montag starred in the reality-television show *The Hills* two decades ago—they were known as Speidi—and there was, about them, a certain crassness, a glibness, a lack of polish, and Pratt’s voters seemed to love that. It made him real, at least, in their eyes. The image was more than a little ridiculous, a little Trumpy, but it captured the Van Halen/Beach Boys/Westside-of-Los-Angeles California, that Pratt’s voters miss.

“You two represent what LA was at its peak,” someone named Natalia posted. “Let’s bring it back, Pratt!!! Love you both.” Lots of fire emojis. Lots of heart emojis.

The message from a growing and very vocal contingent of voters is crystal clear: They want the crazy to stop.

To be crystal clear: These voters are not in an ideological mood. This is not about MAGA or a return to the old Ronald Reagan conservatism, and both Hilton and Pratt, who have been endorsed or sort of endorsed by Donald Trump, will spend the next several months trying to persuade millions of voters who loathe the current White House that they have nothing to do with it. Hilton stressed, more than once, that he’s running a “practical and positive campaign.”

His first-place finish and Pratt’s strong second-place finish (that could change, as more last-minute ballots are tallied) are about the most basic functions of government: making sure no one is shooting up or defecating or having sex on a sidewalk; paving roads; policing parks and bedroom communities; graduating high-school students who can read and do math.

It’s about shutting down the progressive laboratory that California has become over the past few decades: [the ban](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?bill_id=202320240SB1174) on local officials requiring ID at polling booths, the ban on school officials telling parents that their kids are transitioning (that was blocked by the Supreme Court), the exorbitant income tax and gas tax and every other tax, the new “ [ICE alert](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?bill_id=202520260SB98) ” in schools, which gives students’ parents or guardians a heads-up if Immigration and Customs Enforcement agents turn up on campus. And on and on.

Hilton sounded mystified that, after 16 years of progressive control of all major levers of government in California, his Democratic gubernatorial rivals were still running on what he regards as a hopelessly outdated worldview.

---

“It’s very obvious that they are completely out of touch and haven’t come to terms with the failure of their ideology,” he said. “They just cannot accept the scale of their failures.”

And yet.

Few, if any, of the politicos—the consultants, the campaign managers, the bundlers and activists and messaging gurus—expect Hilton or Pratt to prevail. This is California! In 2024, [Kamala Harris trounced](https://www.cnn.com/election/2024/results/california/president) Donald Trump by 20 points. Democratic gubernatorial candidates, in total, received far more votes than Hilton did. ICE is kryptonite! Trump is kryptonite! “ ‘Republican’ is a dirty word in California,” a former Republican state officeholder told me. “That’s a very steep climb for these two.” She meant Hilton and Pratt, the latter of whom, to be fair, is not running as a Republican; the mayoral race is nonpartisan. But still. As Raman put it, Pratt is “mini-Trump.”

That’s why, even with the fires, even with Hollywood exiting the state, even with the Silicon Valley heavyweights moving to Texas or Florida or overseas or (one day) Mars, even with all the rot, the fentanyl, the fecklessness, the corruption, the billions in government waste, large numbers of Democratic voters vote for the party cog, whoever that is. Powered by a mix of anti-Trump fear, inertia, and the muscle of the unions representing the teachers and other government employees, the Democratic juggernaut soldiers on. It will not run the state forever—it wasn’t *that* long ago that California elected Republicans, including conservatives, to the governor’s mansion—but for now, it’s very hard, if not impossible, to imagine its demise.

Few, if any, of the politicos—the consultants, the campaign managers, the bundlers and activists and messaging gurus—expect Hilton or Pratt to prevail.

As of early Wednesday morning, Hilton had just shy of 28 percent of the vote—about 1.4 million voters. Right behind him was former Health and Human Services secretary Xavier Becerra, at 25.4 percent, with a little less than 1.3 million voters. Billionaire hedge-funder Tom Steyer, who spent $213 million of his own money to come in third place, was at 20 percent.

“I expect establishment Dems will coalesce around Becerra,” David Crane told me. He runs Govern for California, a nonprofit that seeks to limit special-interest influence. Privately, other Democrats have told me Becerra is “affable,” “nice,” “vanilla,” “ineffective,” and “the anti-change agent.”

Crane added: “A good number of non-establishment Dems plus a good number of NPPs”—no-party-preference voters, or independents—“could be supportive of Hilton.”

Meanwhile, in LA, Bass, who has been widely criticized for her mishandling of the January 2025 Pacific Palisades fire, which destroyed Pratt’s home, was leading on Wednesday afternoon with 35 percent of the vote—compared with Pratt’s 30 percent. In third place was Raman, an LA city councilmember, with about 22 percent of the vote.

When we last spoke in May, [Pratt told me](https://www.thefp.com/p/spencer-pratt-la-mayor-race-voter-anger) he was “very confident” of “winning outright” on primary night. That didn’t happen, but he did appear to squash any hopes that Raman had of prevailing.

Mike Bonin, a former LA city councilmember whom one Democrat called “the godfather of LA progressives,” urged patience. “We count slowly,” Bonin told me. “One thing to keep in mind is what we see tonight”—election night—“is probably not what we’re going to see when the votes are done. Democrats have been voting late.”

A Democratic strategist in LA added that younger voters “never pull the lever in the polling booth—they do drop-off, day of, and we haven’t collected those ballots yet.” On primary night in 2022, billionaire developer Rick Caruso, a former Republican, led Bass by five points, and he ultimately lost by seven, he pointed out. “That’s a 12-point swing. The difference between Nithya and Pratt is eight.”

Still, on Wednesday morning, progressives were feeling dismayed. They had seen Raman [break down](https://nypost.com/2026/06/03/us-news/far-left-la-mayoral-candidate-breaks-down-in-tears-as-she-falters-behind-spencer-pratt-and-karen-bass/?utm_medium=social&utm_source=twitter&utm_campaign=nypost) at her election night party. They knew the math was not on their side.

It wasn’t just Raman who was losing. It was the angry, vaguely radical progressivism that she represents. Last month, Raman, an MIT-trained urban planner who is often compared to New York City mayor Zohran Mamdani, sat down for a one-hour interview with radical-chic Twitch streamer Hasan Piker—he who went to Cuba on an anti-American publicity tour and whitewashed the Chinese regime’s innumerable crimes against humanity and declared that 9/11 was just desserts. During the interview, Raman, sounding as if she were being subjected to a purity test, agreed with Piker that Israel—which has become progressives’ defining issue—had committed “genocide” and is an “apartheid” state.

On Wednesday morning, progressives were feeling dismayed.

Late Tuesday, Piker told his viewers that, as he watched the election returns come in, he felt “ [blackpilled](https://x.com/thestustustudio/status/2062067089209258419?s=20) ”—hopeless. He was outraged that Hilton was on top and Pratt was coming on strong.

It wasn’t just Raman or the governor’s race. In San Francisco, Piker’s candidate of choice in the race to replace retiring Congresswoman Nancy Pelosi—Saikat Chakrabarti—came in third behind longtime state senator Scott Wiener and Connie Chan, who sits on the San Francisco Board of Supervisors. Just weeks ago, Chakrabarti—a tech entrepreneur worth as much as $200 million who served as Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez’s chief of staff— [appeared at a rally](https://www.instagram.com/reel/DYGDXzbiM1d/?hl=en) with Piker, who showed up in a black tunic reminiscent of Mao Zedong.

Democratic San Jose mayor Matt Mahan, who, as of early Wednesday, was in sixth place in the gubernatorial primary, told me his campaign had “elevated” the policy debate.

But the future of the Democratic Party—a party that, like the Republican Party, appears to be searching for itself, pulled in wildly different directions by radicals and moderates and identitarians—is uncertain.

Mahan, who lived down the hall from Mark Zuckerberg when they were at Harvard, said he’s unsure when, if ever, Democrats will steer back, away from the old politics, toward a more reform-minded program that is less tribal, less anti-innovation. “I can’t predict when things will get to a point where voters are fed up enough to demand it,” Mahan said from his campaign headquarters.

He said that he had run against “the establishment” in San Jose, just as fellow Democratic mayor Daniel Lurie had run against it in San Francisco. He seemed to be referring to the likes of Becerra and California governor Gavin Newsom and the whole Democratic apparatus that helped push Becerra into second place. Mahan added: “I think our cities are beginning to give people hope.”

---

He sounded not that dissimilar to Pratt or Hilton, who imagine themselves helping to overthrow the bureaucracy that, they believe—that many Democrats acknowledge, privately if not publicly—is suffocating the California that was and could be.

“The tool kit we needed to address the injustices of the ’60s and ’70s is not what we need today,” Mahan said. “Then, we needed to address civil rights and environmental protection. These were core needs that required a very strong administrative state with a robust judicial system and a body of law and an ability to sue polluters and use regulation to protect rights. That tool kit was very successful, but in today’s world, where we have market failure—we’ve broken the insurance market, we’re seeing more and more corporate headquarters leave the state—I just feel like that tool kit is mismatched to the problem set.”

Caruso, who had considered running again for mayor or even throwing his hat in the gubernatorial ring, said he was “optimistic” about things—despite Mahan, whom Caruso endorsed, not coming out on top. He suggested that Mahan might run again. “Matt’s not going anywhere,” he told me.

When I told him what I had heard from countless Angelenos—that they might consider supporting Pratt if they knew Caruso was quietly advising him—he laughed.

“You know,” Caruso said, “I ran into Spencer a couple of weeks ago, but it was just hello for a few minutes—we saw each other at a restaurant.” Caruso hadn’t endorsed anyone in the primary, and he wasn’t sure what he was going to do now. “I’m not going to predict the future on whether Spencer can win or not win. I’ve supported the city no matter who the mayor is. Let’s see how this unfolds.”
