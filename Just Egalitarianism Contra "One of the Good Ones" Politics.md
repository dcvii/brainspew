I’ve been searching for a while now for the sentence that explains what I’m doing. Not the content beat, not the bibliography or byline list. Not the policy positions and political rankings I keep in my notes. No, *th* e sentence—the thing that makes an essay on race eliminativism, a defense of AI-assisted writing or art, a critique of compelled speech, and an investigation into police misconduct all cohere as one project rather than a scattering of disparate impulses. How I can unify my field of inquiry, go beyond the veil of “content creator” (said descriptively more than pejoratively) who has “takes.”

I found it, of all places, in the intellectual history of Zionism. Not in its geopolitics, which I’m not here to litigate today, but in its founding psychological insight: that there is something irreducibly degrading about begging bigots for rights. The early Zionists looked at centuries of Jewish apologetics—the endless refutations of blood libels, the performances of civic loyalty, the careful cultivation of the “good Jew” who might finally be granted what he was *owed* all along—and concluded that the entire enterprise was a trap. Not because the refutations were false, but because trap was structural and total: every answer submitted to the antisemite’s court **confirmed the court’s jurisdiction**. The plea itself was the concession. Zionist's’ answer was to stop auditioning for their own right to exist. And I now join them.

For the refusal is portable. It doesn’t belong to one people or one century. It’s the load-bearing move beneath every emancipation movement. It’s the subtext of Egalitarianism, that beauty framnework I’ve called “ [the notion without a name](https://edokwin.substack.com/p/the-excellence-of-egalitarianism).” But it is, in fact, ***worth*** **the name** —and its central move is one I intend to make, and keep making, across everything I write.

Today I will tell you what I’m doing and why.

---

## Audition and Tribunal

Here is the structure, stated once so I can point back to it forever: bigotry operates as a courtroom. The bigot appoints himself judge, places your identity on the docket, and offers you a deal: *perform innocence*, and perhaps you’ll be granted standing. Prove you’re not like the others. Distance yourself from the statistics. Be articulate, be grateful, be legible, be one of the good ones—and your rights, which were never his to grant, will be provisionally extended, subject to revocation at the next news cycle.

The audition is unwinnable by design. That’s not a bug in the arrangement; it **is** the arrangement. A verdict that must be re-earned daily. Because it is not a verdict, not really; it’s a leash. And the person who spends his life performing for that court—however skillfully, however sincerely—has accepted the one premise that should never be accepted: that what he is belongs on a docket at all. That their approval matters.

So the first commitment of Just Egalitarianism is jurisdictional, not argumentative. I don’t rebut the identity tribunal. I deny that it’s valid as a court. What you are—your ancestry, your complexion, your sex, your orientation, the wattage of your inborn intelligence, every accident of birth and formation—is **permanently,** **universally** ***off the docket***. No one carries original sin on immutable grounds, and no one carries original virtue either. There are no “good ones” because there was never a legitimate, majoritarian category of “bad ones” to be sorted out from. The dichotomy was always false. No one was born wrong, no one must beg mercy from bigots.

This is a denial of the bigot’s self-appointed judgeship, and their fellow travelers’ existence as a social court. The distinction matters because it changes what answering looks like. When a libel is in the air, you can still put the truth on the record—not as tribute paid to the libeler, but as a free fact for everyone else listening. “Born this way” was always a legitimate rebuttal to indictments that presumed choice, conspiracy, and predation. It only curdles when it’s offered as a plea, when it concedes that the trait would need excusing if it were chosen. Rebuttal, yes. Tribute, never.

## Genetic Fallacy Wears New Clothes

If identity tribunals only tried people, this framework would be narrower than it is. But the same fallacy that puts persons on trial for their origins…puts *works* on trial for how they were constructed, and one of my running projects is to name and fight the newest version of it. Bigotry everywhere will be brought to heel. So that we as a culture can heal. Can learn, can grow, can thrive. Without bogus verdicts based on zealotry or empty, fallacious arguments regarding effort or craft.

I will address one form head on now. I call it **synthphobia**: the reflexive conviction that anything touched by artificial intelligence is presumptively fraudulent, worthless, or corrupt. (A common slur is “slop,” which I will hencefire try to avoid using.) The synthphobe believes that a work is guilty until its parentage is proven pure. Like a child being submitted to a genealogy test for citizenship. Watch the pattern. A story wins a prize; someone runs it through a detector; the mob demands the author prove a negative. An essay is good, therefore suspicious. A writer discloses using a tool and is asked, in effect, to demonstrate that the work is really his—to audition for the standing his sentences should have earned on their own.

This is the genetic fallacy running at industrial scale, and it’s structurally identical to the older bigotries. The work is not read; the origin is interrogated. Merit is not assessed; provenance is policed. And the accused is offered the same rotten bargain: perform innocence, disclose, apologize, submit to the detector, and perhaps we’ll deign to evaluate what you actually made on terms other than how you made it. I decline that bargain on behalf of the work for the same reason I decline it on behalf of persons. Judge the sentences. Judge the argument. Judge the result. The docket has no line for ancestry—not for authors, and not for their tools.

I’ve addressed this before and I’ll be elaborating on it at length in the months ahead, because I think synthphobia is going to be one of the defining prejudices of this decade, and because almost no one is describing it as what it is: not a quality standard, not a labor position, but a purity regime. Run by bigots and zealots, high on their own supposed moral license. Not unlike those who would burn books in the name of “decency,” or would burn symbols in front of people’s homes and places of worship to scare Othered minorities into compliance or exile.

## Bigotry as Central Ill

Let me be precise about the enemy, because “bigotry” gets used loosely and I intend to use it properly. *Bigotry* is judgment of persons by essence rather than conduct—stereotype in lieu of character assessment, verdicts rendered on what someone or something is instead of what someone *does* and the truth value of claims. It is the central ill of political life. Not one problem among many, but the root system from which the others grow. And its two most durable lieutenants deserve naming, because they often travel under respectable cover.

The first is authoritarianism, in both of its flavors. As collectivism, it dissolves the person into the class, the bloc, the demographic—your interests read off your category, your guilt assigned by membership, your standing contingent on the group’s standing in someone’s grand account. As particularism, it dissolves the person into the tribe—rights for us, sanctions for them, the ingroup exempted from the standards it enforces on the outgroup. Both are bigotry, with a governing philosophy attached. Both put identity back on the docket and call it loyalty, prudence, or civilizational salvation. Both fail the one test that matters: symmetry. Any standard I won’t apply to my own side is not a standard, it’s a self-serving weapon.

The second is sacralism, and this one hides better because it doesn’t need a god. Sacralism is the elevation of any proposition beyond the reach of scrutiny—the recreation of blasphemy law, whether the protected object is a religion, a gender metaphysics, an ultra-nationalist manifesto, or a racial cosmology of inherited guilt. Its signature moves are compelled speech and original sin. When you are required to *affirm it* —to recite the acknowledgement, to use the mandated formula, to confess the inherited stain of your ancestors or your appearance—you are witnessing sacralism at work, and it does not matter one bit whether the liturgy is supposedly conservative or purportedly progressive. Secularized original sin is still original sin. Compelled affirmation is still compelled speech. A society that forbids the state from establishing religion and then lets any ideology establish itself by mandate has changed the vestments and kept the inquisition.

Against all of this, the commitment is simple and I intend to hold it with an almost tedious consistency: claims are always scrutinizable, immutable characteristics never are. Every religion, every ideology, every political program, every sacred cause submits to criticism on the merits. Every person’s essence stays off the docket. The bigot reverses this—shielding his dogmas while interrogating your nature—and the reversal is the tell. I will not let this kangoroo court persist.

## Release Valves — Free Association

There’s a piece of this framework that took me longest to see, and it concerns the destruction wrought by **disregarding** ***freedom of association***. A free society runs on pressure valves. Free speech is only sustainable because social opprobrium exists as its counterweight: the bigot may speak, the government may not compel or detain on speech alone, and the rest of us may make his views anathema as a matter of social accountability. This is what the real fathers and mothers of free speech absolutism (), rightly understood, were fighting for. Alexander Meiklejohn, Evelyn Beatrice Hall, et al wanted a check on government, not a demand on the public. This is what their illegitimate, would-be heirs never understood. We teach them.

Take away the opprobrium—demand that bigotry roam free of stigma, judged only “on the merits” as though irrational contempt and blood libels were merely discreet ideas to be debated ad nauseam—and you don’t get more freedom, you get a public that starts *begging* for censorship, because you’ve removed the only lawful outlet for its judgment. Stigma is what a free society uses instead of a censor. That’s the first valve. We must keep it clear, available, and well maintained.

The second valve is exclusion or separation, and we have spent two decades welding it shut. People need the lawful ability to say “this club, this space, this activity doesn’t include you,” and to have that be the end of it. The women’s gym, the men’s retreat, the ethnic fraternal order, the hobbyist circle with its own membership rules— **these are** ***not*** **the commons**. Public commerce and the state must be blind and open to all comers; that’s the floor, and it’s non-negotiable. But voluntary association ***above*** **that floor** must be free to be exclusive, because forced integration of voluntary life doesn’t produce harmony. We’ve run that experiment. The results are in.

The record of quotas, caps, and engineered composition reads the same everywhere: resentment, gaming of the system, social division, and a public that trusts its institutions, to say nothing of its fellow citizens, far *less* than before. When people are denied the small, lawful “no,” they stockpile the grievance and spend it somewhere worse. We need to let them say “yes” as a free choice, where “no” isn’t off the table.

And here is the symmetry that makes the valve a **principle** rather than a mere *convenience:* the excluded and the critics keep their valves too. The exclusive club is lawful; calling it contemptible is also lawful; the government takes no side. Nobody is conscripted—not *into* the association, not into approval of it, not into pretending to maintain an opinion they don’t hold. That’s what pluralism actually is. Not universal affirmation, but bounded liberty with the exits and the judgments left open.

## No Revanchism

One iron law, stated without hedging: **no revanchism.**

The answer to a century of identity tribunals is not identity tribunals under new management. The answer to misogyny is not misandry. The answer to racial castes is not a racial spoils system with the polarity flipped. Every movement that has tried to cure essence-judgment with reverse essence-judgment has kept the machinery and merely reassigned the defendants. But the machinery itself is the disease. Original sin does not become justice when the sinners change complexion.

This is also, not incidentally, the deradicalization position, and I want to be explicit about the mechanism because almost nobody in the anti-extremism space states it. Rigged auditions radicalize. The man who performs innocence and is convicted anyway learns the game is fixed; and those fixed games produce defectors—the recruiter’s entire pitch is “they will never accept you, so stop asking.” The recruiter need not be a bigot, but this pipeline is a goldmine for bigotries new and old.

A politics of permanent stain manufactures extremists as reliably as a factory built to purpose. The counter-program is stigma that is real but liftable: contempt for views and conduct, never for characteristics or origins; standards applied symmetrically; and **an exit that actually works**. Renunciation must clear the record. The reformed must be readmitted. Relitigating a retracted position should itself become bad form—double jeopardy for the repentant is just the audition with extra steps.

A society that stigmatizes ideas but forgives people produces ex-extremists. A society that stigmatizes essences produces lifers, whether they’re literally imprisoned or just culturally ghettoized. Forgiveness, it turns out, isn’t the soft option. It’s the hard one, both to do and in its compounding effects. It is a social counterterrorism move.

## What I’m For

Refusals alone make a crouch, not a life, so let me end with the affirmative.

I am for blindness where blindness is owed: the state, the law, the market’s open door, the standard applied without checking anyone’s identity at point of entry. I am for neutrality where neutrality is owed: government above the fray of association and conscience, taking no side in the lawful choices of free people. I am for merit as the aspiration that makes formal equality real. For criteria that are public, symmetric, answerable—and aimed at *the work* rather than the worker, with a crucial boundary kept: merit allocates opportunities, it does not rank souls. And I am for forgiveness as a civic discipline, because a society that cannot let people out of their worst chapter has decided to be governed by its grudges. I am for justice, not revanchism.

True justice is restorative and responsive, never preemptive, nor predatory. It repairs identifiable harms to identifiable people; it does not go hunting for victims where none exist, and it does not collect debts from the unborn on behalf of the dead. Revanchism is wrong not just because it’s vindictive, but because it prizes the same things the bigot prizes: total war, endless spoils, and othering of its enemies forever.

This is the ethos that will drive my nonfiction for the remainder of this year and, I expect, well beyond it. The investigations, the essays, the criticism, the fights I pick in the comments—one project. Every piece will be, in one register or another, an argument against the audition: against the tribunal that tries what people are, against the purity regimes that try what works are made from, against the sacralisms that exempt themselves from scrutiny while demanding your recitation, and against the counterfeit cure of revanchism. ***I will not submit.*** You shouldn’t either.

Nobody has to prove they’re one of the good ones. Not to the bigot, not to the commissar, not to the detector, not to me.

Court’s adjourned. Now we can talk about what you actually did, said, claimed, and made—which was always the only conversation worth having. So let’s have it! 👇🏿
