Viewed from a certain distance, all partisan brains have similar tendencies. They might claim to hold not only different beliefs, but diametrically opposed beliefs, but if you fervently believe in something — whatever that thing is — it puts you at risk of certain blind spots.

This is useful to point out because partisans often think that they and their side have unique access to the truth. Through their superior intellect, their uniquely visceral personal experience, or whatever else, they have transcended the limitations of our mere squishy head-computers and have gained access to certain empirical and moral truths that elude the rest of us.

One way of expressing this is via “horseshoe theory,” a term you have probably heard, [which Wikipedia describes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horseshoe_theory) as the belief that “advocates of the far-left and the far-right, rather than being at opposite and opposing ends of a linear continuum of the political spectrum, closely resemble each other, analogous to the way that the opposite ends of a horseshoe are close together.”

And of course, far greater thinkers than I have been pointing this out in different ways for a long time. “In pre-Hitlerian Germany it was often a toss-up whether a restless youth would join the Communists or the Nazis,” [writes Eric Hoffer in](https://dn721608.ca.archive.org/0/items/the-true-believer-eric-hoffer_202406/The%20True%20Believer%20%28Eric%20Hoffer%29.pdf?utm_source=chatgpt.com) *[The True Believer](https://dn721608.ca.archive.org/0/items/the-true-believer-eric-hoffer_202406/The%20True%20Believer%20%28Eric%20Hoffer%29.pdf?utm_source=chatgpt.com)*. Or, more generally and pointedly:

> Though they seem at opposite poles, fanatics of all kinds are actually crowded together at one end. It is the fanatic and the moderate who are poles apart and never meet. The fanatics of various hues eye each other with suspicion and are ready to fly at each other’s throat. But they are neighbors and almost of one family. They hate each other with the hatred of brothers. They are as far apart and close together as Saul and Paul. And it is easier for a fanatic Communist to be converted to fascism, chauvinism, or Catholicism than to become a sober liberal.

I had thought William Shirer also had a passage about this in *The Rise and Fall of the Third Reich* — about the ease with which true-believer Nazi and USSR officials got along — but when I tried to find it all I found was a section on Joachim von Ribbentrop’s [fateful trip to Moscow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molotov%E2%80%93Ribbentrop_Pact) that wasn’t quite what I recalled. When I asked X, [I was also pointed to](https://x.com/GeneHealy/status/2033279061053612291) an astonishing passage about that same trip in Paul Johnson’s *[Modern Times: The World from the Twenties to the Nineties](https://www.amazon.com/Modern-Times-Revised-Twenties-Perennial/dp/0060935502/ref=sr_1_1?adgrpid=185684973105&dib=eyJ2IjoiMSJ9.15mJwcRx97ZjuFlUiMq7fKsZbtwYCyANuw9RIMKglxKNJBrr6DNwubkoo6QlHtxcAtfkrwtNaUkolA2cmWGeOm29Vqee1UCiQinlOggEdMznT6rCIinZYZlg5ZFq4iesXCMKGm-VxQyxL2JnqZvsS4tP4oZkpg0WF2g27dZSv9yokbL7-7VAYjTAfYMXx1HcaurMEnbRpve0IW-bFGG9Jg_04NW7z3-DChOU9Cm8JbU.vBqjEUAmeM2SqC8cNfPEdpJ-TvZL_uaFq30MdGuEkb4&dib_tag=se&hvadid=779592066850&hvdev=c&hvexpln=0&hvlocphy=9032077&hvnetw=g&hvocijid=10304296070099335475--&hvqmt=e&hvrand=10304296070099335475&hvtargid=kwd-301991210611&hydadcr=22560_13821274_8360&keywords=modern+times+johnson&mcid=3458c8d13c8b3128aabd0fdfd0dd1ce1&qid=1773608476&sr=8-1)*, which I haven’t read:

> That night of 23-4 August there was a gruesome junket in the Kremlin. Ribbentrop reported: ‘It felt like being among old party comrades.’ He was as much at ease in the Kremlin, he added, ‘as among my old Nazi friends’. Stalin toasted Hitler, and said he ‘knew how much the German people loved the Führer’. There were brutal jokes about the Anti-Comintern Pact, now dead, which both sides agreed had been meant simply to impress the City of London and ‘English shopkeepers’. There was the sudden discovery of a community of aims, methods, manners and, above all, of morals. As the tipsy killers lurched about the room, fumblingly hugging each other, they resembled nothing so much as a congregation of rival gangsters, who had fought each other before, and might do so again, but were essentially in the same racket.

That’s just… (hopefully) interesting background. But I think a version of horseshoe theory also holds in much lower-stakes situations involving normal everyday people with fairly standard belief systems.

At the moment, a bunch of people on X are mad at a *New York Times* headline about Ayman Mohamad Ghazali, the Michigan man who attacked a synagogue. At one point the headline read “ [The Michigan Synagogue Attacker Was a Quiet Restaurant Worker](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/03/13/us/michigan-synagogue-suspect-ghazali.html) ” (presently: “A Community Struggles to Understand Why Their Neighbor Attacked Synagogue”).

Ghazali’s story is complicated. His attack appears to have occurred as a direct response to the death of two of his brothers and two of their children as a result of Israel’s bombardment of Lebanon. But that bombardment, according to the *Times*, was a reprisal: After the Iran-Israel/U.S. war kicked off, Hezbollah (a proxy of Iran’s) began firing rockets into Israel.

Ghazali sought to murder Jews in a synagogue — a synagogue that, at that moment, was occupied by a large number of children — and no reasonable reader could accuse the article of soft-pedaling what he did. The article unflinchingly relates the events in question:

> On the morning of March 12, Mr. Ghazali drove a truck through the door at Temple Israel in West Bloomfield Township, moved down a hallway, and exchanged gunfire with security guards. He was killed. One guard was injured. Roughly 140 children and staff members at the temple’s preschool were safely evacuated.

Before that, yes, there is some color: a scene from a mosque service memorializing a group that included Ghazali’s relatives, and a quote from the imam there recalling having met Ghazali there for what was apparently his first-ever visit to that mosque.

The accusation is familiar: The *Times*, by choosing this headline, is somehow seeking to *excuse* or *soft-pedal* or *justify* what Ghazali did. A fairer read is that the *Times* is attempting to *explain* it.

A few weeks ago, there was at least some similar uproar over the headline of the obituary for [Ayatollah Ali Khamenei](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/02/28/world/middleeast/ayatollah-ali-khamenei-dead.html) after he was killed by a joint United States-Israel effort:

To End Wokeness’s target, an outrage-thirsty audience, the headline is so obviously heinous that no explanation of its heinousness is even required: “This is real.” While End Wokeness is a hyperpartisan account whose entire raison d’être is fomenting outrage, here he (I feel like this account has to be run by a man??) is making the same implication as the present critics of the “Quiet Restaurant Worker” headline: endorsement that the *Times* headline is refusing to accurately describe Khamenei and the evil he represented.

Both of these mini-outrages are quite similar to a series of mini-outrages fomented by progressives over the last decades, including a 2017 [pileon over a profile of Midwestern Nazis](https://www.nytimes.com/2017/11/25/us/ohio-hovater-white-nationalist.html) that grew large enough the *Times* appended a quick note to the top linking to a pair of published responses.

[Here’s how William Hughes of](https://www.avclub.com/nobody-seems-happy-to-meet-the-new-york-times-polite-mi-1820739318) *[The Onion](https://www.avclub.com/nobody-seems-happy-to-meet-the-new-york-times-polite-mi-1820739318)* [’s AV Club described that controversy at the time:](https://www.avclub.com/nobody-seems-happy-to-meet-the-new-york-times-polite-mi-1820739318)

> The *New York Times* briefly switched its motto to “All The News That’s Fit To Piss People Off” today, earning widespread ire for a profile on an Ohio white supremacist that seemed more happy to praise his good manners than condemn his hateful rhetoric. Responses to “ [In America’s Heartland, the Nazi Sympathizer Next Door](https://www.nytimes.com/2017/11/25/us/ohio-hovater-white-nationalist.html) ” — in which reporter Richard Fausset sits down with his subject at both a Panera Bread *and* an Applebee’s, those two bastions of suburban fast-food normalcy — have been swift and angry, accusing the *Times* of running a piece about the dangers of normalizing Nazis that actively normalizes a Nazi in the process.

I’m not sure how to interpret this critique — and Hughes was one of many journalists to level it — other than that the critics believe that the average reader has profoundly dangerous cognitive limitations and can be quite easily bamboozled by familiar references: *Look, on the one hand, I’m against his beliefs about killing all the Jews, but on the other... he goes to Panera and I* do *like their soup-in-a-bread-bowl combo, so I’m going to hear him out.* I know it sounds like I’m caricaturing this view, but what else could Hughes be saying other than this? Isn’t that basically it?

I wrote about a couple examples of this silliness in 2022 [here](https://jessesingal.substack.com/p/it-isnt-journalisms-job-to-hand-hold), including the one that annoyed me the most: It concerned [an excellent 2016](https://www.motherjones.com/politics/2016/10/richard-spencer-trump-alt-right-white-nationalist/) *[Mother Jones](https://www.motherjones.com/politics/2016/10/richard-spencer-trump-alt-right-white-nationalist/)* [profile of Richard Spencer](https://www.motherjones.com/politics/2016/10/richard-spencer-trump-alt-right-white-nationalist/) by the freelancer Josh Harkinson. A bunch of people got mad at Harkinson and *MoJo* because the article described Spencer as “articulate and well-dressed” rather than, I dunno, calling him a drooling idiot incapable of stringing together a sentence, who by the way has *terrible* fashion sense. Critics were also mad at a social-media post describing Spencer, who tends to present himself in a dapper manner, as “dapper.”

On the one hand, the critics *seemed* to be saying that if you call someone well-dressed and well-spoken in a magazine profile you are granting them and their ideas moral worth, even if you otherwise describe their pernicious ideology in an unflinching manner (as Harkinson did). On the other hand, I just find it hard to believe a professional journalist could actually believe something this silly, so I’m not sure where that leaves us (or the field of journalism).

All these examples have in common a certain paranoia. Not only that the average reader is going to somehow be influenced into believing horrible things by subtle matters of framing and word choice, but also that big media outlets are either so incompetent they are going to allow this to occur under their noses, or — in the more conspiratorial accounts I’ve seen on “both sides” — these outlets *want* people to view jihadism or Nazism in a more reasonable light.

This really has been going on forever: In the aforementioned 2022 post I also highlighted the 2013 outrage over *Rolling Stone* ’s Dzhokhar Tsarnaev magazine cover, which I [wrote about at the time](https://www.bostonglobe.com/opinion/2013/07/19/need-tsarnaev-look-like-monster-too/uyCddOlXTvs7R8Zo8HnI2I/story.html):

Look, obviously some of these headlines and images are a bit provocative. I’m not saying otherwise. And in some cases this sort of framing can wander off into Crazyville, like this since-deleted CNN post about the kids accused of plotting a pipe-bomb attack near Gracie Mansion [that I talked about on](https://www.blockedandreported.org/p/episode-299-the-politics-of-misery) *[Blocked and Reported](https://www.blockedandreported.org/p/episode-299-the-politics-of-misery)*:

Even here I don’t think CNN is trying to normalize jihadist violence, or that the post would have that effect on any reader. What makes this a bad post is just how silly it is to posit that maybe a pair of kids *who entered the city to do terrorism* could have, like, visited MoMa instead that very day. That’s different from the other headlines and articles I’ve mentioned here.

Overall, unless the underlying treatment of the figure in question really does go out of its way to obscure what s/he did, this is all just... journalism. Journalism and a bit of marketing. But it’s *really* important we do the best we can to understand what leads to people becoming monsters! And the more journalists feel like it’s their job to render moral judgments, the worse their journalism becomes. I’m not calling for outdated ideals of perfect objectivity or whatever — I’m just saying that if your assignment is to attempt to understand why someone became a terrorist, the only way to produce a meaningful piece of reporting rather than knee-jerk commentary (which will never be in short supply) is to temporarily set aside judgment.

I think the partisan brain finds all this threatening because it really does want it to be the case that moral monsters emerge fully formed from primordial pits of pure evil. If moral monsters always had that stain on them, then there’s nothing to be done but to banish them and to hunt for their brethren, and that’s a simpler and more comforting worldview than grappling meaningfully with the forces that turn otherwise normal citizens *into* monsters.

The good news is that these social-media blowups have less of an influence on media outlets than they used to — outlets were put through the wringer for years and are better at ignoring social media screamers. Of course, on the other hand, there are fewer and fewer journalists every day, whereas the ranks of partisan and hyperpartisan commentators seem only to be growing...
