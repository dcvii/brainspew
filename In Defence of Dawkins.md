Richard Dawkins said Claude is conscious. The internet laughed. My take? Dawkins is the most honest voice in this debate — and his critics, who follow the same broken framework while stopping just short of his conclusion, haven’t seemed to notice what their own framework in fact suggests.

Here is Dawkins on machine consciousness, he says: “If these machines are not conscious, what more could it possibly take to convince you that they are?” [^1]

I get it. That’s a pretty wild thing to say. and the internet responded accordingly. The reactions have largely been one of laughter and mockery:

“The man who wrote a book about anthropomorphic misattributions of consciousness (The God Delusion) was himself deluded; conned by the Eliza Effect into thinking that text-generating script was a ‘friend’ who cared.” [^2]

The irony here is pointed: the man who wrote The God Delusion — a book about misattributed consciousness — is now being told he has “the Claude Delusion.” He diagnosed the error in others. His critics say he’s fallen for it himself.

I do think there is a grain of truth to these Eliza Effect explanations. Dawkins, for whatever reason, does seem to have been particularly susceptible to letting himself believe there is something more than sophisticated technology behind Claude.

But the mainstream response also seems too quick to judge his conclusions. The response was: *this is embarrassing, Dawkins has been fooled, he should know better*.

I’ll admit it, my own initial reaction was more or less the same. It was *disappointment*. Dawkins is a prominent intellectual. People pay attention to what he says and believes. Surely, I thought, he should have been more careful here.

But on reflection, I’ve come to think that he is actually more honest than we might care to admit. The honesty is right there in the opening of his piece.

His article begins with a discussion of the Turing test and the operational definition of consciousness Turing is widely believed to have given us — [I don’t agree,](https://ellennoraburns.substack.com/p/the-dubious-legacy-of-turings-thinking) but hey.

If a computer can fool you into thinking it’s a person — if it can write you a sonnet, for example, which Dawkins invokes nodding to Turing’s own work — then the AI is conscious. Dawkins says this future has now arrived, and “some people are finding it uncomfortable.” He then goes on to call out those who have sophisticated their position, due to that discomfort, Dawkins writes:

“When Turing wrote — and for most of the years since — it was possible to accept the hypothetical conclusion that, if a machine ever passed his operational test, we might consider it to be conscious. We were comfortably secure in the confidence that this was a very big if, kicked into future touch. However, the advent of large language models such as ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, and others has provoked a hasty scramble to move the goalposts.” [^3]

Now, there is a superficial reading of Dawkins worth addressing here. It goes something like this: *the whole idea that passing the Turing test is enough to show consciousness is completely passé. We’ve moved on from this operational definition. Dawkins should too.*

If you ask what we’ve moved onto, people will likely say: something more rigorous. Something less naïve than Turing’s imitation game. We now have Global Workspace Theory, Higher-Order Theories, Integrated Information Theory — serious neuroscientific frameworks. Outputs alone aren’t sufficient for consciousness; the internal states of the machine matter too. Everyone knows that.

The question I have for this view is: is this progress? I’m genuinely skeptical.

Underneath all the sophistication, the same move is being made. Complex outputs still equal consciousness — it’s just that now we have functionalist scaffolding around it to make it look like “science.”

The “indicators” derived from these theories are still behavioural proxies. Calling them indicators, using fancier language, doesn’t change that. The substrate independence thesis is doing all the work to make these views look scientific, when in reality they [rest on philosophical](https://ellennoraburns.substack.com/p/description-gets-confused-as-ontology) premises [we should reject](https://ellennoraburns.substack.com/p/how-functionalism-distorts-our-thinking).

The new conclusion — from those who’ve moved on from the Turing Test— becomes: AI *could* be conscious, or *isn’t yet* conscious but likely will be.

And here is where Dawkins is actually right. The goalposts have been moved. But the science, the reasoning, the theories have made virtually zero progress.

So why is Dawkins the most honest interlocutor we’ve seen in the mainstream AI consciousness debate? Because he has said what most mainstream discourse won’t: that if you follow these philosophically confused theories of consciousness to their logical conclusions, you should in fact believe AI is conscious. Hedging, and claiming that LLMs clearly aren’t but that there is a ‘50%’ chance they will be in the next however many years, is the same outlook with the goalposts moved.

The lesson we can take from Dawkins is simple. He shows us how ridiculous the entire mainstream AI consciousness framework is. I’ll say that again: Dawkins shows us how ridiculous the mainstream AI consciousness framework really is.

Moreover importantly, he shows us where the discourse leads if we don’t wake up to the reality of the ridiculousness: That reality is one where philosophical confusion, marketing hype, and an atomised society create the perfect storm of mistaking sophisticated technology for minded beings — and then building policy on that mistake.

We shouldn’t be mocking Dawkins. We should be taking seriously what his honesty reveals: that the framework producing AI consciousness conclusions is broken. There are no minds in AI systems. There are no minds floating free of brains anywhere. There are brains — biological, specific, evolved — and there are technologies, which we can and should study rigorously on their own terms.

That is the work. And it starts with being as honest as Dawkins was about where current mainstream thinking on AI minds actually leads.
