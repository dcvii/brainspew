The astronauts on NASA’s moon mission returned home Friday evening, completing a [historic lunar flyby](https://www.wsj.com/science/space-astronomy/artemis-astronauts-are-going-where-no-human-has-gone-before-99962e92?mod=article_inline) after drifting back to Earth under parachutes.

The Orion capsule that ferried the crew during their roughly 10-day mission landed off the coast of San Diego at 8:07 p.m ET, broadcast on a NASA livestream.

Since launching last week, NASA’s Artemis II mission has taken its crew farther from Earth than anyone has previously traveled, circling around the moon’s far side.

After positioning Orion for re-entry, the spacecraft [screamed through Earth’s atmosphere](https://www.wsj.com/tech/ai/a-fiery-re-entry-awaits-the-artemis-astronauts-1c43dc95?mod=article_inline) at speeds of more than 24,000 miles an hour for a period. Temperatures were expected to near 5,000 degrees for part of the return.

A series of parachutes deployed to slow Orion’s descent to about 20 miles per hour. A recovery team including Navy personnel en route to retrieve the crew—Reid Wiseman, Victor Glover, Christina Koch and Jeremy Hansen.

“What a journey,” Wiseman, the mission commander, said shortly after the landing, according to NASA’s livestream. He said the crewmembers were in good condition.

The astronauts reported some communications glitches as they waited for the recovery team to extract them out of the Orion crew vehicle, which could be seen bobbing in the ocean around 8:30 p.m. ET.

“We are back in the business of sending astronauts to the moon, bringing them back safely and \[are\] set up for a series more,” NASA Administrator Jared Isaacman said from the deck of a Navy ship engaged in the pickup.

The Artemis II mission was a back-to-the-future moment for the U.S. space program. The National Aeronautics and Space Administration pulled off daring moon trips over five decades ago, but has struggled for years to do it again. Its [current Artemis effort](https://www.wsj.com/science/space-astronomy/nasa-artemis-moon-mission-explained-361f7775?mod=article_inline), envisioned as launching a new era of space exploration, has been bogged down with delays and cost overruns.

This month the stars aligned for Artemis II. NASA, industry and the crew delivered a high-stakes mission that marked humanity’s first visit to the vicinity of the moon since the final Apollo program lunar landing in 1972.

Along the way, the astronauts gathered voluminous data about the moon, flew further from Earth than anyone else, and witnessed an eclipse. They tested life support systems on the [Lockheed Martin](https://www.wsj.com/market-data/quotes/LMT) -developed Orion spacecraft and returned to Earth using a [heat shield](https://www.wsj.com/science/space-astronomy/nasa-heat-shield-test-astronaut-flight-1ce26626?mod=article_inline) that had raised concerns among some NASA engineers.

They also became [overnight celebrities](https://www.wsj.com/science/space-astronomy/viral-moments-from-artemis-ii-the-moon-trip-for-the-digital-age-e0d2f43f?mod=article_inline). Onlookers back on Earth tuned in as Hansen shaved, Koch shot photos with a large camera and Glover worked out. A Nutella jar [floated through the cabin](https://www.wsj.com/science/space-astronomy/nutella-jar-artemis-space-nasa-113cf88c?mod=article_inline), and the crew [acted out their own sitcom opener](https://www.instagram.com/p/DWwuHPfCZ8Z/).

“Tuning into NASA’s livestream every few hours just to watch people casually exist in space … because astronauts really are the coolest thing a human being can possibly become,” one X user wrote.

Each part of the mission was designed to set up NASA and its partners for the future.

“This is a relay race,” Koch said during a press briefing Wednesday. “We’re always thinking from the perspective, ‘What is the next crew going to think about this?’”

Artemis II had its share of problems. On Thursday, NASA officials said they were monitoring a small leak inside of Orion’s propulsion system, which could require redesigns.

Then there was the toilet. The Universal Waste Management System had [a few balky moments](https://www.wsj.com/science/space-astronomy/the-toilets-working-again-artemis-astronauts-first-day-in-space-058f26ea?mod=article_inline) during the mission, prompting the crew to use plastic containers for liquid waste.

Companies and government agencies touted their contributions to the mission, including Japan-based [Nikon](https://www.wsj.com/market-data/quotes/JP/XTKS/7731), which provided the cameras used onboard, and the European Space Agency, which developed Orion’s propulsion system.

President Trump earlier this week called the astronauts: “America is a frontier nation and the four brave astronauts of Artemis II, you really are modern-day pioneers.” Members of Congress and Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney also called in.

Before [the mission’s April 1 launch](https://www.wsj.com/science/space-astronomy/nasa-launches-astronauts-on-historic-moon-flight-da6703da?mod=article_inline) from Florida, Sen. Mark Kelly (D., Ariz.), a former NASA astronaut, told reporters he was a bit jealous of the astronauts’ trip. “This is a place that we all can often come together, and unite around a common goal,” he said.

After landing, NASA plans to transport the crew to Houston to be reunited with their families, and undergo debriefings and medical checks.

NASA officials and industry engineers are already pushing toward Artemis III. During that flight, scheduled to occur next year, the agency wants Orion to practice docking in low-Earth orbit with lunar landers that Elon Musk’s SpaceX and Jeff Bezos’s Blue Origin have been developing.

In 2028, NASA aims to conduct at least one landing of astronauts on the lunar surface.
