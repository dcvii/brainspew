One of the things that hostile contact produces, apart from combat, is information. The actual command reflexes and efficiency of the combatants is put to the real test.

The Islamic Republic's manhunt for the downed US weapons system officer was an actual test of their mosaic defense; of the IRGC, the basij and the Artest or regular army to capture a single man and in the process inflict punishing losses on their rescuers.

Ultimately the Iranian mosaic failed to recapture the F-15 backseater or kill forces sent to recover him which included the construction of a forward runway, the deployment of a multiple SOF teams and ultimately the extraction of these same. The US suffered equipment losses but these were in a sense wastage, not directly inflicted by the IR.

But why did the IR fail? From the available video and reports coming out, the Islamic Republic's national priority and international profile search appeared half-hearted (with many locals uncooperative) and disorganized. When a wall of fire blocked the lightly armed basij many bravely died but they had no way of calling for reinforcement or bringing heavier weapons to bear.

Although one should not too much of the Islamic Republic's evident slow and uncoordinated response it seems reasonable to conclude that the month long attacks on the regime's command and control have had some effect. In any event it is not the impregnable wall presented in their claims.

If they could not handle a single forcible extraction deep inside their territory, which the Russians, Chinese, and Turks arguably could have, could they handle a hybrid uprising in multiple locations?

Taken at face value the IR's hold seems less than absolute. Exactly how weak it is and whether it is \*infirm enough\* to challenge further is something I certainly don't know and suspect no one, even in the Pentagon, can say for sure.

But it is reasonable to surmise that both inside the IR and in US and Israeli councils commanders are poring over reports asking themselves: what did we learn?
