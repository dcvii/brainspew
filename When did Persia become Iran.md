Q: I'm thinking about Iran today. When did it stop being Persia?

---

Iran has been the country's name domestically for a very long time — Iranians have referred to their land as "Iran" (derived from "Aryana," meaning land of the Aryans) since ancient times. The change you're thinking of is really about international usage.

In **1935**, Reza Shah Pahlavi formally requested that foreign governments and international organizations use "Iran" instead of "Persia" when referring to the country. Before that, Western nations had long called it Persia, a name derived from the ancient region of Pars (or Fars) in the southwest of the country.

So it wasn't so much a renaming as a correction — bringing international usage in line with what Iranians had always called their own homeland. The word "Persian" still lives on, of course, in references to the Persian language, Persian culture, the Persian Gulf, and Persian cats, among other things.

Q: Often, I have heard Iranians wanting to call themselves Persians to distinguish their culture from their national politics. Is this common?

---

Yes, this is a fairly well-documented phenomenon, particularly among the Iranian diaspora. There are a few layers to it worth unpacking.

Among Iranians living abroad — especially in the United States, where there's a large community particularly in Los Angeles (sometimes nicknamed "Tehrangeles") — identifying as Persian rather than Iranian became noticeably more common after the 1979 Islamic Revolution. The hostage crisis and the subsequent decades of tension between Iran and the West created a climate where "Iranian" carried political baggage that many felt didn't reflect who they were. Calling themselves Persian was a way of signaling their connection to a rich, ancient civilization — poetry, art, philosophy, cuisine — rather than to the Islamic Republic.

There's also a genuine cultural pride dimension that's separate from politics entirely. Persian civilization is thousands of years old, and many Iranians feel that identity runs much deeper than any particular government. Referencing Cyrus the Great, Hafez, or Rumi connects to something they see as more authentically theirs.

That said, it's not universal and can be a sensitive topic within Iranian communities themselves. Some Iranians feel that using "Persian" is a form of denial or even shame about their nationality, and they embrace "Iranian" proudly regardless of their feelings about the government. Others point out that "Persian" technically refers to a specific ethnic group within Iran — the country also has significant Azeri, Kurdish, Arab, and other populations who are Iranian but not ethnically Persian.

So it's genuinely complex — part political distancing, part cultural pride, and part a debate within the community about identity and what it means to own where you're from.

Q: Have these ethnic groups settled in various parts of the country? Are the major cities more multi-ethnic? What kinds of division (I assume) led to the relative ease with which the Islamic Revolution succeeded?

---

These are really interconnected questions, so let me work through them together.

**Geographic distribution of ethnic groups**

Yes, Iran's ethnic minorities are largely concentrated in border regions. Azeris (probably the largest minority, making up perhaps a quarter of the population) are concentrated in the northwest, in provinces like East and West Azerbaijan — and notably, Tehran itself has a huge Azeri population, making it quite mixed. Kurds are in the west along the Iraqi and Turkish borders. Arabs are concentrated in Khuzestan in the southwest, near the Iraqi border. Baloch are in the southeast, and Turkmen in the northeast. The interior of the country and the central plateau tend to be more predominantly Persian.

Tehran, as the capital and economic magnet, is genuinely multi-ethnic, drawing migration from across the country. Isfahan, Shiraz, and Mashhad are more historically Persian-dominated, though all large cities have become more mixed over the 20th century through urbanization.

**The divisions that fed the Revolution**

This is where it gets rich. The 1979 Revolution succeeded so swiftly partly because it was actually a coalition of very different groups who shared a common enemy in the Shah but had vastly different visions for what came next. The Shah had alienated an remarkably broad spectrum of society simultaneously.

The religious classes — the clerics and the bazaari merchants closely tied to them — resented his secularizing modernization program, which they saw as an assault on Islamic values and their own traditional social power. His White Revolution of the 1960s, which included land reform and women's suffrage, threatened both religious authorities and large landowners.

The secular left, including Marxists, socialists, and liberal nationalists, despised him for the 1953 CIA-assisted coup that had overthrown the democratically elected Prime Minister Mohammad Mosaddegh, and for his brutal secret police, SAVAK, which suppressed political dissent viciously. Many intellectuals and students fell into this camp.

The middle classes and the bazaar merchants were frustrated by economic mismanagement — the oil boom of the 1970s had actually created rampant inflation and inequality, and his rapid modernization felt chaotic and corrupt rather than genuinely developmental.

Ethnic minorities had their own grievances about political and cultural suppression — the Shah's aggressive Persian nationalism left little room for Azeri, Kurdish, or Arab cultural expression.

Khomeini was extraordinarily shrewd in building a revolutionary coalition across these groups without revealing the full theocratic vision until after power was secured. The left and liberals believed they would be partners in whatever came next. They were not. Within a few years of the Revolution, Khomeini had systematically crushed the left, purged the liberals, and suppressed ethnic minority uprisings in Kurdistan and Khuzestan. The coalition that toppled the Shah quickly discovered it had been a vehicle for a very particular project.

So in a sense, the very breadth of grievances — ethnic, religious, economic, and political — made the Revolution's initial success almost inevitable once momentum built, but it also meant the aftermath was a brutal settling of accounts among former allies.

Q: How successful have those groups retrenched and resisted the excesses of the Islamist regime? Have migration patterns changed? Have conversions been coerced? Are certain industries nationalized?

---

These are all distinct threads, so let me work through each.

**Ethnic and political resistance**

The Kurds mounted the most immediate and sustained armed resistance after 1979, and the regime suppressed them brutally — there were mass executions of Kurdish leaders in the early 1980s. Kurdish groups like the KDPI (Kurdish Democratic Party of Iran) have maintained low-level resistance for decades, operating partly from Iraqi Kurdistan. The Baloch in the southeast have also produced persistent insurgency, with groups like Jundallah and its successors carrying out attacks well into the 21st century. Arab separatists in Khuzestan have occasionally been active too. None of these groups have come close to threatening the regime territorially, but they've never been fully pacified either.

The more interesting resistance has been civil and cultural. Azeris, despite their large numbers and significant representation even within the regime itself (Khamenei is of Azeri origin), have periodically protested cultural suppression — there were significant Azeri protests in 2006 after a newspaper published a cartoon many found demeaning. Kurdish cultural expression has been persistently suppressed, with teachers of Kurdish language and cultural activists regularly imprisoned.

The women's protest movements, particularly after Mahsa Amini's death in 2022, showed that resistance to the regime cuts across ethnic lines — it was genuinely nationwide and multi-ethnic, which seemed to unsettle the government considerably. Whether it represents a new phase or another cycle remains an open question.

**Migration patterns**

The brain drain from Iran has been one of the most dramatic of any country in modern history. It began immediately after the Revolution, when the Shah's supporters, many wealthy secular families, and significant portions of the Jewish, Armenian, and Bahá'í communities fled. Iran had a substantial Jewish population before 1979 — perhaps 80,000 to 100,000 — which shrank to somewhere around 10,000 to 15,000 today, most of the emigration happening in the revolution's immediate aftermath. The Bahá'í, Iran's largest non-Muslim religious minority, faced (and continue to face) systematic persecution — they're denied higher education and many professions — and many have emigrated.

The exodus has continued in waves tied to political events and economic crises. Educated professionals, engineers, doctors, and academics leave in enormous numbers — Iran consistently ranks among the world's highest in educated emigrant outflows. Economic sanctions have accelerated this, as the currency has collapsed and opportunities have dried up. The 2022 protests produced another notable wave of departures of young, educated Iranians.

Internally, the wars with Iraq in the 1980s displaced enormous populations from Khuzestan and border regions. Urbanization has continued steadily, with Tehran becoming one of the world's larger megacities, partly through migration from economically marginalized provinces.

**Religious coercion and conversion**

This is an area where the regime's record is genuinely dark, though it operates through structural pressure as much as direct coercion. Apostasy from Islam is effectively punishable by death under Iranian law, though actual executions specifically for apostasy are relatively rare and usually wrapped in other charges. The pressure is more systemic — leaving Islam means losing access to inheritance rights, marriage recognition, and facing serious social and legal jeopardy.

The Bahá'í have faced the most systematic persecution of any group — their faith is considered heretical rather than a separate religion, their marriages aren't recognized, their cemeteries have been bulldozed, and their leaders have been executed. Practicing Christianity is legal for recognized ethnic minorities like Armenians and Assyrians, but converting from Islam to Christianity is not, and evangelical churches that sought converts among Muslims have been repeatedly raided and their leaders imprisoned. There have been notable cases of pastors sentenced to years in prison.

There's an interesting and somewhat under-reported phenomenon of private irreligion — surveys and anecdotal evidence suggest that actual religious practice and belief among ordinary Iranians, particularly the young and urban, has declined substantially under the Islamic Republic, possibly as a reaction against state religion. The regime has in a sense made Islam its political brand, which has alienated many from religion itself.

**Nationalization**

The economy was heavily nationalized immediately after the revolution, and remains so in significant ways. The oil and gas sector, which dominates the economy, is state-controlled through the National Iranian Oil Company. Banking was nationalized. Major industries were seized from their owners — many of whom had fled — and handed either to the state or to bonyads, which are quasi-public religious foundations nominally set up for charitable purposes but which operate as enormous conglomerates with deep ties to the Revolutionary Guards. The bonyads controlling large portions of the economy — estimates suggest they account for perhaps a third of GDP — operate with minimal transparency and are effectively outside normal government oversight.

The Revolutionary Guards (IRGC) themselves have become a massive economic actor, with stakes in construction, telecommunications, energy contracting, and smuggling networks. The sanctions regime has paradoxically strengthened them in some ways, since their networks are better positioned to circumvent sanctions than the formal private sector.

There has been periodic rhetoric about privatization, and some state assets have nominally been sold, but much of this has been transfers to IRGC-linked entities rather than genuine private market development. The result is an economy that combines state control, religious foundation capitalism, and military-industrial complex in ways that make meaningful reform very difficult, since so many powerful interests depend on the current structure.

Q: Much has been said about the use of Iranian proxies in geopolitics. To what extent is the foreing exchange of Iran centered around such relationships? Are Iranian borders closed to outsiders? Are their citizens prohibited from travel?

---

These questions touch on some genuinely complex and intertwined dynamics.

**Proxies and foreign exchange**

The proxy network — Hezbollah in Lebanon, Hamas and Islamic Jihad in Gaza, the Houthis in Yemen, various Shia militias in Iraq, and others in Syria — is primarily a strategic and ideological investment rather than a revenue-generating one. Iran is the patron, not the recipient. Estimates of what Iran spends annually on Hezbollah alone have ranged from several hundred million to over a billion dollars depending on the period and the source. So these relationships actually represent a significant outflow of resources, funded largely by oil revenues routed through mechanisms designed to evade sanctions.

The economic logic from Tehran's perspective is that these groups extend Iranian influence and deterrence far beyond its borders cheaply relative to conventional military projection. Hezbollah in particular functions as a forward deterrent against Israel. But it's a strategic calculation, not a commercial one — Iran isn't earning foreign exchange through proxies, it's spending it.

Where Iran does earn foreign exchange is primarily through oil and gas exports, which continue despite sanctions through shadow fleets, ship-to-ship transfers, falsified documentation, and sales heavily discounted to willing buyers — China being by far the largest, absorbing the majority of Iranian oil exports in recent years. Gold, petrochemicals, and some agricultural products also generate export revenue. The sanctions have dramatically reduced the volume and price Iran can command, contributing to severe currency depreciation, but they haven't eliminated export income entirely.

There's also an illicit dimension worth acknowledging — the IRGC's smuggling networks bring in considerable revenue through drugs, weapons, and consumer goods, much of it bypassing the formal economy entirely.

**Iranian borders and foreign visitors**

Iran is not remotely as closed as North Korea, and this is a point often lost in Western media coverage. Foreign tourists do visit Iran, and before the sanctions era intensified and COVID arrived, tourism was actually a growing sector — Iran has extraordinary historical sites, from Persepolis to Isfahan to Yazd, and was actively courting visitors. The infrastructure exists.

The major practical barrier for Western visitors is the banking system — US sanctions mean no Western credit or debit cards work in Iran, so visitors must carry large amounts of cash. American, British, and Canadian citizens face additional restrictions, generally being required to take guided tours rather than traveling independently, and their applications are scrutinized more carefully.

Israeli passport holders are flatly barred from entry, and having Israeli stamps in a passport can cause difficulties. The political relationship between Iran and Israel being what it is, this is unsurprising.

The regime does use border control selectively and politically — dual nationals, journalists, academics, and activists are sometimes detained upon entry or prevented from leaving, used effectively as diplomatic bargaining chips. This has been a persistent pattern, with several prominent cases of Iranian-Americans being held for years. It creates a real chilling effect on diaspora Iranians considering visits to family.

**Iranian citizens and travel**

Ordinary Iranians are generally permitted to travel abroad and hold passports, which distinguishes Iran from truly closed states. However, there are several layers of restriction and practical difficulty.

Men subject to mandatory military service cannot leave until they've completed it or obtained an exemption, which creates a significant constraint on young men. Certain professionals — academics, scientists, particularly those connected to the nuclear program — face travel restrictions. Dissidents, journalists, and activists are routinely denied passports or have them confiscated.

For most Iranians, the practical barrier to international travel isn't legal prohibition but economic devastation. The rial has collapsed so dramatically against hard currencies that foreign travel has become unaffordable for most of the population. What was once a middle-class aspiration has become a luxury. This economic imprisonment is arguably more effective than legal prohibition would be.

The visa dimension adds another layer — many countries make it difficult for Iranian passport holders to obtain visas, partly due to security concerns and partly due to diplomatic tensions, so the Iranian passport is among the weaker travel documents in the world in terms of visa-free access.

There's a poignant irony in all of this — Iranians are historically and culturally a well-traveled, cosmopolitan people with a strong tradition of education and intellectual engagement with the wider world, and the combination of regime restrictions, economic collapse, and international sanctions has progressively walled off a population that doesn't particularly want to be walled off.

Q: What is the current population in Iran? How vulnerable is it to the kinds of conditions (like famine in Egypt) that led to Arab Spring -like revolutions?

---

**Population**

Iran's current population is approximately 93 million, making it the 17th most populous country in the world. It's a young country demographically — the average age is around 32, and the fertility rate has fallen to 1.44, well below the natural replacement rate of 2.1.

**Vulnerability to revolution — the comparison with Arab Spring**

This is a genuinely interesting question because Iran has several characteristics that both increase and complicate revolutionary potential.

The conditions that fed Arab Spring — economic stagnation, high youth unemployment, food insecurity, entrenched corruption, and a sense of blocked futures — are all present in Iran to a significant degree. The rial has lost something like 80-90% of its value over the past decade due to sanctions and mismanagement. Inflation has been severe and persistent. The educated young, who make up an enormous share of the population given the demographic bulge from the post-revolutionary baby boom, face limited economic prospects. The brain drain we discussed earlier is itself a symptom of this — people who can leave, do.

Food security is a real concern. Iran is not self-sufficient in wheat and basic staples, and the combination of sanctions, currency collapse, and periodic drought has made food inflation particularly painful for ordinary households. This is closer to the Egyptian conditions you mention than is often acknowledged in Western coverage focused primarily on nuclear and geopolitical questions.

The 2019 protests, triggered by a sudden fuel price hike, were notable for their breadth and ferocity — they spread to over 100 cities and were met with one of the bloodiest crackdowns in the Islamic Republic's history, with credible estimates of several hundred killed in just a few days. The 2022 Mahsa Amini protests were similarly widespread and sustained over months, crossing gender, ethnic, and class lines in ways that clearly alarmed the regime.

So what separates Iran from Egypt in 2011? Several things.

The Islamic Republic has a much more sophisticated and layered security apparatus than Mubarak's Egypt did. The IRGC, the Basij militia, intelligence services, and regular police create overlapping systems of surveillance and coercion that are deeply embedded in society. The Basij in particular recruits from economically marginalized communities, giving the regime a genuine social base willing to use violence against protesters.

The regime has also learned from both its own experience in 1979 and from watching Arab Spring unfold elsewhere. It moves quickly to cut internet access during unrest, it arrests organizers preemptively, and it has shown willingness to use lethal force without the hesitation that characterized some Arab militaries in 2011.

There's also no equivalent of the Egyptian or Tunisian military standing apart from the political leadership and ultimately refusing to fire on crowds. The IRGC is ideologically committed to the system in a way the Egyptian army was not to Mubarak personally.

And yet the regime's legitimacy has clearly eroded substantially, particularly among urban youth. The Islamic Republic's great original source of legitimacy — the Revolution itself, the martyrdom of the Iran-Iraq war, the religious authority of the Supreme Leader — resonates far less with a generation born after all of that. The gap between official ideology and lived reality has become almost impossible to paper over.

The honest assessment is that Iran has been in a slow-burning pre-revolutionary condition for some years, but the security apparatus remains capable enough to prevent it from reaching ignition point — at least so far. Whether that changes depends on factors that are genuinely hard to predict: an economic shock severe enough to overwhelm the repressive capacity, a split within the elite, a miscalculation by the regime, or simply the cumulative weight of a population that has largely stopped believing.

Q: Let's dig down into the questions of national vs religious fidelity. If educated youth tend to be less religious in the current generations, what are some of the reactions to fatwas issued by the leadership? How divisive and consequential are these?

---

This gets at one of the most fascinating tensions in the Islamic Republic's structure, because the entire system of government — velayat-e faqih, the guardianship of the jurist — rests on the premise that religious authority and political authority are unified and legitimate. When that religious authority loses credibility, the whole edifice is theoretically undermined.

**The structure of religious authority**

It's worth understanding first that Shia Islam has always had a more decentralized, almost market-like structure of religious authority compared to Sunni Islam. Shia Muslims are expected to follow a living marja — a "source of emulation" — a senior cleric whose religious rulings they accept as binding on personal matters. Crucially, there have always been multiple competing marjas, and a believer chooses which one to follow. This created a tradition of religious pluralism within the clerical establishment that the Islamic Republic has never fully suppressed, even as it tried.

Khomeini's innovation was the claim that the supreme jurist should have not just religious but political authority over the state. This was actually controversial among senior clerics from the beginning — Grand Ayatollah Khoi in Najaf, then the most senior Shia cleric in the world, never accepted it. Grand Ayatollah Sistani, who succeeded him and remains enormously influential today from Iraq, similarly operates on a model of clerical quietism — the idea that clerics should guide but not directly rule. So even within the religious establishment, the Islamic Republic's foundational premise has always been contested.

**Reactions to specific fatwas**

The most consequential and revealing example remains Khomeini's 1989 fatwa against Salman Rushdie for The Satanic Verses. This was a genuinely watershed moment — it demonstrated the regime's willingness to project religious authority beyond Iran's borders in ways that horrified Western governments and much of Iran's own educated class. Within Iran, public reaction was complex: outward dissent was dangerous, but among intellectuals and urban professionals, private disgust was widespread. It crystallized a sense that the regime was willing to embarrass Iran internationally and subordinate everything to ideological posturing.

More domestically consequential have been fatwas and religious rulings on everyday social behavior — on music, on the dress code, on gender segregation, on satellite dishes, on the internet. The enforcement of these has always been uneven and has created a distinctive Iranian culture of public compliance and private defiance. Satellite dishes are technically illegal but visible on rooftops across Tehran. Western music is officially banned but privately ubiquitous. This gap between official religious decree and lived practice is so wide and so openly acknowledged that it has become almost a form of passive mass resistance — a society-wide silent referendum on the regime's religious authority.

Khamenei's religious credentials have always been a particular vulnerability. He was elevated to Supreme Leader in 1989 despite being a relatively junior cleric — a hojatoleslam rather than an ayatollah — and his title was essentially upgraded by political fiat. Senior clerics of genuine religious stature have never fully accepted his religious authority, even when they've had to accept his political power. This means his fatwas carry an asterisk in the eyes of traditionally religious Iranians who care about proper scholarly credentials, which is an irony the regime has never resolved.

**The reformist clerical challenge**

Some of the most interesting resistance has come from within the religious establishment itself. Figures like Grand Ayatollah Montazeri — who was Khomeini's designated successor before falling out with him — became vocal critics of the regime from within a framework of religious legitimacy. Montazeri's critiques of human rights abuses and his challenges to Khamenei's authority were particularly damaging precisely because they came from someone with genuine clerical standing. The regime effectively kept him under house arrest for much of his later life, but his fatwas and writings circulated widely anyway, offering religious cover for those who wanted to resist the system on its own terms.

Mohammad Khatami's presidency in the late 1990s and 2000s represented another dimension of this — a reformist current that tried to argue for a more pluralist, democratic interpretation of Islamic governance. The Green Movement of 2009, which erupted after the disputed re-election of Ahmadinejad, had significant clerical support, including from Mir Hossein Mousavi and Mehdi Karroubi, both of whom remain under house arrest to this day. The movement's suppression was brutal, but it demonstrated that the fracture ran through the system, not just against it.

**The current generation's relationship with Islam**

What's happened since is arguably more radical than political opposition. Survey data — always difficult to interpret in authoritarian contexts where people are reluctant to answer honestly — suggests that formal religious identification and practice among younger Iranians has declined dramatically. Some researchers have described what amounts to a quiet mass apostasy, not dramatic or public, but a generational turning away from Islam as the state has made it synonymous with oppression, corruption, and hypocrisy.

The supreme irony the Islamic Republic has generated is that by making religiosity compulsory and associating it with the state's most unpopular features — the morality police, economic mismanagement, corruption in the clerical class, international isolation — it has arguably done more damage to Islam in Iran than any secular reformer could have. Young Iranians who might have had a natural, culturally embedded relationship with their faith have instead experienced it as a tool of surveillance and control.

There's a notable revival of interest in pre-Islamic Persian identity as a form of cultural resistance — Nowruz, the Persian new year, and ancient Zoroastrian traditions have taken on an almost political valence as expressions of an identity that predates and transcends the Islamic Republic. The celebration of Chaharshanbe Suri, the fire-jumping festival on the eve of Nowruz, has become almost defiantly large in recent years, with the regime periodically trying and failing to suppress it.

**The divisiveness question**

Where this creates real division is between the regime's remaining social base — more rural, more traditionally religious, more economically dependent on state and religious institution networks — and the urban, educated population that has largely disengaged from official religiosity. This isn't simply a class divide, though it has class dimensions. It's more a divide between those whose identity and livelihood remain tied to the system and those for whom the system has become an obstacle to everything they value.

The regime is aware of this and has tried various tactics — periodic loosening of social restrictions, economic populism, nationalist appeals during moments of external threat — to maintain enough of a coalition to survive. What it cannot do is resolve the fundamental contradiction between a government whose entire legitimacy claim rests on religious authority and a population that has in large numbers stopped accepting that authority as legitimate.

Q: Have significant geopolitical changes in the gulf states been a reflection of the Iranian peoples' sentiments? Are there long running regional rivalries and kinships that drive the dynamic or is it more based on the ambitions of the powerful? How might growing antagonism by foreign countries affect Iranian citizens' view of their leaders?

---

These questions cut to the heart of how regional politics actually works versus how it's often portrayed, and the answers are genuinely complicated.

**The Gulf states and Iranian sentiment**

The short answer is that geopolitical shifts in the Gulf have very little to do with the Iranian people's sentiments and almost everything to do with elite calculations among states. The Abraham Accords, the Saudi-Israeli normalization process, the 2023 Chinese-brokered Saudi-Iran rapprochement — these are moves on a chessboard played by governments, not expressions of popular will anywhere in the region.

Iranian citizens watching Gulf states like the UAE and Saudi Arabia grow enormously wealthy, cosmopolitan, and internationally connected while Iran has been progressively isolated and impoverished generates a complex mixture of resentment, envy, and wounded pride. Many Iranians are acutely aware that their country, with its far deeper history, culture, and human capital, has been reduced to a pariah state while Gulf monarchies with far shallower roots have flourished. That awareness fuels domestic discontent with the regime more than it generates any solidarity with foreign governments.

**The Sunni-Shia dimension**

The most structurally important rivalry in the Gulf is the Saudi-Iranian one, and it operates on multiple levels simultaneously — theological, ethnic, and geopolitical — in ways that make it genuinely difficult to disentangle.

The theological dimension is real but often overstated in Western analysis. The Saudi state promotes a Wahhabi/Salafi form of Sunni Islam that is doctrinally hostile to Shia practice, considering it a form of heresy or idolatry. Iran is the preeminent Shia power and sees itself as the protector of Shia communities everywhere. These aren't just diplomatic talking points — they reflect genuine theological animosity with centuries of roots, including periodic Saudi destruction of Shia holy sites and the systematic discrimination against Saudi Arabia's own Shia minority concentrated in the oil-rich Eastern Province.

But it would be a mistake to reduce the rivalry purely to theology. Iran and Saudi Arabia are also competing for regional hegemony in a classically realist way that would exist regardless of religious difference. They are the two largest powers in the Gulf, they both want to shape the region's political order, and they have directly opposing interests in Yemen, Lebanon, Iraq, Syria, and Bahrain. The religious framing is partly genuine and partly instrumentalized by both sides to mobilize populations and delegitimize opponents.

The ethnic dimension adds another layer. Arabs and Persians carry centuries of complicated history — the Arab conquest of Persia in the 7th century that brought Islam to Iran is remembered differently in Tehran than in Riyadh. Persian cultural condescension toward what they sometimes privately regard as culturally shallower Arab Gulf states is a real phenomenon, and Arab anxiety about Persian ambitions is equally real. The Islamic Republic's rhetoric about Arab rulers being corrupt American puppets, and Saudi rhetoric about Persian imperialism, both tap into genuine historical anxieties even when deployed cynically.

**Is it elite ambition or deeper forces?**

Both, but with an important nuance. The rulers of Gulf states operate within structural constraints — sectarian geography, historical memory, resource competition, great power patronage relationships — that they didn't choose and can't entirely escape. Saudi Arabia's rivalry with Iran isn't purely the invention of the Al Saud family, though the family's particular character shapes how it's expressed. But the specific forms the rivalry takes — proxy wars, oil price manipulation, diplomatic maneuvering — are absolutely products of elite decisions made in palaces with minimal popular input.

The Gulf monarchies are autocracies with varying degrees of social contract. The UAE and Saudi Arabia have used oil wealth to buy a relatively quiescent citizenry that largely accepts political passivity in exchange for economic comfort and social services. Their foreign policies don't reflect popular sentiment because popular sentiment isn't really solicited. When MBS launched the Yemen war in 2015, there was no Saudi public debate. When the UAE normalized relations with Israel in 2020, Emirati citizens weren't consulted. These were sovereign decisions made by rulers who could make them.

Iran is somewhat different in that it has a more genuinely contested domestic politics — not democratic, but with real factions, real elections with real (if constrained) competition, and a population that does express political views loudly when given any opening. The regime's foreign policy adventurism, particularly the enormous cost of supporting Hezbollah, the Houthis, and Syrian Assad while ordinary Iranians struggle economically, is a genuine source of domestic anger. The slogan "Neither Gaza nor Lebanon, I give my life for Iran" became prominent during the 2019 and 2022 protests — a direct popular rebuke to the regime's proxy expenditures.

**Foreign antagonism and domestic opinion**

This is where the dynamics get genuinely subtle and the regime has historically been quite skilled at manipulation.

External pressure and foreign threats have traditionally been the Islamic Republic's most reliable tool for consolidating domestic support and delegitimizing opposition. The Iran-Iraq war, which Iraq initiated with American encouragement in 1980, served as an almost miraculous gift to the revolution — it allowed the regime to wrap itself in nationalism, demand sacrifice, execute opponents as traitors, and postpone reckoning with domestic failures for eight years. The logic of "rally around the flag" is universal, and Iranian leaders understand it perfectly.

The nuclear negotiations and the JCPOA illustrated this dynamic in reverse — when the Obama administration offered genuine diplomatic engagement, it actually complicated the hardliners' position domestically, because the reformist argument that engagement was possible gained credibility. Trump's withdrawal from the JCPOA and the reimposition of maximum pressure sanctions were, from the regime's perspective, almost welcome — they restored the narrative of an implacably hostile America, gave the hardliners economic conditions they could blame on sanctions rather than mismanagement, and undercut moderates who had argued for engagement.

Israeli strikes on Iranian assets and the periodic Israeli-Iranian exchanges of 2024 created a genuinely complicated public reaction inside Iran. There's a documented phenomenon where Iranians who are deeply hostile to their own government nevertheless feel something like nationalist reflexes when Iran is attacked externally. The regime is not identical to the nation in most Iranians' minds, and an attack on Iranian territory or assets activates an identity that goes beyond politics. The regime exploits this skillfully.

However — and this is where the current moment may be different from the past — the cumulative weight of decades of "external threat" rhetoric may have exhausted its effectiveness with significant portions of the population. When ordinary Iranians see the regime shoot down a Ukrainian passenger jet and then lie about it for days, when they see their currency destroyed and their prospects eliminated while the IRGC funds militias across the region, the nationalist framing becomes harder to sustain. A population can only be told so many times that its suffering is America's fault before it starts questioning why other countries facing American pressure seem to manage better.

The most dangerous scenario for the regime — and the one its security apparatus is most designed to prevent — is one where external pressure becomes severe enough to cause economic collapse that the "foreign enemy" framing can no longer contain, and internal collapse becomes a real possibility. The regime's calculation has always been that it can manage enough economic pain to maintain control. What it cannot model precisely is where the threshold lies — and neither can anyone else.

Fast forward to the present. Recent protests, riots and executions taking place in Jan & Feb 2026 seem to be ongoing. What are the major reasons for them happening now?

---

What you're asking about is genuinely momentous and still unfolding — as of today, February 20, 2026, Iran is in the midst of what appears to be one of the most severe crackdowns in the Islamic Republic's history. Let me lay out what we know.

**How it started**

The protests began on December 28, 2025, initially sparked by economic grievances, with the first demonstrations occurring among Iran's shopkeepers. This is a familiar triggering pattern — the 2019 uprising also began with a sudden economic shock. The underlying causes are everything we've discussed: currency collapse, inflation, unemployment, and a population that has been economically squeezed for years by sanctions, mismanagement, and the regime's diversion of resources to proxy networks abroad.

The protests subsequently spread, with students and other Iranians joining, and demands widened beyond economics to include an end to Iranian support for armed groups in the Middle East and even calls for restoration of the monarchy through Reza Pahlavi. That last point is significant — monarchist sentiment has been growing as a distinct political current, particularly among younger Iranians who see it as a secular nationalist alternative to the theocracy.

**The crackdown — unprecedented in scale**

What happened next was extraordinary even by the Islamic Republic's grim standards. Iranian authorities in 2025 carried out executions on a scale unseen since the late 1980s, and the deadly crackdown on protests that erupted in late December escalated sharply in early 2026, with security forces carrying out mass killings of protesters and bystanders, with the death toll rising to the thousands.

Sources close to Iran's Supreme National Security Council and the presidential office report that the killing of protesters was carried out on the direct order of Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, with full approval from senior state officials. The council allegedly authorised live fire, which was executed mainly by the IRGC in what is described as a deliberate, organised operation exceptional in scale and intensity.

The death toll figures vary enormously and are deeply contested because the regime shut down communications. As of January 25, total death toll estimates ranged from 3,117 per the Iranian government to upwards of 36,500, with Time reporting that a specific list included over 30,000 protest-related deaths registered in civilian hospitals for just January 8 and 9 alone. Even taking the most conservative figures, this represents a massacre of historic proportions.

The Iranian authorities imposed a near-total internet shutdown during the crackdown, restricting communication inside the country and limiting the flow of information about the killings to the outside world.

**The nature of the repression**

The methods being documented are particularly disturbing. The Guardian documented security forces using shotguns, rifles with live ammunition, and heavy DShK machine guns against protesters, with a Tehran doctor stating that security forces were shooting to kill.

Detainees were subjected to torture and sexual assault while in custody, and were reportedly injected with unknown chemical substances linked to multiple deaths in custody.

As recently as February 19, the CEO of Tehran's main cemetery publicly confirmed that security forces used coup de grâce — a final fatal shot to the wounded — widely during suppression of the January protests.

Iran's judiciary announced it has begun confiscating assets of people who supported the protests, with prosecutors seizing the property of prominent individuals and business owners who "invited and encouraged young people to riot." One prominent philanthropist and food chain owner was arrested after supporting protests and had all his property confiscated even after his release, after being forced to sign a letter of apology.

The coercion of families has been particularly cynical. The Islamic Republic pressured families of detainees to attend pro-government rallies marking the anniversary of the 1979 Revolution, telling them that only if they complied and attended would their detained relatives be spared execution or have their sentences reduced.

**Why now — the specific triggers**

Several factors converged to make this moment particularly explosive. The economic situation deteriorated sharply through 2025. By end of December 2025, Iran's authorities had executed over 2,000 people — the highest number of known executions since the late 1980s — over half for drug-related offenses. The regime was already in a heightened state of repression before the protests even began, which suggests a leadership that felt threatened and was pre-emptively hardening.

The Iran-Israel conflict of 2025 is also part of the backdrop. Human Rights Watch found that Israeli forces unlawfully attacked Evin Prison in Tehran on June 23, killing and injuring scores of civilians. The regime's military setbacks, the degradation of Hezbollah, and the exposure of Iran's actual military capabilities against Israel undermined the regime's prestige and removed one of its key sources of nationalist legitimacy.

**The ongoing situation today**

As Iran enters February, the fortieth-day commemorations of uprising victims were held on February 16 and 17 with large memorial ceremonies and protest gatherings across multiple cities. Far from being solely acts of mourning, these events evolved into powerful displays of sustained public anger and political defiance. In Shia tradition, the 40th day after death is a major mourning observance — and these commemorations have historically served as catalysts for renewed protest, as they did during the 1979 Revolution itself.

Prison hunger strikes have expanded to 56 facilities, with protesters chanting slogans including "Execution is gasoline on the fire of the uprising" and "This is the last message; if you execute, there will be an uprising."

**International response**

President Trump warned the regime against executing protesters, saying the US would take very strong action, and a US carrier strike group arrived in the region on January 26. The UK announced new sanctions targeting Iranian finance, energy, transport, and software industries.

What makes this moment different from 2019 and 2022 is the sheer scale of the killing, the apparent directness of Khamenei's personal orders, and the fact that the regime seems to have made a calculated decision to attempt a definitive, final suppression rather than a managed crackdown. Whether that calculation proves correct — or whether the scale of the killing has crossed a threshold that even the security apparatus cannot contain — is the central question hanging over Iran right now.
