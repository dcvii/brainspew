The war carries several risks for China, potentially choking off the significant portion of its oil imports that runs through the Strait of Hormuz and highlighting the limits of its support for countries whose partnership it seeks in challenging the global dominance of the U.S.

Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi said Sunday that it was “unacceptable” for the U.S. and Israel to attack Iran while carrying out negotiations, “still less to blatantly assassinate a leader of a sovereign country and instigate regime change.”

China “supports the Iranian side in safeguarding its sovereignty, security, territorial integrity and national dignity,” Wang said, speaking with Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi by phone on Monday.

There was [little else beyond words](https://www.wsj.com/world/an-isolated-iran-finds-chinas-friendship-has-limits-be947372?mod=article_inline) that China could offer Iran.

Instead, Beijing is likely to position itself as an upholder of the international order and avoid ensnaring itself in a protracted Middle East conflict while preparing to work with whoever governs Iran after the fighting stops, analysts say.

That pattern follows Beijing’s limited support for the government of Venezuela, another close partner of China’s, when the U.S. military captured President Nicolás Maduro in January. It could also presage China’s stance if the U.S. were [to take action against Cuba](https://www.wsj.com/world/americas/the-u-s-is-actively-seeking-regime-change-in-cuba-by-the-end-of-the-year-1d0f178a?mod=article_inline), with which Beijing has what it calls an “ironclad friendship.”

As the U.S. [upends China’s geopolitical partners](https://www.wsj.com/politics/national-security/under-trump-pressure-iran-finds-its-friends-are-of-little-help-1cd57bb2?mod=article_inline), Chinese leader Xi Jinping’s continuing efforts to build a coalition of like-minded nations will likely be tarnished. He has promoted ambitious campaigns such as the Global Security Initiative and Global Development Initiative that lay out a vision of an alternative to a Western-led world order.

China, together with Russia, helped Iran join two multilateral groupings where they are charter members: the so-called Brics bloc of emerging economies in 2024 and the Shanghai Cooperation Organization, a security-focused body, in 2023.

But membership in such groups ultimately added little to Iran’s security. “It is very discouraging for anybody who believes that China was offering an alternative because there is no alternative,” said Alicia García Herrero, chief Asia Pacific economist for the French bank Natixis. “China won’t be there if you need them.”

Still, the war against Iran offers some bright spots for Chinese policymakers. The U.S. is straining its military capacity while [depleting its stockpile of munitions](https://www.wsj.com/world/middle-east/u-s-races-to-accomplish-iran-mission-before-munitions-run-out-c014acbc?mod=article_inline), particularly the type of weapons that might be used in any sort of conflict with China over Taiwan.

The U.S. is also providing the Chinese military with a glimpse of its latest equipment and tactics. The U.S. has moved military resources out of Asia, a shift that could prove durable if the war in Iran doesn’t end quickly.

The strikes on Iran have given China an opportunity to portray the U.S. as leading what Wang, the foreign minister, called “the regression of the world to the law of the jungle.”

“From the Chinese perspective, this is just another example of how the Americans are a source of instability while we are the source of stability,” said Andrea Ghiselli, a lecturer in international politics at the University of Exeter.

The entanglement of several Gulf states, which [Iran has attacked with long-range drones and missiles](https://www.wsj.com/world/middle-east/by-striking-its-neighbors-iran-has-deepened-the-gulfs-resolve-to-fight-back-f2883367?mod=article_inline), adds to the complexity of the war for China. Saudi Arabia, for instance, sold more crude oil to China last year than Iran did. And Chinese investments in Saudi Arabia and the United Arab Emirates far exceed its investments in Iran. If China were to take steps that would help Iran attack its neighbors, it could harm Beijing’s ties with those critical countries.

Wang alluded to those concerns in a phone call on Monday with Oman’s foreign minister, Badr bin Hamad al-Busaidi, saying that “spillover of the war does not serve the fundamental and long-term interests of Gulf states.”

The war already has implications for China’s core interests in the region, which are mostly economic and energy security, said Tuvia Gering, a China researcher at the Institute for National Security Studies in Israel. “I don’t think that China right now can do or is willing to do much but to observe first where the war is going to take Iran, the U.S. and Israel,” he added.

China and Iran, along with Russia and North Korea, are part of what some Western officials had labeled the Crink nations, an unofficial grouping of states that want to resist the U.S. and its allies. While there are some examples of cooperation among them, such as China and North Korea’s assistance to Russia’s war effort in Ukraine, [their collective support for Iran has been limited](https://www.wsj.com/world/iran-china-russia-north-korea-alliance-2aab00a0?mod=article_inline).

Russia and Iran collaborate on the production of weapons, such as the Shahed drone, which Russia has copied and used to bombard Ukrainian cities and energy infrastructure. China has provided electrical components and raw materials that have been used in weapons in [Russia](https://www.wsj.com/world/chinese-firms-are-selling-russia-goods-its-military-needs-to-keep-fighting-in-ukraine-11657877403?mod=article_inline) and [Iran](https://www.wsj.com/world/iran-orders-material-from-china-for-hundreds-of-ballistic-missiles-1e874701?mod=article_inline).

China and Iran announced an economic cooperation agreement in 2021 that called for China to invest $400 billion in the Middle Eastern country over the course of 25 years. But progress has been slow and hampered by U.S. sanctions on Iran.

The two countries have used a complicated system to obscure oil exports from Iran to China, using ship-to-ship transfer to disguise Iranian crude as coming from other countries such as Malaysia while repaying Tehran with [infrastructure investments that avoid international banks](https://www.wsj.com/world/middle-east/how-china-secretly-pays-iran-for-oil-and-avoids-u-s-sanctions-b6f1b71e?mod=article_inline).

The relationship is generally a lopsided one, with Tehran relying far more on Beijing than the other way around. China buys roughly 90% of Iran’s oil exports, though that only accounts for about 12% of China’s total oil imports.

Iran’s [threat to attack ships in the Strait of Hormuz](https://www.wsj.com/world/middle-east/trumps-war-on-iran-threatens-to-drive-up-oil-prices-and-inflation-8fda38a8?mod=article_inline), a critical chokepoint for energy supplies from the Middle East, has caused oil and natural-gas prices to surge for customers globally.

China has taken several steps to insulate itself from a shock disruption to energy imports. It has built up a strategic national oil reserve while promoting electric vehicles and other technologies to curb its reliance on oil consumption, [which is expected to peak by next year](https://www.wsj.com/world/china/china-oil-demand-lower-b5ae15ed?mod=article_inline).

“The Chinese have been preparing for this situation for a while,” said Ghiselli, who is also head of research at the ChinaMed Project, which analyzes China’s impact in the Mediterranean region.

HONG KONG—As the U.S. and Israel have pummeled China’s closest partner in the Middle East, killing Iran’s leader and calling for its people to overthrow the government, Beijing has responded with sharp denunciations, but little more.
