Retired General Frank McKenzie commanded CENTCOM from 2019 to 2022 — the years that set in motion everything now unfolding in Operation Epic Fury. His book *The Melting Point*, published by Naval Institute Press, is essential reading for anyone trying to understand how we got here. Mooch sits down with him to walk through the key inflection points: the aborted 2019 strikes that emboldened Iran, the Baghdadi raid, the Soleimani takedown, and what McKenzie thinks of the campaign now at the thirty-day mark.

McKenzie is unsparing on the decision not to strike Iran after the RQ-4 shootdown in July 2019. He had the plan, had the targets, believed the strike would kill no one because the Iranians knew it was coming — and then bad data on collateral damage made its way to the president and the operation was called off. In McKenzie’s view, that decision sent Iran down the path that eventually led to Soleimani. He’s equally direct on Soleimani himself: not Rommel, he says, but Reinhard Heydrich — uniquely effective, uniquely dangerous, and killed by the British in 1942 precisely because he was so capable. McKenzie recommended the strike, stands by it, and argues the destabilizing effect on Iran’s command structure is one of the threads that runs directly to where the regime sits today.

On the Baghdadi raid, McKenzie lays out the reasoning that led to a ground operation instead of a strike: 12 children in the compound, the need for undeniable DNA confirmation, and the desire — however unlikely — to give Baghdadi a chance to surrender. The decision meant putting over a hundred Americans at risk to avoid killing children. He doesn’t second-guess it. Baghdadi blew himself up in a tunnel with two children and a suicide vest. The Belgian Malinois, Conan, went in after him. McKenzie notes, with some dry understatement, that today they’d send a drone instead — that’s how fast the technology has moved since 2019.

His assessment of Epic Fury at plus-thirty is striking. When McKenzie ran simulations of this conflict as CENTCOM commander, he says he would have rejected the current situation as impossibly optimistic — too much in America’s favor to be credible. The military campaign, in his read, is going well. Where he applies the analytical brakes is on the strategic endgame. He argues Kharg Island represents a genuine sweet spot: it’s Iranian soil, it controls ninety percent of Iran’s oil exports, and critically, it’s reversible — which is why, he suspects, the infrastructure there hasn’t been destroyed. You can give it back. That makes it a bargaining chip rather than a crater. Getting the MEU through the Strait and up the Gulf is the operational risk, but the planning for it, he notes, has been underway for decades. His fingerprints are on it. So are his predecessors’. Admiral Cooper is the one who gets to execute.
