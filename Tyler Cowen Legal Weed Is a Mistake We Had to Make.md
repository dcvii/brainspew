I supported legalization, and I didn’t expect such widespread addiction. Yet it’s still better than asking the state to thwart popular demand.

Should we regret marijuana legalization? By one recent count, [24 states](https://mjbizdaily.com/map-of-us-marijuana-legalization-by-state/) have legalized marijuana for recreational use since 2012. Seven additional states [have decriminalized it](https://www.ncsl.org/civil-and-criminal-justice/cannabis-overview) to varying degrees, and the Trump administration, in April, [moved state-licensed](https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/justice-department-places-fda-approved-marijuana-products-and-products-containing-marijuana) medical marijuana from Schedule I to Schedule III.

But I hardly know anyone who is happy with how this has turned out, and if I do, they may be too busy inhaling pot to express their opinions to me.

A Pew Research Center survey in January showed that 55 percent of Americans support legal weed for medical and recreational use, but that’s down from a peak of [60 percent](https://www.pewresearch.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/20/2026/05/SR_26.05.26_marijuana_topline.pdf) in 2021. I’m among that slice of people who have reconsidered whether legalizing would go well. Initially, I had the core libertarian intuition that consenting adults should be able to do with their own bodies as they wish. I have stayed at that point, but my intuition that this process would go smoothly has taken a real battering.

If you walk through major American cities such as New York or San Francisco, you (and your kid) may smell pot frequently. Yet it was never the intent of the law to have usage so plainly evident in public spaces. Instead, many of us were hoping for a libertarian paradise where everything would be tucked away at home, hidden from public view. That is not what we got, as it seems large numbers of people want to go around smoking marijuana.

Today’s marijuana is also [much stronger](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/public-health/articles/10.3389/fpubh.2024.1442522/full) than what was smoked or consumed by earlier generations. “Pot psychosis” is a common phenomenon, and an increasing body of evidence ties marijuana consumption to [motor-vehicle crashes](https://www.iihs.org/news/detail/crash-rates-jump-in-wake-of-marijuana-legalization-new-studies-show) and [poor workplace performance](https://www.ajpmonline.org/article/S0749-3797\(24\)00258-7/abstract).

Another argument for legalization was that a legitimate marijuana market would bankrupt the gangs and cartels that sell it. That has not happened either. Legal marijuana is often more expensive than the illegal version, in part due to regulations, and so the illegal income continues to flow. Black markets exist alongside the legal markets, and it is difficult for the law to justify handing out stiff sentences to the illegal sellers, given the general public’s acceptance of pot.

It was never the intent of the law to have marijuana usage so plainly evident in public spaces.

In short, we received plausible variations of legalized marijuana, with variations across over two dozen states and, as an initial supporter of pot legalization, I cannot claim triumph or vindication.

It’s possible that legalizing marijuana has helped to lower crime rates. It also might be true that marijuana has substituted for alcohol and alcoholism for many users, since drinking has [decreased decisively](https://news.gallup.com/poll/693362/drinking-rate-new-low-alcohol-concerns-surge.aspx). Still, an old-fashioned part of me feels that even if alcohol is worse for you than strong pot, the old-fashioned drink culture of socializing, with rough and tough macho figures such as Jack Kerouac and Jackson Pollock, was better and more creative than what we have been replacing it with (**NB**: I am myself a well-known teetotaler, and also have never tried marijuana).

So what adjustment in my views do I need to make?

I do not think we can or should go back to the bad old days of illegal marijuana. For one thing, a majority of Americans still support it, including [44 percent](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2026/05/26/facts-about-marijuana/) of Republicans or those who lean Republican. That means recriminalization will not happen, and if it did, it would be extremely oppressive in nature, throwing many well-meaning people in jail and turning ordinary users and sellers into lawbreakers.

As a libertarian compromise, I favor a regime that gives a civil fine to anyone caught smoking or vaping marijuana in public. Many would ignore such tickets, but they might push marijuana out of the public square just a bit, to the benefit of our children.

As for viewpoint revisions, we have to conclude that human beings—or at least Americans—are weaker and less productive than we thought they were. They are more prone to addiction and more vulnerable to increases in the potency of addictive products. Pot has become stronger, and by some standards, “better.” Demand has increased accordingly, and paternalistic responses are going to bring us only so far in that battle.

If so many people are so weak as to consume so much pot, they are also too weak to tolerate effective laws against it. Recall that we arrived at marijuana legalization because laws against marijuana were neither effective nor a socially supported solution.

So I am suggesting acceptance. Few are disappointed when wealthy people fail to give half of their income each year to charity. Whatever you might prefer, we have realized that is simply how humans are—namely, pretty selfish. We still can praise and admire those who go above and beyond the call of duty. And we can still use tax law and social norms to nudge people to give more than they otherwise might have.

If so many people are so weak as to consume so much pot, they are also too weak to tolerate effective laws against it.

Whether people prefer marijuana, alcohol, or something else, public regulation of addictive substances is never going to be very orderly or effective. And we face a future in which many drugs may be more potent and more addictive yet, as already has been the case with fentanyl.

The present and also future of mankind is a world where reasonably high levels of self-discipline are needed to do well. The journalist Daniel Akst pointed this out in his 2011 book *[Temptation: Finding Self-Control in an Age of Excess](https://bookshop.org/a/93116/9780143120803)*, and we are now living it full force.

I would rather cope with that world than face the full nanny state, backed by modern, AI-intensified surveillance techniques to boot. Concentrating more power in political authorities hardly solves the basic problem. If marijuana and sports gambling can manipulate weak individuals, so can unscrupulous political leaders. A greater realization of individual weakness does not translate into a case for more government action; if anything, it suggests the opposite. Better to allow our social problems to fester in a more decentralized fashion, rather than reinforce our social pathologies through a manipulative and dysfunctional leader at the very top.

In the longer term, we may need to look to medications, such as GLP-1 drugs and their offshoots, which seem to curb some forms of addictive behavior beyond the appetite for food. Alternatively, some individuals may choose self-surveillance, with self-imposed penalties for bad or addictive behavior. Perhaps your AI, or a hired third party, docks your bank account every time you puff on a joint. I am not convinced such services ever will become popular, but that should be taken seriously as an indicator of what people really want to do. We can at least give them better options for self-constraint. If they rarely choose such options, then perhaps for many of those people, marijuana consumption is not a matter of weakness but a very well-established preference, whether we like it or not.

One lesson is that a lot of different policies won’t work as well as we might have expected. Consider the recent growth of sports gambling. We now see sports gambling as a major problem, especially among young men, often leading to [excess debt and financial desperation](https://finance.yahoo.com/economy/policy/articles/young-americans-falling-behind-bills-173500107.html?guccounter=1&guce_referrer=aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cucGVycGxleGl0eS5haS8&guce_referrer_sig=AQAAAMcm4-ksQDfzQRzQhhCVRcHLSaS7D-bXFEFsLzIWj7_FAZRbiBM_-GksWZnuW0xmYp-pwCCHY9SlME0nuUzt5uGTdVZUA_cjQOOXMl2OEubjEnxWjsdeVxiRTLuKxasOrlyK0QY4XpOc0d25yuWbOXmPvUml2Dg8iaqX6DUSWf9H).

It is easy to think of some [recent court decisions](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/17pdf/16-476_dbfi.pdf) as mistaken, but sports gambling already was [moving toward legal status](https://www.multistate.us/insider/2018/5/24/supreme-court-opens-sports-betting-legalization-to-states) in some states. Tens of millions of Americans were interested in doing it, or were doing it already. Congress has repeatedly refused to ban or significantly restrict sports gambling. How long were we going to hold it at bay? So many Americans have smartphones, and the internet can easily connect us to gambling sites in other states or offshore. With the rise of crypto and other financial innovations, using banking restrictions and credit-card bans to keep people off those sites has only a limited shelf life. Nonetheless, it still might make sense to apply various nudges, such as making it harder or impossible to advertise sports gambling on television.

In short, it is time to realize that paternalism is far less workable than in times past. Our government does not have the credibility, the control over information, or the control over our lives to pull it off.

I do understand that is in some significant ways bad news, as voluntary choice is overwhelming some of us with bad outcomes.

My response is to start by accepting some steps backward, holding paternalist tyranny at bay, and hoping some longer-run cultural and technological adjustments will make this all more workable.

If you have a better solution, I would love to hear it.
