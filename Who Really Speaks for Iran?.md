The biggest challenge facing American negotiators this week as they head to Islamabad will not be wrangling over the removal of enriched uranium trapped beneath Iran’s destroyed nuclear facilities. It won’t be setting the terms for opening the Strait of Hormuz. And it won’t be trying to end the regime’s support for terrorist proxies in the Middle East.

As important as all of those issues are, the primary question now for the team led by Vice President J.D. Vance is understanding exactly who is calling the shots in Tehran.

Only a week ago, the Trump administration believed that the Iranian regime was run by a committee of [five senior officials](https://www.thefp.com/p/meet-the-new-faces-of-the-iranian) that included the two men leading their delegation in the Islamabad negotiations: Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi and the speaker of Iran’s parliament, Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf.

Now it appears that the regime’s real power is wielded by [Ahmad Vahidi](https://understandingwar.org/research/middle-east/iran-update-special-report-april-18-2026/), the chief of the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC). He is on the five-man council, but he is not part of the team negotiating a deal to end the war.

This helps explain the confusing messages coming out of Tehran and Washington. According to two Trump administration officials I spoke with, the president believed he was close to a deal with Araghchi and Ghalibaf on Friday. He posted on [Truth Social](https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/116420275523158052), in all caps, that the Strait of Hormuz was open. He followed that up with a post that said America would [take possession](https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/116420395293904982) of Iran’s [buried enriched uranium](https://abcnews.com/Politics/trump-suggests-us-iran-work-remove-nuclear-dust/story?id=131856150) —the “nuclear dust,” he called it—and that America would not even pay for it.

One Department of Defense official briefed on the Iran negotiations told me Friday that the talks were “inching toward a deal.” This source added that there were two primary issues remaining. One was Iran’s claim that it had a “right to enrich” uranium under the Nuclear Nonproliferation Treaty. The second was what combination of sanctions relief and unfrozen assets the regime would receive if it allowed the 1,000 pounds of 60 percent enriched uranium trapped under its facilities in Natanz and Isfahan to be removed.

The Iranian side also indicated a deal might be in the offing. On Friday morning, Araghchi [posted on X](https://x.com/araghchi/status/2045121573124759713?s=43&t=byiETAKtWmu04PPbl8hMcQ): “The passage of all commercial vessels through Strait of Hormuz is declared completely open for the remaining period of ceasefire.”

But by that evening, the Tasnim News agency, a reliable mouthpiece for the IRGC, accused Araghchi of spreading bad information. “Publishing this tweet, without any verbal explanation or at least sufficient written explanations, constitutes a complete lack of tact in communication,” Tasnim [published on X](https://x.com/Tasnimnews_Fa/status/2045161514579943896). “It is obvious that the Foreign Ministry itself must either reconsider this type of communication or the Secretariat of the Supreme National Security Council must fulfill its duty.”

Those words were backed up by action on Saturday when IRGC speedboats [fired on](https://www.theintelligencer.net/news/top-headlines/2026/04/iran-fully-closes-strait-of-hormuz-over-us-blockade-and-fires-on-ships-2/) French, Indian, and British vessels seeking to cross the strait.

Trump still maintained on Sunday that the Islamabad talks were on. He announced on Truth Social that the [U.S. delegation](https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/116431297579272777) would be arriving on Monday evening in Pakistan’s capital. But that post also threatened Iran’s regime. “We’re offering a very fair and reasonable DEAL,” Trump said, “and I hope they take it because, if they don’t, the United States is going to knock out every single Power Plant, and every single Bridge, in Iran. NO MORE MR. NICE GUY!” On Sunday afternoon, Trump announced that the Navy had [boarded and seized](https://www.washingtonpost.com/world/2026/04/19/trump-iran-war-hormuz-strait-negotiations/) an Iranian ship trying to break the U.S. blockade.

Meanwhile, on Sunday afternoon, the official news agency of Iran’s regime ran a story that said the Ghalibaf-Aragchi team [would not be participating](https://www.iranintl.com/en/202604195885) in the second round of ceasefire talks in Pakistan.

So where does that leave things? Trump may have spooked the IRGC when, in his announcement that the Iranians had lifted their blockade of the Strait of Hormuz, he said the U.S. military blockade would remain in place until a full deal was reached. According to the Defense official, this was interpreted by Vahidi and others in the IRGC as an unacceptable provocation. This may explain why the IRGC closed the strait after Aragchi announced it would be open.

But there is a deeper problem as well. If Vahidi and his IRGC henchmen are now the real power in Iran, then war is almost certain to break out after the two-week ceasefire expires on Wednesday. The [Institute for the Study of War](https://understandingwar.org/research/middle-east/iran-update-special-report-april-18-2026/) on Saturday concluded in its daily analysis that the IRGC had consolidated control over the negotiations. This means that Ghalibaf and Araghchi “do not have the authority to independently determine Iran’s negotiating positions.”

No matter the status of the talks, Trump would be wise to find out exactly who his real interlocutors are before sending his vice president to Pakistan to negotiate with Iranians who lack the power to make an actual deal.
