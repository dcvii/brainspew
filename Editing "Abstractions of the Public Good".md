It’s difficult for me to talk about the public good in the abstract because I do not serve the public directly. As I look back on my career there has been a single constant idea in my mind. In fact it is so deeply ingrained that I have a specific intersection of streets I was at a stoplight for when I came up with the aphorism.

***“I’d rather make 1 million dollars from 17 people, than 17 dollars from 1 million people.”***

The context was the second album by rapper 50 Cent, which disappointed some fans of his. He was entirely dismissive of the criticism. What would he care about some ten thousand or fifty thousand haters? I asked myself about that kind of situation and a lot of things suddenly made sense to me. Firstly, I’m not that kind of person, and mine is not that kind of art. I would much prefer to build something that would provide lasting value at the level of institutional change. Something worth, in my creative burst of glory, a million dollars of value that only a few wealthy geniuses would recognize. Even writing this out today produces new insights and contrasts.

You see today I am working for the County of Los Angeles as a driver in support of the primary election coming up on June 2. I’ve got the badge and the yellow vest. I’ll be a cog in the machine of collecting and counting millions of ballots. I have already worked in the state of the art facility with hundreds of other citizens. I have already started to feel that well-wrapped sense of everyday camaraderie with people who don’t get paid to *think*, but get paid to *do*. This will be my second union job.

**TikTok Choreography  
**If you’ve ever done manual labor of any sort in coordination with other people, then you understand something about the zone you can get in doing things with your hands and body. There comes a point where the person you’re standing next to doing a repetitive thing gets a feeling for where their hands end and where yours begin. This is a fascinating area of human endeavor and we instinctually understand it. TikTok has revealed this to us in its endless rotation of both slapstick and choreography, two ends of the same physical drama.

Inevitably I have been drawn to the long form in which I watch some handy fellow build a shelter on the side of a wooded slope, or dig a mudbath deep into the earth or even tear down 20 years of weeds, trees, trash and obstructions surrounding an old abandoned building. Most people know the story of the Salt Bae, some handsome dude who prepared steaks and became internet famous.

**Trivial Knowledge**  
As a kid, I was devastating at Trivial Pursuit. I also read the tactical book on how to win at Monopoly. There was also a very challenging game to my values called Dealer’s Choice which became an excellent lesson in the **marginal revolution** by gamifying the business of buying and selling. Playing with my brother, it was very hard for me to understand that he’s trying to lie to me and I’m trying to lie to him about the condition of a cool car. We had limited chances to actually ‘look under the hood’ which implied a breaching of trust. It turned out that I was a better liar than he was. It was a game I didn’t like winning so much. I much preferred Monopoly, Trivial Pursuit, building circuits, memorizing lyrics, cooking breakfast and fixing broken windows. Objective good for the house.

These days, my baby daughter absolutely destroys us in word games. I have never once in her 29 years beat her in a game of Boggle. There’s a Netflix version of the game we play on our cell phones with the big screen. We also watch a channel on YouTube called **Extra Medium Stuff**, in which people get paid to answer, through several elimination rounds, a series of random questions on geography, math, TV, the Periodic Table, and mostly things you have a vague idea of memorizing in highschool. These run about 20 minutes and the biggest prize is $1000, but sometimes as low as $200.

It’s interesting to know that two countries have absolutely square flags, and that only Nepal’s is a concave pentagon. It’s times like these, at least for me, that I become very curious about the value of what I know.
