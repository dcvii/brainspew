Biden’s antitrust cops promised that blocking a JetBlue-Spirit merger would help consumers. We’re seeing the results.

There are a lot of ways to kill a business. You can mismanage it. You can drown it in debt. Or, in a more modern Washington twist, you can force it to compete on the government’s terms and leave it without a chance of survival.

That last option was favored by President Joe Biden and his antitrust brain trust, and it just led to the demise of Spirit Airlines. The ultra–low-cost carrier had carried on for 34 years before it finally folded on Saturday, stranding passengers and putting roughly 17,000 employees out of work. The Trump administration had floated a $500 million bailout in return for a 90 percent government stake in the company, but most bondholders preferred to liquidate instead of dragging out the pain.

Policy wasn’t the only factor behind Spirit’s collapse. The cost of fuel has spiked several times since 2022, including during the ongoing Iran war, and inflation has squeezed the budgets of its price-sensitive customers. But Spirit was blocked from its final chance to turn around in 2024, when the Biden Justice Department blocked its proposed merger with JetBlue on antitrust grounds.

At the time, progressives cheered. Senator Elizabeth Warren declared, “This is a Biden win for fliers!” [while warning](https://www.foxbusiness.com/politics/sen-warren-blasted-cheering-blocking-merger-might-have-saved-spirit-airlines) the merger would mean “fewer flights and higher fares.”

Fast-forward two years, and fliers have something even worse than “fewer flights” on Spirit: no flights at all.

Antitrust is a valid and necessary tool, but only when it actually expands consumer choice. Used poorly, it risks becoming less about protecting competition and more about a broader ideological skepticism of business that can narrow the very choices it’s meant to defend.

---

The Biden administration’s antitrust philosophy treated every mergeras inherently suspect. Smaller was always supposed to be better.In airlines, that meant freezing Spirit in place. Elsewhere, it has meant scrutinizing or blocking deals that might have produced stronger, better-scaled competitors.

The government argued that letting JetBlue absorb Spirit would eliminate a needed competitor. That might have been true in a static world. But in the real world, airlines operate in a world of volatile jet fuel prices, labor shortages, and razor-thin margins. Spirit had accumulated billions of dollars in losses and carried substantial debt into the merger fight. Blocking the deal didn’t preserve a healthy, low-cost rival; it only locked in a fragile one.

Transportation Secretary Pete Buttigieg supported the Justice Department’s lawsuit, [arguing the merger](https://www.foxbusiness.com/politics/sen-warren-blasted-cheering-blocking-merger-might-have-saved-spirit-airlines) would “eliminat\[e\] the largest, most aggressive ultra–low-cost competitor” and “substantially reduc\[e\] competition.”

Now, even some Biden-world figures have reportedly expressed second thoughts. That’s not surprising. “Given the news today that Spirit Airlines is shuttering and thousands of people are losing their jobs, I think we should honestly assess whether the \[Merrick\]Garland DoJ stopping the JetBlue merger with Spirit Airlines was the right call,” [wrote Neera Tanden](https://x.com/neeratanden/status/2050559455100604514?s=20), former top domestic policy adviser, on X Saturday. Antitrust enforcement is supposed to be grounded in real-world outcomes, not just structural ideals.

To understand the stakes, consider the customers who actually flew Spirit. It was the last, best choicefor millions: college students hunting for $49 fares, families stretching budgets for a once-a-year vacation, workers chasing affordable routes across the country. Its famously unbundled pricing model—pay for the seat, then everything else—was easy to mock but economically meaningful. It forced competitors to keep prices in check.

Good antitrust policy is not about stopping deals for the sake of stopping them, or signaling toughness toward big business. It’s about whether a given tie-up will leave consumers better off.

Meanwhile, Warren stood by the original decision, reiterating that mergers like the proposed deal risk higher prices and fewer choices. That concern wasn’t invented. But in Spirit’s case, the feared outcome arrived by a different route.

None of this means airline mergers should be waved through without scrutiny. Airline consolidation has a mixed history, and there are legitimate concerns about reduced service and higher prices. The combined business also might have buckled under the same pressures that ultimately sank Spirit. And the proposed Trump administration bailout likewise stood little chance of changing Spirit’s fundamentals.

But good antitrust policy is not about stopping deals for the sake of stopping them, or signaling toughness toward big business. It’s about whether a given tie-up will leave consumers better off. In Spirit’s case, regulators answered that question too narrowly. And more broadly, a persistent skepticism of large businesses risks repeating that mistake in other sectors.

---

Ironically, the blocked merger might have created a larger airline better able to compete with the industry’s dominant carriers. Instead, its absence eliminated a disruptive rival entirely, strengthening the big players that antitrust policy is meant to discipline.

In Washington, it’s tempting to treat antitrust as a weapon to play around with—standing up to big business, drawing bright lines, claiming victories. But markets are less interested in symbolism than the real-world viability of the businesses involved.

Antitrust remains one of the most powerful tools policymakers have. Used well, it can expand opportunity, lower prices, and foster innovation. Used carelessly, or ideologically, it can do the opposite. The lesson of Spirit’s demise isn’t that antitrust is misguided. It’s that it must be applied cautiously, with a focus on what consumers actually experience.
