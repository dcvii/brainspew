In late March, a small investment fund that owns tiny stakes in SpaceX and Anthropic started trading on a stock exchange. Within four days, people had bid the price up more than 1,500% above the actual value of what the fund owns. Trading was halted twice because the price was moving too fast. Investors were paying sixteen dollars for every one dollar of real value, not because they didn’t know the math, but because the fund was one of the only ways to get a piece of companies that haven’t gone public yet.

That’s not investing. That’s a line outside the club. And the club is about to open its doors.

SpaceX, OpenAI, and Anthropic, collectively valued at over $3 trillion, are all planning to go public within the next twelve months. Together they want to raise roughly $170 to $195 billion from investors — against a market that handled $47 billion in IPOs all of last year. ## First, the scale problem

When a company “goes public,” it sells a chunk of itself to investors for the first time. That’s all an IPO is. The company gets cash it can use to grow, and investors get shares they can trade.

Every IPO in America last year raised a combined $47 billion. That was the best year in a while, a real recovery after a few dead years. That’s all the biotechs, all the tech companies, all the industrial firms, everything. Forty-seven billion dollars, total.

SpaceX alone is planning to raise $50 to $75 billion. In a single offering. That would be the largest IPO in history by a factor of two, blowing past Saudi Aramco’s 2019 record.

Then OpenAI plans to raise $60 billion or more. Then Anthropic plans to raise $60 billion or more.

Three companies. Roughly $170 to $195 billion they want to pull from investors. Against a market that handled $47 billion last year.

If you’re thinking “that doesn’t add up,” you’re right. It doesn’t. And everyone involved knows it. Which brings us to the trick.

## Why they’ll sell almost nothing (and why that’s the problem)

When a company goes public, it typically puts 15-25% of its shares on the open market. That slice is called the “float,” the portion that you and I can actually buy and sell. The rest stays with founders, employees, and early investors.

SpaceX is expected to put only about 3.3% on the market. That’s it. Just enough to raise $50-75 billion while Elon Musk and early investors keep almost everything.

This is how you square the circle. You don’t need the market to cough up $450 billion (which would be required at a normal 25% float on a $1.75 trillion valuation). You just sell a tiny sliver. The math works, on paper.

But here’s what happens when you make a tiny amount of something available and an enormous number of people want it: the price goes haywire. It’s the Ticketmaster problem applied to the stock market. Constrained supply. Overwhelming demand. The price you end up paying reflects the scarcity, not the underlying value.

Think about it this way. If a restaurant has 200 seats and 250 people want a reservation, the experience is roughly normal. If a restaurant has 10 seats and 10,000 people want in, you’re not paying for dinner anymore. You’re paying for access. That’s what a 3% float at a trillion-dollar valuation looks like.

PitchBook’s analysts have already done the math on SpaceX specifically. At a 3.3% float, they expect the stock to swing 20-30% on a single piece of news, roughly double the volatility of Tesla, and Tesla is famously one of the most volatile large stocks in existence. This isn’t a side effect. It’s a feature of the structure.

And all three companies are likely to use the same playbook.

## Now here’s where your retirement account comes in

This next part is the thing that hasn’t gotten nearly enough attention, and it’s the reason I wrote this piece.

If you have a 401(k) or any kind of retirement fund, there’s a very good chance it holds index funds. An index fund is simple: instead of trying to pick winners, it just buys every company in a given list. The S&P 500 index fund buys shares in all 500 companies in the S&P 500. The Nasdaq-100 fund buys all 100 companies in the Nasdaq-100. Your fund does this automatically. You don’t pick the stocks. The index does.

This system works beautifully, until someone games the list.

This is what’s happening. On May 1, Nasdaq’s new index rules take effect. Under the old rules, a company had to trade publicly for months, sometimes a full year, before it could be added to a major index. The logic was simple: let the market figure out what a company is actually worth before forcing every index fund in the country to buy it.

Under the new rules, a company as large as SpaceX can be added to the Nasdaq-100 after just fifteen trading days. Three calendar weeks after its first day of trading.

And here’s the critical wrinkle: Nasdaq will use a company’s *total* market capitalization, all $1.75 trillion, to determine whether it qualifies for the index. But the actual index *weight* gets capped: for companies with less than 20% free float, Nasdaq limits the weight to five times the float percentage. SpaceX’s 3.3% float gets treated as roughly 16.5%. That’s not the full $1.75 trillion. But it’s still a massive forced-buying event, because every dollar of index weight translates into billions of mandatory purchases by funds that track the benchmark.

SpaceX reportedly made fast-track index inclusion a condition for choosing to list on Nasdaq instead of the New York Stock Exchange. The company understood exactly what it was asking for.

S&P is considering similar fast-track rules for the S&P 500, and FTSE Russell is doing the same for its indexes. Between the three of them, more than $30 trillion in fund assets are connected to these benchmarks. When a stock gets added, every fund tracking that index is *required* to buy it. Not “encouraged.” Required. It’s automatic. It’s written into the fund’s rules.

So here’s the chain of events:

1. SpaceX goes public in June, selling 3-5% of itself.
2. Within three weeks, Nasdaq adds it to the Nasdaq-100, weighted at roughly five times its actual float — enough to make it one of the heaviest stocks in the index on day one.
3. Every fund tracking the Nasdaq-100 is now required to buy SpaceX stock.
4. Those funds are competing with each other to buy from a pool of shares that represents only 3-5% of the company.
5. The price spikes, not because of some revelation about the company’s value, but because the buying is mandatory and the supply is artificially small.

Bloomberg Intelligence has run the numbers. If all three companies sell 5% of their shares and get fast-tracked into major indexes, funds would need to buy between $24 billion and $48 billion worth of stock. And because professional money managers are also measured against these indexes (meaning they fall behind if they don’t own what the index owns) the total wave of buying could absorb the majority of all available shares within days.

More than half the tradeable stock, potentially bought in a single week, by funds that have no choice.

That’s your 401(k). That’s the target-date fund you picked when you started your job and haven’t thought about since. Those vehicles will buy SpaceX, OpenAI, and Anthropic on your behalf, at whatever price the market is offering, during a period of historically limited supply. You don’t get a vote.

## What happens after the headlines

When these stocks start trading, they will almost certainly spike. Every financial outlet will cover the “stunning debut” and the “massive day-one gains.” That coverage will make the listing feel like a validation of the company’s value.

It isn’t. It’s a symptom of the supply constraint. When only 3-5% of a company is available for trading and trillions of dollars in fund money gets funneled through that tiny opening, the initial price reflects scarcity, not fundamentals.

The real question, the one that actually determines whether this was a good deal for you, is what happens when the scarcity ends.

Here’s how that works. When a company goes public, the founders, early employees, and investors who already own shares aren’t allowed to sell them right away. They’re “locked up,” typically for 90 to 180 days. The logic is straightforward: you don’t want insiders dumping their stock on day one and crashing the price.

But when that lock expires, three to six months after the IPO, the 97% of the company that *wasn’t* available starts flowing onto the market. Supply goes from a trickle to a firehose. And the people selling are the insiders who got in at a tiny fraction of the public price. A venture capital firm that invested in SpaceX at a $46 billion valuation in 2020 is sitting on a 38x return at $1.75 trillion. When the lock expires, they sell. That’s their entire business model.

The buyers on the other side? That’s the index funds. That’s your retirement account. That’s the automatic demand that was built into the stock from week three.

Insiders sell into the post-IPO price. Index funds bought at the supply-constrained price. The gap between those two prices is a wealth transfer. There’s no polite way to say who it transfers from and who it transfers to. But if you have a 401(k), you’re on the wrong end.

## Why this matters if you work in AI

If you’re at a pre-IPO AI company, and not one of these three, you should understand that the ground is shifting beneath you, too.

AI startups captured over half of all global venture capital last year: roughly $270 billion, according to an analysis of PitchBook data. That sounds like a feast. But the money didn’t spread evenly. OpenAI’s $122 billion raise, Anthropic’s $30 billion raise, and xAI’s $20 billion raise accounted for a staggering share of the total. The number of startups getting funded actually *fell* even as total dollars rose. The money is concentrating into fewer and fewer companies at larger and larger scale.

This is what a winner-take-most capital market looks like. Venture firms that haven’t been able to cash out their investments for five straight years are now watching the only realistic path to a payday, IPOs, get dominated by three companies whose listings will vacuum up Wall Street’s attention and capital for the next 12-18 months. If you’re the fourth- or fifth-best company in your AI category, you’re being told, politely or otherwise, to find a buyer or find a bridge loan.

This has a direct effect on your equity. If you’re holding stock options or restricted shares at an AI startup, the value of those shares depends on your company eventually having a “liquidity event,” an IPO, an acquisition, something that lets you turn paper into cash. That event has to happen in a market with finite capacity. And right now, the market’s capacity is being consumed by companies much larger than yours.

The number on your equity statement is real in the same way a concert ticket is real before you check whether the venue exists.

## The quiet part

There’s one more thing that reframes all of this, and nobody leading the hype cycle wants to talk about it.

OpenAI is expected to lose $14 billion in 2026. Not a little. Fourteen billion. Its annual cash burn is projected to reach $57 billion by 2027. It doesn’t expect to be profitable until 2030. Even after raising $122 billion in its last private round, the largest private fundraise in the history of technology, the company has roughly 18-24 months of operating runway before it needs to raise again.

And here’s the part that makes the IPO timeline make sense. In January 2025, Sam Altman stood next to President Trump and announced Stargate, a $500 billion AI infrastructure project that would build massive data centers across the United States. It was supposed to be the physical backbone of the AI revolution, owned and operated by OpenAI.

It didn’t work out that way. OpenAI tried to finance the construction itself. Banks said no. Lenders looked at a company burning billions a year with no path to profitability until 2030 and refused to underwrite the buildout. The company walked away from a planned expansion of its flagship Stargate campus in Abilene, Texas. Total projected compute spending has been revised down from $1.4 trillion to roughly $600 billion, and instead of owning data centers, OpenAI is now renting capacity from Amazon Web Services, Google Cloud, and others.

Read that sequence carefully. The company that announced a half-trillion-dollar infrastructure project at the White House couldn’t get traditional lenders to fund it. The debt markets looked at the books and passed. So now the public equity markets, meaning your index funds, your 401(k), your retirement savings, are the next door to knock on.

The IPO isn’t a celebration. It’s a funding round. The public market is the lender of last resort after every other source of capital has either been tapped or has declined.

In February, SpaceX absorbed xAI, Musk’s AI company, which was burning through roughly $1 billion per month. SpaceX itself is in better financial shape, profitable, growing, real business, but it’s now carrying the weight of an AI moonshot that has yet to show it can sustain itself.

Anthropic has a potential accounting problem that could surface during the IPO process. The company counts certain cloud computing credits from Amazon and Google as revenue, essentially, its cloud partners give it computing resources, and Anthropic books the value of those resources as income. Bank of America estimates these cloud payments could reach $1.9 billion in 2026 and up to $6.4 billion by 2027. If regulators decide that treatment doesn’t fly and force Anthropic to change how it reports those numbers before listing, its revenue story shrinks significantly.

None of this means these are bad companies. They’re not. They’re building technology that is genuinely reshaping how work gets done. The AI revolution is real.

But the financial event you’re being invited to participate in, whether you want to or not through your index funds, is not a simple bet on that revolution. It’s a bet made at artificially high prices, through a deliberately tiny supply of shares, fast-tracked into your retirement account by index rules that were rewritten in the last sixty days to serve the interests of the companies doing the listing.

## What actually happens next

Something has to give. There are really only three ways this plays out:

**The floats stay tiny and insiders wait years to sell.** This preserves the headline valuations — SpaceX at $1.75 trillion, OpenAI at $1 trillion — but it means those numbers are aspirational, not real. A stock price set by 3% of the company trading hands is not the same thing as a stock price set by an open market. If you hold equity at one of these companies, the number on your statement looks life-changing. But you can’t spend it until the market actually agrees, at scale, to pay that price. That could take years.

**The valuations come down.** If public AI stocks correct, and some analysts are already warning about valuation compression in the sector, it becomes much harder for private companies to list at these levels. An S-1 filing is a moment of truth. It’s the first time these companies have to show their real financial statements to the public. If the numbers don’t match the narrative, the market will reprice them.

**The forced buying creates a self-reinforcing loop.** Index inclusion drives mandatory buying. Mandatory buying drives the price up. A higher price validates the valuation. A validated valuation drives more index weight, which drives more mandatory buying. If it works, these companies maintain their valuations through structural demand rather than fundamental analysis. The problem is that self-reinforcing loops in financial markets have a name. They’re called bubbles. And they have a predictable second act.

I’ll be honest — I don’t know which of these wins, and I’d be cautious about anyone who says they do. My instinct says the valuations come down. The pricing structure here is artificially inflated by design, and structures like that tend to correct. But I’ve also watched artificially inflated things stay inflated for far longer than anyone predicted. The forced-buying mechanism here is genuinely new. We haven’t seen index rules weaponized at this scale before, and the people designing this structure are not amateurs.

So I’m not making a prediction. I’m telling you to understand the machinery before it runs, because by the time your 401(k) statement shows up with SpaceX in it, the decision will already have been made for you.
