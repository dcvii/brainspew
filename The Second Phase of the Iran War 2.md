At an underground complex deep inside central Iran’s Zagros Mountains, Western satellites in February detected dump trucks, cement mixers, and cranes [fortifying entranceways](https://isis-online.org/isis-reports/imagery-update-new-developments-at-pickaxe-mountain-tunnel-entrances) into a nuclear facility known as Pickaxe Mountain. Iranian officials have previously told United Nations inspectors that the site—buried under more than 300 feet of rock—was designed to house a manufacturing line for centrifuge machines. But U.S. and Israeli officials believe Pickaxe may have a far more important role for Tehran: producing nuclear fuel and warehousing enriched uranium potentially transferred from other sites destroyed by American and Israeli bombs over the past year.

President Trump hinted at his administration’s interest in Pickaxe at a White House press conference last week. “They were starting work at another site, a different site... that was protected by granite,” [he said](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/politics/watch-live-trump-holds-news-conference-to-answer-questions-about-iran), without mentioning Pickaxe by name. (A U.S. official confirmed he was referring to the mountain site.) “They wanted to go a lot deeper, and they started the process.”

Pickaxe is one of dozens of nuclear sites that the U.S. and Israel are likely to strike in the coming weeks as their war against Iran enters what senior Trump administration officials told me is its “second phase.”

Still, numerous sites in Iran’s nuclear supply chain, including Pickaxe Mountain, remain.

Much of the first two weeks of the campaign has focused on degrading Iran’s offensive military capabilities, including ballistic missile batteries, attack drones, and naval assets, which allow Tehran’s theocratic rulers to project power far beyond Iran’s borders. American and Israeli strikes have also eliminated, in recent days, many inside Iran’s elite military chain of command, including Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei and his top generals and national security advisers.

Last Friday, the U.S. also struck Iranian military positions on Kharg Island in the Persian Gulf, through which [90 percent](https://www.thefp.com/p/the-island-at-the-heart-of-irans) of the country’s oil exports flow. Trump said he hasn’t yet ordered attacks on Iranian energy installations there, but would do so if Tehran continued to block Western commercial traffic transiting through the Strait of Hormuz.

The next phase of the war will focus heavily on Iran’s remaining nuclear assets, according to Trump administration officials. They said the president remains committed to eliminating any future ability of the current Iranian regime, or any government that may succeed it, to reconstitute a nuclear program that was severely diminished by the U.S. and Israel in June’s 12-day war.

Last summer, American stealth B-2s, using massive GBU-57 bunker buster bombs, flattened Tehran’s primary nuclear fuel-production sites at Fordow, Isfahan, and Natanz. And Israel’s military and spy service killed roughly a dozen of Iran’s top nuclear scientists, including those who were part of an [Iranian military unit called SPND](https://www.iranwatch.org/iranian-entities/organization-defensive-innovation-and-research), which American and Israeli intelligence believe oversees Tehran’s atomic weapons research.

Still, numerous sites in Iran’s nuclear supply chain, including Pickaxe Mountain, remain. And the UN’s nuclear watchdog, the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA), has reported that [roughly 900 pounds](https://www.newsweek.com/iran-uranium-missing-iaea-grossi-2090434) of Iran’s stockpile of highly enriched uranium is buried under the sites destroyed by the U.S. and Israel last June, particularly Fordow and Isfahan. This includes 440 pounds of materials enriched to near weapons-grade uranium.

The Trump administration and Israel are currently assessing how to disable these remaining nuclear facilities and access, if not seize, the enriched uranium stocks, U.S. officials told me. The further dismantling in recent weeks of Iran’s military capabilities, including Tehran’s air defenses, could ease the ability of the U.S. and Israel to destroy Pickaxe and other facilities, and potentially open the path for American and Israeli ground forces to seize the canisters storing the enriched uranium.

Satellite view of construction at a mountainous site south of the Natanz uranium enrichment facility, where Iran is building an underground centrifuge assembly plant. (Satellite image © 2019 Maxar Technologies via Getty Images)

But these U.S. officials also said that deploying U.S. forces into underground tunnels at places like Isfahan and Pickaxe pose major risks for President Trump in a war that so far has produced only limited American fatalities.

Over the weekend, the Pentagon [dispatched more warships](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/03/14/us/politics/marines-iran-war.html) and an amphibious assault group, consisting of around 5,000 Marines and sailors, to the Persian Gulf. A Marine Expeditionary Unit, or MEU, is often used in complex ground operations, such as what would be needed to access Isfahan or Pickaxe. “The coming week is going to get very violent,” a senior Trump administration war planner told me.

Last summer, just days before the onset of the 12-day war, Iran informed the IAEA in Vienna that it was constructing a new enrichment facility “ [in a secure location](https://www.npr.org/2025/06/12/nx-s1-5431395/iran-nuclear-enrichment-un-compliance),” due to the increased pressure placed on the Islamic Republic by the UN agency. U.S., Israeli, and UN nuclear analysts eventually gravitated toward Pickaxe Mountain in central Iran as its likely location, due to the construction taking place around the site and the fact that it is just one mile from the Natanz enrichment plant, which was largely destroyed in June.

Pickaxe Mountain, though, poses a quandary for U.S. and Israeli military planners. Its depth makes it difficult to target even with the Massive Ordnance Penetrators (MOPs), or GBU-57s, employed last June. The Pentagon successfully destroyed the Fordow enrichment facility, which is also built deep into a mountain, by shooting roughly a half-dozen MOPs down ventilator shafts into the mountain. It’s unclear if Pickaxe Mountain has similar vulnerabilities, or if U.S. and Israeli ground forces would be needed to destroy whatever’s inside the facility.

The next phase of the war will focus heavily on Iran’s remaining nuclear assets.

Efforts to destroy or seize Iran’s enriched uranium stockpiles also pose a major risk, U.S. officials told me. *The New York Times* [reported this month](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/03/07/us/politics/iran-nuclear-site-uranium-intel.html) that U.S. intelligence analysts determined that Iran could start to access the highly enriched uranium entombed at the Isfahan plant south of Tehran through entrance points cut into the bombed-out facility. American forces could seek to enter through the same openings in a bid to seize and safeguard the fissile materials. But this could result in harrowing underground battles for U.S. or Israeli troops.

“I don’t think I would want to be in the military force that has to try to go into some narrow tunnel, and for some indeterminate amount of time, that could be heavily booby-trapped,” said David Albright, president of the Institute for Science and International Security, who has extensively studied Iran’s enrichment facilities.

Even as the war enters its third week, the Trump administration and Iran continue to argue about Tehran’s nuclear ambitions. Trump’s chief envoy to Iran, Steve Witkoff, said this month that during a series of negotiations he held with Iranian diplomats just before the war, the Islamic Republic refused to make any concessions on its nuclear program. He said that Iran’s foreign minister, Abbas Araghchi, admitted during a meeting in Oman that his government had enough enriched uranium for 11 nuclear bombs and that it would never give it up. “You’re not going to get from us diplomatically what you could not achieve militarily,” Witkoff told [the radio host Mark Levin](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bIXxkfbEa7Q) about his encounter with Iran’s top diplomat.

Araghchi has fired back that neither Witkoff nor President Trump’s son-in-law, Jared Kushner, had the technical competence to understand the nuclear file and that an agreement had been obtainable. But on Sunday, Araghchi said he now saw no reason to return to the negotiating table and that the war would continue. In an interview with Margaret Brennan on CBS’s *[Face the Nation](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/iranian-foreign-minister-abbas-araghchi-interview-trump-face-the-nation/)*, he said “We never asked for a ceasefire, and we have never asked even for negotiation.”
