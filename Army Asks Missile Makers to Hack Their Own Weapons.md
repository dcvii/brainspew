The U.S. Army is pushing defense contractors to provide access to weapons’ software to improve their interoperability and help integrate them with emerging artificial-intelligence tools.

The Pentagon said Tuesday that nine companies have agreed to integrate data from systems ranging from radar to surface-to-air missile batteries. Officials said participants include traditional contractors like [Lockheed Martin](https://www.wsj.com/market-data/quotes/LMT) and [Boeing](https://www.wsj.com/market-data/quotes/BA) as well as newer companies such as [Palantir Technologies](https://www.wsj.com/market-data/quotes/PLTR) [PLTR -0.65%decrease; red down pointing triangle](https://www.wsj.com/market-data/quotes/PLTR) and Anduril Industries.

“We had a lot of closed systems,” Army Secretary Dan Driscoll said in a briefing. “Our air- and missile-defense systems existed within environments that didn’t allow them to connect easily with our next-generation command and control.”

The project targets a long-term frustration for military officials: Different weapons systems aren’t always designed to talk to each other. Weaving together multiple arrays of sensors, vehicles and weapons is often a drawn-out process that leads the government to issue more engineering contracts, driving up costs.

The Army said the new project will kick off in the coming weeks with a “hackathon” at Fort Carson, Colo., for company engineers to test new software interfaces.

Officials said they would use Anduril’s Lattice software platform to validate the engineers’ work. The testing role is a sign of the venture-capital-backed company’s growing role in major Pentagon contracts despite [some setbacks](https://www.wsj.com/business/anduril-industries-defense-tech-problems-52b90cae?mod=article_inline) in early tests.

Anduril in March clinched a contract valued at up to $20 billion over the next decade that will consolidate work under multiple Army projects.

The Pentagon’s [Golden Dome antimissile program](https://www.wsj.com/politics/national-security/trump-golden-dome-america-defense-contractors-7bc332a4?mod=article_inline) has launched its own effort to develop command-and-control software across a consortium of companies. The Wall Street Journal in March reported that Anduril and Palantir [were playing leading roles](https://www.wsj.com/politics/national-security/anduril-palantir-are-developing-golden-dome-missile-shields-software-63c36db4?mod=article_inline) in the software project, which is scheduled to showcase some of its capabilities later this summer.

The military is prodding contractors to make their weapons more compatible with AI models. The Defense Department last week reached agreements with [eight AI companies](https://www.wsj.com/tech/ai/top-ai-companies-agree-to-pentagon-deals-for-classified-work-9c621e78?mod=article_inline) to use their software tools in classified settings.

AI has turbocharged the U.S. and Israeli war effort against Iran, which has incorporated new software to plan and execute operations, [the Journal has reported](https://www.wsj.com/tech/ai/how-ai-is-turbocharging-the-war-in-iran-aca59002?mod=article_inline).

Troops often rely on battlefield hardware designed decades ago. The military sees an opportunity to make its operations more efficient by clearing the way for older hardware to interact with newer software, including AI models. An interceptor system’s highly detailed missile-tracking data, for instance, can be hard to share in real-time with other military systems, including the defenses of allied nations.

“What we are asking these companies—and it is an ask—is expose the interface,” Army technology chief Alex Miller said in an interview. That will help assure the government that companies are good partners, he said, while providing “a great way for their engineers to show what they can do.”
