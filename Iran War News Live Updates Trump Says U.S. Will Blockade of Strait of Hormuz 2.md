### [Why Trump’s Use of Term ‘Blockade’ Has Broad Implications](https://www.wsj.com/livecoverage/iran-war-latest-news-israel-us-lebanon-2026/card/why-trump-s-use-of-term-blockade-has-broad-implications-FgM05lPLn1FGXiF7Fvk6)

![A vessel at the Strait of Hormuz on Sunday.](https://images.wsj.net/im-47869428?width=620&height=413)

A vessel at the Strait of Hormuz on Sunday. Reuters

By using the word “blockade” to describe how the U.S. intends to take control from Iran of shipping at the Strait of Hormuz, President Trump has invoked a term with broad implications for military action.

Legal experts say nations avoid the word blockade because it carries the weight of a declaration of a war, unlike an “embargo” designed to stop particular goods or “quarantine” aimed to halt the spread of something dangerous. Trump likewise used the word blockade to describe plans to stop ships from exporting Venezuelan crude a few weeks before ordering the U.S. military to seize its president.

When President John F. Kennedy threatened to stop the Soviet Union from shipping nuclear missiles to Cuba in 1962, he called the action a “quarantine.” Beijing is viewed as more likely to declare a quarantine of Taiwan to put a squeeze on the democratically run island rather than a blockade to avoid signalling it has launched a war, according to China analysts. Britain undertook a North Sea blockade of German ships during the First World War by declaring the waters “a military area.”

“A blockade is a belligerent operation to prevent vessels and-or aircraft of all nations, enemy and neutral, from entering or exiting specified ports, airports, or coastal areas belonging to, occupied by, or under the control of an enemy nation,” according to a definition published by Oxford University Press.
