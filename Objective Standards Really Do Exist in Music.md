A few weeks ago, I got pushback because of a passing comment I made in the article [“My Nine Rules of Criticism.”](https://www.honest-broker.com/p/my-9-rules-of-criticism)

“Music has far more objective standards than most people realize,” I wrote. For some readers, these were fighting words. Naysayers came back at me in full Big Lebowski mode—declaring: *That’s just, like, your opinion, man*.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!3MN8!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_lossy/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0db40b42-0b8e-433e-9273-be9a563c354c_500x281.gif)

That’s how they see *all* evaluations of music—and of other art forms, too. Everybody has an opinion, they tell me, and they’re all equally subjective.

I can see why this is a popular view. It’s a very democratic notion, and a great equalizer. A music critic gets a vote, but so does the butcher, the baker, and the candlestick maker.

Everybody picks their favorite song, their favorite musician. Whether it’s Beethoven and Bach, or just “Disco Duck,” it’s all good, man.

I wish I could agree with this.

It would burnish my populist (and [poptimist](https://www.nytimes.com/2014/04/06/magazine/the-pernicious-rise-of-poptimism.html)) credentials. But I’ve spent too far too many years in music, and in almost every kind of capacity. What I’ve learned from these cumulative experiences permanently banishes me from the ranks of populists and poptimists.

I’ve performed and recorded. I’ve written songs and have my own music publishing company. I’ve raised money from investors and launched various music businesses. I’ve produced albums. I’ve licensed music. I’ve taught music in classrooms and practice rooms. I’ve written thick books of music history, and more than a thousand shorter reviews and articles.

Put simply, I devote hours every day to music—and have been doing this for dog’s years. So I can tell you with total confidence: All musicians are not created equal.

I know this with certainty because there are at least *some* objective tests and standards.

I was planning to write about this at a future point. But when I saw Rick Beato’s latest upload to YouTube, it spurred me into action—because he conveys the essence of this reality in just a few seconds.

Here Beato revisits [his breakout video from ten years ago](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=t3Cb1qwCUvI). It went viral with a vengeance—and for a good reason.

But it’s a very simple video. All he does is test the musical aptitude of his eight-year-old son Dylan.

The test starts by asking his son to identify individual notes, but it soon gets harder—much harder. Rick eventually presents the child with thick, dissonant polychords, and asks him not only to identify the notes, but also describe the sounds in terms of music theory.

Dylan handles it like it’s nothing. And he is just eight years old. It’s really quite stunning.

Go to the 2 minute 30 second mark here, and watch. You will see why this caused a sensation when it was first released—and generated more than four million views on YouTube.

![](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QAiD3tiDY0I)

After watching this, do you still think all musicians are created equal—and that there are no objective differences? Is everything just opinion and personal preference?

This debate is more relevant than ever now, given the controversy over the [recent ranking of songwriters in the](https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2026/magazine/greatest-american-songwriters-alive.html) *[New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2026/magazine/greatest-american-songwriters-alive.html)*. Some observers felt that the internal criteria applied in the construction of this list had little to do with musical standards.

In other words, it was a Lebowski kind of list.

And now the *Times* seems to be nervous about its own list, publishing an alternative one (more on that below).

Rick’s video validates my own experience.

I remember my stint in music education. When I was teaching at Stanford, there were times when I had to audition 10-15 jazz pianists over the course of a single day—and then had to rank them from best to worst, so they could be assigned to ensembles that matched their level of ability.

This actually proved quite easy to do. In fact, the differences in talent were so obvious that I’m confident any another trained judge would have arrived at almost exactly the same ranking as mine.

And here’s where it gets interesting. This difference was even true when I had to audition children with almost no experience in music. Even starting with that blank slate, the differences in musicality were striking.

The reality is that you can actually measure a person’s musical aptitude, and those who have this demonstrable *gift* possess a huge advantage in making music. Some people are so creative that they can thrive *despite* this gap, but the gap exists nonetheless.

I’ve seen the same thing as a performer. I’ve played with all levels and types of musicians, but when I started performing with top-notch professionals in my twenties, it was clear how much more they could hear than the average amateur.

And when I found myself playing piano with Stan Getz, I got the chance to discover what a one-in-a-million musical ear can deliver on the bandstand. Stan heard *everything* happening around him and fed on it, taking whatever substitute chords or thematic hints I played, and *making me sound better* because he knew how to get the most out my contributions in his own playing.

That was his gift—and a huge reason for his success and artistry.

I had a conversation with [sax great Bud Shank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bud_Shank) back then. Bud knew that I was hanging out with Getz, and wanted inside details on how Stan practiced. I had to tell him that Stan really didn’t do much practicing. Shank was disappointed to hear this, but told me that he wasn’t surprised—because the greats really are different.

If you have lived through these kinds of experiences, you know that subjectivity is a myth when it comes to gauging musicality. Or sometimes it’s even worse—it’s a scam that allows power brokers to promote lesser talents for their personal gain. (Yes, I know you’re shocked to learn that this happens in the music business.)

If we were discussing sports, everybody would know all this immediately. That’s because we are familiar with sports competitions—where the superior talent is declared a winner. So if you can’t decide which runner or swimmer or tennis player is superior, you put them into a competition, and then you get an objective answer.

When there are exceptions—for example, when LeBron James’s son shows up on the Lakers, and stays on the roster even though he only makes 31% of his shots—fans complain about the unfairness.

They’re right to do so. They know that standards exist, and want them applied. A music critic should want the same thing.

Let me be clear, I’m *not* saying that perfect pitch is necessary for musical excellence. In fact, a strong sense of relative pitch may be just as useful, and might help out in certain situation. (Singers with perfect pitch often struggle when accompanied by an out-of-tune piano, for example, and can sound like they’re missing the notes.) But a discerning ear is almost necessary to achieve greatness in music.

Aiming for the highest levels of musical expression without that discerning ear is like trying out for NBA with a small body and slow feet. Maybe it’s not impossible, but the odds are stacked so heavily against you that you are advised to pick another profession.

The only exceptions I’ve seen happen when a musician with a mediocre ear has some other demonstrable strength that compensates for this lack. For example, consider the case of \[name removed\] who achieved greatness as a musician through sheer conceptual creativity, or \[name removed\] who rose to the top of the rock world via good looks and charisma.

But the lesson of those exceptions is *not* that everybody is equal and it’s all subjective. The real takeaway is that there’s always some genuine advantage at play in these things, and it often can be measured. (You can even measure good looks and charisma, via surveys, etc.) Subjectivity plays no part in this.

I want to move on to the topic of music criticism—where I will likely stir up a hornet’s nest. But before I do that, I must lay out what I’ve just said in clear bullet points—because the last time I engaged in this debate, opponents “reframed” (i.e., distorted) my views to make them easier to refute.

They had to do this because my actual stated views are very much common sense, and aligned with what people have known for centuries.

You can boil it all down to this:

1. Musical talent is real. Some people have more of it than others.
2. Many aspects of musicality can be measured and quantified—by ear tests, auditions, etc.
3. People with these demonstrable skills have a huge advantage as musicians, and are more likely to create great work of lasting merit.
4. There are exceptions, just as there are in every field. For example, there was one player in the NBA this year—and only one—who was under six feet tall. So it’s possible to thrive despite a disadvantage in a key metric. But exceptions don’t invalidate the metric.
5. A person lacking inherent musical talent will need to compensate in other ways. This can be through good looks, charisma, verbal or conceptual creativity, or maybe just a trust fund from mom and dad. But this still doesn’t mean that everything is subjective—not in the least. It just replaces musicality with some other (possibly less praiseworthy) metric.
6. Opinions and verdicts on music that ignore all these realities aren’t trustworthy—and are (in this instance) truly subjective.

As I said above, all this should be obvious. But these statements are still controversial because the existence of musical talent is threatening to many powerful interests in the culture.

Consider the case of the *New York Times*, which replaced John Rockwell as *Arts and Leisure* editor back in 2002. This was done to align entertainment coverage with the pop culture consumption of the mass public. As one commentator summed things up, the goal was [“less Peking Opera and more Britney Spears.”](https://observer.com/2002/02/raines-era-dawns-at-times-with-botox-sturm-und-drang/)

That makes *business* sense—but *artistry* is something different. It’s hard to push Britney over Bach, or decimate your jazz coverage, unless you replace musical standards with other criteria. And we all know what these “other criteria” are in the Age of the Internet—they are clicks and views.

In that world of clicks, music criticism that focuses on musicality is inconvenient. It’s much better for a media outlet to focus on gossip, scandal and lifestyle reporting. The last thing editors want—and let’s be honest, editors have been the enforcers of this degradation—is treating music as music, songs as songs.

This removal of musical standards from music journalism is so common now, that most of us just accept it. But that [recent video of the](https://www.honest-broker.com/p/rick-beato-versus-the-ny-times) *[New York Times](https://www.honest-broker.com/p/rick-beato-versus-the-ny-times)* [insiders discussing the greatest living songwriters](https://www.honest-broker.com/p/rick-beato-versus-the-ny-times), was such an extreme example that even the Average Joe couldn’t help but notice the absurdity. Here were people ranking songwriters without showing any knowledge of songwriting as a *musical* craft.

It was like a gathering of Big Lebowskis.

And now the *Times* has tried to rectify matters by [publishing an alternative list of great songwriters](https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2026/magazine/best-living-american-songwiters-reader-picks.html) —as picked by its readers.

But you will notice what they didn’t do. They didn’t share the ranking determined by the 250 actual “music insiders” who were invited to nominate artists for the initial article.

(Full disclosure: [I was one of the nominators](https://www.honest-broker.com/p/the-ny-times-asked-me-to-pick-the). It was the only time I’ve done anything for the *Times* in many years. But I only agreed because I mistakenly thought I was participating in an actual vote, where my ballot would have weighting in the final tabulation.)

By the way, even the so-called “music insiders” might not be the best judges. I’d like to see the choices of actual songwriters, especially those who have composed hits. *That* would be far more revealing than either of the two published *Times* lists.

For the same reason, [I have suggested that the Grammy awards should be chosen as follows](https://www.honest-broker.com/p/30-ways-to-revitalize-arts-and-culture-14c):

> All Grammy Awards will be decided by a small group of elite judges—no more than 100 or 200 of the greatest living musicians. I’m talking about people on the level of Paul McCartney, Joni Mitchell, Stevie Wonder, etc. No industry shills allowed!
> 
> The awards would immediately gain enormous credibility. Fans would pay attention to the winners.
> 
> Also, getting named a judge would become a huge honor—even bigger than winning a Grammy itself. New judges are chosen by the existing judges, who can only fill vacancies after a retirement or death.

What does all this say about music critics? Here, too, I believe that my views are reasonable.

I’m not opposed to critics who lack an acute musical ear. But when they act as if this metric doesn’t exist or is irrelevant to music as an art form, it’s hard to take their opinions seriously.

For them, music really is just another kind of fad or fashion, driven by trends and lifestyle posing. Maybe there’s an audience for that kind of music criticism, but I’m definitely not part of that audience.

That said, there are people with intelligence and well-developed taste who can still be excellent music critics without this kind of ear—I’ve known more than a few of them. But they almost always spend a lot of time with skilled musicians, and thus manage to assess musicality indirectly by learning from those who do possess finely-tuned ears. Over time, their taste is formed, and is built on a solid foundation.

So I’m not taking the elite line that everything can be determined by an ear test or an audition. But I am saying that it’s pure foolishness to ignore the benefits of a good musical ear or demonstrable skills in music-making. When the culture goes too far in that direction—as is happening now—foolishness has far too much sway over our music.

One final note: The solution here isn’t trying to fix the *Times* or other organizations. It will almost certainly involve the creation of parallel institutions, built on firmer ground and fairer standards. I will have more to say about that in the future.
