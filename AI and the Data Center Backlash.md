A report this week by real-estate brokerage [CBRE Group](https://www.wsj.com/market-data/quotes/CBRE) showed that data-center construction slowed late last year amid permitting headaches and delays connecting to the grid. Many states are threatening to impose restrictions on data centers unless they build their own power sources.

The Trump Administration and 13 Governors across the Mid-Atlantic and Midwest recently proposed that AI developers have to “bring your own power” to run new data centers. If they don’t want to do that, the companies would have to pay significantly higher electric rates or agree to let their power be curtailed during shortages, such as during this winter’s polar air blasts.

Some AI developers have protested the ultimatum, but they have to appreciate that data centers are becoming political targets as electricity costs rise. Americans don’t want to pay higher utility bills to support data centers that will benefit companies with a trillion-dollar market cap. Data centers can draw as much electricity as entire cities, and they’re projected to consume about 12% of U.S. power by 2028, up from 2% before 2020.

This increased demand is pushing up electricity prices in wholesale markets. Connecting new data centers to the grid also raises transmission costs. The PJM Interconnection grid, which spans 13 states from Illinois to New Jersey, has become ground zero in the fight over how to pay for AI growth.

Some states in the PJM region, especially Virginia and Illinois, have dangled tax breaks to attract data centers. The flurry of new sites is straining the grid, because many baseload coal and nuclear plants retired last decade, as they struggled to compete with cheap natural gas and subsidized renewable power.

Now something will have to give, but it needn’t be reliable power or America’s AI ambitions. Some AI hyperscalers already are building their own power plants to avoid the permitting hassles of grid connection. [Microsoft](https://www.wsj.com/market-data/quotes/MSFT) wants to reopen Pennsylvania’s retired Three Mile Island nuclear plant. Meta has announced deals with advanced nuclear startups.

But restarting a closed nuclear plant can take several years, and building advanced nuclear generators will likely take more than a decade. Renewables paired with batteries can’t provide reliable power around the clock.

That’s why Elon Musk is building natural gas plants in Tennessee. Because of a global shortage of large gas turbines, Mr. Musk’s xAI and some companies are jury-rigging gas plants by installing clusters of small turbines and even retrofitting old jet engines. Necessity is the mother of innovation.

Meantime, China is relying on coal to power its AI buildout. China commissioned more than 50 new large coal plants last year, compared to about 20 a year in the previous decade. China commissioned more coal power capacity last year than India did over the prior decade. So much for Beijing’s climate pledge to phase down coal power this year.

Hyperscalers prefer to draw electricity from the grid, since it’s cheaper than building their own generation. It also lets them claim their data centers are running on wind and solar power, even if the grid is backed up by fossil fuels. But the Democratic Party’s drive to close nuclear, natural gas and coal plants prematurely to be replaced by solar and wind is one of the largest economic-policy mistakes in decades.

The bill is now arriving, and AI firms that abetted the wind and solar fantasy will have to pay part of it. The U.S. can’t afford to lose to China in the race for AI, and Americans shouldn’t have to pay for the cost of tech companies’ green virtue signaling.

President Trump sought to mollify a growing public backlash against AI data centers Tuesday by proposing to require that companies build their own power plants. It’s a good idea that could help accelerate innovation and ease electric rates, though it will require AI companies to sacrifice their green posturing.

“Many Americans are also concerned that energy demand from AI data centers could unfairly drive up their electric utility bills,” Mr. Trump said. “We’re telling the major tech companies that they have the obligation to provide for their own power needs.” This may soon become a political and business necessity.
