It's relatively common today to hear people say we have no free will. The most well known determinist I know —i.e., someone who believes that all actions are caused in a way that leaves no room for free will — is [Robert Sapolsky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Sapolsky).

When you read his work and listen to him talk about free will, it’s clear that he genuinely believes we have no free will. And I’ll be honest, I’m sympathetic to his work because of the earnestness with which he discusses it, but also because he believes that determinism should make us *more* compassionate human beings, than less. I’m also sympathetic to his view because there is a grain of truth in it.

In our daily lives, there are so many forces acting on us (structural, cultural, social) that there is a case to be made that a lot of our actions are far more influenced than we might believe — and some border on unfree.

However, on the question of whether there is freedom generally — that is, independent of particularly hard cases, and independently of how we should structure our societies norms around responsibility - I think there is.

First, I think we should *systematically* distinguish between two questions

(1) is there free will in the world? (the metaphysical question of whether we are free or unfree). The answer to this I think is yes. The fundamental capacity to choose otherwise exists as a capability in all human beings.

(2) was such and such a person under such and such circumstances able to make use of their *capacity* for free will? The answer to this question will vary by context, circumstance, and other such factors. And it’s not a scientific or metaphysical question. For want of a better word, it’s a practical one.

Why separate these questions? Because they require different tools, different conceptual lenses and different outlooks.

To answer (1) we’re going to read the science, judge the conclusions drawn from the experiments, see if these experiments draw good conclusions etc

To answer (2) we need to look at completely different things: social and political forces, personal history, the environment of the individual, and so on.

Usually, these questions are collapsed into the same question, but in my view shouldn’t be. I’ll focus on (1) in this post

#### Is There Free Will?

There’s a strange assumption that crops up in the free will research — which appears to be heavily tied to the metaphysical thesis of determinism — that if your brain displays any activity prior to your awareness or *report* of wanting to act, the prior brain activity somehow threatens freedom.

This was Benjamin Libet’s conclusion in his paper ‘Do we have free will?’ [^1]

Libet begins his experiments by stating a definition of free will as requiring the presence of two conditions. First, there should be ‘‘no external control or cues to affect the occurrence or emergence of the voluntary act under study’’ (p, 47); second, the subject must feel in control of their action when it is being done, feel that they want to do the action, and feel that the action is of their own initiative.

He then argues that this definition of freedom is threatened by his experiments and as such, we need a new definition of freedom, which he locates in a ‘veto’ function that allows us to veto or not veto the decision to act.

#### The Experiments

To test Libet’s initial definition of freedom, subjects are asked to flick their wrist whenever they feel like doing so, and the brain’s ‘readiness potential’ —defined as ‘‘electrical indication of certain brain activities’’ (p, 49) — is measured.

It was consistently found across several trials that readiness potential reliably preceded any conscious awareness of the will to act by about 350-400 milliseconds, and preceded an action by about 200 milliseconds.

In other words, the brain shows signs of preparing for action before one can articulate that they intend to act *and* before they do act. As Libet puts it, ‘‘the initiation of the freely voluntary act appears to begin in the brain unconsciously, well before the person consciously knows he wants to act!’’ (p, 51).

Libet seems to conclude from these experimental results that the second condition on freedom —that is, the condition which says one must be in control of their action, feel they want to do the action, or initiate the action themselves —has not been met. For that condition to be met one would need the brain’s readiness potential to emerge at approximately the *same time* as the conscious intention to act: ‘‘if conscious will were to follow the onset of \[reading potential\], that would have a fundamental impact on how we could view free will’’ (p, 49).

In response to these results, Libet thinks we should restrict our view of freedom: we can no longer think of freedom as the voluntary formation of an intention to act, given that an intention to act is *involuntary* if it is *unconscious*. We should think of it as the ability to veto whether we act on these involuntary intentions.

#### Does This Conclusion Work?

It seems that THE crucial assumption in Libet’s reasoning is that an unconscious intention is an involuntary one. But this doesn’t follow in any real sense.

Think about what the experiment actually shows. Your brain displays electrical activity associated with preparing to move about 400 milliseconds before you consciously report wanting to move. Libet interprets this as: your brain *decided to act before you did*, therefore you didn’t really decide. There’s a few problems here.

First, it tacitly assumes that ‘your brain’ and ‘you’ are different. While this is true in an informal everyday life sense, there is no such distinction that any science should take seriously, in my opinion. It is Descartes outdated metaphysics doing the work. Moreover, even disregarding that issue: there’s another interpretation, a far simpler one: it simply takes about 400 milliseconds for a decision forming in the brain to reach conscious awareness.

That’s it. Still your decision. Nothing exciting to see here.

We don’t typically think that the unconscious processing underlying regular activities — catching a ball, navigating a crowd, driving—threatens our freedom. A lot of what the brain does operates below the level of reportable awareness. Do we need to conclude from this that we are all just automatons?

The answer is a resounding ‘no’.

#### What Science Actually Tells Us

Here’s what I think is being missed in most of this debate: the neurosciences don’t currently explain voluntary action at all, not in the sense that would be needed to threaten free will. Moreover, that there are results in other sciences worth paying attention to in this debate.

One example is MIT neuroscientists Emilio Bizzi and Robert Ajemian, *A Hard Scientific Quest: Understanding Voluntary Movements*. These scientists reviewed what is known about the neural processes underlying ordinary human actions like lifting your arm or reaching for a glass of water.

They describe the question of how the brain translates a wish to move into muscle contractions as “awfully complicated” and “only partially understood.” What remains entirely unknown, they write, is how the cortical areas of the frontal lobe “construct the spatiotemporal patterns of neural activity necessary to activate the spinal cord, enabling it to execute a specific movement’’ (p 94).[^2]

To add to the complexity of muscle coordination, the authors highlight how little time we really have to pre-plan complicated actions, such as shooting for a goal when playing soccer, concluding that there is plainly ‘‘neither the time nor the inclination to explicitly formulate the command signals to control the millions and millions of muscle fibers’’ (p 84) [^3] needed to execute an action.

Notice how different their assumptions are.

They assume that it is simply ludicrous to expect muscle activity formation to be a conscious decision for us: how the nervous system coordinates the millions of muscle fibres firing simultaneously to keep a soccer player, to use their example, moving in a controlled rather than chaotic way, is an incredibly complex task for the brain and body. The authors reason that the brain must therefore rely on an ‘‘effective simplifying strategy’’ (p, 84), and illustrate how progress has been made in discovering what that strategy is.

In other words, we know something about the mechanisms underling how we manage to coordinate our muscles — and this remains, the authors write, ‘‘one of the fundamental questions in motor neuron science’’ (p, 84). What we *don’t know* is how the decision to move gets made in the first place.

This matters *enormously* for the free will debate. If science doesn’t explain why you act one way rather than another, then the claim that science shows free will is an illusion goes well beyond what the science shows. The threat to free will isn’t a scientific finding — it’s a philosophical assumption being read into results that don’t support it.

Libet himself seems to have had some awareness of this. Buried near the end of his paper, he writes that the evidence for determinism precluding free will is “not available,” and that determinists haven’t even proposed an experimental design that could test the theory. It’s interesting, then, that he goes as far as to hinge freedom on a veto function that must operate 100ms before an action takes place.

#### The Two Questions Again

This is why I think the distinction I drew earlier is so important.

Whether free will exists — whether human beings have the capacity to act otherwise — is a scientific question, and on current evidence, the sciences don’t tell us it is not true. Given we phenomenally believe we are free, and given the science does not suggest otherwise, I think we should assume we have freedom.

On the other hand, the scientific question of whether free will exists is hopelessly inadequate for deciding whether someone was, *under such and such circumstances,* free to do otherwise in the more robust sense of possess agency in that moment.

Whether a particular person, under particular circumstances, was genuinely free to act differently is a separate question. And here Sapolsky’s instinct is right: there are so many forces — structural, historical, neurological, social — that shape what we do. In at least some situations, the answer might just be that one’s freedom was too seriously constrained to meaningfully exist in that moment.

That’s worth taking seriously, not as a reason to abandon free will, but as a reason to think harder about the conditions under which people actually get to use it.

It’s worth being clear on whether we have free will, not because it’s a nice thought to believe in, but because *nobody has actually shown it isn’t there*.

As far as we know, the capacity to do otherwise — to run left instead of right, to veto the urge to act — is real, poorly understood, and exists.
