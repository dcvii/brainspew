Patron saint of clicking ‘read more’ and becoming less well informed - somewhere in Bordeaux.

I’m fresh from watching a YouTube video about an extraordinarily chivalrous German WW2 fighter ace. The story gave me shivers, which I’ve learned is a personal red flag. I don’t trust emotional reaction to information (de-linking the two was harder than that statement implies and I’m still ‘working’ on it).

Sure enough, the video was 90% bullshit, with a grain of truth. You can [watch it yourself](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5ghB1vp74YE), if you want to waste 8 minutes of your life - or just go see the comments about how marvellous it is and how people are so glad that it wasn’t made by AI that they immediately subscribed for more.

Since embarking on creating the definitive historical archive for an overlooked WW2 unit ([24th Cavalry Reconnaissance Squadron (Mechanized)](https://www.24thcavrecon.org/)) I’ve learned a bit about military matters and consequently developed a sixth sense for nonsense. These days I’m a citation fanatic and if there’s no source document or firsthand testimony about something, I don’t trust the stories about it.

So it was with that video and it took all of ten minutes to establish that it was just a manifestation of what I’ve come to see as ‘content culture’.

I think I made up the concept of ‘Content Culture’, so here’s a definition.

**It is the making and dissemination of ‘content’ that is the raison d’être in that world, not love of a subject.**

Inevitably it’s also where truth goes to die.

I’m losing count of the Facebook pages I now block for this reason. You might know it as ‘slop’.

If I’m sounding like a pompous old nitpicker who enjoys spoiling other people’s fun that’s because I am and I do enjoy it.

And the reason is that those who make it and those who form their credulous audience are disrespecting people I’ve come to deeply appreciate and admire. You come over here to Europe in December 1943 with a significant chance of dying or carrying shrapnel in your body for the rest of your life, not to mention the terrible memories that made some of ‘my’ guys hit the bottle or take their own lives afterwards, and some ‘creator’ in Sri Lanka or California makes money inventing stories about that period. If I had the skills I’d hack the lot of them and post their snivelling faces and addresses in a hall of ̶s̶h̶a̶m̶e̶ ̶ shits.

Call me old-fashioned but if (outside of work) you’re producing writing, audio or video about which you have neither the knowledge nor subjective attachment, chances are your outputs will be gruel - however emotionally affective it is. In fact, the more effective in creating emotional affect, the worse it’s likely to be.

Content Culture is why so many of us cannot stomach LinkedIn, but at least no one is making up shit about chivalrous Luftwaffe pilots on there.

You’ll see the impulses that drive it on Substack by visiting the relevant subreddit for discussing life on this platform. Day after day it’s little more than pleas for advice on growing your subscribers, how someone used Notes to get 500 subscribers in their first month and rarely about the pleasure and satisfaction they take in carving out their niche and developing their voice.

But, back to my hobby horse of WW2 ‘content’. You might think that it’s harmless entertainment, this long after the fact, or even that it’s good in some way that the memory of that time is kept alive in this way. No it isn’t. Every post designed to invoke or provoke anger, tears, nostalgia, moral uplift and pride is clambering over thousands of ruined lives to just make money, unless it’s a genuine reflection of history. This includes the ‘share so people remember’ framing, when all it will ever do is produce false memories.

---

The problem with false WWII stories is not just that some people get facts wrong. I get facts wrong on my website, from time to time. Then I feel bad and correct them. But the culture I’m talking about here is one that treats every subject as raw material for engagement. Of course, I’m just obsessing about WWII, but in general anything promoting grief, heroism, sacrifice, politics, personal trauma, AI panic, living a ‘spiritual’ life, productivity advice and even *writing* itself almost always seems to me to be the processing and packaging of something into units of ‘content’.

The actual topic is then more of a by-product. You can imagine the people who made the YouTube video up top never asking ‘is this true?’ because they’re only asking ‘does this work?’. Annoyingly, the answer to the second question is always ‘yes’.

Never have I seen anyone on the Substack subreddit talking about whatever it was that seized them enough to make them write regularly about something. Just ‘growth’, ‘strategy’, ‘conversion’, posting frequency, pricing tactics, packaging... *optimising.* Subject matter seriousness is never discussed.

The impression I get is that many people sign up here to get readers whether or not they have anything particularly urgent to say to readers.

It’s funny that, according to Google, my 24th Cav Recon website now attracts more engagement than the benchmark for niche military history projects. The reason it’s funny is that I never thought of it as a microhistory niche for which I could create ‘content’. It was just something I felt like doing because I was annoyed that there was no reliable information about the men who liberated my town. The subject exerted a claim on me and now it feels like an obligation to do it right. The fact that so many people are appreciating it is a delightful bonus, not *the point*.

On D Day this year I’ll be posting a page about the first man in the squadron killed in action and that’s about as far as optimising for engagement that I care to go.

But the process I’m following isn’t...

What can I post today?  
What will trigger engagement?  
What will make people subscribe?  
What story shape will travel?  
What emotion can I activate?

It’s...

What happened?  
How do I know?  
What is the source?  
What remains uncertain?  
Why does it matter?

Yes, I’m a saint.

My story of Anton J. Elvesaeter will matter because he never made it to my town 9 days after losing his life on his first day on French soil. But he matters to this day and there’s a memorial to him in a small town in Norway, from where he had emigrated before signing up with the US cavalry. And none of the crappy content farms would ever dream of mentioning him. So it’s my job.

It probably sounds like I’m mounting nothing more than a moral argument here, but there’s more to it than that.

The real stories are messier and more interesting than the simplistic distillations that appear in things like that video about German ace Hans Joachim Marseille and Spitfire pilot John Hamilton Nicholls, whose plane he *didn’t* actually cripple before escorting it to safety and wobbling his wings in salute before peeling off. (Hans was already dead when John limped his Spitfire home). The bit in the film that literally made me shiver and then question that reaction as too affective to trust.

I did some digging and found that the properly cited stories of the men mentioned in that video were already striking enough without being artificially grafted together and embellished.

Truth is more interesting than fiction and that is what matters most to me. But ‘content culture’ doesn’t see this.

I’m aware that I only became so sensitive to unsupported claims because I ground out some domain expertise doing the 24th Cav Recon work. And then I kept seeing how damned hard it really is to restore a single life to the public record accurately, with so many unsupported claims often muddying the record.

What can be done, you might ask.

Back to those awful Facebook pages. My approach was initially to label them in the comments as inaccurate and to engage positively with expert commenters pointing out the misleading aspects. But then I noticed those pages appearing more frequently in my feed and it dawned on me that all Facebook was seeing was ‘Mike engages with this content so that gets his algorithm tweaked to receive more of it’.

That’s the root of it. That’s the incentive these people are responding to. It’s structural. And the experts offering their corrections are doubtless also seeing even more from the same content gruel producers than if they’d moved along without comment. This is why I now block such pages. At one level I’m being somewhat of a crank, but this tiny act of attention hygiene slowly improves my experience of WW2 Facebook where there are still countless people doing great work.

I’m posting this behind a paywall because I love the people who properly support Rarely Certain and I’m not interested in optimising for reach. AKA being the change I want to see. Lol.
