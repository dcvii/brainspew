Not long after he arrived on the Stanford University campus in 2022 as a 17-year-old freshman, [Theo Baker](https://theobaker.info/) received a tip about the school’s president, the neuroscientist Marc Tessier-Lavigne. Baker, the son of two prominent [Washington, D.C., journalists](https://pagesix.com/2023/10/30/society/student-who-brought-down-stanford-president-lands-book-deal/), had joined the staff of *The* *Stanford Daily* and was looking for a story he could dig into. And here it was: On a website called [PubPeer](https://pubpeer.com/), a forum for discussing scientific papers, critics were claiming that papers coming out of Tessier-Lavigne’s lab contained manipulated and fraudulent data. And that it had been going on for years.

Tessier-Lavigne has focused much of his career on searching for the key that might unlock Alzheimer’s, a disease that has long frustrated scientists. Prior to becoming a university president—he headed The Rockefeller University before taking the Stanford post in 2016—Tessier-Lavigne oversaw Genentech’s huge 1,400-person laboratory. His private sector work had [made him wealthy](https://www.benzinga.com/sec/insider-trades/0001437435/marc-tessier-lavigne), and, in the world of Alzheimer’s science, famous. He had co-authored several papers that were considered pathbreaking, especially [a 2009 paper](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature07767) published while he was at Genentech. The paper had four authors but as the lab leader, Tessier-Lavigne was listed as the “principal investigator,” or PI. Calling the research a “significant breakthrough,” he boasted that it could ”turn our understanding of Alzheimer’s upside down.”

Over the next decade-plus, that paper, which had been published in the prestigious scientific journal *Nature*, was cited [816 times](https://retractionwatch.com/2023/12/18/former-stanford-president-retracts-nature-paper-as-another-gets-expression-of-concern/) by other researchers. A Duke University neurologist excitedly told *Nature* that “We have yet to get a disease-modifying drug that works. So we’re missing something, and maybe this is one of the missing pieces.” [Genentech devoted](https://www.gene.com/download/pdf/2008_annual-report.pdf) substantial resources to “develop both antibodies and small molecules that may attack Alzheimer’s” based on the Tessier-Lavigne findings.

Except that, as Baker was discovering, the supposed breakthrough was nothing of the sort. Inside Genentech, he discovered, some of the company’s top executives believed “that the research had been based on falsified data.” At least one believed the problems were serious enough that Tessier-Lavigne should retract the paper. He refused. It also turned out that Genentech had been [unable to replicate](https://www.gene.com/download/pdf/Findings-of-2023-Genentech-Review-of-2009-Nature-Paper-and-Related-Research.pdf) Tessier-Lavigne’s results, casting further doubt on their veracity. But this fact was never disclosed to shareholders or the general public. By 2012, a year after Tessier-Lavigne left the company, Genentech quietly abandoned its research effort based on the 2009 paper.

Not being a scientist, Baker lacked the skill to sniff out fraud in a scientific paper by himself. For this, he enlisted the help of a woman named [Elisabeth Bik](https://scienceintegritydigest.com/about/). A microbiologist by training, Bik plays a relatively new role in the scientific world, that of a “sleuth” who uncovers image duplication and other examples of outright fraud in scientific papers. Bik hadn’t focused on Tessier-Lavigne’s work before Baker called, but once she started looking at his papers on PubPeer, she quickly found evidence of doctored images. At least four papers, she told Baker, had “serious problems.”

Stanford president Marc Tessier-Lavigne speaks at the university’s 131st Opening Convocation Ceremony in 2021. (via Stanford University/Facebook)

After confirming Bik’s conclusions with two other sleuths, Baker wrote his first story in November 2022. “Stanford President’s Research Under Investigation for Scientific Misconduct, University Admits ‘Mistakes,’ ” [read the headline](https://stanforddaily.com/2022/11/29/stanford-presidents-research-under-investigation-for-scientific-misconduct-university-admits-mistakes/). When a Stanford spokesperson downplayed the issue, Bik went on the record to disagree. “There appear to be a lot of visible errors in these papers, and some duplications are suggestive \[of\] an intention to mislead,” she emailed Baker.

Over the course of the next eight months, Baker wrote more than a dozen further stories about various problems with papers Tessier-Lavigne had co-authored. Editors at scientific journals like *Nature* and *Science* began asking questions of their own. The university’s board of trustees brought in a high-powered law firm to conduct an investigation. In mid-July 2023, the trustees’ investigators issued [a damning report](https://boardoftrustees.stanford.edu/sites/g/files/sbiybj31576/files/media/file/scientific-panel-final-report.pdf) that “confirmed a pattern of falsified research emerging from the labs he ran,” as Baker put it in his account of the report. However, the investigators accepted the Stanford president’s insistence that any wrongdoing had been done by underlings, and that he himself had been unaware of the problems. And they concluded that the 2009 paper was not a product of fraud but rather an unacceptable lack of rigor. (Tessier-Lavigne, who continues to [run a lab](https://tessier-lavigne-lab.stanford.edu/) at Stanford, did not respond to interview requests for this story.)

The ramifications of scientific fraud go well beyond a researcher who cheats to get ahead.

In mid-July, in addition to announcing that he would [retract three of his papers and correct two others](https://tessier-lavigne-lab.stanford.edu/issues-five-papers-and-planned-actions), Tessier-Lavigne also said that he would resign. Six weeks later, on his last day as president, two more papers were retracted, including the *Nature* paper that was supposed to turn our understanding of Alzheimer’s “upside down.”

[In a note](https://retractionwatch.com/2023/08/31/stanford-president-retracts-two-science-papers-following-investigation/) he wrote alongside the retractions, Tessier-Lavigne said, “I regret the impact of these issues on the scientific community.”

Science has a fraud problem. Scientific progress requires experiments that rigorously and objectively test hypotheses. Yet a surprising number of experiments conducted today are neither rigorous nor objective. Science is now rife with retracted papers, doctored images, and hyped results. Scientists have sometimes replaced the images from one experiment with images from a different one to make the results look better. They have made claims unjustified by the underlying research. And far too often, they’ve designed experiments that aren’t really experiments at all because the results are preordained.

Oftentimes, this deception is happening in the biomedical arena, where the funding is greatest, the pressure to produce results is acute, and the ultimate stakes are human life and well-being. Jonas Salk refused to patent his polio vaccine because, he said, it belonged to humanity. Now, by contrast, scientists are frequently so keen to collect the rewards of professional advancement that they compromise the search for treatments for terrible diseases. And the consequence isn’t just the withdrawal of an individual paper. Falsified results lead to years of misdirected efforts by other scientists. Entire sectors, like Alzheimer’s research, have been led astray in this way, with much-heralded research, like Tessier-Lavigne’s, presenting false paths—and false hope for cures.

As Jay Bhattacharya, the director of the National Institutes of Health (NIH) and acting director of the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, [put it](https://www.wsj.com/opinion/the-man-who-fought-fauci-and-won-trump-nih-nominee-jay-bhattacharya-covid-cancel-culture-4a0650bd) to *The Wall Street Journal* last year, “The ultimate consequence of that is that clinical advances that we think we have ended up not working to actually help people.”

Fifteen years ago, two science journalists, [Adam Marcus](https://retractionwatch.com/meet-the-retraction-watch-staff/about-adam-marcus/) and [Ivan Oransky](https://retractionwatch.com/meet-the-retraction-watch-staff/about/), started *[Retraction Watch](https://retractionwatch.com/)*, an online newsletter chronicling scientific fraud. In a recent issue, *Retraction Watch* noted that its database now includes some 63,000 retractions. And that number only includes the fraud and mistakes that have been *caught*. “We said publicly that we think the retraction rate should probably be around 2 percent of all scientific papers,” Oransky told me. “I figured we would get pushback with people saying, ‘You’re being alarmist.’ Instead the only responses we got were from people who said we were being too conservative, and that it should be more like 5 or 10 percent.”

What has happened to science—and to scientists? The core belief for many decades was that scientists were above reproach. Nobody believes that anymore. As we shall see, the modern culture of science—cutthroat, mercenary, winner-take-all—practically invites fraud. And the consequent stories exposing fraudulent research have eroded the public’s view of science; according to a [November 2024 Pew survey](https://www.pewresearch.org/science/2024/11/14/public-trust-in-scientists-and-views-on-their-role-in-policymaking/), only about a quarter of the American public has “a great deal of confidence” in science to act in the public interest.

#### ‘They All Feel the Pressure’

For decades, the scientific community believed that it was next to impossible to commit fraud. That was partly because journals like *Nature* and *Science* commissioned outside scientists to judge a paper for accuracy and soundness prior to publication. Surely these peer reviews, as they were called, would uncover hidden problems. A second backstop was replication: An experiment was supposed to be replicated by other scientists before its validity was universally accepted. If it couldn’t be replicated, for whatever reason, its findings were discounted.

These cherished assumptions about scientific integrity were first shattered in 1981 when a thirtysomething scientist named John Darsee was [caught faking data](https://www.nytimes.com/1983/06/14/science/notorious-darsee-case-shakes-assumptions-about-science.html) in a heart study at Harvard University. Investigators ultimately found that over the course of 14 years, Darsee had published over 80 fraudulent papers and abstracts in *Nature*, *Science*,and other prestigious journals. Among those for whom the scandal served as a rude awakening was Arnold Relman, the editor of *[The New England Journal of Medicine](https://www.nejm.org/)*. In an editorial, he wrote, “What kind of protection against fraud does peer review offer? The Darsee affair gives a clear answer: Little or none.”

Was there much scientific fraud before Darsee? Surely there must have been, though it is impossible to know, because no one was looking for it. What is indisputable is that some of the examples of fraud that have emerged over the past two decades or so have been truly shocking—and disastrous.

There was the high-profile anesthesiologist Scott Reuben, whose research on the efficacy of certain pain-relief drugs for postoperative healing had been widely accepted in the field. The discovery in 2009 that Reuben had never actually conducted those clinical trials left the anesthesiology profession in a “shambles,” *[Scientific American](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/a-medical-madoff-anesthestesiologist-faked-data/)* [concluded](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/a-medical-madoff-anesthestesiologist-faked-data/).

There’s Gregg Semenza, a professor of genetic medicine and director of the vascular program at Johns Hopkins’s Institute for Cell Engineering—and a Nobel laureate—who has had 15 papers retracted since 2022. (Most of the papers were published prior to 2022.) Neither he nor Johns Hopkins—the university that is the biggest recipient of federal science funding—has ever explained why his lab published so much problematic research.

“We know that science is wrong most of the time.”—Brian Nosek, psychology professor

Or Sam S. Yoon, a former researcher at Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center, one of the premier oncology hospitals in the world, who has had 26 of his papers questioned by a scientific sleuth. In one instance, [according to](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/02/15/science/columbia-cancer-surgeon-sam-yoon-flawed-data.html) *[The New York Times,](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/02/15/science/columbia-cancer-surgeon-sam-yoon-flawed-data.html) “* photos of tumor-stricken mice, used to show that a drug reduced cancer growth, had been featured in two previous papers describing other treatments*.”* (Yoon today is the chief of [surgical oncology](https://columbiasurgery.org/about/division-surgical-oncology) at Columbia University’s medical center.) Or another famous oncology hospital, the Dana-Farber Cancer Institute in Boston, [agreeing in December to pay](https://www.statnews.com/2025/12/16/dana-farber-15-million-settlement-manipulated-data/) a $15 million fine for producing government-funded research that, among other things, “duplicated images to represent different testing conditions,” [according to the Justice Department](https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/dana-farber-cancer-institute-agrees-pay-15m-settle-fraud-allegations-related-scientific).

Or the case of Sylvain Lesné, a neuroscientist whose fraudulent 2006 paper significantly set back Alzheimer’s research. Lesne, who worked in a lab at the University of Minnesota, Twin Cities, was the lead author of an influential paper that supposedly pinpointed a protein linked to Alzheimer’s. Over the next dozen years, a great deal of research was predicated on Lesné’s finding; the paper was considered so important that it was cited more than 2,500 times by other scientists. In 2024, 18 years after its initial publication, and two years after a dogged critic, Matthew Schrag, and an investigative reporter with *Science* magazine, Charles Piller, [exposed the paper’s fabricated images](https://www.science.org/content/article/potential-fabrication-research-images-threatens-key-theory-alzheimers-disease), it was retracted. [Lesné finally resigned](https://www.science.org/content/article/alzheimer-s-scientist-resigns-after-university-finds-data-integrity-concerns-papers) his tenured position last year.

Or, as a final example, take the case of Eliezer Masliah, the man who ran the NIH’s National Institute on Aging’s neuroscience division. In 2024, Masliah was [removed from](https://www.science.org/content/article/research-misconduct-finding-neuroscientist-eliezer-masliah-papers-under-suspicion) his government post after a staggering 132 of his papers were found to have falsified so-called Western blots, common scientific images used to show the presence of protein. In his role as head of a division responsible for allocating a research budget of $2.6 billion, Masliah had enormous influence in deciding which Alzheimer’s research got funding.

“It is no stretch to think that the slow progress on Alzheimer’s disease is linked” to the amount of scientific work that has turned out to be fraudulent, Bhattacharya said during [his NIH confirmation hearing](https://www.realclearpolitics.com/video/2025/03/05/watch_live_dr_jay_bhattacharya_confirmation_hearing_to_lead_nih.html).

To understand why there is so much scientific fraud, we need to start with a simple number: 2 million. That is the approximate number of research scientists and engineers in the U.S. today— [almost eight](https://www.nationalacademies.org/read/21309/chapter/2#4) times as many as 50 years ago. Many of them are young postdoctoral researchers working in big university labs. These “postdocs” are all under pressure to conduct experiments that will stand out so they can get co-author credits in the same handful of prestigious journals. A successful scientific career depends on such publishing triumphs.

“A couple of decades ago, a scientist could just be a scientist and do his thing,” said Elisabeth Bik. “And now they all feel this pressure, either from a university or, if you’re a postdoc, from your mentor, to publish more papers.”

The labs themselves can be pressure cookers, with lab leaders demanding the kind of successful results that bring big research grants and public acclaim. For some postdocs, the urge to use fake data when their experiments are not producing the desired result becomes overwhelming. In their report to the Stanford trustees, the Tessier-Lavigne investigators noted, for instance, he “tended to reward the ‘winners’ (that is, postdocs who could generate favorable results) and marginalize or diminish the ‘losers’ (that is, postdocs who were unable or struggled to generate such data).” If you wanted to keep working for Tessier-Lavigne, in other words, you couldn’t afford to work on experiments that didn’t make a splash.

Science journals also distort incentives. By their nature, experiments sometimes, even often, fail to prove the hypothesis being tested. Failing to confirm a hypothesis is a crucial part of scientific discovery. But the journals aren’t interested in publishing papers that document a failed experiment. They only want papers that announce successful results—the more headline-worthy the better. So that becomes the singular goal of any ambitious researcher.

And it’s not just out-and-out fraud that this demand for splashy findings emboldens. Far more common is a kind of willful blindness that has become all too common in science. Researchers want a certain result, so they talk themselves into believing they have obtained it. “Suppose I’ve done an experiment 15 times in my lab and only three of them show my hypothesized result,” said Brian Nosek, a psychology professor at the University of Virginia School of Medicine who has studied science’s warped incentives. “I may convince myself that those three are the ones that really belong in the paper, and the other 12 are flawed methodologically.”

Brian Nosek, a psychology professor at the University of Virginia School of Medicine, has studied warped incentives in science. (Courtesy Brian Nosek)

As Charles Piller, the *Science* reporter, told me, “When people’s entire careers and legacies and money for research and their personal benefit are tied up with one way of looking at a problem, then they are only going to look at the problem in that one way.” The result is “science” that may reward the researcher and the lab, but is meaningless in terms of making progress in curing a disease.

#### ‘My Eye Just Caught It’

The most controversial website in all of science is [PubPeer](https://pubpeer.com/). Created in 2012 [by Brandon Stell](https://www.science.org/content/article/pubpeer-s-secret-out-founder-controversial-website-reveals-himself), then a 38-year-old American working at the National Center for Scientific Research in Paris, it was originally meant to serve as a Reddit-like forum where researchers could discuss scientific articles. But it soon evolved into something else: a place where sleuths like Elisabeth Bik could search for scientific fraud. That is its primary function today.

Many scientists find it infuriating to have to deal with PubPeer sleuths. For one thing, Stell allows anonymity on the site, so researchers sometimes have no idea who is criticizing their work. For another, when a PubPeer sleuth sends their findings to a scientific journal, the scientist has to drop everything to deal with the journal editors and, they hope, avoid a retraction. When a paper is retracted, there is no distinction made between a paper that is flawed due to an honest mistake or one that has fake data or manipulated images. And finally, the PubPeer sleuths aren’t always right.

Thomas Südhof, for instance, is a 70-year-old Nobel laureate and a giant in the field of brain research. He told me that he felt that his Nobel Prize had made him a target of sleuths who wanted to make a name for themselves. Dealing with his critics has become “a major occupation,” he said. [On his lab’s website](https://med.stanford.edu/sudhoflab/integrity---pubpeer.html), Südhof has a lengthy section about PubPeer in which he writes that the “public shaming of scientists causes mental anguish, ruins careers, and wastes resources.” Of the 674 papers his labs have produced over the decades, he says, 14 contained errors flagged by PubPeer—while PubPeer critics have flagged another 28 papers where the accusations were unfounded.

Stell is unapologetic about what PubPeer has become. In [a podcast interview](https://www.stemcell.com/academic-publishing-brandon-stell.html) he gave last year, he said that the desire of important journals to publish groundbreaking results “incentivizes questionable research.” PubPeer, he felt, put the emphasis on the quality of the data rather than on the prestige of the journal where a paper was published.

Not surprisingly, Bik also believes that PubPeer is the only real brake on the misaligned incentives that dominate labs. Before PubPeer, she said, “if you saw a problem with a paper, your only recourse was to write a letter to the editor of the journal. But in many cases, folks like me would have our letters turned down. And there was no other platform to warn people about errors.”

Bik, 60, has been sleuthing full-time since 2018. After doing her doctoral and postdoctoral work in the Netherlands, where she grew up, she got a position in a microbiology lab at Stanford in 2001. In 2013, she stumbled across a PhD thesis that had plagiarized her work. She also discovered two images that she realized had come from different experiments. “I wasn’t looking for that,” she told me. “My eye just caught it.” Intrigued, she began scouring other papers for mistakes or fraud, turning it into a kind of hobby.

Bik left Stanford to work for several biotech companies, but soon realized that she was more drawn to her hobby than her job. So she decided to become a sleuth full-time. She doesn’t get paid for this work, but earns most of her money making speeches. In 2023, she won the [Einstein Foundation Award](https://award.einsteinfoundation.de/) for Promoting Quality in Research, which came with a £200,000 (approximately $270,000) prize. She used the money [to endow a fund](https://www.science.org/content/article/renowned-scientific-integrity-investigator-endows-fund-support-fellow-sleuths) to help support other sleuths.

Although there are dozens of sleuths who scour papers on PubPeer, Bik is the best known for a variety of reasons. The first is that journal editors take her critiques seriously. “She’s never sent me any image where she wasn’t correct,” said Holden Thorp, the editor in chief of *Science* magazine. When Bik spots a doctored image, it will almost always lead a university or a journal—usually both—to undertake an investigation. Reporters regularly call her when they come across suspected fraud. Theo Baker told me that he came to trust Bik because “she never made bigger claims than what she could see. She was essential to the early parts of the story.”

A second reason for her prominence goes back to her uncanny ability to spot images that have been manipulated. Today, most sleuths use AI and other digital tools to uncover problems, but long before AI arrived on the scene, Bik was able to sniff out photos that had been photoshopped or “borrowed” from a different experiment. She could detect Western blots that had been manipulated. To the untrained eye, a Western blot looks like a series of lines that contain small black smudges. But to a scientist they are “a way of characterizing the amount and type of protein in a tissue sample,” said Charles Piller, the *Science* journalist. “Bik was just preternaturally capable of seeing these things. She has a talent that few have.” Thorp told me that Bik’s naked eye is often better at spotting a problem than the modern digital tools.

I asked Bik why she had shifted careers to devote herself to sleuthing. “I’m not out to damage people’s career,” she replied. “But I find it maddening to see that people are building reputations and winning awards based on sloppy work or based on fraudulent work. And I just care too deeply about science to let that happen.”

#### The Scarlet Letter of Science

Like Bik, Holden Thorp is part of the movement to bring quality control to science. Before [he took over](https://www.science.org/content/article/aaas-names-chemist-holden-thorp-editor-chief-science) in the fall of 2019, the journal, which is part of the American Association for the Advancement of Science, had to be dragged kicking and screaming into retracting a paper, especially if the authors didn’t agree with the decision. In one case that [came to light](https://retractionwatch.com/2023/08/31/stanford-president-retracts-two-science-papers-following-investigation/) years later, Tessier-Lavigne sent the magazine several corrections for two of his papers—which the journal ignored.

Today, *Science* gets automatic notifications whenever someone posts a criticism on PubPeer of a paper *Science* or one of [its five niche journals](https://www.aaas.org/journals) has published. It employs two people whose job is to keep track of the concerns that come in, and run papers through the programs that seek out manipulated images or falsified Western blots. (Other journals also now have full-time fact-checkers.) Thorp told me that he spends 25 percent of his time dealing with doubts raised about papers and decisions about whether they should get a correction, a retraction, or an “expression of concern,” which journals now slap on papers when their veracity is called into question.

The modern culture of science—cutthroat, mercenary, winner-take-all—practically invites fraud.

Thorp, 61, is a chemist by training. He rose through the ranks of academia rapidly; by 2008, at the age of 43, he became the chancellor of his alma mater, the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill. It did not end well. UNC became embroiled in a major scandal revolving around no-show classes for athletes. By his own admission, Thorp was ill-equipped to handle the scandal and its aftermath. In 2013, he joined Washington University in St. Louis as its provost, and stayed there until he landed the editorship of *Science*.

Three days into his new job, Thorp got a call from an old colleague, [Frances Arnold](https://cce.caltech.edu/people/frances-h-arnold), a biochemist at Caltech and a Nobel laureate. She said, “I need to retract [my paper](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aaw9068) ”—a paper that had been published in *Science* earlier that year. Thorp asked her what had happened. “A postdoc faked the numbers, but it doesn’t matter. It was my fault as the principal investigator.”

Arnold declined an interview, but sent me an email that read, in part, “It’s obvious that one should retract papers known to be wrong—it cleans up the literature and saves grad students from trying to reproduce faulty work. It’s not obvious why we make it so hard to do.”

But it *is* obvious. At the very least, retractions are embarrassing. At worst, they are career-ending. Even if a retraction is not the result of fraud, it is still painful to endure. And a retracted paper never goes away. As Thorp told me, “We don’t publish on paper anymore. We publish on the internet”—where fraudulent or mistake-ridden papers live forever, except that the word “Retracted” is stamped across the top of them. It’s the scarlet letter of science.

“We’ve gotten much more aggressive about going ahead with corrections and retractions,” Thorp told me. “Now, if the data are unreliable and we can determine that on our own, either because the authors tell us, or because our own tools allow us to analyze it, or because one of these sleuths have shared something with us that’s so compelling that we can make that judgment on our own, then we don’t need a determination of whether it was intentional or not. We’ll go ahead and retract the paper.”

To Südhof, the willingness of journal editors like Thorp to retract a paper without the author’s consent illustrates how PubPeer has changed the dynamic between journal and scientist—and not for the better. “There’s no presumption of innocence,” he told me. “We are the supplicants to the journals.”

Thomas Südhof is a 70-year-old Nobel laureate and a giant in the field of brain research. (Christopher P. Michel)

But to Thorp, dealing with sleuths and PubPeer and more skeptical journal editors like himself is simply the price of doing business for a modern scientist. “It is part of doing research in the internet age that you have to deal with this,” he told me. “This is just a fact of the world that we’re in. If a criticism comes in and it’s valid, you need to engage and get things straightened out, no matter how annoying you find it.”

#### ‘A New Culture of Science’

Here’s the rub: People like Elisabeth Bik and Holden Thorp—and Theo Baker and the journalist Charles Piller—may be doing the Lord’s work, but they are not changing what’s wrong with the practice of science. It’s the scientific equivalent of whack-a-mole. What is needed is something that goes well beyond sleuths and righteous journal editors—a new culture of science that will reduce the opportunities for fraud, lessen the pressures and warped incentives that lead to it, and put focus back on advancing the cure for diseases like Alzheimer’s rather than scoring citable papers.

Scientists today work in an environment that promotes fragmentation and secrecy. Experiments are guarded until researchers are ready to announce them in public papers; when the results are not what the scientist wants, they are suppressed. Postdocs in labs work on experiments largely in isolation, keenly aware of their career prospects and often presented with the temptation to fudge results. When papers are submitted to journals, they are evaluated by anonymous reviewers with little stake in the ultimate value of the work. For scientists genuinely trying to make world-changing discoveries, their careers can be hurt by insisting on doing honest and honorable work. For those, on the other hand, who want to defraud their peers and the public, it offers plentiful opportunities to take advantage of the system.

One place to start fixing, as Ivan Oransky and Adam Marcus [wrote in](https://www.theatlantic.com/science/archive/2025/02/rfk-kennedy-vaccines-scientific-literature/681681/) *[The Atlantic](https://www.theatlantic.com/science/archive/2025/02/rfk-kennedy-vaccines-scientific-literature/681681/)* last year, is the peer review process. Some 3 million papers are now being published annually—”far too many...without adequate peer review or even editing,” they wrote. Obviously, the “publish or perish” mentality needs to change, but so does peer reviewing. If we’ve learned anything since Darsee in the early 1980s, it’s that having peers review a paper (for free!) doesn’t provide any guaranteed that fraud will be uncovered.

Some researchers have suggested requiring that the peer reviewers’ comments be published alongside a journal article. This has come to be known as “open peer review.” *Nature* started experimenting with that in 2020, and last year itmade [including the peer reviews mandatory](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-01880-9) for all papers it publishes. Oransky and Marcus, as well as others, have suggested going further and removing anonymity from the reviews, a step that is likely to give reviewers a far greater shared interest in the integrity of the work.

But that’s just one small step, and not nearly enough to fix what ails science. Science needs more comprehensive reform—a true cultural change. And there are finally some glimmerings of that emerging from something called the “open science” movement.

Holden Thorp, editor in chief of the journal *Science*, is photographed outside his home in Orlando, Florida. (Octavio Jones/ *The New York Times* via Redux)

If the movement has a leader, it’s Brian Nosek, the University of Virginia psychology professor who, in 2013, co-founded the [Center for Open Science](https://www.cos.io/about). “The values of science are not rewarded in practice, and those values include transparency and openness,” he told me. And that’s what he and his fellow travelers are hoping to change.

For starters: When a paper is published, almost all of that underlying information—essentially the thought process behind the experiment—remains with the lab. The open science movement advocates publishing not just the paper itself, but every piece of underlying data for anyone to examine. That, says Nosek, “offers you the opportunity to check my work, to challenge my work, or extend my work in different directions.”

Publishing everything possible about experiments has the dual virtue of helping honest scientists build on work, while making it far more difficult for dishonest scientists to commit fraud.

Here’s another important step: changing the incentive structure that inherently rewards scientists whose experiments prove a hypothesis and punishes those whose experiments fail. The biggest rewards will always go to scientists who make major discoveries. That will never change, and nor should it. Curing diseases and making major contributions to human knowledge is ultimately what the enterprise is about. Yet right now we have a system that pushes this to an extreme: Scientists who get a successful result get all the advantages that come with publication. Those whose experiments fail get nothing—and the public never learns of the failure.

Falsified results lead to years of misdirected efforts by other scientists.

“We know that science is wrong most of the time, right?” Nosek said. “We are taking big shots. We’re looking in new directions. We’re studying things we don’t understand. False starts are common, normal, and shouldn’t be viewed as an embarrassment. They’re part of the discovery process.”

Thus the most radical of the open science movement’s ideas: creating a system where scientific journals agree to accept a paper *before they know the results*. “Researchers still need to get papers published, but what that requires will change,” he said. “The scientist submits an outline of the proposed experiment. And if the journal editors believe the research is important, then the editors make a commitment to accept the final result regardless of outcome.”

In this model, Nosek envisions that scientists won’t look just at the conclusion, but at how the science was done, and all the value that can be derived from analyzing the work. “When reviewers look at the final work,” Nosek says, “they won’t say, *Were you right*? Instead, they evaluate whether you did what you said you were going to do, and whether you interpreted the outcomes responsibly. The researcher is rewarded for asking important questions and designing effective experiments, not proving a hypothesis.”

None of these changes are likely to happen tomorrow. But they’re not pie-in-the-sky either. Young idealistic scientists are seeking new ways to do better science; journal editors would love to no longer be in the “gotcha” business; and bit by bit, elements of the open science movement are starting to catch on. There’s hope, in other words.

The ramifications of scientific fraud go well beyond a researcher who cheats to get ahead. It causes an already skeptical public to further lose trust in scientific findings. It causes scientists to spend years trying to build on findings that turn out to be wrong. Most importantly, it sets back the effort to cure diseases like Alzheimer’s.

Every profession has its share of cheats and charlatans. But with science the stakes are simply higher, which is why it is so important to change the culture of science, to create new and better incentives—and to do it now.
