Iran still has thousands of ballistic missiles in its arsenal that it could use by retrieving launchers from underground storage areas, according to American officials familiar with U.S. intelligence assessments.

The assessments come as the U.S. is working to cement a cease-fire that would fully open the Strait of Hormuz and also insulate Iran, American troops and states in the region from further attacks.

Some American officials said they are concerned that Iran will use the break in fighting to reconstitute some of its missile arsenal.

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth told reporters this week that Iran’s missile program is “functionally destroyed” with launchers and missiles “depleted and decimated and almost completely ineffective.”

The picture painted by U.S. intelligence reports, however, indicates that Iran can reconstitute some of its missile force. While U.S. officials say that more than half of Iran’s missile launchers have been destroyed, damaged or trapped underground, many of those launchers that remain can be repaired or dug out from underground complexes, U.S. officials said.

Iran’s missile inventory also has been roughly halved in the conflict, yet it retains thousands of medium- and short-range ballistic missiles that could be pulled out of hiding or retrieved from underground sites, said U.S. and Israeli officials.

Iran has well under 50% of the one-way attack drones it had at the start of the war—because it has expended many of them during the conflict and Tehran’s weapons production sites have been attacked by the U.S. and Israel, U.S. officials say. But the regime could acquire similar systems from Russia to use against its neighbors, U.S. officials said.

Iran also still has a small inventory of cruise missiles—which could be used to target ships in the Persian Gulf or U.S. troops who might try to seize an island—if negotiations broke down, U.S. officials say.

“The Iranians have shown a remarkable ability to innovate and reconstitute their forces quickly,” said Kenneth Pollack, a former C.I.A analyst who is vice president for policy at the Middle East Institute. “They are a much more formidable opponent than most Middle East militaries other than the Israelis.”

American analysts are not forecasting that the Iranian military will soon return to the missile and drone numbers it had before the war, given the effort the U.S. and Israel has made to strike Iran’s defense industry. But Iran has proved to be a resilient foe, and some U.S. government analysts project that Iran could still field some of its ballistic missile launchers.

Israeli officials say that around two-thirds of Iran’s ballistic missile launchers were taken out of commission during the war, but note that the Iranians could retrieve many of those that were trapped underground by U.S. and Israeli strikes. Israeli officials also say Iran still has more than 1,000 of the estimated 2,500 medium-range missiles Iran had at the start of the war. The rest were either fired or destroyed.

A spokesman for U.S. Central Command, which oversees U.S. forces in the Middle East, declined comment and pointed to recent statements by top U.S. military officials about the scope of the American and Israeli air campaigns.

On Wednesday, Gen. Dan Caine, the chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, said the strikes had shattered Iran’s defense industrial base. He said that the U.S. had dropped more than 13,000 munitions, striking missile and drone storage sites, its navy and the country’s defense industry “to ensure that Iran cannot reconstitute the ability to project power outside their borders.”

White House spokeswoman Anna Kelly said that the U.S. military has achieved all of its objectives.

“This profound military success has allowed Vice President Vance, Special Envoy Witkoff, and Jared Kushner to negotiate with maximum leverage in order to end these threats to American troops and our homeland for good,” Kelly said. “As the Vice President said this morning, he is hopeful for a positive conversation if the Iranians are willing to work in good faith.” Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner are U.S. envoys who are joining Vance for talks with Iran Saturday in Islamabad, Pakistan.

Iran, which had a weak air force before the war, has long relied on its missile arsenal to project power against its foes and to deter attacks. Hunting enemy missiles was a major challenge for the U.S. in its 1991 war with Iraq as many of the Scud missiles the Iraqis used were mobile. To stop the missile firings, the U.S. and its British ally sent teams of special forces into Iraq and did not rely entirely on air strikes.

But the U.S. and Israeli attacks against Iran’s missiles were carried out entirely from the air. Further complicating U.S. and Israeli efforts, Iran had missile complexes that were buried deep inside mountains. Israel concentrated on using air strikes to close the exit tunnels for the missile launchers, but the bases themselves proved hard to destroy, Israeli officials said.

While Israel could not reduce Iran’s ability to launch missiles to zero, Israeli officials said it was an achievement to reduce their firing capabilities to between 10 to 15 missiles a day for most of the war, in contrast to dozens daily during the opening days of the battle.

Israeli officials say Iran cannot currently manufacture more missiles, and the rate at which Tehran may rebuild their program will depend on what kind of help they receive from Russia or China.

Constraining Iran’s future military capabilities will not only depend upon the threat of new attacks by U.S. and Israeli forces, but also on sanctions and export controls. One of Iran’s demands for ending the conflict is the lifting of all primary and secondary sanctions, which penalize countries that do business with Iran.

“One of the huge takeaways of this is that Iran, with a tiny fraction of its earlier capability, can still be the dominant actor controlling peace and security in the Gulf,” said Jon Alterman, of the Center for Strategic and International Studies. “They have this great advantage that every day they don’t lose, they win, and every day we don’t win, we lose.”
