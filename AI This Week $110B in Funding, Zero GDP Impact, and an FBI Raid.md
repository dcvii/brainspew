### The AI Accountability Report — Week of Feb 24, 2026

This week, Sam Altman announced $110B in funding. Goldman Sachs says AI has boosted GDP by “basically zero.” Block threw a $68M party for 8,000 employees, then laid off half of them. And the FBI raided a school superintendent’s home over a $6.2M AI chatbot that lasted 96 days.

These aren’t opinions. They’re receipts. Here’s what actually happened.

---

### The Money Trail

Nvidia posted $68.1B in quarterly revenue. Seventy-three percent year-on-year growth. Data centres made up 91% of it. Gaming GPUs — the thing Nvidia was actually built on — now account for 11%.

Jensen Huang said the quiet part loud: “Compute equals revenue. Tokens generate revenue.”

He’s right. The money is real. But where is it going?

Goldman Sachs says AI has contributed “basically zero” to GDP. $68B in chip sales. Zero economic return measured at the macro level. That’s not an innovation cycle. That’s a supply chain.

Then on Thursday, Sam Altman announced $110B in new funding. The investors include Nvidia and SoftBank. Follow the loop: Nvidia invests in OpenAI. OpenAI spends the money on Nvidia chips. Nvidia’s revenue goes up. Nvidia invests more.

That’s not a market signal. That’s a circular purchase order structured as an investment. The money doesn’t prove AGI is coming. It proves the chip pipeline needs guaranteed buyers.

---

### The Human Cost

Block — Jack Dorsey’s payments company — laid off 4,000 people this week. That’s nearly half the company, from roughly 10,000 employees down to under 6,000. Third round of layoffs. Stock jumped 24% the morning it was announced.

But the number that matters is $68M.

That’s what Block spent on a three-day company festival in Oakland, Q3 2025. Jay-Z performed. Anderson.Paak. T-Pain. Soulja Boy. They flew 8,000 employees in from around the world. It was filed in their earnings as “general and administrative expenses increased by $68.1M... primarily driven by an in-person company event.”

200 days later, 4,000 of those employees were gone.

$68M divided by 4,000 laid-off workers is $17,000 per person. That’s roughly what each of them would have received in a month’s severance. Block’s gross profit was $10B. Revenue was $24B. The company wasn’t struggling.

Dorsey warned: “Within the next year, I believe the majority of companies will reach the same conclusion.”

He’s probably right about that too.

Every one of those 4,000 people signed a severance agreement. Severance comes with a release of claims — you waive your right to sue. Companies don’t spend hundreds of millions on releases for claims that don’t exist. Under the WARN Act, federal law requires 60 days notice for mass layoffs over 50 employees, or 60 days pay in lieu. The legal machinery isn’t accidental. It’s a purchase.

### Grift of the Week

The FBI raided Los Angeles Unified School District Superintendent Alberto Carvalho’s home and office on February 26th. The reason: a $6.2M contract for an AI chatbot called “Ed,” built by a startup called AllHere.

The timeline tells the story.

July 2023: LAUSD signed the $6.2M contract. March 2024: the chatbot launched at 100 of LAUSD’s lowest-performing schools, serving 540,000 students. April 2024: founder Joanna Smith-Griffin and Superintendent Carvalho presented it together at a Silicon Valley AI conference. June 14, 2024: AllHere shut down. The chatbot lasted 96 days.

LAUSD had paid $3M in advance before the company collapsed. Smith-Griffin has since been charged with securities fraud, wire fraud, and aggravated identity theft. She raised roughly $10M by misrepresenting her revenue and customer base. She spent investor money on a house down payment and her wedding.

Debra Kerr — an ed-tech salesperson with a longtime connection to Carvalho from Miami-Dade — was also raided. Her son worked for AllHere.

This isn’t new territory for Carvalho. In 2020, he helped secure a $1.57M donation from a company with pending contracts — what investigators called an “appearance of impropriety.”

Meanwhile, on X this week, a tool called OpenClaw was being promoted as a free, open-source AI agent. One contributor. Ten commits. Repository created 48 hours before the launch thread. Five name changes in three months, with hundreds of malware redirects in the project’s history. Documentation that claimed “16 security layers” and a “WASM kernel” linked to placeholder pages. The website was down.

The post claimed $0 cost. It actually runs on Claude, GPT, and DeepSeek APIs — roughly $750 or more per month for real usage. The free thread drove people to comment and follow, then a reply promoted a paid product called StealAds.

Different scale. Same playbook. Give away something that sounds too good to be true, because it is.

### The Price Lie

Every automation wave comes with the same promise: this will make things cheaper. AI is no different. But history has a receipt for that claim.

ATM fees. In 1998, the average out-of-network ATM fee was $1.97. In 2025, it’s $4.86 — a 147% increase. The average monthly checking account fee is now $15.65. The average minimum balance to avoid that fee is $10,705. Banks automated everything. Customers paid more, not less.

Manufacturing. US manufacturing output per hour has increased roughly 600% since 1980. In that same period, middle-wage workers saw a 6% pay increase. Less than 0.2% per year. Steel production went from 10.1 hours per ton to 1.5 hours. The gains went to the top — high-wage workers rose 41% in the same window.

Groceries. US food prices rose 23.6% from 2020 to 2024. In 2022, food inflation hit 9.9% — the fastest since 1979. Self-checkout was deployed nationwide during exactly this period. Prices went up.

Now retailers are rolling out electronic shelf labels — screens that replace paper price tags and allow prices to change multiple times per day based on demand. That’s not deflation technology. That’s surge pricing applied to groceries.

The pattern is consistent. Automation doesn’t lower consumer prices. It increases margins for the company and finds the ceiling of what customers will pay. AI won’t be different, because the incentive structure isn’t different.

### What I’m Watching Next Week

\*\*Nvidia’s $78B guidance.\*\* They’re forecasting Q1 2027 revenue of $78B against expectations of $72.6B. If they hit it, the supply chain deal narrative strengthens. If they miss, the circular capital question gets louder.

\*\*LAUSD fallout.\*\* FBI raids on a school superintendent don’t end with the raid. More names, more subpoenas, and likely more connections between ed-tech vendors and district officials.

\*\*The layoff language.\*\* Dorsey said most companies will reach the same conclusion within a year. Watch for how the next round of layoffs gets framed — “AI-driven restructuring” is the phrase to look for. The word “restructuring” does legal work that “layoffs” doesn’t.

\*\*UK Biometrics.\*\* Commissioner William Webster called for independent oversight and pre-authorization for police facial recognition. But UK visa policy already requires biometric enrollment for applicants — senior officials can “excuse or defer” enrollment. Oversight for the state. Mandatory compliance for everyone else.
