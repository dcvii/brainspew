What evidence is there that in California illegal aliens have voted in elections, signed petitions or have been stricken from voter roles. Who has this information and what is the enforcement mechanism?

Evidence of illegal aliens (undocumented immigrants) voting, signing petitions, or being removed from voter rolls in California is limited, isolated, and primarily tied to administrative errors or individual fraud rather than widespread or systemic activity. Official audits, prosecutions, and watchdog reports document small numbers of improper registrations (some corrected before voting), rare proven illegal votes, and targeted removals. No credible sources show large-scale impact on elections. Claims of massive fraud often rely on unverified data or are contradicted by investigations. Voting in Elections
- Registrations of non-citizens (including some undocumented) occur due to errors or false statements on forms. The strongest documented examples come from county-level records obtained via lawsuits:
	- Public Interest Legal Foundation (PILF) reports: In San Diego County (2006–2018), 264 non-citizens were registered and/or removed after self-reporting or confirmation. Similar small numbers in San Francisco (28) and Riverside (6). In Alameda County (2024 disclosures), at least 54 self-admitted non-citizens were registered (some for years, up to 14), with evidence of mail-ballot returns or in-person voting attempts (typically rejected). Orange County had at least 17 non-citizens registered since 2020.
		- 2018 DMV Motor Voter errors: ~1,500 people (mostly legal non-citizens, per DMV; explicitly not undocumented AB 60 license holders) were erroneously registered due to processing glitches. These were canceled by the Secretary of State.
- Actual illegal voting: Extremely rare and mostly individual cases, not mass occurrences.
	- Isolated prosecutions: A 2012 Escondido case involved an undocumented immigrant who pleaded guilty to using a stolen U.S. citizen’s identity to register and vote. A 2019 Sacramento case included an illegal alien convicted of voting while non-citizen plus ID fraud. Older example: 1996 congressional race probe alleged ~624 non-citizen ballots (some undocumented) in a close Orange County contest, but it did not change the outcome and was dropped.
		[congress.gov](https://www.congress.gov/116/meeting/house/108824/documents/HHRG-116-JU00-20190129-SD020.pdf)
		- 2025 DOJ example: A non-citizen (described in reports as undocumented in related coverage) in Orange County received an unsolicited mail-in ballot despite ineligibility, triggering federal scrutiny.
		[justthenews.com](https://justthenews.com/nation/states/center-square/doj-files-suit-against-ca-county-over-noncitizen-voters)
		- Broader studies (Heritage Foundation database, Brennan Center, etc.): Nationwide, only dozens of non-citizen voting convictions over decades; California-specific cases are few and often involve legal residents or residency fraud rather than undocumented immigrants. Audits (e.g., 2016 SOS review) found zero opened investigations into illegal immigrant voting despite public claims. Most experts across the spectrum describe it as “vanishingly rare” with negligible impact.
Note: Some California cities (e.g., San Francisco, Oakland) legally allow non-citizen parents (including undocumented) to vote in local school board elections only—this is a separate, court-upheld program and does not apply to state/federal races.Signing PetitionsThere is no documented evidence of illegal aliens specifically signing California ballot initiative or recall petitions in any significant or verified way. Initiative petitions require signatures from registered voters, which are cross-checked and verified by county elections offices against voter rolls. Any non-citizen involvement would stem from improper prior registration (see above). Recent 2026 investigations into petition fraud in San Francisco involve paid collectors allegedly forging signatures using other registered voters’ information—not non-citizens themselves signing.

Stricken (Removed) from Voter RollsYes, this happens regularly in small but documented numbers. Counties remove ineligible voters (including non-citizens) upon discovery:
- Via self-reports (common when non-citizens naturalize or correct errors), tips, audits, or cross-checks.
- Examples: PILF-documented removals in the hundreds across sampled counties (as above); Orange County maintains records of such cancellations (DOJ sought data from 2020 onward). Statewide, millions of inactive registrants have been purged via lawsuits (e.g., Judicial Watch actions in Los Angeles County). The 2018 DMV cases were proactively canceled.
Who Has This Information
- Primary holders: County Registrars of Voters (e.g., Orange County, Alameda, San Diego, Los Angeles) maintain detailed cancellation records, voting histories, and applications. The California Secretary of State (currently Shirley Weber) oversees statewide data via VoteCal and DMV coordination. The DMV holds original registration trigger data.
- Others with access or obtained data:
	- Watchdog groups (PILF, Judicial Watch, Heritage Foundation) via public records requests, FOIA-style lawsuits, or settlements.
		- Federal: U.S. Department of Justice (Civil Rights Division) and DHS—actively requesting/suing for non-citizen removal records under HAVA/NVRA (e.g., 2025 lawsuit against Orange County Registrar for refusing unredacted data). Voter data is privacy-protected; full details are not public but obtainable through legal channels. Recent federal efforts (2025–2026) to access more data for immigration/enforcement purposes have faced court pushback on privacy grounds.
Enforcement Mechanism
- Criminal penalties: Illegal registration/voting is a felony under California Elections Code (e.g., false statements = perjury/wobbler up to 3 years) and federal law (18 U.S.C. § 611: up to 1 year prison + fines; deportation risk). ICE/FBI/DOJ prosecute when linked to immigration violations.
- Administrative/roll maintenance: Counties and SOS must keep accurate lists under the federal National Voter Registration Act (NVRA) and Help America Vote Act (HAVA). Removals occur via self-reports, jury summons mismatches, death/SSN checks, or complaints. No proof-of-citizenship is required at registration (self-attestation under penalty of perjury + DMV data suffices), but errors trigger cancellations.
- Petitions: Counties verify signatures against official voter rolls; fakes or ineligible ones are invalidated (Elections Code §§ 18600+).
- Detection/Prosecution: Relies on oaths, limited database cross-checks (DMV/SSA), post-election audits, tips, and naturalization processes. DAs, state AG, U.S. Attorneys, and ICE handle cases. DOJ can sue counties for non-compliance with list maintenance. Deportation follows convictions. Critics note the system’s reliance on honesty (especially in sanctuary policies and Motor Voter) limits proactive checks; defenders cite rarity of convictions as proof of effectiveness.

In short, while vulnerabilities exist (auto-registration errors, no ID/proof-of-citizenship mandate), available evidence from government records, audits, and lawsuits shows only isolated incidents of illegal aliens on rolls or voting—most corrected administratively—with no proof of election-altering scale. Data is held and enforced primarily at the county/SOS level with federal oversight. For raw records, contact specific county registrars or review PILF/Heritage reports.

Claims of thousands of faulty Arizona voter registrations at non-residential (e.g., commercial, PO box-only, vacant lots, or strip malls) addresses stem primarily from partisan audits and watchdog reports but have not been substantiated at that scale as active fraud impacting elections. Official reviews and county rebuttals show most flagged issues were administrative errors, data mismatches, or legal allowances rather than widespread invalid registrations leading to votes.
- Cyber Ninjas 2021 Maricopa County audit (post-2020): Flagged ~23,000–53,000 “questionable” ballots due to “prior addresses,” PO boxes, or commercial database mismatches (e.g., Melissa.com). Debunked by Maricopa County officials: Auditors used unreliable third-party data with “soft matches” (just name + birth year), causing false positives (roommates, family changes, twins). PO boxes are legal for mail forwarding—voters remain registered at their residential address (illegal to register at PO boxes). Only ~37 potential double-votes referred to AG out of tens of thousands flagged; no systemic fraud or outcome change. The audit itself confirmed Biden’s win.
- Watchdog reports (e.g., PILF/Public Interest Legal Foundation): Have documented hundreds of registrations tied to commercial/vacant addresses statewide via public records requests. These highlight list-maintenance gaps, but counties (and state) routinely remove upon verification. Homeless voters may legally use public/shelter addresses; many are explainable errors, not fraud. No prosecutions or overturned elections tied to thousands of such cases.

Academic/government reviews (Brennan Center, News21, courts) confirm rarity; most common types:
- Absentee/mail ballot fraud (most prosecuted): Harvesting, false witnessing, dead-person voting, tampering. Egregious example: North Carolina 9th Congressional District (2018)—Republican operative Leslie McCrae Dowless ran an illegal harvesting scheme (paid workers collected ballots, falsified witness signatures, tampered/mis-mailed). State Board of Elections refused certification; new election ordered. Guilty pleas/charges followed; race flipped in redo. Rare case where fraud demonstrably affected certification.
- False registrations / ineligible voting: Non-citizens, felons, double-voting, canvasser fraud. Examples: Isolated convictions (e.g., 1996 CA House—624 non-citizen ballots alleged, didn’t flip seat; scattered modern cases like felons or identity theft). Scale negligible.
- Vote buying / altering counts: Rare local (e.g., old East St. Louis schemes).
- Administrative/machine issues: Chain-of-custody lapses, tabulation errors (e.g., 2020 Antrim County MI human error, corrected). No widespread proven tampering.

Isolated prosecutions (e.g., dead relative ballots, duplicates) occurred but tiny.Diebold Voting Machine ControversiesNot entirely “shrill”—technical vulnerabilities were real and rigorously demonstrated, but claims of actual widespread election-rigging were often overstated without evidence.
- 2003–2006: Princeton researchers (and others) showed Diebold AccuVote-TS machines hackable in minutes via memory cards/viruses—undetectable vote changes, no paper trail. California decertified some; CEO’s partisan ties fueled suspicion. Harri Hursti demos confirmed risks.

- Outcome: Prompted nationwide reforms (Help America Vote Act mandates paper trails/audits; most states shifted to optical-scan or BMDs with paper). No proven real-world tampering altering federal outcomes (e.g., 2004 claims investigated, no evidence). Similar modern cases (Dominion in GA court): Lab hacks shown, but no exploitation in elections. Legitimate security concerns drove improvements; conspiracy versions lacking proof were shrill.
Most Egregious Violations in Apportionment, Gerrymandering, and Other ManipulationsGerrymandering has the clearest record of egregious, outcome-shaping manipulation (far more impactful than voter fraud).
- Partisan: North Carolina 2016 congressional map (GOP legislature explicitly aimed for 10R–3D despite ~50/50 statewide vote—extreme packing/cracking); Wisconsin Act 43 (2011, gave GOP supermajority despite losing popular vote); Maryland 6th (Dem flip of GOP seat); recent Texas mid-decade redraws and Illinois/CA creative maps. SCOTUS Rucho v. Common Cause (2019): Federal courts cannot review partisan gerrymanders (political question—no manageable standard). States vary (some independent commissions).
	[supremecourt.gov](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/18pdf/18-422_9ol1.pdf)
- Racial: Still justiciable. Shaw v. Reno (1993): “Bizarrely shaped” districts (NC “I-85 snake”); Miller v. Johnson (1995): Race cannot predominate; Allen v. Milligan (2023): AL map violated VRA (second Black-opportunity district required).
- Apportionment (House seats via census total population, including non-citizens per Constitution): Debates over accuracy (2020 undercount efforts, citizenship question blocked by courts). No successful exclusion; benefits high-immigration states. Historical one-person/one-vote (Baker v. Carr, Reynolds v. Sims 1960s) struck malapportionment.
Other manipulations:
- Voter roll purges (FL 2000 felon list errors disenfranchised thousands of eligible voters—mostly Black).
- Polling place changes/cuts in minority areas (VRA challenges).
- Private funding disparities or partisan election officials (post-2020 “Zuckerbucks” debates).
In summary, demonstrated problems are real but narrow and prosecutable (fraud rare; absentee most common). Diebold-style machine risks were legitimate but fixed. Structural manipulations like partisan gerrymandering have been the most egregious and hardest to remedy federally.
