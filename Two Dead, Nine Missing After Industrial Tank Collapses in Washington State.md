Two people died and nine others remained missing after a chemical tank collapsed in Longview, Wash. Officials said there was no hope of finding additional survivors.

A tank holding white liquor, a chemical used to make paper, failed Tuesday morning, releasing about 500,000 gallons of the product into a plant owned by a subsidiary of Nippon Paper Industries, local fire officials said. White liquor is a highly caustic alkaline chemical that is used to break down wood into pulp as part of the papermaking process. It can cause corrosive burns, according to the National Institutes of Health.

About 25,000 gallons of the chemical remains in the tank and has largely stabilized, allowing some recovery efforts, said Scott Goldstein, chief of Cowlitz 2 Fire & Rescue on Wednesday afternoon.

First responders are relying on information from co-workers of the unrecovered employees for their possible locations, officials said. At the time of the tank failure, there was a shift change, and employees were in nearby workspaces and break rooms. In some places, the facility was still filled with waist- or chest-deep water from a ruptured main mixed with some of the spilled chemical, officials said.

“I would not say they went missing; they were in their workspaces when this blast occurred,” Goldstein said.

First responders took 10 people to the hospital Tuesday, including eight workers and one firefighter, according to Matt Amos, battalion chief of the Longview Fire Department. One individual was reported dead Tuesday. Officials said Wednesday that another died at the hospital from injuries. The firefighter was released from the hospital Wednesday, officials said.

An official from the company said the mill’s monitoring system reported two spikes in a high-pH material released from the facility on Tuesday morning: one around 7 a.m. at the time of the spill and one about two hours later. White liquor is a high-pH material.

While the cause of the tank’s failure was unknown, fire officials said there was no threat to the surrounding community’s air quality or drinking water. Some amount of the chemical spill entered the nearby Columbia River and dike system, officials said Wednesday.

About a dozen carp were found dead in the dikes, according to Washington Gov. Bob Ferguson. The full environmental impact was still being assessed.

Concern about the structural integrity of the tank delayed recovery efforts, according to the Longview Fire Department. Recovered bodies will need to be decontaminated before they are taken to the Cowlitz County Coroner’s Office for identification and family notification, the department said Wednesday afternoon.

The tank failure in Longview, located in Cowlitz County about 130 miles south of Seattle, was the second notable incident involving a chemical tank at an industrial site in recent days. An [industrial tank](https://www.wsj.com/us-news/climate-environment/californias-chemical-leak-threat-what-to-know-bf77523a?mod=article_inline) at a plant in Garden Grove, Calif., began leaking last week, forcing thousands of residents to evacuate. Fire officials there lifted the evacuation orders Tuesday after stabilizing the site.

Ferguson said the Washington National Guard would assist with search and recovery efforts in Longview.

“Everyone is working very hard together in full cooperation, which will help us get through this challenging time,” Ferguson said.

Nippon Paper Industries said it apologized for the immense concern the tank failure has caused the community.

“We express our deepest condolences and offer our heartfelt sympathies to the bereaved families,” the company said.
