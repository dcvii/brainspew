*Boston* magazine just [published an article about Steven Pinker](https://www.bostonmagazine.com/education/2026/04/12/steven-pinker-profile/) headlined “Can Steven Pinker Save Harvard?” Subheadline: “But the celebrity professor’s own record raises a question: Is he the right guy for the job?”

First of all, I don’t get that “but.” It’s not referencing anything! It’s like the original headline was going to be something like “Steven Pinker Wants to Save Harvard,” and then someone changed the headline without changing the subheadline.

Setting aside my overreaction to a minor copy-editing error, this conceit is also a bit much — it’s very magazine-y. No one, including Steven Pinker, thinks Steven Pinker is (single-handedly) going to “save Harvard.” The article is really about a few different things, most of them summed up in the very first paragraph: “His critics call him a cover for racists,” writes author Robert Huber. “He’s been accused of providing intellectual ammunition to the alt-right, and of dismissing inconvenient evidence when it doesn’t fit his theories.”

Huber doesn’t dive deeply into the idea that Pinker has “dismiss\[ed\] inconvenient evidence” — he mostly just quotes a couple of academics who have made that claim. So I’m more interested in the (re-re-re-re-)litigation of the idea of Pinker *providing cover for* or *legitimizing* bad actors. There’s a very long, involved controversy here that mostly has to do with virulent anger, on the part of a small but loud group of academics, about Pinker’s status as one of the [leading public-intellectual debunkers of “blank slatism,”](https://www.amazon.com/Blank-Slate-Modern-Denial-Nature/dp/0142003344) or the idea that all humans are basically the same, and that any differences that emerge are the result of external factors.

I find it surprising, in 2026, that adherents of the more sweeping anti-Pinker view have done so poor a job of addressing counterarguments to their position (I’m going to table the narrower and more standard academic debate over whether he has gotten this or that wrong in his books; obviously, it’s legitimate to closely read and critically respond to the work of as influential a figure as Pinker). Their myopia on this matter can, I think, be explained by their own form of blank slatism. They believe that people are more or less blank slates, with regard to political opinions, until they decide which scientific beliefs to adopt. Similarly, political ideologies are only adopted because they are seen as having scientific legitimacy.

So, the argument goes: Without figures like Pinker, who are at best useful idiots and at worst quiet but intentional enablers, the alt-right would have far less intellectual fuel and wouldn’t have gained the power it has gained. Or if they *aren’t* arguing this, I don’t understand how they could possibly have remained so mad at Pinker for so many years.

But there’s another way of looking at things: Humans have certain hardwired or nearly hardwired tendencies. We’re very good at carving the world into us versus them, for example. Is there a society anywhere that doesn’t do this? There are a million strains of us-versus-them-ism. There are relatively “soft” versions like civic nationalism, in which differences that in other contexts might matter a great deal are overlooked (or partially overlooked) in favor of a big collective identity. That’s America, at its best, though there are obviously also downsides when it comes to xenophobia in times of war, and so on. But civil nationalism is cupcakes and cotton candy compared to the harsher, more evil forms of us-versus-them-ism — racism, Nazism, and so on.

Those harshest forms — the forms where you get beaten or dispossessed of your stuff or even *killed* if you aren’t *us* — don’t *really* rely on any particular scientific evidence about human nature. Obviously, the Nazis justified what they were doing by reaching for monstrous and idiotic forms of race pseudoscience, but no one anywhere thinks the Nazis would have packed up their Panzers and gone home if not for the pseudoscience. Then–cutting-edge racist pseudoscience acted as a fresh coat of paint on their antisemitism, which was drawn from much more ancient European strains that had nothing at all to do with the scientific method and in fact, existed well before it. The Nazis used the racist pseudoscience to justify a revolutionarily murderous political project they had already decided to carry out. All the other most murderous regimes operate similarly with regard to science — they use it as a justification. No one tinkered in their lab until they had enough information about human nature and then used those findings to determine who to kill. The desire to kill — or at least to exclude and marginalize — comes first. Human groupishness and monstrousness are prescientific.

Of course at the margins, there could be highly read individuals who travel some sort of slippery slope from Pinker all the way to Nazism. But there isn’t really anything an individual thinker can do about that: “If I have been misleading or unclear in a way that would egg on deplorable actors, I would take responsibility for that,” Pinker says. “But if I express things perfectly clearly — there’s a huge world out there. I can’t take responsibility for how some random person out on Twitter interprets a paper or an interview if there’s no content in the interview that would actually egg on or encourage them. And I can’t boycott every forum whose members hold some opinion that some third party finds repugnant.”

In the hothouse of insular academic circle, once a thinker like Pinker is identified as the root cause of some problem, he’s then held to fairly ridiculous standards with regard to his conduct and public statements. I’m not saying this to imply Steven Pinker has a hard life or is in some cosmic sense treated unfairly — Steven Pinker will be fine and I think most of us would trade our careers for his. My point, rather, is that this just gets really *stupid* very quickly.

Take, for example, a 2017 incident summed up by Huber:

> This isn’t for the faint-hearted, being a lightning rod, especially given the past decade’s atmosphere. In late 2017, for instance, during a panel discussion at Harvard about free speech, Pinker said, “Political correctness has done an enormous amount of harm in the sliver of the population that might be — I wouldn’t want to say ‘persuadable,’ but certainly whose affiliation might be up for grabs. The often highly literate, highly intelligent people who gravitate to the alt-right: Internet savvy, media savvy, who often are radicalized in that way.” Pinker was actually arguing that by shutting down debate, the left was pushing smart, contrarian people toward the alt-right — not because the alt-right was correct, but because it was the only place willing to engage certain questions.
> 
> Still, the right had a field day. Neo-Nazi website the Daily Stormer ran a headline that read, in part: “Harvard Jew Professor Admits the Alt-Right Is Right About Everything.” The left hammered Pinker for giving ammunition to extremists, regardless of his intent.
> 
> And this is the pattern: Bad actors and dark thinkers have appropriated Pinker’s research and writing for their own ends—and Pinker has done little to stop them.

I actually [wrote about this incident in 2018](https://www.nytimes.com/2018/01/11/opinion/social-media-dumber-steven-pinker.html), describing it simply as evidence that social media is making us dumber. Huber, on the other hand, seems to be squinting pretty hard to try to figure out something that Pinker did wrong here. As readers, what we end up with is this sequence: Pinker says something measured and reasonable (and, in my view, correct), a literal neo-Nazi outlet completely misrepresents what he said, and Pinker is then accused, in a magazine, of having “done little to stop” such malevolent actors, as part of a “pattern.” What’s he supposed to do? Any asshole can come along and misinterpret anything you say. Should Pinker have written a letter to the editor of the *Daily Stormer* demanding they edit his headline? I mean, first of all, of course he shouldn’t. But even if we indulge in this thought experiment, another criticism of Pinker is that he “legitimizes” bad actors. Of course if, upon having been misrepresented, he engaged with one of those actors, that would only prove the accusation!

What I’m saying is this whole thing is a bit of a trap. The only way Pinker can avoid these criticisms is to just stop saying anything, which is what his critics want, because they disagree with him. The disagreement, at least when it comes to his most vociferous critics, is primarily about politics: They are leftists who feel very attached to blank slatism and he is a liberal who has vociferously rejected blank slatism. But “liberal professor isn’t Marxist-y and blank-slatey enough” isn’t an argument anyone cares about — “liberal professor is giving aid and comfort to Trumpy racists, and maybe he isn’t *even* a liberal” has slightly more punch to it.

Before I wrap this up — and relegating the Jeffrey Epstein stuff to a footnote [^1] — I can’t resist taking one last shot at the silly “platforming” question.

> Pinker likes to say he manages his “controversy portfolio carefully.” But that means the trouble he might get into — not the trouble he creates for others by lending his credibility to people like \[Charles\] Murray, with whom he engages rather than dismisses. Late last year, he and Murray had a back-and-forth in the *Wall Street Journal* about Murray’s views on “terminal lucidity” proving the existence of the soul; Pinker, ever skeptical of faith, chastised Murray for reaching beyond the data. But the debate itself was the point: Whether Pinker won the argument didn’t really matter — Murray got the platform, a serious intellectual exchange with a Harvard cognitive scientist.

I laughed at “Murray got the platform.” *You guys:* Charles Murray is an extremely successful conservative sociologist. His second-most recent book, *[Coming Apart: The State of White America, 1960–2010](https://www.amazon.com/Coming-Apart-State-America-1960-2010/dp/030745343X)*, was a *New York Times* bestseller. His most recent one, *[Taking Religion Seriously](https://www.amazon.com/Taking-Religion-Seriously-Charles-Murray/dp/1641774851)*, which was published this last autumn, appears to have sold well too (taking number of Amazon reviews as a rough but not useless proxy), and got reviewed or otherwise covered by *[The Wall Street Journal](https://www.wsj.com/arts-culture/books/taking-religion-seriously-review-faith-from-finish-to-start-029052ca)*, *[The New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/12/09/opinion/religious-belief-skepticism-doubt.html)* [(via Ross Douthat)](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/12/09/opinion/religious-belief-skepticism-doubt.html), *[Quillette](https://quillette.com/2026/02/01/god-of-the-yawning-gaps-taking-religion-seriously-charles-murray-review/)*, and [Andrew Sullivan](https://andrewsullivan.substack.com/p/charles-murray-on-taking-religion), among others.

The problem is that within progressive communities, Murray’s name has been dragged through the mud so thoroughly that it’s just assumed that he’s, like, scrounging by on Substack crumbs somewhere, desperate for mainstream media attention. That just isn’t the case! And it’s important to understand this because it is important to inhabit actual reality. If instead you build yourself a more comfortable but less real reality in which Charles Murray has been *disgraced* and *no one serious listens to him*, where does that get you? If you don’t want Murray to have intellectual power, needn’t you inhabit the *correct* reality, in which he does, if only so you can push back?

Steven Pinker has had a long, successful career as a public intellectual. Surely there are real debates to be had about his output. But all these “legitimizing” and “platforming” and “being used by” accusations... I really think they’re quite half-baked.

[^1]: I don’t want to ignore the Epstein stuff, but it’s really tangential to the subject of this article so I’m sticking it in a footnote. Huber mentions that Pinker’s name came up a lot in the Epstein files. This isn’t a surprise given that Pinker was one of a number of public intellectuals Epstein admired and wanted to get closer to. But there just isn’t much *there* there in terms of any real relationship between Pinker and Epstein. Huber writes:

> The only \[email\] from Pinker himself — to an Epstein assistant in March 2012, four years after the conviction \[[for solicitation of an underage prostitute](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2008/jul/02/usa.internationalcrime1)\] — said he’d be “delighted to meet with him” when Epstein visited Harvard. “I probably shouldn’t have said yes,” Pinker says now, “but I was being polite—he was a donor to Harvard.” (Pinker says they didn’t ultimately meet up.) 

At an event all three men were at in 2014, [Lawrence Krauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lawrence_Krauss) also asked Pinker if Epstein could come over to his table to say hi, which culminated in a photo of Pinker and Epstein. Pinker tells Huberman he regrets having said yes.

I think there’s a reasonable argument — an argument it sounds like Pinker himself wouldn’t really dispute — that he went along to get along when it came to post-2008 Epstein. I’m very anti-shunning, but I think a lot of people would have saved themselves a lot of trouble by adopting the not-exactly-draconian approach that it’s generally best to simply not associate professionally with, or take money from, someone who has pleaded guilty to solicitation of a minor, even if that’s the “only” thing they did. Of course, in Epstein’s case, this was only the [tiniest tip of the iceberg](https://www.nytimes.com/2019/07/10/us/politics/acosta-epstein.html).
