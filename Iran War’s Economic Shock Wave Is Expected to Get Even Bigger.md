President Trump’s [naval blockade](https://www.wsj.com/world/middle-east/iran-cease-fire-terms-explained-3a35fb92?mod=article_inline) of Iran risks further upending a global economy already battered by weeks of war, escalating a regional clash into a worldwide financial shock that could prove more devastating than the fighting itself.

The U.S. blockade on ships entering or exiting Iranian ports is set to drain more oil from a tight market, prolong the squeeze on other key commodities flowing through the Strait of Hormuz and inject significant uncertainty into the global economy.

[Oil prices jumped](https://www.wsj.com/livecoverage/stock-market-today-dow-sp-500-nasdaq-04-13-2026?mod=article_inline) on Monday. Aluminum prices surged to a four-year high amid fears of prolonged supply squeeze from a region producing nearly a 10th of the world’s supply of the key industrial metal.

The oil shock is already rippling through Asia, forcing some factories to slash production and a small but growing number of gas stations to ration fuel. Airports across the region are starved for jet fuel with no quick fix in sight, and some airlines are already paring back flights.

For Gulf states, the damage is shaping up to be the worst in decades—sparking major economic contractions and shattering their reputation as havens for business and tourism. In Europe, the continent’s already weak economic growth is slowing to a crawl.

The U.S., a net energy exporter, is less likely to see shortages, but higher prices at the pump are hitting consumers. President Trump on Sunday conceded that energy prices might not fall soon and could still be higher when voters head to polls in midterm elections this fall.

On Monday, European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen said the continued closure of Hormuz is “greatly damaging” for Europe. The European Union is working on plans such as temporarily easing its subsidy rules and encouraging member states to coordinate gas purchases, she said.

“Unquestionably, everyone is worse off,” said Gordon Flake, chief executive at the Perth USAsia Centre at the University of Western Australia.

One of the few winners could be Russia, where oil and gas account for around a quarter of government revenue.

There will no doubt be political shock waves, too. In recent days in Ireland, angry farmers blocked roads, fuel terminals and a refinery to protest rising fuel prices. In India, a rise in cooking-gas prices due to the war has exacerbated labor unrest among factory workers upset over stagnant wages. After thousands of contract workers protested outside factories in one northern Indian state last week, the state government increased the minimum wage there by 35%. Soon, factory workers in a neighboring state took to the streets.

Across the world, the rising cost of living will likely translate into frustrated voters looking to take it out on incumbent parties. On Sunday, voters in Hungary handed a [landslide victory to the opposition](https://www.wsj.com/world/europe/hungary-election-results-orban-b2dbd42a?mod=article_inline), ending 16 years of rule for Prime Minister Viktor Orbán.

The depth of the economic impact will depend on how long the disruption of the Strait of Hormuz, where a fifth of the world’s oil was once transported, continues. The bad news: Traffic in the waterway is unlikely to return to prewar levels soon.

Even if the fighting ends for good—a big if—the hit to energy prices is likely to be long-lasting. Shipping delays will further hold back supplies, as will damage to key energy facilities in the Gulf. Crude oil prices likely won’t return to prewar levels before the end of 2027, Germany’s state-owned KfW bank warned in a recent note to clients.

And the surge in prices is already [stoking inflation](https://www.wsj.com/economy/cpi-inflation-report-march-2026-bb353007?mod=article_inline), likely pushing central banks to increase interest rates and hurt global economic growth.

Across Asia, the supply squeeze is already starting to bite.

South Korea’s legislature over the weekend passed more than $17 billion in emergency relief, including cash subsidies of up to roughly $400 for low-income citizens and other measures. [Asiana Airlines](https://www.wsj.com/market-data/quotes/KR/XKRX/020560), the country’s second-largest carrier, temporarily halted more than a dozen round-trip flights to China and Cambodia through May. Vietnam Airlines has also cut flights.

On Monday, Japanese toilet giant [Toto](https://www.wsj.com/market-data/quotes/JP/XTKS/5332) halted orders for prefabricated bath units because of a shortage of naphtha, a critical petrochemical derived from crude oil.

As one of the first nations hit by a naphtha squeeze, Japan is also seeing the shock waves spread to hospitals, where healthcare executives are urgently calling for domestic stockpiles of medical plastics, including surgical gloves and dialysis syringes. Japanese Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi said she has assigned a team of ministers to tackle the shortages, but denied a major shortage, saying Japan still has secured at least four months’ worth of naphtha.

Disruptions to [helium supply](https://www.wsj.com/world/iran-war-chokes-off-helium-supply-critical-for-ai-bf020a3f?mod=article_inline), meanwhile, could hit South Korea and Malaysia’s semiconductor industries. Some Filipinos who had been working in the Middle East are returning home because of the fighting, meaning the loss of remittance money for their families. Expensive airfares could reduce global tourism revenues.

“These spillover effects are across the board in every single sector,” said Blake Berger, deputy director at Verve Research, a Southeast Asia-focused think tank. “It doesn’t matter if you’re in Singapore or Brunei, Cambodia or Laos, you’re feeling the impact of the war in Iran one way or another.”

Germany, Europe’s biggest economy, was supposed to see a real uptick in growth for the first time since the pandemic this year due to debt-fueled spending. But the country’s top economic forecasters have now cut their forecast to 0.6% growth this year from 1.3%.

A significant question mark is how long the Strait of Hormuz stays shut. If shipping through the strait is constrained for another two months, global growth would weaken and only return to its previous trend by the end of 2028, according to UBS. A longer shutdown would shave a full percentage point off global growth and could cause a shallow recession in the U.S.

Iran’s Gulf neighbors are bearing the brunt of the fallout. Many Gulf energy exporters can’t get their supplies to market. Qatar suffered major damage to its LNG facilities, and repairs could take up to five years. Across the Gulf, repairing damaged energy infrastructure could cost over $25 billion, according to consulting firm Rystad Energy.

Capital Economics forecasts Qatar’s gross domestic product to shrink by 13% this year, the United Arab Emirates’s by 8%, and Saudi Arabia’s by 6.6%. Qatar’s GDP shrank by 3.6% during the pandemic in 2020.

Heavy industry in the Gulf and Europe is buckling. The U.A.E. and similar Gulf states have also seen their business models—reliant on projecting stability in a volatile region—take a severe hit.

The Gulf’s pitch as a premier global hub for sports, exhibitions and conferences has also been hurt, with everything from financial and cryptocurrency conferences to Formula One races in the region being scrapped in recent weeks.

The conflict threatens the region’s booming tourism industry as well as its aggressive push into technology and artificial intelligence. High-profile infrastructure, including Amazon data centers, were struck.

A hostile Iran will remain a constant tail-risk for the region even if the war ends, said Hamzeh Al Gaaod, an economist who focuses on the region. “That is something businesses will have to factor in. That is an added risk premium,” he said.

Postwar, energy prices could carry a permanent premium for as long as Iran retains the ability to weaponize the Strait of Hormuz.

“There will be ongoing questions about the predictability, certainty, and stability in the wider Gulf region with an Iranian regime that is battle-hardened and more extreme than the one that existed before,” said Mujtaba Rahman, head of Europe for global risk analysis firm Eurasia Group. “The implications are very far reaching.”
