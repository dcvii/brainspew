Spotify insiders probably saw this coming. In the months leading up to the all-time high, they sold a massive number of shares. CEO Daniel Ek (now Executive Chairman of the company) sold relentlessly—to a degree I’ve never seen before from a corporate leader.

Here’s a summary via *[Music Business Worldwide](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/daniel-ek-has-now-cashed-out-800m-in-spotify-stock-with-his-latest-transaction-banking-28-8m/)*.

I note that Spotify hit its all-time high on June 26, 2025. It’s down a staggering 40% since that time. So Ek looks very smart indeed.

Ek wasn’t the only insider selling. [In late 2024, I wrote about stock sales](https://www.honest-broker.com/p/strange-and-dazzling-things-are-happening) from the Chief Financial Officer, the Chief Technology Officer, the General Counsel, and others. These [insider sales have continued into 2026](https://www.tradingview.com/news/tradingview:0da1c7c756acd:0-spotify-technology-insiders-sold-shares-worth-over-21m/).

Insider selling isn’t always a warning sign—corporate execs have legitimate reasons to cash out and diversify. But when I see it on this large scale, I get suspicious.

So what is *really* happening at Spotify?

During this period of decline, the company has focused on two strategies—so that’s the first place to look for the source of the problem.

1. Spotify has pursued price increases aggressively in all its major markets. In January, the [company announced another hike for US subscribers](https://techcrunch.com/2026/01/15/spotify-raises-its-subscription-prices-in-the-u-s-again/) —the third increase in three years.
2. Spotify has embraced AI with a vengeance—even as other platforms step back. In May, the [company bragged about its new deal with Universal Music Group](https://newsroom.spotify.com/2026-05-21/universal-music-group-spotify-licensing-agreements-fan-made-covers-remixes/), which allows users to create their own AI cover versions and remixes of hit songs.

These moves have definitely boosted visibility and cash flow. But Wall Street isn’t impressed. The share price has dropped even more than subscription prices went up. So what gives?

> ### “Spotify doesn’t view music the same way I do. So it is pursuing a customer capture and harvesting approach.”

The short answer is *subscription fatigue*, and it’s happening everywhere. Too many media companies have boosted their subscription prices lately—and some have done so repeatedly.

Spotify clearly sees the source of the problem, and is scrambling to respond. Check out some of its moves from the last few weeks.

- Last month [Spotify actually reversed a price increase](https://www.androidauthority.com/spotify-price-hike-reversed-3666536/) —in India. That’s a sign that it may have lost confidence in its price-boosting strategy.
- A few days ago, [Spotify launched a new program to sell concert tickets to fans](https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/18/spotifys-reserved-ticket-sales-to-musics-superfans-are-now-going-live/). This is an odd decision for a company whose very existence has had a devastating impact on live music. But now it wants to make a buck from the same performance ecosystem it has ravaged.
- And just this week, [Spotify announced an expansion of its ad program](https://musically.com/2026/06/24/spotify-seeking-to-grow-ad-business-via-ai-ad-voice-overs-and-automation/). This is another indicator that it is desperately seeking ways of growing revenues without raising prices.

But subscription fatigue is bigger than just this one company. If you look at the share price for Netflix—which has also boosted prices aggressively—it’s even worse than Spotify’s.

What would I do if I ran Spotify?

For a start, I’d take a look at its competitor Qobuz, which somehow managed to grow its user based by more than 200% in just one year. Qobuz has thrived with the following strategy:

- **Fair treatment of musicians** Qobuz payments to artists per stream are [six times what Spotify gives out](https://www.digitalmusicnews.com/2026/05/26/qobuz-fastest-growing-streaming-music-platforms/).)
- **High audio quality.**
- **Partnerships with live music events** (for example, [the Newport Jazz Festival](https://community.qobuz.com/news-en/qobuz-summer-festival-highlights-at-we-out-here-newport-folk-newport-jazz))
- **Only human curation of music.**
- **Only human music is recommended.**
- **Strict restrictions on AI (**for example, Qobuz will never generate AI music itself).
- **Fair prices to consumers** ([less than Spotify](https://www.honest-broker.com/p/is-spotify-vulnerable)).

If I ran Spotify, I would do pretty much the same thing. (I’d also improve the user interface and make some other enhancements—which I will probably write about at a future date.) In other words, I’d focus on fair dealing, partnering, and quality.

These are core values that attract customer loyalty and support the larger music ecosystem. You can build on them. Over the long term, they make a company stronger, while squeezing and manipulating eventually backfire.

But Spotify doesn’t view music the same way I do. So it is pursuing acustomer *capture* and *harvesting* approach. That may give a short term boost to profits, but will eventually backfire—because it creates so much ill-will and distrust.

We are seeing this all play out in real time. So I expect that I will revisit this topic in the near future.
