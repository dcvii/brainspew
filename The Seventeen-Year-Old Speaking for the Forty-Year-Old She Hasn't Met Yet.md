*“I’ve done my research and I’m prepared to accept the risks.”* If you have not heard your child say some version of this yet, you likely will. It is the sentence that closes conversations, that silences parental objections, that establishes the young person’s status as an adult in charge of her own body. It is meant to be unanswerable, because it borrows the language of adult medical decision-making — informed consent, autonomy, bodily sovereignty — and applies it to a person who has barely reached sexual maturity, what to speak of cognitive. The sentence works, most of the time, on parents who have absorbed the same language and who are afraid of appearing to override their child’s agency. I want to talk about why that sentence does not work on me, and why it should not work on you.

The premise of informed consent, in adult medical practice, is that the person receiving the information can meaningfully assess it. Experience is what makes that assessment possible, and experience is precisely what a seventeen-year-old cannot have accumulated in the amount of time she has been alive. When a forty-year-old is told about the potential side effects of a medication, she brings to that conversation a body that has lived. She has had a bad flu; she has torn a ligament; she has watched her mother go through menopause, her father chemotherapy, and her aunt, death; she has friends whose marriages ended, whose children got sick, whose plans fell apart. She knows, in her body, that things she thought would never happen do happen, and that when they happen they matter. She has been humbled by time in ways a seventeen-year-old, however intelligent, has not been.

I have never met a thirty-year-old, or a forty-year-old, or a fifty-year-old, who would happily hand over the design of her later life to her seventeen-year-old self. This is not a thing that exists in the world. Every adult I know has looked back on some version of her earlier self with a mixture of affection and disbelief, and often with the specific thought that she is grateful she did not act on what she was so sure of at seventeen. The developmental gap between seventeen and forty is not small, and it is not simply that the older person has accumulated more information. The older person is, in a real sense, a different person, with a different nervous system, a different relationship to her own body, and a different sense of what matters. To imagine that seventeen has the standing to sign contracts on behalf of forty is a category error.

This clarifies what is actually happening when your child comes at you with the informed-consent language. She is speaking as if she were the adult who will one day live with the outcome; she is not that adult, and she cannot access what that adult would want. What she can access, and what she is actually reporting to you, is what her current self, in her current body, in her current social world, thinks would make her feel better right now. Those perceptions are worth taking seriously as feelings and fantasies, but they are not the same thing as a judgment about a future she has not yet met. Treating them as equivalent is where the confusion lives, both in her mind and in the culture that has taught her to speak this way.

The move I want to give you here is to hold ground on behalf of that future person. Standing in for her future self is not a violation of your child’s autonomy; it is the version of parental responsibility the situation calls for. When the young person in front of you is being asked to make a decision she does not have the developmental equipment to make, someone must stand in for the person who will live with the outcome. That someone, until she can be trusted to do it for herself, is you. You are her advocate, and your advocacy takes the form of refusing to let the child version of her sign anything irrevocable on the older woman’s behalf.

There is a phrase that sometimes helps to name this out loud, delivered without cruelty and with something closer to affection: *that’s very seventeen of you.* Or, when appropriate, *that’s very twenty-two of you.* It is a small verbal inversion of the “okay boomer” line that adolescents have been using to dismiss the older generation. What you are doing with it is naming, gently, what your child cannot see: her certainty is age-shaped. She is not wrong to feel what she feels; she is wrong to think that what she feels is a stable foundation for permanent decisions. The line lands because it is true. Most adults, hearing themselves at seventeen, would agree.

When you take this position, you will need to be prepared for a particular kind of rebuttal, which usually sounds something like this: *I know the risks. I’ve read about them. I’m prepared to live with them.* The temptation is to receive this as a reasoned response you can answer with more information. It is not that. It is a rehearsed statement produced by a person who has read some material with a motivated conclusion already in place, and who has metabolized every risk as either exaggerated or acceptable. If she encounters a story about a young woman whose double mastectomy left her with chronic pain and an inability to breastfeed her own baby, she will explain it away as a rare complication, or as something that will not happen to her, or as a price worth paying. This is what motivated reasoning does; you cannot argue her out of it with better facts, because facts are not what she is using.

What you can do is refuse to accept that her “informed” statement means what it claims to mean. You can be plain about this. You might say something like: *I hear that you believe you have done your research. I want you to hear me on this. There is nothing you, at seventeen, can say to me that would convince me you have the standing to make this decision. The reason lies with the person who will actually live in this body when the consequences arrive. She is someone I know you have not yet met, and I am not going to hand her over.* Delivered without heat, delivered with the calm of someone who has thought about this longer than her child has been alive, this position lands. Not necessarily in the moment; she may storm out. But the seed goes in. She has now met the version of you that will not be moved by the informed-consent script.

There is a second reason to hold this posture, which is that doing so preserves something for your future relationship with her. If she someday looks back on this period and recognizes, as many young women eventually do, that she was not equipped to make the decisions she was so certain about, she will need someone to have held the line. Both detransitioners and parents of desisters alike report, in retrospect, that the parents’ holding the line helped make coming back possible. The daughter could afford to rage against that refusal in the moment; what she could not afford, in the long run, was to have had no one refusing. If you cave to the informed-consent script now, you deprive her of the person she may most need later.

I want to close with a small metaphor I sometimes offer parents who are struggling to hold this posture, because it feels like they are the only one, and because their child is very loud, and because so many voices in the culture are telling them they are the ones getting it wrong. Be a tree. Plant your feet and imagine roots going down beneath you into the ground; you do not have to move or shout. Your job is to stay standing, patient and rooted, for as long as it takes. The storm will pass over the tree. What the tree offers is not its ability to argue with the storm, but its refusal to be blown down by it. That is the version of parental authority this situation calls for. Your seventeen-year-old will not thank you for it now. Your forty-year-old daughter, if you are fortunate enough to meet her, will.
