[Cuba](https://www.wsj.com/topics/place/cuba) ’s regime is under pressure as foreign benefactors have vanished, but it’s still hoping for relief from its old friends at the United Nations. Its problem is that U.S. Ambassador to the U.N. Mike Waltz is willing to tell the truth about [Cuba](https://www.wsj.com/topics/place/cuba) that the regime doesn’t like to hear.

The long-time [Havana](https://www.wsj.com/topics/place/havana) narrative, echoed in much of the U.S. media and parts of Congress, is that Cuban poverty is caused by U.S. sanctions. Cuba has called for an emergency session of the General Assembly on Friday on what it calls the U.S. “blockade.” The U.S. lost the vote Tuesday (136-9 with 30 abstentions) on whether to hold the session, but not before Mr. Waltz and his deputy, Jeff Bartos, catalogued [Havana](https://www.wsj.com/topics/place/havana) ’s role in the island’s misery.

“For 67 years, the Cuban regime has enriched itself, its ruling elites, while abusing its people, strangling private enterprise, criminalizing dissent, and clinging to failed communist economics,” Mr. Waltz told the U.N. He spoke about some of the political prisoners in jail for writing poetry and music. And he said that while Cubans live in the dark owing to the failure of the electricity grid, there’s still enough fuel for the Castro family’s private jets.

Mr. Waltz also took apart the claim that the U.S. blocks Cuba’s access to world markets. “If there’s an embargo, a blockade, whatever name they want to put on it, how did humanitarian aid from Belize, Canada, Chile, China, Colombia, the European Union, Mexico, Spain, Russia, Uruguay, and the U.N. itself reach the country just in the last few months?” Mr. Waltz asked. “There is no ring of Navy warships sitting around this island blocking trade or humanitarian aid going into Cuba. It’s fake. It’s false. It’s a lie. Period.”

The U.S. has sanctioned the regime’s big shots, but it sends “a half a billion dollars a year in commodities to Cuba” and it sent “more than $100 million in aid” this year alone, Mr. Waltz said. America also works “with the Vatican and the Catholic Church to deliver food and medicine to the needy. Aid that we also provided—additional aid after the most recent hurricane.” Some blockade.

All of this left Cuban Ambassador Bruno Rodríguez sputtering in rage as he banged the desk and accused the American of failing to observe “decorum,” and calling him “a liar.” That’s called hitting a nerve.

So “why does Cuba continue to call for this debate and come to this microphone?” Mr. Waltz asked his colleagues. “The answer is simple: because blaming the United States is the only economic plan Havana has left.”
