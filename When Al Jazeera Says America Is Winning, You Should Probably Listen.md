## A friend challenged me for citing an Al Jazeera editorial. Here’s why that challenge misses everything that makes the editorial significant.

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1400/format:webp/1*jKmi_uYzNOU4Xe0uqa1o7Q.jpeg)

A friend sent me a message last week. Polite, but pointed. He had seen my post — the one where I mentioned, almost in passing, that even Al Jazeera was now arguing that the US-Israel campaign against Iran was strategically succeeding. His objection was simple: “Al Jazeera? Really? That’s your source?”

It’s a fair instinct. For twenty years, Al Jazeera has been the Qatari-funded network that gave platform to Hamas political leadership, covered Western military operations with barely concealed hostility, and reliably provided the emotional counternarrative to any American or Israeli strategic claim. Citing it in support of a Washington-Tel Aviv position feels, at minimum, like a category error.

But that’s precisely why it matters. And that’s what I want to explain here.

> *“The significance of this editorial is not what it says. It’s who is saying it — and why, right now.”*

## What actually appeared, and where

On Al Jazeera’s website, in late March 2026, an op-ed appeared under the byline of ==Muhanad Seloom — a professor of International Politics and Security at the Doha Institute for Graduate Studies, with a résumé that includes advisory work for the US State Department and several defense and intelligence agencies.== The piece was titled, without ambiguity: *“* [*The USA-Israel strategy against Iran is working. Here’s why.*](https://www.aljazeera.com/opinions/2026/3/16/the-us-israeli-strategy-against-iran-is-working-here-is-why)*”*

Seloom’s argument is methodical. He writes from Doha — where, he notes, Iranian missiles have triggered evacuation alerts and Qatar Airways has begun emergency flights out of the capital. He was not writing from a comfortable distance. He distinguishes carefully between the costs of the campaign (which he acknowledges: 1,400 civilian casualties, global economic disruption, American losses) and its strategic balance sheet — which he says the critics are systematically miscounting.

> **–90%  
> **==Iranian ballistic missile launches in 17 days: from ~350 on Feb 28 to ~25 by March 14. Drone launches followed the same curve: 800+ on day one, 75 by day fifteen. “Hundreds of launchers rendered inoperable,” Seloom writes.==

He goes further. Iran entered 2026, he writes, holding 440 kg of uranium enriched to 60% — enough material for multiple weapons, and ==fewer than two weeks of additional work away from bomb-grade fissile material==. The campaign struck Natanz. It rendered Fordow inoperable. It is now, in its second phase, targeting the industrial base needed to rebuild enrichment capacity. ==The logic, as Seloom presents it, is not to bomb Iran into submission but to impose reconstruction costs so high, and so permanent, that the strategic clock is reset by decades.==

The Strait of Hormuz threat, he addresses directly: yes, ==Iran can close it, but 90% of Iranian oil exports pass through it==. Closing it is an act of self-immolation as much as aggression, and it would alienate China — Iran’s principal economic partner — faster than any American sanction ever has. The naval capacity needed to sustain such a closure is, in any case, being reduced daily.

> “The question is not whether the Strait will reopen. It’s when — and with what residual Iranian capacity.”  
> — Muhanad Seloom, Doha Institute for Graduate Studies

On the proxy network: the death of Supreme Leader Khamenei has removed the apex of the command structure. The IRGC’s senior leadership has been decapitated. The proxies — Hezbollah, Iraqi militias, the Houthis — are now executing pre-authorized response protocols, which Seloom reads not as a sign of Iranian reach but as a sign of a system anticipating its own destruction. They will continue to strike. But in a progressively disorganized, politically costly way for the governments hosting them.

## Now: why does the byline matter more than the argument?

Al Jazeera is not the BBC. It does not have an editorial firewall between the newsroom and the ownership. Every senior observer of the Arab media landscape treats the network as, essentially, the broadcast arm of the Qatari emirate — the Al Thani family, which also supplies the Prime Minister, the Defense Minister, and most of the senior government apparatus. An authorized, prominent op-ed on Al Jazeera is not one journalist’s opinion. It is a signal.

So the question becomes: what changed in Doha?

The answer is in the article itself, but you have to read it geographically. Qatar has long maintained a studied ambiguity toward Iran — partly ideological, partly structural. Qatar and Iran share one of the largest natural gas deposits in the world (the South Pars / North Dome field). They have to coexist. Qatar has therefore quietly hosted Hamas’s political leadership for years, maintained back-channel relations with Tehran, and positioned itself as the indispensable mediator between incompatible worlds — a profitable role that required keeping both Washington and Tehran at least partially satisfied.

> *“Iran did not just threaten Qatar’s allies. It attacked Qatar’s capital, its energy infrastructure, its airline. The ambiguity is over.”*

That strategic neutrality is now gone — not because Qatar has converted to a new ideology, but because Iran attacked Qatar directly. Iranian missiles struck civilian areas of Doha. Qatar Airways initiated evacuation flights. The Al Thani family, which has spent decades building Qatar into a hub of international mediation and sovereign wealth accumulation, watched missiles fall on its own city. The calculation changed. Not morally. Transactionally.

This is, incidentally, the same dynamic playing out with the UAE. The Gulf Arab states that had maintained a complex, risk-managed relationship with Iran are being forced — by Iranian military action against them — into the American coalition. Iran has done what decades of American diplomacy could not: compel the Gulf states to choose, unambiguously.

## What my friend’s objection actually reveals

==My friend’s skepticism toward Al Jazeera as a source operates on a model of media that is, in this context, the wrong model==. He was applying the logic of source evaluation appropriate to a journalistic outlet — *is this source reliable? Does it have an editorial agenda?* Both fair questions when you’re reading an investigative report or a factual claim.

But what I was citing was not a factual claim from Al Jazeera. I was citing the fact *that* Al Jazeera published it. The source’s identity is the story. An editorial in Le Monde arguing that French foreign policy is failing carries different weight than the same editorial in the Élysée’s official newsletter. Not because Le Monde is more reliable — but because the Élysée has incentives to deny failure that Le Monde does not. When the incentive structure flips — when the entity with every reason to maintain a narrative decides to abandon it — that is information. That is signal.

Al Jazeera had every structural incentive to maintain its traditional framing: American overreach, Israeli aggression, the Arab world as perpetual victim of Western and Zionist power. That framing is its brand, its audience, its political utility to the emirate. Publishing the opposite required authorization at the highest level. It means someone in the Al Thani family looked at the strategic situation and decided that the old narrative was now a liability — that maintaining the fiction of Iranian resilience served no Qatari interest.

That decision, made in Doha, is geopolitically significant independent of whether Seloom’s military analysis is correct in every particular. It signals that the Gulf Arab consensus is shifting. That Iran’s strategy of regional deterrence through proxy networks and nuclear adjacency has failed to prevent a catastrophic military degradation. That the states which were supposed to stay neutral — or quietly supportive — are publicly recalibrating.

## A note on the limits of the argument

To be precise about what I am and am not claiming: Seloom’s article is not proof that the military campaign is wise, proportionate, or strategically complete. He himself concedes the post-war plan is incomplete, the political communication has been confused, and the “endgame” question remains open. An Iran degraded to the point of strategic incapacity is not the same as a stable Middle East. The knowledge required to rebuild a nuclear program cannot be bombed away. The 1,400 civilian dead are not an accounting entry.

What the article represents — and what my citation of it was meant to convey — is more limited and more specific: that the campaign has achieved measurable military objectives at a rate that has surprised analysts, including analysts who were skeptical of it; and that states with strong incentives to deny this are beginning to acknowledge it.

==That’s not a moral endorsement. It’s a strategic reading. The two are different exercises, and conflating them is how most of the commentary on this conflict has gone wrong==.

## Why this matters for how we read the war

The dominant Western media narrative — *no plan, no endgame, rising costs, possible quagmire* — is not wrong about the costs. It is measuring the wrong variables. Seloom’s phrase deserves to be quoted directly: “The critics are counting the price of the campaign, but ignoring its strategic balance sheet.” The price is visible, daily, in real time. The balance sheet accrues more slowly, less cinematically, in degraded missile inventories and destroyed centrifuge cascades and fractured proxy command structures.

This is not a comfortable argument. It does not make the civilian casualties smaller or the regional disruption less real. But strategic analysis is not a comfort service. It is an attempt to understand what is actually happening, with what consequences, toward what plausible outcomes.

When Al Jazeera — that Al Jazeera — publishes an op-ed arguing that the US-Israel strategy is working, the correct response is not to dismiss it because of the source. The correct response is to ask: ==what does it mean that this source is saying this, now?==

That’s the question I was trying to flag. I’m glad my friend pushed back. It gave me the occasion to explain it properly.
