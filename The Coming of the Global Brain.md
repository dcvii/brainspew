I wanted to take the opportunity of the publication of Robert Wright’s [new book](https://www.amazon.com/-/de/dp/B0FYQKSMZJ/ref=sr_1_1?__mk_de_DE=%C3%85M%C3%85%C5%BD%C3%95%C3%91&crid=1UEOUJE63SVV3&dib=eyJ2IjoiMSJ9.M_neaaZoLB6IyLONe5P3MYH8gAyZjXUMsgbCBVFqXS0LyScmtwa5JQ7CD8VU3Ip9K8A_MEPzB-wul7gDHNkp3VqnN0DgSOPoGHzJAVD3b62qKWHsBJyhlmx4vo5CnSAHyx9ISXdHuvNKLwf20Z4kPAt88bdS96D0aavpSSq6Xe9Gs0tEMfFjp1YkZ6POoqP8ia4Jni65jCQtbA0m9jShSkKQUBHKsRM3OV65V_NK8Po.nGCYwk__xWjjEj03m6n9oQR2sTGMPFE3cls0PDwQAtE&dib_tag=se&keywords=wright+robert&qid=1783782306&sprefix=wright+robert%2Caps%2C205&sr=8-1) *The God Test: Artificial Intelligence and Our Coming Cosmic Reckoning* to spell out my agreements and disagreements with his evolutionary vision. Wright is, I think, correct in that we are immersed in the maturation process of a global brain and that this framing can help us understand the political and moral challenges of our time. However, we disagree about the evolutionary forces that drive the maturation process.

## The God Test

Most discussions about AI suffer from intellectual provincialism. The topic is so broad, its potential implications so far-reaching, that intuitions that serve us well in other endeavors break down here. Being versed in science fiction would today appear to be an essential part of education — science fiction has always been at war with intellectual provincialism. And I don’t know whether you have noticed, but you are living in the plot of a science fiction novel.

Think what you want of Teilhard de Chardin, but he too was a fighter against intellectual provincialism. In many ways his scifi philosophy seems to become more and more topical by the hour. A rebellious Jesuit priest, de Chardin developed a vision of the cosmos where evolution tends to generate more and more interconnected states of organization, from single cells to multi-cellular life to complex brains to a world-spanning culture. Extrapolating this trend, de Chardin predicted that evolution aimed at creating a “brain of brains” of which he saw the modern telephone networks as its first dendrites. At the end of evolution the universe would then reach a point of maximal connectedness which he called the Omega point. From a scientific perspective de Chardin’s work might often seem wanting. He thinks that the trends of evolution he observes are expressions of some kind of inherent life force of which there is, to date, no evidence. Still, it is hard to deny that there is something profoundly prescient about his thought even if his talent lay more in the realm of observation than in the realm of formulating theories about what he observed.[^1]

Robert Wright has positioned himself as an heir of Chardin’s vision early on, struggling to cleanse it from its vitalist connotations and anchoring it firmly in modern evolutionary thinking. In *Nonzero: The Logic of Human Destiny* (read it!), published in 1999, he laid out the theory that evolution, biological and cultural, has a pretty clear direction towards complexification and cooperation. And we don’t have to assume a vital force to understand how this happens — the real explanation is more interesting anyways. Technological evolution tends to create situations where there is a nonzero-sum dynamics, situations where it is advantageous to all actors to cooperate. In a nonzero-sum situation there is no strong antagonism between self-interested egotism and cooperation. Thus, biological and cultural evolution both tend to find these cooperative equilibria — cells combine into multi-cellular life, insects into hives, humans into nations. Cooperators just tend to outcompete non-cooperators.

*Nonzero* has a nice 2000s utopian end-of-history-vibe to it. Well, seems the end of history has been delayed. So now that Wright returns to his earlier Teilhardian thoughthe does so with a more dire message: AI-induced technological acceleration will heighten the stakes of finding a novel cooperative equilibrium. If we unleash the power of AI blindly we are sure to perish. The technology invites thinking in extremes. In its light, one may find it quite hard to imagine a future that is not either clearly dystopian or clearly utopian. This is why Wright uses the terminology of a “God Test”. The challenges we are facing are the kind of test that a God may put to his creation. Find the wise path to use this technological gift and you and your descendants shall live in a peaceful heaven ever after. But if you fail you shall burn in cybernetic hell.

Even though it is not as good as *Nonzero*, I found much to like about *The God Test*. As someone working in the AI field, I see it as the kind of short read I can recommend to non-specialists who want to understand why the insiders are getting so excited. The guiding metaphor for how contemporary neural-net-based AIs works, namely that they are a little like an artificial analogue of natural selection, will give non-specialist readers the right kind of intuitions about the technology, even if the details might be off a little.

However, I think the best thing about the book *The God Test* is that it gives us a *cosmic* vision of where AI technology may lead us. This is all the more praiseworthy as it comes from a guy who does not seem to be an optimist by nature, writing a book in a sub-genre where books have titles like *Our Final Invention* and *If Anyone Builds It, Everyone Dies*. The process we are immersed in right now may well be the most exciting explosion of creation and novelty that ever happened in the history of the universe. There is no way this will not be profoundly destabilizing. But whether it spells doom or glory is an open question. And the answer to this question may depend to a large extent on us.

## Nonzerosumness is not Enough

There is clearly merit to the idea that evolution is following some kind of vector. Complexity and interconnectedness are increasing and it seems they are increasing increasingly fast. The explanation for this is not obvious.

Wright thinks that the accumulation of nonzerosumness, i.e. mutually beneficial possibilities of cooperative behavior, explains the increasing connectedness of life and culture, presumably leading to a brain of brains, even though some of these brains might be silicon based. But, as I shall now argue, the accumulation of nonzerosumness cannot do this explanatory work. For nonzero-sum dynamics merely describe that under certain conditions some degree of cooperation is the optimal strategy. For instance, we can stipulate some population of hominids that behave “dovish” or “hawkish”, i.e. to be naturally prone to cooperation or prone to taking advantage of others. Where two doves meet everyone profits. Where two hawks meet this might end in a fight, which might end badly for everyone involved. Where a hawk meets a dove however, the hawk will get all the goods collected by the dove. So we might end up with a payoff matrix like this:

$$
\begin{aligned}
H & D \\ H & - 10 & 40 \\ D & 0 & 20
\end{aligned}
$$

If you want to complain that this is highly abstract and artificial then you are missing that point. The point is simply that some degree of cooperative behavior can emerge for purely self-interested (evolutionarily speaking) reasons. For it might seem that evolution by natural selection, be it on the biological or cultural level, will, by a ruthless logic of the survival of the fittest, in the long run only produce hawks. And it is true that in a population of doves being a hawk is highly advantageous. However, brief reflection will tell you that in a population of hawks it is advantageous to be a dove — for that will reduce the chances of deadly conflict even though you might be taken advantage of. Thus, evolution will tend to produce results closer to stable equilibria that contain some doves and some hawks. And this is just to say that purely Darwinian evolution will often tend to produce remarkable degrees of altruistic behavior where the underlying dynamics are right, i.e. where the relevant payoff matrix is populated by the right values.

The thought that nonzerosum dynamics can explain the Teilhardian tendency of evolution to generate ever more complex organizations, from multicellularity to the global village of human culture, is inviting. *Cooperation just works.* However, I think once the idea is formulated this clearly it should become clear that nonzerosum interactions can *not* fully bear the explanatory burden put on them in Wright’s world view. For evolutionary game theory only tells us that *if* the payoff matrix is structured in the right way, then cooperative complexification of interactions will happen. But it does not tell us which values to put into our payoff matrix. It does not, by itself, tell us why the above kind of values should be any more likely than:

$$
\begin{aligned}
H & D \\ H & 0 & 40 \\ D & 0 & 20
\end{aligned}
$$

In this case being a dove is just a losing strategy. In order to justify why the first kind of matrix is more likely to occur one has to appeal to something outside of evolutionary game theory. Without such an explanation evolutionary game theory will only tell us that cooperation can sometimes happen. And it is precisely an explanation of this kind that both *Nonzero* and *The God Test* are thin on. Wright hints that the evolution of technology creates sources of nonzerosumness but I just don’t see a systemic reason why technological evolution should inherently produce matrix-1-like setups more often than matrix-2-like ones. Sure, technology may sometimes make cooperation more likely, but it can also lower the costs of conflicts.

But there is hope for us naturalist Teilhardians! I think there is a more or less intuitive story about why complexification happens and even why it is actually happening increasingly fast. And that is the story of metaevolution, the theory that the medium of evolution itself evolves. Uncontroversial examples are sexual reproduction or horizontal gene transfer in microbes. Both of these mechanisms are hacks that increase the rate of the induction of helpful novelty into the genome as the usage of time-tested genetic information is a much better mutation engine than random exploration. The usage of such mechanisms is an evolutionary adaptation that increases the rates of evolutionary adaptation. Note that in both cases this happens by introducing the possibility of *vertical information flow*. In the case of microbes this vertical flow is more or less blind. In the case of sexual selection vertical flow is made even more efficient by the introduction of a selective gating mechanism, namely preferential mating. All of these mechanisms, from blind mutation to blind vertical gene transfer to sexual reproduction controlled by mating preference form a hierarchy of more and more sophisticated evolutionary mechanisms. Evolution evolves.

Metaevolutionary dynamics are, I think, more or less uncontroversial when we restrict ourselves to biology.[^2] But the domain of culture is, I think, just a metaevolutionary extension of the biological domain. In culture, evolution discovered a medium for accelerating the adaptive process a millionfold. Memes, coined as the cultural equivalent of genes, are just much faster replicators with replicator dynamics that can be controlled much more easily. In the cultural domain, you can observe your peers and only copy the behavioral patterns that seem to work. The world of the genome with all its mutation accelerators knows no comparable mechanism. Culture is the ultimate dynamics of adaptive vertical information flow.

There is a deeper point here, namely that metaevolution predicts the complexification of life and culture in the sense of promoting greater and greater connectedness. If generalized organisms are not only in a struggle for better adaptations to their environment, but also in a struggle for better mechanisms of finding these adaptations then, as the above examples should have made clear, there is clear advantage in enabling vertical flow. Vertical flow is information flow that violates clear distinctions between cells, individuals and cultures. Plausibly, today’s globally networked world is a result of this ever increasing web of vertical flow in the service of cultural evolution. Close off to it and be out-competed. We can frame this in terms of nonzero-sum dynamics of course, but the actual explanation of why nonzero-sum situations arise at all is due to the fact that metaevolution favors vertical information flow.

I have worked these out somewhat more [here](https://theanticompletionist.substack.com/p/3c73741b-2031-4a74-84f1-df9d762c7fcc?postPreview=paid&updated=2025-10-04T16%3A11%3A18.391Z&audience=everyone&free_preview=false&freemail=true). At any rate, I do not pretend that metaevolution is a fully worked out framework. In particular, if biological and cultural evolution are phenomena on one and the same spectrum, what is the overarching replicator?! Even in cultural evolution the relevant replicators seem to be hard to define (where does one meme stop and the next meme begin?), but for any generalized evolutionary theory these issues just get worse. But still, I think the general outlines of the metaevolutionary framework are well-defined enough to give us a hint why (a) evolution tends towards complexification and (b) this complexification can entail accelerated complexification.[^3]

What does all this entail about AI? Well, unlike in the story of nonzero-sum dynamics, the emergence of AI has a natural place within the metaevolutionary framework. The implications of this are scary, for it might seem that our time as the champions of the evolutionary process may be over quite soon. But I think only time will tell whether these are indeed apocalyptic prospects. After all, our species has been immersed in a metaevolutionary acceleration for all its history and was able to ride the wave. Thecentral question, and here I agree with Wright, is how the trend of complexification will continue. What can the role of an individual human be within a brain of brains? I obviously cannot answer this question, a question, by the way, that greatly troubled de Chardin. But I do find the thought consoling that we have, in some sense, always been immersed in such a brain of brains. We are not quite riding the metaevolutionary wave. We *are* the wave.

[^1]: Chardin’s *The Phenomenon of Man* is still readable. It’s full of powerful metaphors, alternative cognitive frames and quite a bit of bad science.

[^2]: For a nice popular presentation of further tricks that evolution has to make itself more effective, see Andreas Wagner’s *Arrival of the Fittest*.

[^3]: Work on dissipative adaptation may show that replicators are overrated. Here the general idea is that statistical mechanics predicts a certain propensity of non-equilibrium systems to adapt to external stimulation and turn that stimulation into global entropy. Maybe, so the idea, life is just an expression of this propensity raised to the nth degree? Now under certain conditions, dissipative adaptation should also favor regimes of faster dissipative adaptation, i.e. predict dissipative meta-adaptation. Then specific kinds of replicators are just symptoms of an underlying meta-adaptive dynamics of the entire system.
