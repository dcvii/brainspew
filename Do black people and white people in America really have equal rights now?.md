](https://www.quora.com/notifications)[Do black people and white people in America really have equal rights now?](https://www.quora.com/Do-black-people-and-white-people-in-America-really-have-equal-rights-now)

Yes. Of course.

Now I get a little irritated when people compare black vs white because all you can get out of that is a racial answer. If yes, then then racism, if no then no racism. I mean why bother with any geography, statistics, sociology, psychology or medicine if you have your racial answer?

It's a rather odd question considering who is President right now. Equal rights have always been a premise of the Constitution and a more than half a million people died in the Civil War over the question of the rights of the African in America.

But let me get to the underlying question, which is always, can the average black American expect (X) equal to the average white American. The answer is no because the averages aren't descriptive. Every study done since I was in high school has basically said equality exists across races \*if you control other variables\*. Now, me. I like to talk about zip codes. Why? Because back in the 70s the big deal about racial equality was all about public education. Essentially every other judge in conflicted areas said the answer was forced bussing. This is something that we all kind of understand in terms of conventional wisdom without knowing how that wisdom started. The terms 'inner city' and 'underprivileged' were the beginning euphemisms for black youth because the idea was that they weren't failing because they were black, but because they lived in dysfunctional neighborhoods. In those days to call somebody 'ghetto' was considered more insulting than it is today, primarily because black neighborhoods included people with middle class values as well as those with ghetto mentalities. Bottom line, integration began a migration out of the 'inner city'. And so began the breakdown of the very idea of 'The' Black Community.

So if you look at zip code 90016, where I grew up, you get one set of demographics. If you look at zip code 90277, where I live now, you get another set of demographics. I guarantee you that black Americans who live here get more (X) than black Americans who live there. I know. I left there to get here. But we may as well throw in Ferguson MO which is 63135.  
Now you can go and eyeball a zipcode comparison at this website. [Zip Code Comparison](http://www.mapszipcode.com/zip-codes-comparison/ "www.mapszipcode.com")

Now this may seem strange to some people, but I've been doing zip code comparisons like this for over 20 years. I literally did research to find the most affluent black neighborhoods in the country - I wanted to find out where I wanted to live. Since I had friends with fancy degrees who studied race, they pointed me to Massey & Denton. So here's a note for you. If anybody ever uses the term 'institutional racism' you can point them to my old, sorry web page that I used way back when that mattered to me and I really did study up on racial residential patterns. But you have to understand that I came to that kind of study with absolutely no question in my mind that my Civil Rights were intact. For me, it was all about the money. So how could I live around affluent and wealthy black folks, and what was it like in other black neighborhoods? Anyway here's some old stuff I did back in the 90s. [The Neighborhood Project](http://www.mdcbowen.org/p5/np/index.htm "www.mdcbowen.org").

I'm going to quote something out of the Neighborhood Project just to remind people that I've been around this block...

> #2. I have a theory which goes a little something like this. policy perscriptions made for white poor folk are no good for black poor folk. that is because white poor folk live close to white and black middle class folks - they can easily follow their leads. black poor folk, on the other hand are more isolated from any middle class folk. they have no immediate role models. furthermore, "go check in with the employment agency" may be good advice In 90008, however in 11238, it may be worthless. in both infrastructure and role-modelling, many policy prescriptions made may not work. getting the details is all worth it. in every argument where the 'colorblind' point of view seeks to minimalize race and speak strictly in economic terms, the question of geography nullifies their arguments. why? because america is still profoundly racially segregated residentially.
> 
> #3 there is also some work necessary in understanding the economics and politics of every community. for another example - recall the saying 'nigga please, you work for ups'. (that was biz markie). it turns out, by dint of some research that i just did that ups is the largest employer in cerritos california, with about 5500 jobs. cerritos is the hometown of tiger woods, and the median household income is about 47,000 bucks. cerritos is about 7.5% black. this is a town that didn't exist in 1950. now i know something about the town and it ain't paradise, but i often shake my head and wonder why some black folks would remain in a town with a 300 year legacy of racism knowing full well that they have the skills to drive a ups truck.

  
The clear and sterling point here is that because there are so many black Americans, you can and will always find plenty of examples of anything you ask for. The problem, of course, is that most of the time, people are looking to confirm their biases, not do actual research. That facts are as clear as the black shirtless men on the corner, and black university presidents. There's no one answer for 40 million Americans.

I'm going to also just drop this little nugget on you, which should inspire your curiosity to see what other kinds of folks like this exist. [Frank E. Petersen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_E._Petersen "en.wikipedia.org")
