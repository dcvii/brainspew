Victor Davis Hanson, Ben Shapiro, Deborah Lipstadt, and others respond to Niall Ferguson and John-Clark Levin’s claim that AI, social media, and Holocaust denial herald the death of history.

***“The goal of my entire career as an historian was to ensure that citizens learned the lessons of history—including the vital lesson that antisemitic rhetoric can be translated into genocidal reality if it is adopted by a modern state. Yet today, as AI permeates social networks, anti-history is spreading like a cancer.”***

***So wrote Free Press columnist Niall Ferguson after publishing, along with artificial intelligence researcher John-Clark Levin, a provocative essay titled “The Death of History.” In it, they argue that the institutions and methods that for decades have helped preserve historical truth are now being overwhelmed by AI and social media. Ferguson’s conclusion? “I have to admit: I have failed utterly.” It is an extraordinary admission from one of our nation’s leading historians, and one that has raised many questions in the days since: Have historians failed? Is truth itself losing its authority? Or is this just a new battlefield in an old fight?***

***To explore those questions, we turned to a group of leading historians, writers, and public intellectuals. Below, Victor Davis Hanson, Deborah Lipstadt, Bernard-Henri Lévy, Ben Shapiro, and others weigh in on whether we really are witnessing the death of history—and what, if anything, should come next. —The Editors***

### Victor Davis Hanson: Historians Must Rise Up

Niall Ferguson is, of course, correct that AI and social media supercharge sensationalized pseudo-history offered by the half-educated. He is also correct that the spread of pernicious lies allows a fake past to alter a real present.

But these dangers hardly suggest history is dead, and that historians are now defeated. The message of history is timeless, the medium of the new technologies transitory. Just because modern pumps deliver 3,000 gallons of water per minute versus 19th-century hand pumps that strained to produce three gallons does not mean the essence of water itself was changed by radically new delivery systems.

The nature of war remains understandable because human nature is constant—despite the bewildering new technologies of death that each generation conjures to supposedly “reinvent” war. In other words, figures such as [Darryl Cooper](https://www.thefp.com/p/victor-davis-hanson-the-truth-about), an online historian and Holocaust revisionist, have no monopoly on such methods of dissemination, some of which could equally be used to correct his nonsense—if there were a will to do so.

One reason why these electronic provocateurs succeed is the silence of historians. Too many are reluctant to wade into the online miasma and use their expertise for the public good. Academics as a species are not adverse to publicity—in fact, they yearn for public recognition—but rarely at the expense of crossing the internet mob. Daryl Coopers proliferate because the university turned history into melodrama and campus historians, too often, into nodding human bots.

“Too many are reluctant to wade into the online miasma and use their expertise for the public good.” —Victor Davis Hanson

Similarly, the farce that was *The New York Times* ’ 1619 Project lived on far longer than it should have, since only a few American historians took the time and the heat to dismantle the faddishness and incoherence of its progenitor, Nikole Hannah-Jones.

But as the university continues to lose credibility with the public, and as renegade podcasts proliferate, historians must accelerate their own public outreach through online courses, intellectually honest historical podcasts, and, yes, new technologies that can crowd out the chaff of counterfeit history.

To suggest this is impossible is not just to surrender history to electrified ignorance, but to surrender learning itself.

He has written or edited 24 books, including [The Second World Wars: How the First Global Conflict Was Fought and Won](https://bookshop.org/a/93116/9781541674103).***

### Deborah Lipstadt: Our New Age of Holocaust Denial

During my 2000 London libel trial, when Holocaust denier David Irving sued me for calling him a Holocaust denier, I received many messages of support. The two I treasure most were from London cabbies. One day, during an exceptionally bleak period when the testimony in court concerned the capacity of gas chambers, I hailed a taxi to take me to the High Court. As the driver, who had no reason to know who I was, handed me my change, he added: “Get the bastard. You’re fighting the good fight.”

On the day of my victory, as I was crossing Piccadilly Circus, another cabbie spotted me, slowed down, leaned out of his window, and, flashing a V for victory sign, yelled: “Well done, Madam.” I am not sure either of those things would happen today. Certainly not if those two drivers spent much time on social media platforms.

I used to divide Holocaust denial into hardcore and soft-core categories. Hardcore, the David Irving variety, was rooted in denial of the facts, misquotes, half-information, and fabrication. After our overwhelming legal victory, we saw far less of that and more of the soft-core variant. It consisted, in the main, of minimizing the horror. (Oh, it wasn’t so bad, what are they complaining about?)

Today, as Ferguson and Levin observe, we have reached a new phase: Holocaust celebration together with the reversal of history. The Holocaust was a good thing. Churchill was the villain. Hitler the good guy.

Like Ferguson and Levin, I am profoundly pessimistic. Society at large must comprehend that truth is in the crosshairs. ### Ben Shapiro: We Must Rebuild Our Institutions

Both Ferguson and Levin are correct to lament the state of historical knowledge and understanding, particularly among young Americans. Should we worry about the rise of a generation that not only fails to understand history, but embraces its worst excesses? That turns genocide into either a systematically redefined buzzword or a joke? That increasingly views the world either through the lens of Howard Zinn or [Houston Stewart Chamberlain](https://www.redalyc.org/journal/5117/511782475005/html/)?

Of course we should be worried—not because people increasingly believe lies, but because they increasingly devalue the search for truth itself. Over the past several decades, “my truth” has substituted for *the* truth; facts have been abandoned in pursuit of “narrative.”

We can attribute this to the politicization of truth-protecting institutions like universities and scientific centers; to the radicalization of media or the hedonism of social media; to foreign interference in our informational systems. The reality is that people only search for truth when they believe the truth to be *inherently valuable.* And if information is merely a weapon or an obstacle to a political objective, then the truth has no part to play.

It is not Niall who has failed, then, but the moral system that once produced historians like him and audiences eager to hear from them. Those systems can only be restored through a commitment to decency and virtue—through rejection of the shape-shifting [Foucaultian](https://iep.utm.edu/foucault/) universe in which power and knowledge are merely halves of the same whole.

“Over the past several decades, ‘my truth’ has substituted for *the* truth; facts have been abandoned in pursuit of ‘narrative.’” —Ben Shapiro

So, what can we do?

First, we can reject intellectual imposters and post-truth grifters. It is not censorship to dismiss the ravings of half-mad lunatics spewing sewage into our discourse. Engaging with such grifters is generally counterproductive; it legitimizes them as possible sources of truth. Second, we can work to minimize the influence of foreign actors who are utilizing our post-truth landscape to maximize their own reach. Third, we must rebuild the social institutions that used to orient us toward truth: church and family, universities and scientific institutions.

All of this is the work of a lifetime. The Library of Alexandria [was destroyed](https://www.history.com/articles/library-of-alexandria-destroyed) over the course of centuries. But its informational seed was also preserved across the centuries. Niall’s work will remain important generations after people have forgotten the propagandistic trash of charlatans like Fuentes and Carlson.

### Tyler Cowen: Don’t Blame AI

Ferguson and Levin are premature, to say the least, in identifying social media and AI as the decisive amplifier of the recent resurgence of antisemitism.

The historical record shows that many eras have had virulent antisemitism, and in many different regions of the world. Until recently, none of these places had algorithms or AI—at least, not in the sense that Ferguson and Levin present. After all, not only have large language models (which is what people usually mean when they talk about AI) been widespread only since late 2022, but they are in fact some of the best, most objective, and [least denialist](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.21753) of all sources on the Holocaust. Try them out yourself.

By “AI,” Ferguson and Levin at times just seem to mean more potent algorithms for social media. If so, the term is mostly misleading in the current context. Keep in mind that Russia has been funding troll farms in the Balkans to push disinformation for some time now. Making it a bit cheaper to produce that content, through modern information technology, is probably not what is making the difference.

There is also a reasonably sized literature on social media and polarization. Serious papers have concluded that YouTube [does not](https://dcknox.github.io/files/LiuEtAl_AlgoRecsLimitedPolarizationYouTube.pdf) encourage polarization, and that staying off Facebook [does not seem to](https://siepr.stanford.edu/news/facebook-went-away-political-divides-didnt-budge) alter people’s political opinions (though it does make them less informed). If anything, the current wave of political polarization seems to have started in the 1990s, fed by cable television, not tech. Initial polarization seems to have increased the most among the groups [least likely](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1706588114?utm_source=chatgpt.com) to be using the internet. I do not think any of this research is the final word, and typically these studies do not consider the technologies of the very last few years, due to publication lags. Still, Ferguson and Levin do not consider this evidence in any detail.

They also fail to consider how deeply rooted the problem is. The Western world has been exposed to an ongoing series of negative events this century, including 9/11, the second Iraq War, wage stagnation, the Great Financial Crisis, and Covid-19. People have reacted to these events in nutty ways, a common theme in world history. Tech is the whipping boy for just about everything these days, but let us not whip up the same kind of fervor to which we object in so many other areas of life.

***Tyler Cowen is the Holbert L. Harris chair of economics at George Mason University and serves as chairman and faculty director of the Mercatus Center.***

### Martin Gurri: Historians Have Never Been More Important

I read Ferguson and Levin’s essay, “The Death of History,” with growing perplexity. Ferguson feels that he has failed as a historian—because of Nick Fuentes? The rise of antisemitic content online should not be lightly dismissed, but it has to be understood in context. We’re talking about the internet. Near-infinite volumes of information, much of it robotically and algorithmically determined, some of it evil and malicious in many different ways, whirl like a maelstrom looking for victims to suck down. It’s a fantasy world. As Ferguson observes, Fuentes’s weaselly antisemitism and Holocaust denial are applauded largely by an army of bots. In reality, only [7 percent](https://www.timesofisrael.com/one-in-five-young-americans-believes-the-holocaust-is-a-myth-poll-finds/) of Americans deny the Holocaust—a number that likely correlates with those who believe the moon landing was staged. I’m not sure how writing history books can prevent crackpotism or “rage bait” charlatanism. The way to combat creeps like Fuentes is to expose their real-world insignificance, unless one can prove, with hard evidence, that they represent a significant segment of opinion.

Ferguson seems to believe that the new fashion for antisemitic content is primarily a problem for the right. That’s also puzzling. Leftist influencers like [Hasan Piker](https://freebeacon.com/media/from-defending-rape-to-excusing-9-11-10-statements-that-define-mainstream-media-darling-hasan-piker/) far exceed in vicious rhetoric anything put out by right-wing gasbags like Fuentes or Tucker Carlson. More importantly, real-world antisemitic violence comes largely from the left. It was a leftist who was charged with [murdering](https://www.ajc.org/news/who-is-elias-rodriguez-what-to-know-about-the-antisemitism-that-fueled-the-dc-shooting) two employees of the Israeli embassy in Washington, D.C. It was leftist groups who [terrorized](https://www.algemeiner.com/2026/07/17/rep-adam-smith-says-he-was-threatened-by-anti-israel-activists-ahead-of-vote/) Congressman Adam Smith into voting against his conscience on aid for Israel. It was leftist protesters who [harassed](https://www.yahoo.com/news/jewish-columbia-students-were-chased-194401690.html?guccounter=1&guce_referrer=aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuYmluZy5jb20v&guce_referrer_sig=AQAAAN1aaEbqC3HZfvroTRp8KD6MJCFmFd3iYifJeXPW6Wu4kxYDI1gLuxZptZ8d0f2McVkS3ep6Qjxmq5L--YFLubsoEc0_wUrpFkmvhQzZ351FLelngzksfR66UDuamqFEorPOUOZG6FhEIYFshvIEXEN9SdhYnuf6WV1CHEBmQmzU) Jewish students at Columbia and other schools. Meanwhile, many in the Democratic Party have embraced “anti-Zionism” as their doctrine—a real-world concern.

The job of the historian isn’t to convert the Nick Fuenteses of the world into models of rationality and goodwill. That’s aiming too low. The true historian—and Ferguson is one of our best—is engaged in an asymmetric war against the most powerful force in the universe: oblivion. The struggle is endless, and thankless, and every victory gets erased by the next generation. Yet the atrocities of the Holocaust still horrify because of authors like Ferguson. Entropy spreads a veil of forgetfulness, aided sometimes by fools, sometimes by vile characters. So long as the human story continues, the historian, who embodies memory, must not yield to discouragement.

### Spencer Klavan: In-Person Conversations Will Save Us

The truth is no different now, in the age of AI, than it was before. But the canons of persuasion—the ways people decide what to believe—have changed. Many once-respected press outlets have betrayed the public’s trust. And, as AI-generated fakery becomes harder to distinguish from real human output, our old means of telling fact from fiction have lost much of their power.

One dire consequence, as Ferguson and Levin show, is that ravingly antisemitic fictions have new potency. Our hard-won understanding that the Holocaust was an abomination, never to be repeated, now looks suspect to many. It’s unlikely that sharpened “fact-checking” or firmer guardrails against “hate speech” will make things better, because those are exactly the sorts of policing that many young people have come to distrust.

Instead, I agree with Levin and Ferguson that, “We cannot engineer our way out of a deeply human truth.” As digital fabrications become more slippery, personal testimony becomes more valuable, more powerful—and more costly.

One of my own historic heroes—flawed, as all heroes are—is [Bernard of Clairvaux](https://www.jewishencyclopedia.com/articles/3113-bernard-of-clairvaux), a founder of the Knights Templar. Amid the war fervor of the Second Crusade, when some clerics were using the passions of the moment to incite violent hatred against Jews, Bernard used his own credibility among fellow Christians to insist that pogroms were sinful. He even [rode personally](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B01F57R0LO?lv=shuf&linkCode=ml1&linkId=8d969c330511b14d65eb01eb23fb81cb&tag=spencerklavan-20&channelId=704&ref_=cm_sw_r_as_gl_api_gl_i_NGMYX9HCSYWKJK3VYP38&plpRedirect=mhFallback) into riot zones to intervene. Bernard’s faith taught him that God had not only spoken the truth but also put his body on the line to drive it home.

I still believe in the power of the written word. But increasingly I find that in-person conversation—face to face, with real love and real stakes—does more to break the dark enchantment of algorithmic hypnotism than shouting more words into the howling online tempest. A successful remedy to antisemitism will require not a few credentialed experts, but the personal conviction of many brave, imperfect human hearts. Saint Bernard of Clairvaux, pray for us.

### Jacob Siegel: How to Survive Our Postliterate World

Ferguson and Levin are right to see AI and social media undermining the knowledge of history while helping to popularize Holocaust denial. Indeed, I made a similar argument a few years ago when [I wrote about](https://jsiegel.substack.com/p/the-revisionist) the sources behind the surge in Hitler apologia online. I concluded that it was not simply the work of motivated ideologues like Carlson, but the symptom of a technological revolution profoundly reshaping every facet of Western civilization, down to its moral and political consciousness.

Unlike the deliberative reasoning of the print era that venerated historical texts, I wrote, “the infinite Now of the digital world has no patience for history.” Instead, “it revives a culture of orality that thrived in the Middle Ages” and with it “medieval technologies impervious to print-age liberal reason.” What would those medieval technologies look like today? Like the viral 5- to 9-second videos of pro-Nazi influencers wordlessly mugging at the camera that Ferguson and Levin reference. That kind of politics of tribal iconography, I argued, is “more similar to medieval mobs than to the ‘masses’ of the 20th century.”

“Surviving in a postliterate world will require historians to make a choice.” —Jacob Siegel

At an astonishing speed, digital technologies are destroying, discrediting, and subjecting to pitiless scorn domains of expert knowledge that built and upheld Western civilization over the past 500 years. This is evident in historical debates in which scholarly authority confronts the charismatic power of Hitler memes and bot swarms. Is history dead? No, but a certain modernist conception of history—as a set of lessons that must be learned to fortify humanity’s progress and avoid its past catastrophes—cannot survive in a world where people no longer read the books and newspapers that once inculcated those lessons.

Surviving in a postliterate world will require historians to make a choice. Either they will have to retreat from public life and forsake their tutelary role for a more monastic and elitist devotion to securing the record of history, or they will have to adapt to the war of narratives in the digital global village and enter the arena with the aim to win. I suspect that any society interested in preserving its identity against the digital swarms will have to find historians willing to do both.

***Jacob Siegel is the special features editor at Tablet magazine and author of [The Information State](https://bookshop.org/a/93116/9781250363121).***

### Dara Horn: How Lies Become Perceived as Truth

Ferguson and Levin’s piece is depressing because we all know that it’s true. The good news is that our civilization has been through this before, and solutions are eminently possible.

As I point out in my forthcoming book, *[The Final Solution to the Jewish Question](https://bookshop.org/a/93116/9781668090169)* (and as others have explored), the problem goes far beyond the death of history, extending to malicious operators who are actively pouring antisemitic lies and distortions into the data sources from which AI large language models draw their “facts.” I also point out how a similar process took hold in spreading antisemitic lies in previous generations and centuries, in each case tied to the appearance of new media—whether the internet, television, radio, offset printing, the printing press, or even the transition from scroll to book. New media is always the Wild West. There aren’t yet norms established around who is credible and who isn’t, and the only arbiter of truth is exposure, popularity, and consensus.

---

Our current new media environment is downstream from an education system that spent 30 years investing disproportionately in STEM, devaluing the humanities to such an extent that many elementary schools don’t even teach history as a daily subject. We’ve given up on teaching basic humanities skills, like how rhetoric and evidence work, or how and why stories are built and believed. My new educational nonprofit, The Tell Institute, introduces students to Jewish civilization and the dynamics of antisemitism. One of our most important lessons introduces students to what psychologists call the illusory truth effect: When you hear a lie told often enough, you start to believe it.

That’s something tyrants and malicious influencers have long known. It’s time our children learned it too. It’s not too late to teach them.

***Dara Horn is an American novelist, essayist, and professor of literature, and the author of The Final Solution to the Jewish Question.***
