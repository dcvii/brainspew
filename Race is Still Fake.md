## What We’re Actually Talking About

Let’s start with a problem of language, because it’s where most of this debate goes wrong before it goes anywhere else.

“Race” is not one thing. It’s several things, bundled together into a single term and then wielded as if the bundle were coherent when it’s very much not. When someone says “race is real,” they might be referencing any of the following, and they are rarely careful about disambiguating which…

**Continental ancestry group (CAG):** a category used in genetics and medicine to describe the broad geographic region from which a person’s family line predominantly originates over many generations. Sub-Saharan African, East Asian, Northern European, Indigenous American, and so on. CAGs are real. Geneticists use them. Doctors use them. They have legitimate, bounded applications in population health and ancestry research. They are not for laypersons or political apparatchiks to play with, no more than machine guns or bioweapons are safe for children.

**Ethnicity:** a sociological category describing shared cultural inheritance—language, food, customs, religion, historical memory, national origin. The American Psychological Association defines it as identity based on “shared culture related to common ancestry and shared history.” Social, meaningful, and importantly, *self-reported*. Also quite variable across contexts.

**Complexion:** what you look like. Skin tone, hair texture, eye shape, epicanthic folds, and related surface-level observable traits. Real in the sense that eyes can see them. Morally and psychologically irrelevant in the sense that left-handedness is irrelevant.

Race, as it is actually deployed in public discourse, science journalism, policy debate, and online argument, is none of these things. It is none precisely because it tries to straddle all of them *vaguely*. It shapeshifts between CAG when someone needs biological credibility, ethnicity when someone needs cultural weight, and complexion when someone needs visible markers. It presents itself as a single concrete category while quietly running a shell game with three moves at once.

Those shells are not incidental. It’s the whole trick. The vagueness is load-bearing.

This is why I use “fake” rather than “socially constructed.” The latter phrase has become the preferred terminology in academic and quasi-academic circles. “Social construct” implies something like: real by agreement, functional, worth taking seriously on its own terms—the way money is a social construct, or traffic laws, or national borders. These things are constructed and also genuinely operative in the world. Race is not, and we must not pretend that it is or give it undue credibility.

Race isn’t failing to be real in the way that paper money fails to be gold bullion. **Race is failing to be what it** ***claims to be*****.** It presents itself as a natural kind—a durable, biologically grounded, scientifically coherent category—and it isn’t one. A fake Rolex isn’t a social construct. It’s a thing that misrepresents itself. Race is like that. “Fake” names that misrepresentation. “Social construct” launders it.

I’ve been told this usage of “fake” is bespoke or unintuitive—that I must mean “fuzzy” or “incomplete.” I don’t. I mean affirmatively fictitious. Counterfeit. The word is doing exactly the work I intend it to do, and I’m not going to sand it down to make the concept more comfortable for people who have built identities around race or racialism. Whether those people are race science enthusiasts or communal racialists who avow racial categories as culturally meaningful, the facts stand all the same. We cannot entertain this fake thing we call “race” without addressing its scientific accuracy, or rather lack thereof. I refuse to have, or help maintain, any sacred cows here. Truth exists, and we can come to know it, regardless of who it inconveniences.

## Why Color-Coded Categories Fail

Before we can discuss race as a scientific claim, we need to observe how thoroughly it fails even as a descriptive system. The color vocabulary that dominates racial discourse—white, black, brown, yellow, red—is not just imprecise. It’s running competing scripts into different kinds of failure with each color.

**“White”** is the most obviously broken. It has at various points in American and European history excluded the Irish, Italians, Greeks, Eastern Europeans, Ashkenazi Jews, Armenians, Spaniards, North Africans, and Middle Easterners—all of whom are now variably included, depending on context, setting or era, and who’s doing the including. Scandinavians, Iberians, and Poles have spent centuries in violent conflict over precisely the distinctions “white” erases. It has no coherent genetic, cultural, linguistic, or geographic referent. The goalposts don’t just move; they were never firmly planted.

**“Black”** is culturally coherent within specific national contexts—Black American identity is historically dense and genuine—but as a racial category it attempts to bundle the Tutsi and the Pygmy, the Zulu and the Fulani, the Somali and the San. These are peoples as genetically distant from one another as an Oceanian is from a European. Sub-Saharan Africa contains more human genetic diversity than the rest of the world combined. “Black” as a racial category papers over the most genetically diverse portion of our species with a color swatch.

**“Brown”** is barely even a community's own self-description. It’s a residual category for people who don’t fit cleanly elsewhere—South Asians, Southeast Asians, Latinos of every conceivable admixture, Middle Easterners (when they’re not “white”), North Africans (see above). It is also the politically correct successor of “Red(skin),” a now defunct term for Indigenous Americans. And it is the term for any Asians who don’t pass as “Yellow” (another defunct term) or sufficiently “fair-skinned.” Overall, it is purely a complexion descriptor cosplaying as an identity, just like the others.

**“Asian”** allegedly covers everyone from Turkey to Japan, Siberia to Sri Lanka. It overlaps with “Brown” and even “White” at the margins. It is commonly meant as shorthand for East Asian, a group which used to be called “Yellow,” but has since just drifted into its current state. It’s effectively hollow. No further argument needed.

Each color category fails in the same way race fails overall: too broad to be genetically coherent, too unstable to be historically consistent, too vague to do scientific work. What remains when you strip the color coding away is always something more specific and more honest—a nation, a region, a family line, a language group, a culture. That’s where the actual meaning lives. It always was.

The three-part replacement vocabulary I’m proposing—complexion, ethnicity, CAG—isn’t a rebranding exercise. It’s a demand for clarity. Each term is bounded. Each term is falsifiable. Each term is honest about what it is and what it isn’t. Race, by contrast, needs the fuzziness. Without it, the concept dissolves.

## “Social Construction” and Why the Academics Are Wrong to Be Cautious

There’s a version of the race-skeptical position that stops short of saying “fake” because it worries about overstepping. Academics in particular have a habit of reaching for what we might call less-wrong language—formulations that hedge, qualify, and avoid strong claims in the name of epistemic humility. This is admirable in genuine cases of uncertainty. It is embarrassing and disreputable when it becomes a way of avoiding the level of specificity and confidence necessary to say what is actually meant. We must say what *isn’t* meant as well, regardless of potential offense.

“Race is a social construct” leaves too many doors open. It invites race realists to say: “yes, the category is socially mediated, but the underlying biological variation is what we’re actually interested in.” It invites communal racialists—people who avow race as culturally significant without fully committing to its scientific accuracy—to say: “exactly, and our community’s social construction of race is meaningful and worth preserving.” Both groups walk away satisfied. Neither has been told anything true.

“Race is fake” closes those doors. It says: the category does not track what it claims to track. The map was drawn by people with agendas, the territory doesn’t cooperate, and the map has been used to cause enormous harm on the basis of its false authority. Revising the map isn’t a cultural imposition, nor political appeasement. It’s the opposite, in effect. And it’s done in pursuit of accuracy.

If someone—a black nationalist, a white identitarian, a heritage-minded conservative—has built their sense of self around a racial category, I’m not unsympathetic to the human reality of that. People build lives around all sorts of things. But the question of whether the thing is true is separate from the question of whether it’s meaningful to you. I won’t pretend a category is scientifically legitimate because abandoning it is emotionally costly. I do analysis, not tribal flattery.

## What the Science Actually Says

The scientific literature on race, genetics, and intelligence is genuinely complicated. Not in the way that complexity is invoked to protect bad ideas from scrutiny, but in the way that decades of research across multiple disciplines, with different methodologies and different blind spots, have produced a picture that resists simple summary. I'm going to try anyway, because the complexity has been weaponized for far too long in this area. The race realist reading of this literature is not the only reading available to an honest person. It is not even the most defensible one.

### Heritability: What It Means and What It Doesn’t

Certain traits run in families. This is not in dispute. Twin studies, even with some confounds, consistently show that IQ heritability estimates range from roughly 40% in childhood to 50–80% in adulthood, making intelligence one of the more heritable behavioral traits studied. The data here is solid and has been replicated extensively.

But heritability is a population statistic, not a destiny. What a heritability estimate of 0.70 means is that, within the population studied, approximately 70% of the observed variation in that trait correlates with genetic variation between individuals. It does not mean that 70% of any given person’s IQ is genetically determined. It does not mean the trait cannot change. And it does not mean—this is the move which breaks everything—that differences in trait averages between groups are therefore genetic in origin. “Hereditarians,” so named for their emphasis on heredity as the controlling factor in determining traits, are out over their skis compared to the data.

This last point is not subtle or contested. It is the basic logic of the statistic. High within-group heritability says nothing about the causes of between-group differences. A garden of genetically identical plants, split between a well-watered plot and a drought-stricken one, will show 100% genetic heritability within each plot (all variation is within-plot environmental noise) while displaying large between-plot differences that are 100% environmental in origin. Heritability within doesn’t determine causation between. Environmental factors have not been made irrelevant.

Heritability is also not fixity. IQ scores rose roughly three points per decade through most of the 20th century—approximately 30 points over a century in many developed countries. This is the Flynn effect, and it is among the most important findings in intelligence research. It demonstrates that environmental factors can move IQ scores by magnitudes comparable to the entire CAG-correlated gap—what some observers mistakenly insist on misleadingly calling a “racial IQ gap”—that race realists treat as a stable, heritable feature of racial biology. There was no comparable genetic change in three generations. They are, by the consensus of the researchers who study them, the product of improved nutrition, better education, reduced exposure to toxins, smaller family sizes, and more cognitively demanding environments.

James Flynn himself noted that the Flynn effect shows environmental factors can create IQ differences of the same magnitude as the gaps in question. That observation hasn’t been overturned. The race realists’ response—that Flynn effect gains don’t reflect “true g” and therefore don’t count—is a post-hoc carve-out that applies only when the evidence is inconvenient.

A further complication: research suggests IQ heritability varies dramatically by socioeconomic status (SES)—from roughly 5% in low-SES families to roughly 50% in high-SES families. Our SES class is the epitome of an environmental factor. If genetic expression of IQ is itself modulated by environment to this degree, observed group differences—which strongly correlate with group differences in SES, nutrition, environmental toxin exposure, school quality, and chronic stress—cannot be cleanly attributed to genetics. The causal arrow is not visible.

### The Polygenic Score Problem

Modern genomics has attempted to locate the specific genetic variants underlying intelligence and build polygenic scores (PGS) that can predict cognitive outcomes. The results are instructive and largely fatal to the hereditarian position.

As of 2024, the best polygenic scores for educational attainment and intelligence predict approximately 10–15% of IQ variance—comparable in predictive power to simply knowing someone’s family income. Height PGS, by comparison, achieves roughly 45% predictive accuracy. This is PhD [Sasha Gusev](https://open.substack.com/users/247615449-sasha-gusev?utm_source=mentions) ’s key comparison, and it matters: *height* is the kind of trait that should look like what race realists imagine IQ to look like. It doesn’t. Not even close.

Gusev, a statistical geneticist at Harvard Medical School who works directly with the genetic data the race realist community cites, has documented how a decade of behavioral GWAS findings were confounded by population stratification—the tendency of genetic studies to pick up environmental and social signals that run in families and communities alongside genetic ones. He describes genomic IQ prediction, in its current state, as “modern snake oil.” He caught Charles Murray publicly boosting fabricated data. He is not a progressive activist. He is one of the few people on Earth *actually qualified* to evaluate these claims, and he says the mechanism isn’t there. His peers and critics have checked his work. It stands.

Eric Turkheimer, one of the foremost behavioral geneticists in the country, and the scientist whose bet Murray lost, stated explicitly in 2025 that “for human behavioral phenotypes we have essentially zero knowledge of how genetic differences produce behavioral differences.” Murray had bet that by 2025 we would have IQ group differences “pretty much figured out.” We don’t. The mechanism gap is unclosed.

### Scientific Consensus

The consensus position on racial IQ gaps is not, as some hereditarian-adjacent commentators like to claim, a matter of politically suppressed disagreement among experts. Nature’s editorial board stated in 2017 that the gap between average IQ scores of Americans from different CAGs “has been falsely attributed to genetic differences between the races.” Both sides of a 2009 Nature debate on the topic agreed that “there is an emerging consensus about racial and gender equality in genetic determinants of intelligence; most researchers agree that genes do not explain between-group differences.”

Race realists frequently cite the Rindermann, Becker & Coyle (2020) survey of intelligence experts, which found roughly 49% of respondents attributed the so-called “Black-White IQ gap” partially to genetic factors. A few things about this survey: it drew only 71 respondents for its core findings. Its respondent pool skewed significantly in one direction politically. This makes the “experts believe this” framing a politically sorted sample *dressed* as scientific consensus. Genes also had the highest variability in ratings of any factor surveyed, meaning this is precisely where expert opinion is least settled, not most. Education, by contrast, was rated the single most important cause of international cognitive differences. What this proves is that “most experts agree” is a misrepresentation of a contested, methodologically limited survey that says, at most, that some researchers hold this view—and that those researchers are disproportionately politically aligned to one side.

## 7 Questions Race Realists Must Answer

I’ve developed the following questions through extended argument with people who hold race realist positions. They’re the minimum standard of specificity that any genuine empirical claim requires. Blood type—a real, discrete biological classifier with actual medical utility—meets this standard routinely. If race is the scientific category its proponents claim it is, answering these questions should be straightforward.

**1\. How many races are there?** Three? Five? Nine? Sixty-seven? The number has ranged across the history of race science, varying by era, researcher, and political context, with no convergence toward a stable answer. A biological taxonomy that can’t specify the number of categories it contains isn’t a taxonomy but a handwave.

**2\. How stable are the racial categories and their boundaries?** What is a “White” person? Would a Jew or an Italian count as one? What about a “Brown” person? Are Native Americans and Arabs—both “brown” by most colloquial norms—the same thing? American racial categories would not recognize many of the cultural and social associations that each label currently carries. If race is a biological reality, why do its categories shift with social conditions rather than genetic data?

**3\. What non-complexion characteristics vary meaningfully across races?** Not skin tone. Not hair texture. Those are complexion—observable surface features. What are the underlying cognitive, behavioral, or physiological traits that vary at the racial level and not at the national, regional, familial, or individual level? If race is doing explanatory work, it needs to do work that these other categories can’t do better.

**4\. What role does environment play?** Not as a factor to dismiss, but as a factor to quantify. What percentage of observed differences between racial groups do you attribute to environmental causes—SES, education, nutrition, toxin exposure, historical trauma, access to healthcare? Give us a real number. This is supposedly science.

**5\. How amenable are these characteristics to environmental change?** The etiology of a trait (what causes it) is a separate question from its malleability (whether it can change). A race realist who answers question four by granting 30% environmental causation needs to then say something about what that implies for intervention. If large IQ gains are achievable through improved nutrition and schooling—and they demonstrably are—what does that do to the policy implications of the genetic component? Why do race realists resist environmental policies?

**6\. What is the magnitude of genetic contribution to between-group differences?** Not “genes play some role”—that claim is trivially true and *unfalsifiable as stated*. Commit to a number. Five percent? Twenty-five? Fifty? What proportion of the observed gap between, say, American IQ scores vary by CAG? Do they attribute it to genetic differences? The failure to commit to a magnitude is the single most reliable indicator that someone is not making an empirical claim. They’re gesturing at air.

**7\. What is the causal mechanism?** What evolutionary process are they proposing? What selection pressure, operating over what time period, produced what cognitive difference between which populations? What are the specific genetic variants involved, what are their effect sizes, and how do they interact? This is question seven because most interlocutors never reach it. And the ones who do? They rarely have an answer beyond “populations diverged, therefore differences.” That’s just a placeholder.

These questions form a progression: categories → traits → environment → magnitude → mechanism. Each builds on the previous and gets more specific about what, exactly, the race realist is claiming. Serious population geneticists can engage most of these with data and appropriate caveats. HBD hobbyists fail at question one and retreat to philosophical sounding slop by question three. I have public evidence on my timeline.

## Blood Types & the Specificity Objection

A common complaint about the seven questions above is that demanding this level of specificity is unfair—that science is messy, categories are fuzzy, and requiring falsifiability is a form of motivated skepticism. But the special pleading is exactly backwards. Plenty of categories withstand the rigor required.

Consider blood type. Blood type is a real, discrete biological category with genuine medical utility. It is heritable. It varies across populations. It actually does what race is supposed to do: it maps onto biology cleanly, it has clinical applications, and it can be determined by a simple assay. Nobody argues that requiring specificity from blood type claims is unreasonable.

Now consider: Japan and South Korea have, for decades, treated blood type as a personality and relationship compatibility system, along the lines of astrology or jyotisha (fortune-telling or cosmic fate). They do daily horoscopes, matchmaking services, and character assessments. The scientific community has consistently found no significant association between blood type and personality. Blood type personality theory is, to the extent it makes empirical claims, objectively pseudoscience. Remind you of anything?

The race realist’s core move—this biological category exists, therefore social policy built on top of it is rational—is the exact move two East Asian countries made with blood type. Yet even as blood type is more biologically real than race, the policies pegged to blood type personality theory are derided—rightly—as pseudoscience. Blood typing has more clearly bounded categories, it has demonstrably better predictive accuracy for the things it claims to predict. And yet the policies and cultural trends built on top of it are still just astrology. We must not follow suit.

The specificity demand isn’t unfair. It’s the minimum. Blood type meets it for what blood type actually does, which is useful for medical professionals. Race doesn’t meet it for what race claims to do, which is unbounded by philosophical design.

### Botanical Berries and the Capriciousness Objection

A second analogous objection is that racial categories, whatever their fuzziness, track something real enough to be useful, and that demanding clean taxonomic boundaries is itself a kind of bad faith. Botany has a useful corrective. In culinary usage, a berry is a small, round, sweet fruit: strawberries, raspberries, blackberries. In botanical taxonomy, a berry is a fleshy fruit developed from a single flower with one ovary: bananas are berries, avocados are berries, pumpkins are berries. Strawberries, raspberries, and blackberries are not.

The botanical definition produces results so contrary to folk intuition that it exposes the folk category as entirely assembled by appearance—we called things berries because they looked small and grew on bushes, not because we were tracking any underlying structure. Not unlike matching someone’s complexion to a color term.

Here’s the crucial move: nobody concluded from the taxonomic revision in botany that strawberries were inferior fruit, or that avocados were now the “master” berry. The reclassification produced no hierarchy. It just produced accuracy.

Racial taxonomy’s hierarchy was never coming from the biology. It was imported from outside—from ingroup chauvinism, from economic interest, from colonial administration—and *retrofitted* onto biology after the fact. When the biology is examined carefully, it draws the lines somewhere completely different.

The “ [White](https://www.pbs.org/independentlens/documentaries/the-history-of-white-people-in-america/) ”/”Real-White”/” [Fellow White](https://forward.com/fast-forward/387372/what-does-fellow-white-people-mean-decoding-the-new-anti-semitic-alt-right/) ” version of this argument is particularly useful: if “white” is a meaningful biological category, then by the same logic, the Italians, and Ashkenazi Jews—each historically excluded from whiteness at various points—should *still* be excluded. Those goalposts moved for social reasons, not genetic ones. Exactly like “berry” meaning different things in a kitchen and a lab.

## Who’s Selling This and Why

Race realism as an intellectual movement has a coherent set of spokesmen, ranging from the seriously credentialed to the frankly embarrassing. I’ve read nearly all the majors. I’ve tussled with some of the names personally. I was, as I sometimes sheepishly admit, a sympathetic audience member once. I got over it.

On the serious end: **Arthur Jensen**, whose work on the heritability of intelligence in the 1960s and 70s is the academic foundation for everything that followed. **Charles Murray**, a political scientist—not a geneticist—whose books *The Bell Curve* and *Facing Reality* argue that cognitive differences between racial groups are real and ==must inform policy==. Murray is the movement’s elder statesman: more careful than most of his allies, more credentialed-seeming, and worth engaging seriously—which is why it matters that the science he relies on has since been substantially undermined by the mechanism gap, the polygenic score data, and the lost bet with Turkheimer.

**Linda Gottfredson,** and Jensen’s broader cohort of psychometricians, form the academic backbone. **Nathan Cofnas** is the most philosophically sophisticated current defender, and his writing makes something important explicit: hereditarianism, for Cofnas, is a political project. He frames it as necessary to defeat progressive politics—if races differ innately, disparities don’t require systemic racism as an explanation, and the entire DEI-adjacent policy apparatus collapses. That framing is useful to us because it admits the conclusion *precedes* the evidence.

Further down: **Steve Sailer**, who coined the “human biodiversity” (HBD) euphemism and whose degree is in marketing. **Jared Taylor**, editor of *American Renaissance*, who at least has the integrity to state plainly what most in the movement merely imply, including a preference for per se white nationalism. **Emil Kirkegaard**, a propagandist and data launderer. And **[Jordan Lasker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordan_Lasker)** aka **Cremieux**, whose Substack presents HBD in a data-forward enough manner to attract the Bayesian Rationalist and Gray Tribe crowd, and who has a documented promotional relationship with Charles Murray on X (formerly known as Twitter), and whose data slides Gusev has directly criticized as misleading.

The black nationalist tradition has its own lineage of racial essentialism that deserves the same scrutiny. **Marcus Garvey**, the movement’s foundational figure, held views on race that were structurally identical to those of his white nationalist contemporaries—a fixed, heritable, civilizationally determinative Blackness that demanded its own separatist political program. The through-line runs to **Amos Wilson**, the psychologist whose work remains heavily cited in contemporary Black nationalist spaces, and who argued that Black people possess distinct cognitive and psychological traits rooted in African ancestry—the HBD mirror image, built for a different audience but resting on the same foundation.

**Ta-Nehisi Coates** ’ reparations framework has a version of the same problem: organizing redistribution along racial lines requires treating race as a stable, inheritable, cross-generational biological category to determine eligibility, a tension the critics like Adolph Reed Jr. identified and Coates never resolved. Then there is **Umar Johnson**, the most explicit contemporary black nationalist with a biological-essentialist framework, who advocates racial separatism on grounds that would be immediately recognizable to Jared Taylor or Nathan Cofnas. The point is not moral equivalence—these movements have categorically different histories and different relationships to institutional power. The point is epistemological: the category is fake in both directions, and building a politics on a fictitious foundation doesn’t become more defensible because the people doing it have been genuinely wronged.

What drives the rank and file of race realism isn’t primarily the science. There are two pipelines. The first is political utility: hereditarianism is attractive to people who want to discredit the premise that disparities require structural explanations. If the gap is genetic, a corrective policy response is off the table—this is Cofnas’s pipeline. The second is arcane knowledge as social signaling: the performance of reluctant truth-teller, the person brave enough to acknowledge uncomfortable facts that polite society suppresses. This pipeline is recognizable by a specific rhetorical tic—what I’d call “faux-sadness,” a performed reluctance to believe what the evidence supposedly compels them to believe. Real scientists don’t perform reluctance about their findings. They argue methodology. The performance is the tell.

Both pipelines produce people who are largely unreachable through evidence, because the evidence isn’t what got them there. I do not debate them anymore.

## Where Meaning Actually Lives

I want to say something to the people for whom this argument is not abstract—people who have built genuine community and identity around racial categories, and who experience the “race is fake” claim as a threat to something real. The *impulse* behind racial identity is understandable: belonging, heritage, continuity, pride. Those are human and worth taking seriously. The **vessel** is the problem, not the drive. And the replacement vessels are already there. They really exist. They already carry the meaning people are reaching for, and they don’t require pseudoscientific scaffolding.

A Black American whose family has been in Mississippi for six generations has a genuine and rich heritage—but it isn’t “African” in any meaningful genetic or cultural sense. It’s Southern. It’s American. It’s a specific geography, a set of churches, a cuisine, a pattern of surnames, a musical tradition, a history of survival under specific conditions. That heritage is real and dense and worth claiming. “Black” flattens it into a continental abstraction that serves neither accuracy nor dignity.

“White” people discovering their actual ethnicities—Italian, Polish, Appalachian, Scots-Irish, Ashkenazi Jewish—find something more specific and more interesting than whiteness ever offered them. Jewishness is a particular case that demonstrates the point well: it’s an ethnoreligious identity with genuine historical depth, multiple distinct CAG components, a documented diaspora history, and a cultural inheritance that “white” would erase entirely.

The future of meaningful group identity—the one that can actually survive contact with honest biology and honest history—runs through national origin, regional culture, family lineage, and language community. Not through color. Not through the drunk cartographers who drew these lines in service of empires that no longer exist.

I’m not prescribing what anyone’s identity *should* be. I’m observing that the replacement for race is already there, already working, and already more honest than the thing it replaces. You need only seek it out.

## Seven Questions, Revisited: The Verdict

Race realism isn’t failing because it’s edgy. It isn’t being suppressed by cowardly academics or canceled by progressive institutions. It’s failing because the actual science has lapped it. These aren’t open questions awaiting better data or more courage. The data came back, and it said “ **no.**”

Murray lost his bet with Turkheimer. The mechanism gap is clear. Turkheimer’s 2025 statement that we have “essentially zero knowledge of how genetic differences produce behavioral differences” in complex behavioral phenotypes wasn’t a provocation. It was a status report. Polygenic scores for IQ explain 10–15% of variance, roughly what family income explains, and the arguably best geneticist working in this space calls their application to race “modern snake oil.”

The Flynn effect moved scores by 30 points in a century without genetic change. The Bekoji problem—a single Ethiopian town of 16,000 people producing 10 Olympic gold medals while neighboring towns with identical ethnic backgrounds and genetic profiles produce none—demonstrates that what looks like group-level biological destiny is actually a running ethos, coaching infrastructure, economic incentives. You can’t ascribe to continental genetics what maps more accurately onto national culture.

The political diehards need to join the mainstream already. Not because the mainstream is always right, but because in this case, the mainstream is where the data has been, and for longer than the pseudoscience concept we call “race” can withstand. The rest of us can stop pretending a map drawn by drunk, bigoted cartographers in the service of essentialist empires is a reliable guide to human nature.
