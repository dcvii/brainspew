The Depository Trust & Clearing Corp., a leading trade processor, plans to convert a batch of shares and Treasurys into digital tokens, the clearinghouse’s latest step toward a fully digital Wall Street.

Almost 40 financial firms and tech providers, from [JPMorgan Chase](https://www.wsj.com/market-data/quotes/JPM) and [Goldman Sachs](https://www.wsj.com/market-data/quotes/GS) to [BlackRock](https://www.wsj.com/market-data/quotes/BLK), Vanguard and the New York Stock Exchange, will take part in a trial run to tokenize securities that Wall Street firms keep at DTCC. The tokens will be stored at the clearinghouse on the blockchain, the digital ledger technology that underpins cryptocurrencies and other digital assets, and made available for trade, DTCC said.

The assets set to tokenize in Wednesday’s process include shares of [Microsoft](https://www.wsj.com/market-data/quotes/MSFT), [Circle Internet Group](https://www.wsj.com/market-data/quotes/CRCL), Invesco QQQ Trust, State Street SPDR S&P 500 ETF Trust and iShares 0-3 Month Treasury Bond ETF, as well as Treasurys of different tenors.

DTCC plans to launch its tokenization program formally in October, when firms that keep assets at the clearinghouse will be able to convert some of their securities into tokens. On Wednesday, participating firms will test the process by instructing DTCC to settle specific transactions on the blockchain. For example, JPMorgan will tokenize a portion of its Invesco QQQ Trust holdings held at DTCC, while retaining the ability to convert them back to traditional shares.

The path to a tokenized Wall Street has cleared in recent months, with many firms now racing to launch their own platforms, funds and deposit networks that move or store tokens.

There are two ways companies are typically putting stocks on the blockchain.

Some use a wrapper—a structure that mimics a stock’s performance. Anyone can trade the tokens, but they don’t give holders the same legal ownership or voting rights granted to shareholders.

A second approach, used by the DTCC, enables tokenized stocks to be interchangeable with real shares and carry the same legal protections and dividends. The tokens can usually only move between an approved number of financial institutions.

“The tokenization of assets and digital blockchain usage are a megatrend,” said Frank La Salla, president and chief executive of DTCC, in an interview. “What we really focus on is safety of the system, resiliency of the system, and working on ways in which we can free up trapped liquidity by using this new technology.”

The DTCC-held tokens are essentially the digital twins of existing shares and can be converted back into traditional shares. They confer the same economic and legal ownership, dividend and governance rights as their underlying shares.

The firms will use the tokenized assets for a broad set of transactions on Wednesday, including collateral transfers, repo transactions and equity trades. The live trades will settle on either HyperLedger Besu, DTCC’s private blockchain, or Canton Network, a Wall Street-backed, privacy-focused blockchain, depending on which one the participating firms choose.

Late last year the Securities and Exchange Commission gave Depository Trust Co., a DTCC subsidiary, the green light to operate a tokenization service for the real-world assets it safeguards, but limited the scope to highly liquid assets.

DTCC safeguards more than $114 trillion in securities, and its subsidiaries processed securities transactions valued at $4.7 quadrillion last year.
