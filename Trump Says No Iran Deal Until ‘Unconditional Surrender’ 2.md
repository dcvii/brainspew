President Trump said the U.S. would hold out for what he called an unconditional surrender and “acceptable” leadership before making a deal with Iran. In a post on Truth Social, he said that the U.S. and allies would “work tirelessly” to strengthen Iran’s economy.

The Israeli military launched a wave of attacks on regime infrastructure in Tehran and pounded Beirut’s southern suburbs with airstrikes that it said were targeting Hezbollah infrastructure.

The Israeli military also said it had dismantled Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei’s military bunker with roughly 50 jet fighters.

Several nations in the Gulf, including Saudi Arabia and Bahrain, said they were targeted by Iranian strikes.

U.S. military investigators think American forces were likely responsible for an airstrike on [a girls’ elementary school](https://www.wsj.com/world/middle-east/iran-us-school-strike-07d8ffac) that killed dozens of children in Iran, according to a U.S. official.

Russia is sharing location information on U.S. military forces in the Middle East with Iran, U.S. officials say.

The U.S. said it is allowing Indian refiners to purchase Russian oil for 30 days while Kuwait is [cutting production](https://www.wsj.com/business/energy-oil/kuwait-cuts-oil-production-as-storage-fills-up-3766b2eb) at some oil fields over a storage crisis. Brent crude futures, the benchmark for global oil prices, rose above $90 a barrel.

The Trump administration said Friday it had worked out a plan to insure ships in the Strait of Hormuz against war risk for up to $20 billion.

He said that the U.S. has significantly degraded the Iranian military and noted that many of the regime’s top leaders have been killed. “Their leaders are gone,” Trump said. “Two sets of their leaders are gone. They're down to their third set.”

“That's the big thing for this week,” Trump said of Iran. “We seem to have a new thing every week.”

In the first seven days of the conflict in Iran, the U.S. military has struck more than 3,000 targets and destroyed 43 Iranian ships, according to government numbers released Friday. At least 1,000 of those strikes have come since Tuesday.

The U.S. and Israel have targeted Iranian command centers, the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps headquarters, air defense systems, missile sites, navy ships and submarines, said U.S. Central Command. “Iranian targets are being decimated by U.S. forces, paving the way for continued delivery of overwhelming American military firepower,” Centcom said in a social media post early Friday morning.

U.S. and Israeli forces launched an extensive military operation against Iran last weekend, carrying out strikes from the air and sea that triggered retaliation from Tehran and threaten wider conflict across the Middle East.

Iran fired waves of missiles and drones aimed at Israel, U.S. military bases, and other sites in the region. Iranian Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei was killed in the opening strikes on Tehran.

The campaign in Iran promises a heavy use of force that has rattled Gulf allies concerned about a wider regional war.

The White House on Thursday evening posted on social media a 42-second video montage of video game, movie and TV show clips edited together with real, unclassified footage from strikes in the ongoing conflict in Iran titled “JUSTICE THE AMERICAN WAY.”

Also included was a clip from the 2008 satirical action film Tropic Thunder, written, directed by and starring Ben Stiller. Stiller on Friday pushed back against its inclusion, asking the White House in a social media post to remove the clip. “We never gave you permission and have no interest in being a part of your propaganda machine. War is not a movie,” Stiller wrote.

Trump administration social media accounts have been full of similar posts in recent days, splicing popular-culture content into military images. On Friday, another video featured clips of baseball players swinging bats and making contact before jumping to footage of explosions.

Among the TV shows and movies clipped in Thursday's video were familiar—though not all American—characters from franchises including Iron Man, Braveheart, Top Gun, Better Call Saul, John Wick, Superman, Breaking Bad, Daredevil and Star Wars.

The Strait of Hormuz earlier this week. Amr Alfiky/Reuters

Iran’s Revolutionary Guards said Friday that the Strait of Hormuz will remain open, but warned that any U.S. or Israeli ships transiting the strategic waterway would be targeted.

“We did not close the Strait of Hormuz and will not, but we will target ships belonging to the U.S. regime and the Zionist entity transiting the Strait of Hormuz,” a spokesman said.

“We recommend that, before any decision, the Americans remember the burning of the American supertanker Bridgeton in 1987 and the oil tankers that have been targeted recently,” he added.

President Trump said Tuesday that the U.S. Navy could begin escorting tankers through the Strait of Hormuz if necessary, but experts say the U.S. currently lacks enough naval vessels nearby to keep oil flowing through the vital waterway.

Iran has sent mixed messages about its intentions to close the strait, initially saying it wouldn't when war broke out and then later saying it would burn any ship that tried to cross. In any case, ship traffic through the strait has come to almost a complete halt.

President Trump said America’s largest weapons makers have agreed to raise production of military munitions following “a very good meeting” at the White House on Friday.

“They have agreed to quadruple Production of the ‘Exquisite Class’ Weaponry in that we want to reach, as rapidly as possible, the highest levels of quantity,” Trump wrote in a [social media post](https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/116184185735585906).

Trump said that manufacturing expansion "began three months prior to the meeting, and Plants and Production of many of these Weapons are already under way.” Earlier this year, the Pentagon said it had [struck deals with Lockheed Martin and RTX](https://www.wsj.com/politics/national-security/rtx-to-dramatically-increase-missile-production-under-pentagon-deals-0b9a8666) to raise production of some of the most in-demand weapons, including Patriot, Thaad and Standard Missiles.

The president said production would also increase of “Medium and Upper Medium Grade Munitions,” similar to the types of munitions used in Iran and during operations in Venezuela. Trump said the U.S. has “a virtually unlimited supply” of these types of munitions.

Executives from BAE Systems, Boeing, Honeywell Aerospace, L3Harris Technologies, Lockheed Martin, Northrop Grumman and RTX attended Friday’s meeting, Trump said. He added that executives would meet with him again in two months.

The Trump administration said Friday it had worked out a plan to insure ships in the Strait of Hormuz against war risk for up to $20 billion. “We are confident that our reinsurance plan will get oil, gasoline, LNG, jet fuel, and fertilizer through the Strait of Hormuz and flowing again to the world,” said Ben Black, chief executive of the U.S. International Development Finance Corporation.

Thousands of ships are currently stuck at either end of the strait. In peacetime, about a fifth of the world's oil supply passes through the waterway daily. Storage tanks are filling up with oil that can’t be shipped, forcing producers to slash output. Oil prices have soared since the start of the war. Futures for Brent crude, the global oil benchmark, rose 27% for the week.

IDFC’s announcement came three days after President Trump said the federal government would provide “political risk insurance” and financial security guarantees for energy trade in the Gulf region. The announcement didn’t address Trump’s offer for the U.S. Navy to escort commercial ships through the strait if necessary. Many ships are able to get insurance but have declined to traverse the strait for fear of coming under attack. Iran has attacked ships in the strategic waterway since the start of the conflict.

The steepest oil-price runup since the early days of the Ukraine war sent benchmark oil prices above $90 a barrel on Friday, a signal that traders are increasingly fearful that the spiraling Middle East conflict could spark an energy shock.

Futures for Brent crude, the global oil benchmark, jumped more 8.5% Friday to 92.69 a barrel, its highest level since 2023. The one-day surge was the sharpest since March 2022. For the week, the price rose 27%, a record.

WTI crude, the U.S. benchmark, surged by 12%, its biggest daily jump since 2020. U.S. crude finished the day at $90.90 a barrel, up a record 36% for the week, according to Dow Jones Market Data.

A succession of shut-ins by major exporters across the Persian Gulf has raised the prospect of an extended disruption of global supplies. Kuwait has [begun cutting production](https://www.wsj.com/business/energy-oil/kuwait-cuts-oil-production-as-storage-fills-up-3766b2eb) at some oil fields after running out of room to store its bottled-up crude, The Wall Street Journal reported Friday.

Energy Secretary Chris Wright [sought to calm anxiety](https://www.wsj.com/livecoverage/iran-war-news-updates-2026/card/u-s-energy-secretary-tries-to-calm-anxiety-over-war-driven-rise-in-gas-prices-gWr7MniKT3j2T8odUAgD) about gas prices on Thursday and predicted the war could end in the next few weeks. But retail gasoline and diesel prices are already catapulting higher across the U.S., further pressuring an American economy that is already showing signs of a slowdown.

A surge in jet-fuel costs is threatening major disruptions for global airlines as the conflict in the Middle East continues to drive up crude-oil prices.

Oil prices are up about 50% so far this year and jet-fuel prices have more than doubled, Deutsche Bank analysts said in a note Friday. The spread between jet-fuel prices and underlying oil prices are exceeding the total per-barrel price of oil in regions like the Gulf Coast and West Coast, they said.

The last time this happened was in 2005 in the wake of Hurricanes Katrina and Rita, which caused significant and widespread damage to the airline industry and played a factor in Delta Air Lines and Northwest Airlines filing for Chapter 11 bankruptcy, the analysts said.

“Absent near-term relief, airlines around the world could be forced to ground \[thousands\] of aircraft while some of the industry’s financially weakest carriers could halt operations,” the analysts said.
