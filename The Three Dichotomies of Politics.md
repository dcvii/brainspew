I’m gonna mention a famous political binary upfront and then ignore it for the remainder of this piece: The Left and the Right split is archaic, if not outright dead. Worse than useless, it is zombified and ersatz. This binary, and variations on, will still be invoked constantly, sure. By politicians, by pundits, and by algorithmically optimized social content. But the binary no longer reliably predicts what someone believes, how they’ll vote, or what they actually want from government. The same is true of any political party duo you can think of in the States or abroad.

Libertarian vs. authoritarian has the same problem. So does capitalist vs. socialist. None of these frameworks were *always* wrong, I’ll confess. Indeed, they were once useful shorthand for specific historical conflicts. Combined, four points together could even form an amusing compass of political positions, one you’ve likely seen before and found value in. But shorthand has a shelf life, and these have expired. Frankly, some time ago. At this point, they’re all on borrowed time. And I’m calling time already. Enough.

What’s needed isn’t a new axis bolted onto the old ones. It’s a fuller set of foundational questions—genuinely distinct from each other, genuinely comprehensive of the major divides, and genuinely honest about what each answer commits you to. Not flattering to any tribe. Not rigged toward a preferred conclusion.

This piece proposes three such dichotomies. The terms have long histories; the framework arranging them mostly doesn’t. The goal is diagnostic as much as descriptive—by the end, you should be able to locate yourself clearly, without the comfortable ambiguity that bad frameworks tend to permit.

## Fixing Broken Semantics and Schema

The problem isn’t just semantic drift, tho that’s real and worth naming upfront. It’s structural. One binary came from the seating arrangements of a European National Assembly long before cars were invented. In relation to the divine rights of kings, not the politics of an informed and enfranchised citizenry. It was a snapshot of one specific quarrel, generalized well past its original scope.

The economics followed in due time, mostly replacing monarchy as the core conflict. Capitalist vs. socialist addresses one vital question about resource allocation while leaving several others either begged or ignored entirely. Then libertarian vs. authoritarian was added as a genuine corrective, a second dimension to a one-dimensional model. But these all still compress too much. We faff about describing styles of governing or preferences about the ownership and distribution of property without saying anything about the substance underneath. About **what** we’re governing *toward*, about *why* we’re allocating resources in the first place.

A few semantic caveats first, and I’ll ask for a little patience with them. Some of these terms carry vernacular baggage. “Collectivism” sounds like an insult to ears trained on Cold War rhetoric. “Particularism” and “sacralism” will be unfamiliar outright. I’m using all of them in precise, theoretically grounded senses—not as compliments, not as slurs. Where a term’s common usage diverges meaningfully from what’s intended here, I’ll flag it as we go. The framework only works if the words do.

## Three Dichotomies, Three Questions

Three foundational divides make up the framework. Each is a genuine dichotomy, meaning there’s no coherent position straddling both poles. You can weight your commitments, qualify your views, hold tensions within your own camp. What you can’t do is occupy both sides at once. The [law of excluded middle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_excluded_middle) applies, and it’s not rigidity for its own sake—a map with no fixed points navigates *nothing*.

A frequent objection holds that adding enough dimensions of variation dissolves the need to choose a side at all: that hierarchy and equality alike are crude flattenings of a richer, multidimensional reality, and that a sufficiently sophisticated person simply lives in the space between. This sounds clever and isn’t. Multidimensionality describes how complicated the inputs are, not how decisions get made. When scarce goods or institutional power require an actual ranking among competing claims, additional dimensions don’t dissolve the need to rank, they just obscure who’s doing the ranking and on what basis. The objection isn’t a third pole. It’s a more elaborate route to one of the two existing ones.

One clarification before moving on. The terms “liberal” and “conservative,” often confusingly taken as a binary, only get name-checked in their most broad and essential forms. That’s deliberate, and probably the most subversive choice in the whole framework. Liberal and conservative aren’t really opposites in this schema. They’re two labels that have absorbed conflicting means, and have been at times (wrongly) treated opposites, more by historical accident and partisan convenience than philosophical fact. Try to keep them in context as loose signifiers.

### Individualism vs. Collectivism

One’s orientation to power and its effects. The question of whether the individual or the collective is the basic unit political and economic life ought to be organized around, and what each is owed or owes in turn.

Individualism means a commitment to personal autonomy, comprehensive rights, and real limits on what any collective body—state, party, or movement—can do to a person without consent. It is a correlate of liberalism, capitalism, and human rights. It is not, we must be clear, any single one of those things. Nor does supporting individualism mean someone is necessarily a liberal, a capitalist, or a humanist.

Collectivism means a commitment to the primacy of the group: the belief that material conditions, social outcomes, or national strength are properly pursued through collective power, even when that requires overriding individual claims to autonomy, property, or dissent. This correlates with tribalism, Marxism (just descriptively speaking), many versions of nationalism, and other group-focused ideologies or identities. Again, one needn’t be any of those things to be a collectivist or lean collectivist. These are associations, not obligations.

This axis subsumes both the libertarian/authoritarian divide and the capitalist/socialist debate, because it asks the prior question both of those frameworks quietly assume an answer to: who has standing to decide, and what stands in their way. A person can be economically heterodox and still be a committed individualist. They cannot be an individualist and a collectivist at once, any more than a circle can have corners.

Anyone who understands how most Western democracies are structured already knows that aspects of capitalism and (soft) socialism run through them. We are living in mixed economies, neither laissez-faire nor Marxist. When thinking in terms of econ then, individualist vs collectivist gets to a much more edifying issue: assuming we could have both open markets and state oversight, both private property and redistribution of resources, which principle should govern these combos? Cuz we sure as shine can’t pretend the answer lies in *banning* capitalism or in preventing all manner of government involvement in economics.

Likewise with authority and liberty. Freedom isn’t free, and plenty of folks who claim to want order actually want something closer to sovereignty or fiat. If we locate the real question at the level of “whose interests break the tie” rather than simply “which pejorative shall we let ourselves be aligned with,” we find ourselves answering more questions, and answering them far better.

### Universalism vs. Particularism

One’s orientation to belonging and community. The question of whether moral and political obligations apply equally to all persons, or are legitimately shaped and bounded by particular attachments: nation, culture, kinship, tradition. Universalism holds that a stranger on the far side of the world is owed the same basic consideration as a neighbor. Particularism holds that proximity, shared history, and common identity create real and legitimate differences in obligation, and that a polity is entitled, even obligated, to prioritize its own.

Both poles have a failure state worth naming honestly. You might be able to guess some already. Still, neither failure state invalidates the pole itself.

Particularism’s is insularity curdling into bigotry: a boundary policed not because it serves a real function but because the people on the other side of it have stopped counting as people. Nationalism again rears up here, but usually in the forms of ultra-nationalism or an arch, ethnically-essentialist national ethos. Tribalism too, be it of a political group, a social cohort, or a religious community.

Universalism’s problems lie in things like a leveling obsession. And in a value nihilism that can’t account for any legitimate winners or hierarchy at all. Equity over equality. Relativism over realism. A worldview so committed to (forced) equality, especialy in outcomes rather than opportunities, that it has no room left for structures or systems that don’t predetermine or pre-sort things for us. No space for respecting familial bonds, neighborhood character, or national pride and patriotism. Universalism’s fail-states end up hollow rather than humane.

The best reason to be a universalist is for formal equality ([egalitarianism](https://edokwin.substack.com/p/the-excellence-of-egalitarianism), correctly understood) and universal rights. An ethical/moral consistency, one without special pleading carveouts, hypocrisy, or loopholes. Even where, we must admit, these rules will apply to our allies the same as our enemies.

The best reason to be a particularist is a pragmatic aversion to universals or absolutes. Perhaps a belief that exceptions don’t prove the rules but invalidate them. A desire for moral and ethical flexibility, especially the kind that recognizes and respects the primacy of one’s group affiliations (including the family unit) over obligations to total impartiality.

### Secularism vs. Sacralism

One’s orientation to knowledge, tradition, and the ineffable. The question of what legitimizes political authority, and how a society relates to claims about the transcendent.

Secularism doesn’t require atheism. It requires that when religious authority and civil authority conflict, civil authority prevails, and that public life runs on shared, revisable reasoning sans theology or sacred texts. Secularism doesn’t say nothing is sacred, it says that the sacred cannot supersede the argued or the civic, the democratic and the deliberated. Not for a dominant cosmology, nor for a minority one.

Sacralism doesn’t require a formal theocracy, at least not in the strictest sense of that term—tho they are often linked. It requires that religious or transcendent authority be treated as properly primary; and that public life, law, and culture ought to be ordered *under* it, even where that ordering is soft, implicit, or operates by intimation rather than explicit decree. A regime that never says “God commands this” but is structured so that a deity’s *presumed* law always wins when push comes to shove? That is sacralist regardless. A certain creed, culture, or cosmology can’t be questioned? Even one that’s ostensibly non-religious? Chances are, this is sacralism.

In some ways, the opposing impulses of presentism/modernism and traditionalism (or even conservativism, in its less partisan-coded valence) can map onto this pairing. As could humanism—in a more cultural or (anti-)spiritual sense than the legalistic usage from the individualism section. In contrast to dogmatism—with this being used in reference to *dogma* and meant to be, we must stress, more descriptive than pejorative. To make it as simple as possible, the secularist wants a tiebreak that doesn’t favor sacred cows or symbols. The sacralist wants the opposite, erring on the side of ancient ways and reverences over shared scrutiny of all schemas.

## Combinations — Non-exhaustive

In theory, eight positions are available across the three axes, assuming we combine all three answers to each implicit question. The problem is, not all are equally common or equally stable in practice. As the explainers above probably indicated. But I will not simply list off eight conflicting combos then. I don’t think that’s entirely elucidating as an exercise. Instead, I will talk principally about six to follow. These are ones most widely seen in our culture today, with key exemplars to illustrate as much. Mapping them is more useful than pretending the political landscape sorts neatly into two or three recognizable camps or factions.

A few notes before the list. Naming a combination isn’t an endorsement or a condemnation; the goal is accurate description. Some combinations will feel intuitive and some will feel strange, and the strangeness is informative. None of these come with an automatic policy platform attached either. The framework identifies orientation, not destination.

### Prominent Combos

**Individualist, universalist, secularist.** The combination most associated with Enlightenment liberalism in its fullest expression: rights held equally by all persons, legitimacy derived from reason and consent, no privileged in-group whose claims outrank anyone else’s. Probably the most internally consistent of the eight, since each commitment reinforces the other two without much friction. Its failure mode is a thin universalism that struggles to account for community, tradition, and the emotional weight of belonging somewhere specific.

**Individualist, universalist, sacralist.** Rarer than it sounds but real, especially within religious traditions carrying strong universal-dignity commitments. Rights held by all, equally, regardless of group, grounded in divine rather than civil authority. Certain strands of Christian humanism and Catholic social teaching land here. The tension is real: when a rights claim and a doctrine conflict, something has to give, and how a person resolves that determines how stable the combination is in practice.

**Collectivist, universalist, secularist.** The dominant combination of the twentieth-century leftist or progressive thought, and still the most recognizable collectivist profile. Material conditions restructured through group/state power, full and equal inclusion regardless of nation or identity, no divine authority anywhere in the justification. Internally consistent on its own terms. Hard socialism, often of a Marxist bent, would be a prominent example.

**Collectivist, universalist, sacralist.** Liberation theology, most prominently. The alignment of collective material transformation and universal human dignity with divine rather than secular justification. Cornel West is the clearest living example: Christian, committed to collective economic transformation, and explicitly universalist about who that transformation is owed to. More coherent than it’s usually given credit for. The three commitments don’t actually contradict each other, they just draw on an unusual combination of sources most observers aren’t used to seeing held together. It is worth acknowledging that some Islamists and their fellow travelers or apologists play lip-service to being this kind of person. The Red-Green alliance in contemporary politics is built on this premise. Many emerging Islamist political figures in the West come to mind.

**Collectivist, particularist, secularist.** Collective and state power paired with insular, ingroup loyalty and no (sincere) religious grounding. Trump-era populism is the clearest contemporary instance. Tariffs and industrial policy overriding market autonomy in the name of national strength, a willingness to deploy state power expansively and indifferently toward certain rights claims, paired with explicit national and cultural insularity, all without divine mandate as the operative justification. The populism here is, as elsewhere in this framework, opportunistic more than anything else. The movement is aimed at strengthening an ingroup’s position rather than genuinely redistributing power. It borrows collectivism’s tools while discarding universalism’s object.

**Collectivist, particularist, sacralist.** Theocracy and postliberalism fit here perfectly, including political Islam, Hindu nationalism, Catholic Integralism, and in several of their peer movements. Collective transformation, or resistance to a liberal world order. Particular and insular loyalty, divine authority as the ultimate justification. More common globally than Western political discourse tends to acknowledge, partly because the Western frame keeps trying to place these movements somewhere on a left/right axis that’s incoherent or self-serving.

### What’s Missing and Why

It’s my contention that, in practice, collectivism is more popular and more common than individualism. The wise H.L. Mencken wrote in his 1926 Book *Notes on Democracy* that the average person doesn't want to be free, they merely want to be safe. Given this, I do think it’s accurate to observe that the combinations of alignments which tend to truly err on the side of individualism are rare. In this case, outnumbered two to one in terms of popularity and internal coherence.

**Individualist, particularist, secularist.** Some might argue that this is the classical liberal or libertarian tradition. Individual rights and skepticism of radical transformation, on a civil rather than religious foundation, with a willingness to grant that obligations to one’s own community or nation are legitimate rather than embarrassing. It has commonly been associated with strains of thought on the amorphous and confused meta entity we have often called “the right.” Many libertarians, Randians, and anarchists seek freedom from state collectivism and from moral or legal universalism simply so they can enact fiefdoms that betray the very individualist principles they claim to support. I don’t think particularism makes commitments to individualism or secularism incoherent per se, but I do think that, in practice, collectivism makes more sense for people of the particularist and secular persuasion. Ultimately, this combination seems to collapse into a kind of nihilistically informed Will to Power. It stands for nothing in itself. And it may simply serve as a vehicle, or really a Trojan Horse, for personal fiat or soft authoritarianism.

**Individualist, particularist, sacralist.** In theory this should be where Burke and his followers actually live, and yet the landscape still seems rather barren. Not to mention contradictory. Some postliberals or anti-liberals do live here, tho they might deny it. Personal freedom alongside no truly binding commitment to universal or absolute norms, is already dicey, as noted above. But add religiosity and/or (neo-)traditionalism into the mix? Seems like a recipe for incoherent disaster. The sacred cannot coexist peaceably with the profane, or with the mentality of someone who wants “freedom for me, not for thee.”

## How Our Dichotomous Answers Interact

Knowing your position on each axis is the beginning, not the end. The more interesting question is how the three commitments relate to each other within a given combination: where they reinforce, where they create friction, and what that friction reveals.

Some combinations are self-stabilizing. Individualist, universalist, secularist is the clearest case. All three commitments share a common root: the primacy of human reason and human dignity as the basis for political life. Disagreements inside this combination tend to be about application and emphasis. An individualist universalist secularist could prioritize negative liberty—“don’t tread on me” and the philosophy of radical freedom from obligations. Another could prioritizes equality of opportunity and access, with positive rights claims being binding on both agents and institutions (even private ones). These might be in genuine tension. Still, they’re arguing from the same basic grammar.

Other combinations are stable not because the tensions inside them are absent, but because those tensions have been historically *managed* rather than **resolved**. Collectivist, particularist, sacralist has survived as a real political tradition for centuries by keeping its competing claims in separate jurisdictions—the church handles one domain, the state another, the market a third. This works until the jurisdictions collide, at which point the combination has to reveal its actual priority ordering. Those collision points—inheritance law, blasphemy, who gets to leave a marriage and on what terms—are where a combination’s true architecture shows.

The collectivist combinations share a structural feature worth naming: whichever axis is doing the collectivizing tends to absorb the other two rather than coexist with them. Collectivism is a totalizing orientation in a way individualism generally isn’t, since individualism’s whole premise is that no single project gets to subordinate everything else. The collectivist logic tends to bend whichever second axis it’s paired with to its own purposes. A collectivist universalist and a collectivist particularist aren’t simply two people who agree on the first axis and disagree on the second.

Twentieth-century collectivist movements absorbed the universalist commitment into a single overriding material analysis; contemporary collectivist-particularist movements absorb national or cultural identity into the same kind of overriding logic, just pointed at the nation instead of the class. This is part of why collectivist-particularist combinations can look unstable from the outside even when they’re functionally durable in practice. Whatever the ingroup loyalty is in service of stays potentially provisional, always liable to be redescribed as a means to some larger collective end rather than treated as a fixed commitment in its own right.

The sacralism axis has its own peculiar dynamic. It’s the one most likely to be underestimated, deferred, or quietly avoided. People who’ll readily identify as individualist or collectivist often resist placing themselves on the secularism/sacralism axis at all, either because “sacralist” sounds inflammatory by association or because they genuinely haven’t interrogated where they think political legitimacy ultimately comes from. But the axis does real work even when it stays invisible. A secularist and a sacralist can agree on every specific policy outcome and still be in fundamental conflict, because the moment a question gets settled by appeal to transcendent authority rather than civil deliberation, the secularist’s entire account of what counts as a legitimate answer has been quietly overridden.

The interaction between the second and third axes deserves particular attention. Particularism and sacralism have a long historical alliance: insular loyalty and divine mandate tend to reinforce each other, since both rest on the idea that *some group* or *some text* is simply set apart from the rest. But universalism and sacralism have their own tradition, one contemporary discourse often forgets or actively dismisses. Liberation theology, the religious register of abolitionism, the explicitly Christian framing running through the civil rights movement: these are universalist sacralist projects, and they’ve been among the most morally serious political movements of the last two centuries. Any honest version of this framework has to account for that, without flattering religion in general or treating secular universalism as the only legitimate form universalism can take.

What the three axes share, in the end, is this: each forces a question that can be deferred but not avoided. A person can go years without consciously choosing a side on any of them. The choice gets made anyway, in every political judgment rendered, every coalition joined, every argument found compelling or absurd. The framework doesn’t create these commitments. It just makes them visible.

## Where Do You Stand?

This isn’t a quiz. There’s no score, no archetype, no shareable result at the end. It’s a set of questions meant to surface commitments you probably already hold but may not have named. Answer them honestly: not how you think you should answer, not how your political community would want you to answer.

Take your time with anything that makes you uncomfortable. The discomfort is information.

### On individualism vs. collectivism

Do you believe individual rights place real limits on collective action, limits that can’t be overridden even by a democratic language or a movement you otherwise support? Or does rights-talk mostly function as a way of protecting existing arrangements against necessary change?

When you imagine a genuinely just society, is it one where individuals pursue their own ends within fair, evenly applied rules, or one where conditions have been fundamentally restructured, even at real cost to some personal freedoms?

If a movement or leader you agreed with on nearly everything else started overriding individual rights or market autonomy to get its way faster, would that be disqualifying, or an acceptable cost of getting where you wanted to go?

### On universalism vs. particularism

Are your obligations to a stranger on the other side of the world, in the relevant moral sense, the same as your obligations to a neighbor or compatriot? Or do proximity, shared history, and common identity create real and legitimate differences in what you owe whom?

When you encounter an institution that has historically excluded a given group, is your instinct to reform it toward inclusion, or to ask whether the exclusion might still be tracking something real about fitness, cohesion, or standards?

Is a polity entitled to prioritize its own people’s interests over outsiders’ as a matter of course, or does that prioritization always need separate, additional justification?

### On secularism vs. sacralism

If civil law and religious law conflict, which should prevail? Does your answer change depending on which religion is in question?

Does political authority get its legitimacy from human consent and deliberation, or from something that exists prior to and independent of human decision-making?

If a policy you opposed were enacted through ordinary democratic process, would you accept it as legitimate? If a policy you supported were prohibited by religious authority, would you accept that prohibition as binding?

### A few notes on what to do with your answers

First: intellectual honesty means following the answers where they lead, not retrofitting them to a label you’d already settled on. If your answers on the first axis are consistently collectivist but you’ve always called yourself an individualist, that’s worth sitting with rather than explaining away.

Second: the combination matters more than any single axis. A collectivist universalist secularist and a collectivist universalist sacralist share two-thirds of a position and can still be in profound conflict over the third. Your full coordinate is more descriptive than any one reading alone.

Third: it’s fine to lean one way on some questions within an axis and the other way on others. The framework asks for a leaning, not a purity test. But if you find yourself genuinely unable to lean either way on any of the three dichotomies, the likelier explanation is unresolved thinking, not a stable third option. Law of excluded middle still applies. You don’t get a “weird third thing” escape hatch. There’s only the difficult work of figuring out which side of each line you’re actually standing on.

## Where I Stand

For what it’s worth, and because a framework that asks everyone else to commit shouldn’t get to hide behind neutrality: I lean **individualist, universalist, secularist**. Individualism comes first and does the most load-bearing work. I don’t think collective projects, however well-intentioned, get to override a person’s basic claims to autonomy, dissent, and self-direction. Not provisionally, not for a transitional period, not because the cause is good enough to earn an exception.

My universalism follows from the same root: if individual claims matter, they have to matter the same way regardless of which individual is making them, a criterion that rules out treating a compatriot’s standing as categorically different from a stranger’s. And the secularism is, for me, close to *entailed by* the other two. Legitimacy that flows from revelation rather than reason has no real mechanism for being checked, corrected, or argued with by the people it governs, which makes it the wrong kind of foundation for anything meant to answer to the people living under it. Moral realism doesn’t require a god to be true, and nothing in this framework is meant to smuggle one in through the back door.

The honest cost, the one worth flagging for anyone tempted to find this combination obviously correct just because I’m the one telling you about it: individualist universalism is genuinely bad at explaining *why* anyone should care more about their own kid, their own street, their own country, than about an equally needy stranger a continent away. The framework’s own failure-state applies to me as much as anyone else in it. I think the position survives the challenge. I don’t think it wins for free or by default. Arguments are always the price of admission.

## No Stable Middle

Every framework like this one is, eventually, *an argument **against** comfort*. The old maps were comfortable because they let almost everyone find a seat without doing much work first: left or right, your tribe already drawn, your enemies pre-assigned, your priors confirmed by people who already sounded like you. Three independent dichotomies, each demanding an actual answer, are less comfortable by design. That discomfort is the entire point of building the thing. Conflict helps us discover truth.

Some readers will get this far and reach for an exit: I’m just pragmatic, they’ll say, I don’t do ideology. Roger Scruton built a version of this exit into his whole account of conservatism—the idea that it isn’t really an ideology at all, just an inherited wariness of abstract system-building, a disposition rather than a doctrine. It’s a clever move and a false one. **Wariness of system-building is itself** ***a position***. One with content, defended on grounds, vulnerable to argument, which makes it exactly the kind of thing this framework is asking you to own up to, not a way of floating above it.

Everyone has answers, implicit or explicit, to all three of these questions: what’s owed to the individual versus the collective, what’s owed to one’s own versus to everyone, where legitimacy ultimately comes from. The pragmatist hasn’t escaped having answers. They’ve just never been asked to produce them in one sitting, which let them avoid noticing the answers were there all along, underneath every vote, every debate, every flash of moral certainty they’ve ever had.

If you’ve made it this far and you’re genuinely unsure where you land on one or more of the three axes, sit with it. Argue with someone who disagrees, and notice which of their points you can’t dismiss. What you shouldn’t do is retreat into “it’s complicated” as a permanent home, or invent some undefined position that claims to float above all three axes at once. There isn’t one. Complexity is real. It just isn’t an escape hatch. It’s a description of how hard the underlying questions are, not a way of avoiding answering them.

The provocation here, if there is one, is small and a little old-fashioned: know what you believe, precisely enough that *you could be shown to be **wrong** about it* in a specific way. Diagnose yourself. Be honest about what you find. Then go argue like you mean it.
