## Conduct in the Iran-Iraq War (1980–1988)

This is a well-documented historical topic where both sides committed serious violations of international humanitarian law, though the nature and scale of their violations differed considerably.

---

### Iranian Conduct

**Violations and problematic practices:**

- **Human wave attacks:** Iran, particularly in the early-to-mid war years, repeatedly sent waves of poorly trained infantry — including young teenagers and even children recruited through the _Basij_ militia — directly into Iraqi defenses and minefields. Tens of thousands of young men died in these assaults. The use of children as combatants is widely condemned as a war crime under modern international law.
- **Treatment of POWs:** Iran held Iraqi prisoners in harsh conditions for years, and many were not repatriated until long after the 1988 ceasefire. Some were held into the 1990s.
- **Execution of prisoners:** There are credible reports of Iraqi POWs being executed, particularly after battlefield victories.
- **The 1988 massacre of political prisoners:** Though directed at domestic opponents (MEK/MKO members and others), the mass execution of thousands of political prisoners — carried out by the Islamic Republic that same year — reflects the regime's general disregard for legal norms.
- **Targeting of civilians:** Iranian missile and artillery strikes hit Iraqi cities, including Baghdad, as part of the "War of the Cities."

**Mitigating factors:**

- Iran was fighting a war of defense after Iraq's unprovoked invasion in September 1980.
- Iran did not use chemical weapons.
- Iranian forces sometimes showed restraint in occupied Iraqi territory (e.g., Faw Peninsula).

---

### Iraqi Conduct

**Violations and problematic practices:**

- **Chemical weapons — the defining atrocity of the war:** Iraq used chemical weapons (mustard gas, nerve agents including tabun and sarin) extensively and systematically, making it one of the largest uses of chemical warfare since World War I. These were used:
    - Against Iranian troops on the front lines throughout the mid-to-late war.
    - Most infamously, against the Kurdish civilian population of **Halabja** in March 1988, killing an estimated 3,000–5,000 people in a single attack — a genocide against Iraq's own citizens, carried out partly under the cover of the Iran-Iraq War.
    - In the broader **Anfal Campaign**, chemical weapons were used in a systematic campaign of ethnic cleansing against Kurds.
- **Treatment of POWs:** Iraqi treatment of Iranian prisoners was brutal. Torture, summary execution, and prolonged detention without repatriation were well documented by human rights organizations.
- **Targeting of civilians:** Iraq deliberately bombed Iranian cities, oil infrastructure, and civilian shipping in the Persian Gulf (the "Tanker War").
- **Aggression:** Iraq was the clear aggressor, launching an unprovoked full-scale invasion.
- **Use of mercenaries and foreign support:** Iraq benefited from Western and Gulf state support that helped sustain its war machine, including tacit tolerance of its chemical weapons use by the United States.

---

### Comparative Judgment

**Iraq fought considerably more "dirtily" by most meaningful measures.**

The use of chemical weapons — internationally banned since the 1925 Geneva Protocol — against both soldiers and civilians places Iraqi conduct in a category of its own. The Halabja massacre alone constitutes one of the worst war crimes of the 20th century's latter half. Iraq also initiated the war through unprovoked aggression, was the more systematic abuser of prisoners, and carried out genocide against its own Kurdish population under the war's cover.

Iran's conduct was far from clean — the use of child soldiers, mistreatment of POWs, and human wave tactics all constitute serious violations. But Iran did not deploy weapons of mass destruction, did not commit genocide, and was responding to invasion rather than initiating aggression.

If forced to a simple comparison: **Iran fought more cleanly**, though neither side deserves anything close to a clean record. The asymmetry is primarily explained by Iraq's unprecedented use of chemical warfare and its genocidal campaign against the Kurds.
