It’s been a while since we’ve heard from that ol’ stick in the mud, Anonymous Rust Dev.

## A luddite’s observations on AI

Somehow, I still conduct my day job without relying on AI regularly. As your friendly neighborhood Anonymous Rust Dev, you might think I’ve found a home for it at this point, but thus far, I’m just as much of a cantankerous old git as the actual [creator of Git](https://www.reddit.com/r/theprimeagen/comments/1ge5hwh/linus_torvalds_reckons_ai_is_90_marketing_and_10/).

> I’ve had people wax poetic, tell me these incredible success stories, and yet they haven’t won me over.

So, what’s holding me back? Am I just resisting the inexorable tides of change? What’s actually going on in my head? As I buck this trend and the peer pressure that goes along with it, am I just setting myself up for failure?

## The observations of a Millennial

As a self-described Luddite, I think it is important to [remember the original Luddites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luddite):

> *The Luddites were members of a 19th-century movement of English textile workers who opposed the use of certain types of automated machinery due to concerns relating to worker pay and output quality.*  
> \- *([Wikipedia](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Luddite.jpg#/media/File:Luddite.jpg))*

The Industrial Revolution may be the single greatest acceleration of societal change across history. We have made numerous advancements as a species, many of them game-changers, but access to industrial processes irrevocably changed how we function. The Luddites had good reason to fear the new paradigm — it was a fundamental attack on their way of life. And yet, they were on the leading edge of a societal evolution that would eventually come to introduce a new “middle” class and an era of prosperity for the Western world.

**...But, at what cost?**

The modern era is built on untold horrors arising from industrialism. Today, the idea of unions rubs so many the wrong way, but they are a product of a massive power imbalance that existed between workers and employers.

The likes of Carnegie, insanely wealthy and famous for his philanthropy, reached that point atop the [backs of mistreated laborers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homestead_strike). It is often joked that OSHA regulations are written in blood, and those who bristle at having to follow seemingly stupid rules should remember that, in their absence, employers could have their workers doing some really nasty and dangerous work, with little to no recourse.

> *Honestly, I can’t begin to do this topic justice. I don’t think we’re ignorant of these horrors; anyone who’s played games in the Bioshock or Fallout franchises, for instance, can see the \[fictional\] course trajectories of worlds where the guard rails weren’t there.*

### Old School

Born in the early 80’s myself, I’ve watched the world change at a rapid pace. My early memories were of things like the family’s Ford LTD, VCR decks, the Nintendo Entertainment System, and eventually I’d even get to a classroom environment where the class had its own dedicated Apple IIe in the corner of the room.

I’d read the works of futurists who said that one day every house would have a computer, and not long after, news broadcasts would do specials about how the Information Superhighway was coming to life and would soon touch our lives.

I don’t doubt that the generation before me was already clinging on for dear life. My grandmother could scarcely turn on the TV with the remote, and we’d be over at her house teaching her which one of the goofy symbols on the remote would start or stop her movie.

Meanwhile, I got to experience the explosion of growth in IT — computers became ubiquitous, dial-up would eventually give way to cable and DSL, internet chat would supplant phone conversations, and eventually lead to the phenomenon of SMS text messaging. And, of course, social media platforms would appear, fundamentally altering social dynamics and how information is broadcast and shared. That, coupled with the appearance of the smartphone, ensures we’re now perpetually tethered to the internet.

Society is still reeling, I think. We haven’t found footing, and old people today struggle to discern fact from fiction when scrolling through their Facebook feeds. My generation, along for the described ride during our formative years, has enough perspective and adaptability to barely keep up with it, if only just.

And the newest generations are being thrust into a world of adult-oriented haptics like touchscreens and voice recognition, or neatly digested information in YouTube Shorts that lets them bypass the learning process that earlier generations were forced to contend with, and I can’t help but wonder what it’s doing to hobble their developmental progress.

> Paradigm shifts have been a thing for the last couple of hundred years, but their frequency is increasing at an alarming rate. We’re being tossed about in ever choppier seas, and at some point, I expect the whole thing to capsize.

And then, we see the appearance of generative AI. The pace at which this technology evolves and its impact on the general populace are at once exciting and frightening.

People who don’t truly understand the technology get to witness this magic genie that seemingly has an answer for everything, while the people who do understand it (insofar as any one of us truly can) know that it’s simply stringing together the works of yore. This tech lands at the worst possible time, when we’re still reeling from the collective shock of the pandemic and dealing with tumultuous sociopolitical upheaval and a deepening divide across multiple facets of society.

## The observations of a freelancer

For much of this, I watch from the sidelines. I have no business overlord forcing AI down my throat; I can only watch with fascination as many businesses, like Google or Facebook, attempt to mandate that their workers use AI in their day-to-day activities. For them, the tool is a shiny new hammer, and they’re going to find a nail eventually.

> It [doesn’t seem to be going all that well, though...](https://futurism.com/artificial-intelligence/ai-failing-boost-productivity)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!y8t9!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F63690352-45d3-4c16-b4ca-255d558c662d_2002x922.png)

Meanwhile, I stay in touch with old colleagues in the web development business. They’ve got it rough — one of the areas in which generative AI excels is in displacing creativity, and digital design studios are especially hard-hit.

Of course, we all [know it’s soulless crap](https://www.businessinsider.com/coca-cola-ai-holiday-ad-glitches-highlight-ai-shortcomings-2025-11), but it’s becoming increasingly difficult to discern the difference between the real and the fiction.

At this point, I honestly don’t know how the real Will Smith feels about spaghetti, and if he came out in a video showing and telling us his love for the stuff, I wouldn’t be able to tell you whether it was true. [r/isthisAI](https://www.reddit.com/r/isthisAI/) gives a glimpse of the struggle to see through the haze.

![Mmmm, spaghetti](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Qrwz!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_lossy/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe0c2a436-63f9-41ee-80c7-5a33967d3ca0_480x240.gif)

Mmmm, spaghetti

...And personally, I find it very hard to tell anymore what’s fake and what’s authentic. I like to think I know what I’m looking for, but the cues are increasingly hard to detect. At least in my own generation, I feel we’ve been sufficiently primed to expect digital misinformation indistinguishable from fact; how much worse for the older generation, with their difficulties keeping up, or for the younger ones who haven’t seen the evolutionary path and are simply being dropped right into the midst of this?

> *I think it’s all by design. We’re currently so inundated as a society with change that we can’t hope to keep up. It’s a functional [Gish gallop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gish_gallop) — we’re assaulted from all sides, and we can only devote so much energy to making sense of it all. Hidden in this assault, I think, are multiple concerted efforts:*

**First**, businesses clearly see an opportunity to displace workers or readjust the power dynamic in their own favor. Unlike the fantasy universe of Wall-E, though, we probably can’t expect the automation to work in favor of the proletariat... Instead, the chasm between the haves and the have-nots will only widen.

- For a brief period during the pandemic, the IT worker was a highly coveted asset that businesses would fight over; now, big business sees a rare opportunity to flip the script and devalue those same workers by claiming AI can do their jobs. Whether that’s true or not, the narrative is being cemented, and soon we should count ourselves “lucky” if a business deigns to give us a job and the time of day to go with it.

**Second**, we’re being conditioned by AI vendors. Free-tier and low-cost AI services were a gateway drug, giving people a taste of what’s possible. As workers increasingly depend on AI to do the dirty work for them and consequently lose the skills or muscle memory associated with that work, there will one day be a rug-pull in which that subscription cost balloons rapidly, and people who got hooked will need to keep their drug dealers happy no matter the price.

- The vibe coding era will be quickly replaced by a “prompt specialist” one, where you can no longer just pick up the tech on a lark, but instead need to sell your soul for access to it.

**Third**, in a rather conspiratorial vein, I also see an emerging technocracy, where certain leaders in the tech space will have positioned themselves to profit from the chaos. I don’t need to name them; you probably already know who I mean, but they will have the keys to the kingdom.

- They will own the data centers, dictate policy, and be the final arbiters of right and wrong. It’s [already happening](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/12/eliminating-state-law-obstruction-of-national-artificial-intelligence-policy/), and will only increase in its efforts over time. There is still a shrinking window of opportunity to fight this, as the digital arms race collides with economic and [infrastructure difficulties](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sL5pItTShys), but if they emerge victorious, then we’ll be facing a grim future.

## The observations of a developer

So, do I think it’s all horrible? No, quite the contrary — I see a place for *tooling* to emerge that can function as a force multiplier, for those who know what they’re looking at and how to accommodate it. Granted, anyone who depends on AI-generated code needs to question the ethics involved; those models aren’t above plagiarizing the works of those who came before.

The major dangers I see are:

- As already stated, there’s a hook of reliance and an atrophy of skills for people who depend too heavily on this stuff
- The illusion that developers are easily replaced can create economic shock
	- *The idea that developers are easily replaced by automation will displace entry-level talent, and when they’re not given the opportunity for growth, the future talent pool will be effectively choked out*
		- *Talented engineers will be needed to come in and fix the mess when the dust settles, but their work will be much harder as they’re forced to comb through an accumulation of slop and codebases absent of a coherent vision*
- The tooling changes so rapidly, and yet people are investing time and effort into the here-and-now; when the tech inevitably evolves, they’ll be left holding a worthless skillset and lost opportunities
- The babysitting required to police the quality of generative output is an opportunity cost and a cognitive load that can effectively harm performance
- As the tech improves, it becomes easier to trust the output and perhaps be less vigilant in policing its quality

There are more reasons for concern, but these are enough to give me pause in my own workflows. Like [Mitchell Hashimoto](https://youtu.be/WjckELpzLOU?t=497), my biggest periods of growth were when the assistive tech wasn’t available -

I likewise credit learning computers with when my childhood computer’s mouse stopped working, and all I had was a keyboard. My coding benefited greatly from forcing myself to use Vim exclusively for a month straight (*and that was before I understood the plugin ecosystem, so no LSP assistance*).

> Even today, as I avoid using snippets or auto-generated coding tools in my JetBrains IDEs, it’s always *me* writing the code, keeping me cognitively tied to my codebase.

That said, while I don’t use anything of the sort today, I think I would personally benefit from AI in an auditing process. Static analysis tools stand to become much better in this AI boom, and even on the off-chance that it hallucinates an answer, I’d rather have a hypercritical nanny than one that lets me get away with bad coding practices or preventable mistakes.

Meanwhile, I do see another potential issue stemming from people increasingly relying on generative AI for their work: at some point, innovation will die. By this, I mean: if our primary toolkit is reaching into what’s already been done, and the AI models only have a recirculating collection of antiquated code to draw from, we’re potentially facing the terminus of novel coding techniques.

- It’s admittedly alarmist — I mean, real coding isn’t *dead*, but from a signal-to-noise perspective, we’re rapidly approaching a place where almost all “new” code is the product of automated tools rather than direct human outputs.

That said, maybe this creates new opportunities for a previously unknown role of *code optimizer*. Businesses can focus on stringing together generative slop that implements business logic at the expense of code cohesion and performance, and can then use their raw-code specialist to walk back over the code and make it good.

It’s not unlike the era when data analysts would create Access forms and maybe some chunky VBA code to make it work, and then hand it off to a “real” developer to turn it from a prototype into a well-oiled machine. I think this will be a messy but lucrative job, sort of like the [guy who maintains sewer pipes](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=cesMrx2_tAU).

## Observations of a code artisan

What a stupid thing for me to take pride in. “Artisanal code” is a trending term, even to the point of being an actual [brand name](https://artisanalcode.com/). After all, you might think that by hand-crafting all of my code, I’m leaving a lot of time on the table spent doing the grunt work, right? Why get hung up on this when AI can probably understand and implement my needs with a few carefully crafted prompts?

Maybe this is the distinction between a software developer and a software engineer. I admittedly spend very little of my time “writing code” — most of my time is actually spent in the problem domain itself, identifying business needs and the corresponding route to achieving them.

If generative tools get my coding time down from 15 minutes per day to 10 minutes, but I spend over 7 hours on reasoning and discovery, then it stands to reason that AI doesn’t really fix my bottleneck.

> *For the record, those numbers are contrived... I do actually write more code than that, but it does give an illustrative look at how I divide my time.*

What’s the upside of artisanal code? Well, as the lone developer on my projects, I have a comprehensive understanding of the product as a whole, and because the code is the product of my own thought process it takes little effort to go back over it and make sense of it during the maintenance phase.

And let me just say, I’ve written some gnarly code, products not necessarily of bad coding practices so much as the requirements of the problem being solved; every advantage I can get during the code comprehension phase of development is a massive advantage.

Thinking more broadly about others’ jobs, I am still convinced that a human coder will create a more “understandable” piece of work than an automated one. While the individual prompts might result in coherent units of work, it’s the collection of these units that makes a program, and if the code isn’t univocal, then it presents a maintenance nightmare. Specifications can help with this, but that presumes that coders always supply them, and that they’re consistently in sync with the code that they drive.

Code is many things to different people: a necessary evil, a means to an end, and to some (myself included), a form of art. I don’t view it purely through a utilitarian lens (though I can be pragmatic when the situation warrants), but instead I use it as a vehicle for communicating ideas and intentions through digital channels.

I’m the only one generally reading my code, so it might seem rather hedonic to worry about “beautiful” code, but to be honest, I often bring code up during client meetings to walk them through how I actually solve their problems. Let me restate that: I show my customers *beautiful Rust code*, fully expecting that it’s written in a way that even a non-coder can follow along. I don’t know how to prompt AI for that kind of code.

## Observation of a dinosaur

The cat’s out of the bag. AI is the new normal — or at least, *some* kind of AI will be. I don’t think the dust is settled on what that looks like yet; we’re still in the infancy of this technology, and there are plenty of ways this rough draft can change. For me to stick my head in the ground and pretend it’s not a thing isn’t an option, and I ignore it at my own peril.

> Still, it’s also early enough that I don’t think it yet has an essential place in my arsenal. Things change quickly, and I might be pleasantly surprised by some shiny new tool that has yet to be created. I’m open to a future that does incorporate AI, even as I contemplate the remote possibility that I’ll be too late to the game to catch up.

I frequently count myself lucky that I’m a freelancer in this job market. The turmoil created by this shakeup is a shame, given how my colleagues struggle to find gainful employment or fight to keep the jobs they already have.

- Much of this is artificial, a stampede of foolhardy business leaders willing to destroy their own talent pools today in pursuit of a future where they control all the levers and dictate the terms of their relationship with software developers. I would have a very difficult time going back into that workforce, if only because I think I know my own value and wouldn’t do well under such idiot bosses.

Just know that, whatever kind of developer you are — viber, artisan, struggling junior, lead who almost never touches code — I’m rooting for you and your craft. We’re about solving problems at the end of the day, and the code is just a part of that. Use the tools that get you to a quality product in the most expedient way possible, but also hang onto just a bit of skepticism and critical thought as you do so.
