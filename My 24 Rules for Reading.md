### These have been invaluable for me—maybe they will help you too.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!O0q9!,w_424,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc6cc6e86-ce40-4ea5-ae5f-81616c7a88d2_1057x352.png)

Long ago, I made a decision to devote time every day to reading books. I didn’t do this for job skills or entertainment. I didn’t do this to earn a credential or for research. And I certainly didn’t do it to show off—the so-called *[performative reading](https://www.newyorker.com/culture/the-lede/performative-reading)* now a meme on social media.

In fact, I never discussed any of this publicly until I finally wrote about my [“lifetime reading plan”](https://www.honest-broker.com/p/my-lifetime-reading-plan) in 2023—more than fifty years after I first embarked on this program. I hardly even mentioned it privately. Only my family members knew how much I read, and the intense focus I put into my intellectual development via books.

So why *do* I read all these books?

I decided at age 13 that I would read books to *attain wisdom* —and I’ve kept it up ever since. Maybe that sounds absurd when mentioned so baldly. So go ahead and laugh if you want.

But here’s the strange thing. I got all those other benefits—job skills, entertainment, etc.—along the way. Even more interesting, I gained a powerful but intangible benefit from these tens of thousands of hours devoted to good books.

As [I explained back in 2023](https://www.honest-broker.com/p/my-lifetime-reading-plan):

> Once I got into my forties, with all this deep learning behind me, it somehow gave me an aura of *gravitas* I’d never possessed before. People started treating me differently—and not because I made any demands. Not in the least. I’m not the kind of person to make demands.
> 
> And it wasn’t like I was quoting Shakespeare and Plato all the time. I tended to keep this literary education hidden from view, except when it was absolutely relevant to the situation at hand—at least hidden from *direct* view. But the nature of this kind of training is that it still shows up indirectly. And that’s what happened in my case.
> 
> In some ways, I was the last person to figure this out. But I saw the changes reflected in the other people I dealt with. It took me a long time to connect all this to the books I’d read.
> 
> But what else could explain it?
> 
> When I spoke before an audience, this behind-the-scenes project seemed to give my words an authority and resonance I hadn’t possessed in my youth. People were now trying to hire me for all sorts of crazy projects—pitching to venture capitalists, negotiating million-dollar deals, etc.—because of this aura.
> 
> I couldn’t even begin to tell you all the wild and crazy ways this changed my life.

Since writing that essay on my lifetime reading plan, many people have asked for more details. How do I pick the books I read? How do I retain what I read? Etc.

With that in mind, I’m sharing some of my rules for reading. Below I list 24 of them.

Before sharing these rules, I want to say that they work for me. But other approaches may be just as valid. So don’t look at these as immutable laws. This is simply one person’s journey, and there are always many different ways of reaching a destination.

### TED’S 24 RULES FOR READING

1. Reading shouldn’t be a *goal* —instead it ought to be cultivated as a *habit*.
2. It should be a relaxing and enjoyable habit, something you look forward to each day. That’s why setting too many reading goals will be counterproductive. It will put pressure on you, and that’s the opposite of relaxation.
3. Remember that this is a lifetime habit. So there is plenty of time to get to all of the great (and not so great) books.
4. For the same reason, you shouldn’t be concerned if you read slowly. I can do speed reading, but I don’t enjoy it—so I reserve it for situations (typically work-oriented) where I’m operating under a deadline. For my daily book reading habit, I go at a more measured pace. This isn’t a race.
5. Always bring a book with you. When other people are forced to wait in line or sit in the waiting room, they get irritated and impatient. But I am happily engaged in my book. This adds to my serenity.
6. When you find an author you love, make sure to read a good biography that follows their life and intellectual development. This is more valuable than reading literary criticism or academic writing about them, and will inspire you in your own life.
7. Make time to read books by people who have different opinions than your own, but always seek out the best examples by the smartest writers. This will allow you to pressure test your own thinking, and teach you tolerance for those with different life experiences and world views.
8. The best way of choosing books is by looking at the consensus of wise people over time. If the best and brightest tell you to read Shakespeare or Plato or Dante, you do well to take their endorsements seriously.
9. Because I take these endorsements seriously, I’ve tended to prefer older books, because they have been validated by generations of readers. You are less likely to go astray with these timeless works.
10. But you also need to engage with your own time, and your own place in the world. So even those pursuing a classic education should give some attention to current works—seeking out the best new books they can find (once again relying on the guidance of wise people with experience and judgment).
11. In my case, I gave priority to the classic books from the past until my forties, and once I got a firm grounding in these, I started reading more current books. So my rule is: Read old books when you are young, and young books where you are old. (By the way, you will get so much more from new books if you have first given yourself a solid foundation in the past.)
12. You are encouraged to disagree with any book, even the most famous. The whole purpose of reading is to develop your own thinking. Critics of a classic education sometimes claim that the “great books” are taught as some kind of propaganda for the past, but nothing could be further from the truth. These books *always* invite response and criticism—that’s what this kind of education is all about. My [immersive humanities program](https://www.honest-broker.com/p/a-12-month-immersive-course-in-humanities) is filled with authors I disagree with. But engaging with them makes me stronger.
13. I should have read more history books early in life—this would have given me a better perspective on the literature of previous centuries. I didn’t ignore social sciences, and made time to study the best works I could find from anthropologists, sociologists, etc., but history (especially surveys and primary sources such as memoirs and chronicles) are invaluable. I didn’t realize this until my forties, when I made a concerted effort to fill this gap.
14. Over time, you will find that your opinions will change as a result of this lifetime reading program. So feel free to return to books you read many years before—and don’t be surprised if your responses have changed as your thinking has matured. The best books have a way of doing this.
15. You will get more from a book if you take notes—either in the margins or in a separate journal. That’s true because the very process of writing notes helps you think through issues. So the notes have a benefit even when you never look at them again.
16. If a book inspires valuable thoughts, write them down *immediately*. This is my single most useful source of inspiration as a writer—my response to the writings of others. When this inspiration comes to you, grab it and see where it takes you.
17. I’ve learned that I retain more if my notes focus on *what this book means to me*. That’s always at the top of my mind while reading. Sure, I’m also concerned with what this book means in the history of culture, or what it might mean to an elite critic. But the most important takeaway should always be how this book relates to you, your life, your values, your way of thinking, your priorities, etc.
18. Don’t be a snob. Learn from all kinds of books. I’ve made a point of reading the timeless novels and grand works of philosophy, etc., and those are my main focus. But I’ve also made time for the best sci-fi, fantasy, horror, and detective fiction I can find. Populist and genre works of this sort account for around 20% of my reading.
19. Once or twice every year, tackle a big difficult book. The most mind-expanding experiences happen when you go outside your comfort zone, and these hard books are essential to this process.
20. Even if you don’t completely understand a book, there is still value in reading a difficult work from cover to cover. I got better as a reader because I tackled hard works that sometimes left me puzzled. Over time, I found it got easier and easier to read difficult books, but I needed to persevere to gain these benefits.
21. Before you announce that some classic book is boring and worthless, consider the possibility that you might not have grasped its value *yet*. I’ve sometimes returned to books of this sort twenty years later, and am amazed at how much I missed the first time around. You are wise to take seriously the consensus of the centuries, and only depart from it after you have reached full maturity as a reader.
22. If you find a subject that interests you—ancient Rome or travel writing or the natural world or Buddhism or whatever—do a deep dive and read 10-20 books on that topic. It’s like earning a mini-master’s degree every time you do this.
23. In addition to all of the above, I obviously had to do reading for my vocation—or rather vocations, because I’ve pursued several over the years. But this professional reading was separate from my lifetime reading program. So I always start the day by reading a good book that has no relationship whatsoever to my career.
24. Find opportunities to talk with others who love books. These will often turn into lasting friendships with the finest companions.
