To a cynical eye, the career of [Lindsey Graham](https://www.wsj.com/topics/person/lindsey-graham), who [died](https://www.wsj.com/politics/lindsey-graham-republican-senator-dies-71-bd02a353?mod=article_inline) Saturday, captures in one tortuous narrative arc the baleful evolution of American politics, especially of the Republican Party, in the 21st century.

Graham’s journey from neoconservative gadfly to fierce Never Trumper to President Trump’s drooling fanboy could serve as a road map to the moral, intellectual and institutional decay of the party of Ronald Reagan.

His conversion bears the hallmarks of the wider collapse across the political spectrum: the triumph of demotic expediency over honor, of opportunistic convenience over consistency, of raw power over principle. His [public](https://www.youtube.com/shorts/MTb7k6HXTSs) shows of [obeisance](https://www.youtube.com/shorts/n3saip0pxTo) to President Trump exemplify the voluntary surrender of the legislative branch of government, a genuflection to executive power alien to both republican democracy and conservatism.

Graham’s 32 years in national politics were divided into two parts, each defined by a [personal allegiance](https://www.wunc.org/2018-08-28/mccain-honored-by-best-friend-graham-in-emotional-senate-speech) to one of two different men: Sen. John McCain and President Trump—about as far apart politically, ideologically and in their personal character as it is possible for two politicians in the same party to be. Other than that they both secured the GOP nomination for president, the thing they most seem to have in common was that they were both incapacitated for much of the Vietnam War: the former because of his detention in a North Vietnamese hellhole, the latter because of bone spurs. For someone to move so enthusiastically from one to the other suggests a plasticity of scruples reflective of the rotten state of polarized partisanship in the republic.

But this is too harsh a judgment. Like most political journalists, to whom he made himself conspicuously available, I spent much time with the senator over his career.

The cynical account of his political evolution is only partially right. He was an engaging, funny and self-deprecating raconteur, ready with the quotable quip and the off-the-record gossip, which always assured him of an audience. But as he effected his Damascene conversion to the man whose takeover of the Republican Party he once described as “bat s— crazy” and “like being shot in the head,” as he trumpeted some of the more cringeworthy sycophancy about his new political master, his public devotion was designed at least in part to protect for his party and his country some enduring principles.

Confronted with a revolutionary change in prevailing political conditions, ambitious people do one of three things. Some refuse to accommodate themselves to the new order, out of principle or the realization that their own future is over, and seek opportunity elsewhere. Some get on the bandwagon, happy to parrot whatever they’re told will preserve their careers. But most try something in between. The aim is to survive and even thrive in the new world by doing the necessary amount of submission, but in the process to preserve the most important pre-revolutionary ideals and principles. This is what I think Graham sought—with mixed success—to do.

I first came across him in his role as a congressman and loyal lieutenant on McCain’s famous Straight Talk Express in the campaign for the 2000 Republican presidential nomination. His affection for the Arizona senator was something of a statement of political independence back then: A more cynically ambitious Southern pol would have presumably signed onto the campaign of the overwhelming favorite and eventual winner, George W. Bush. But it was clear that what drew him to McCain was the idealism that the Vietnam War veteran represented—the idea that America was a force for profound good in the world and that it should use its power, including strategic and military force when justified, judiciously to promote that good. Graham became an impassioned advocate for the same idea when he was elected to the Senate in 2002.

But by 2016, when he briefly ran for president, the politics had changed. He was one of the party’s leaders most closely associated with the foreign-policy failures in Iraq and Afghanistan, which led to the arrival of Mr. Trump committed to an end to “forever wars.” This new turn in popular opinion and Mr. Trump’s embrace of it was understandable. But the risk has always been that the pendulum would swing too far, that wariness of foreign entanglements would lead America to abandon its global role.

This is what Graham worked to prevent, in part through his assiduous sycophancy. He was a passionate advocate for America’s alliances—with Israel especially, but also in Europe and Asia. Recently I spent time with him in Europe, where he tried to pull off the almost impossible task of defending President Trump’s anti-Atlanticism while persuading allies that the U.S. was still committed. His efforts to keep the administration engaged in Ukraine have borne fruit.

His zeal for America was genuine. If political expediency often ruled his mind, his heart was that of a true patriot, dedicated always to the freedom he knew only his country could ultimately achieve.
