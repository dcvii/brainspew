James Burnham’s *The Machiavellians: Defenders of Freedom* (1943) is a work of political theory that seeks to recover a realist tradition in political thought, drawing heavily on figures such as Machiavelli, Pareto, Mosca, and Michels. Burnham’s central argument is that freedom is best preserved not by idealistic or moralistic political doctrines, but by a clear-eyed understanding of how power actually operates in society. He positions the “Machiavellians” as analysts of political reality rather than advocates of any particular ideology, and argues that their descriptive honesty provides a stronger foundation for defending liberty than the utopian or rhetorical claims of democratic or authoritarian systems alike.

At the core of Burnham’s thesis is the claim that all societies are governed by elites. Rejecting the democratic ideal that power resides with “the people,” Burnham draws on Gaetano Mosca’s theory of the ruling class and Vilfredo Pareto’s concept of elite circulation. He argues that a minority—organized, cohesive, and strategically positioned—inevitably exercises power over the majority, which is typically disorganized and politically passive. This elite dominance is not an aberration but a structural feature of all complex societies. As such, political systems should be evaluated not by their professed ideals but by how elites are selected, maintained, and replaced.

Burnham is particularly critical of what he calls “political formulas”—the ideological justifications that ruling elites use to legitimize their power. These formulas, whether democratic (e.g., popular sovereignty) or authoritarian (e.g., divine right or racial supremacy), are not accurate descriptions of how power functions but rather myths that help stabilize regimes. The Machiavellians, in contrast, expose these formulas as rhetorical devices and focus instead on the mechanisms of control, coercion, and consent that sustain elite rule. For Burnham, this demystification is essential: only by understanding the gap between ideology and reality can citizens and analysts meaningfully assess political systems.

A key contribution of the Machiavellian tradition, as Burnham interprets it, is the emphasis on elite circulation—the process by which ruling groups are renewed or replaced. Pareto’s distinction between “lions” (force-oriented elites) and “foxes” (cunning, manipulative elites) illustrates how different types of leadership rise and fall depending on historical conditions. Burnham argues that a healthy political system is one in which elites are not static but are subject to competition and turnover. When elite circulation is blocked, regimes tend toward stagnation, corruption, and eventual collapse.

Robert Michels’ “iron law of oligarchy” further reinforces Burnham’s argument. Michels observed that even organizations committed to democratic principles—such as socialist parties—inevitably develop oligarchic leadership structures. Leaders acquire specialized knowledge, control over organizational resources, and influence over communication, all of which entrench their position. Burnham uses this insight to challenge the feasibility of genuine mass democracy, suggesting that the problem is not the betrayal of democratic ideals but their incompatibility with organizational realities.

Despite this seemingly pessimistic view, Burnham does not abandon the concept of freedom. Instead, he redefines it in institutional and procedural terms. Freedom, in his account, is not the absence of elite rule but the presence of conditions that constrain and balance elites. These conditions include the existence of multiple competing elites, the rule of law, transparency, and the ability of opposition groups to organize and challenge those in power. In this sense, freedom emerges from a dynamic equilibrium among elites rather than from popular sovereignty in any direct sense.

Burnham contrasts this realist defense of freedom with both totalitarian regimes and naïve democratic idealism. Totalitarian systems attempt to eliminate elite competition and impose a monolithic ruling class, thereby destroying the conditions necessary for liberty. On the other hand, democratic ideologies that ignore the realities of elite power risk enabling unaccountable rule by disguising it behind popular rhetoric. The Machiavellians, by exposing these dangers, provide a more durable framework for preserving freedom.

In conclusion, *The Machiavellians: Defenders of Freedom* offers a rigorous critique of political idealism and a defense of a realist approach to governance. Burnham argues that freedom is best protected not by clinging to comforting myths about popular rule, but by designing institutions that recognize and regulate the inevitable dominance of elites. By emphasizing empirical analysis, elite competition, and institutional constraints, Burnham situates the Machiavellian tradition as a pragmatic, if unsentimental, foundation for understanding and defending political liberty.
