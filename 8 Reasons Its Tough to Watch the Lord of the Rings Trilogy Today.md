There are few fantasy adaptations that have reached the high watermark set by Peter Jackson's beloved trilogy. While J.R.R. Tolkien's set the standard for the genre over 70 years ago, it was Jackson's adaptation of the franchise that blew the door open, bringing audiences in to the sprawling Middle-earth canon and the plight of one Frodo Baggins.

Even now, 25 years after the first film's release, *Lord of the Rings* remains, with multiple spin-offs, a hit TV show, and even an upcoming sequel in the form of *The Hunt for Gollum.* With all that in mind, Jackson's *Lord of the Rings* movies were produced in the early 00s. This means that, comparatively, the films have started to show their age in some key ways. Not to say, they are no longer worthy of their classic status, but, more than two decades after first gracing the silver screen, there are a few key things that make the beloved blockbusters a bit tougher to watch upon revisiting.

## 8 The Lord of the Rings Movies Miss Key Parts of the Book

![Rory Kinnear as Tom Bombadil in The Lord of the Rings: The Rings of Power Season 2](https://static0.cbrimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2024/07/rings-of-power-rory-kinnear-tom-bombadil-1.jpg?q=70&fit=crop&w=825&dpr=1)

Image via Prime Video

*The Lord of the Rings* was supposed to whittle 1,000 densely written pages down to three movies, a Herculean undertaking that became a monumental achievement. In this regard, Peter Jackson did a spectacular job of condensing the story without straying too far from the main plotline. On the other hand, many fans were frustrated by the excisions of far too many book sequences. Information about family trees and cultural traditions isn't as necessary as the characters themselves.

Saruman's death was changed to reflect his life, murdered atop the tower of Orthanc by his pathetic servant, Womtongue. The novels dedicated an entire chapter to the Scouring of the Shire, and the absence of that scene was felt in *The Return of the King*. Moreover, it wasn't necessary to remove [Tom Bombadil, as his character represented a higher power](https://www.cbr.com/the-rings-of-power-creators-tom-bombadil-is-god-theory/) that always held sway in Tolkien's world. Now that *The Rings of Power* has been filling in the blanks left by *The Lord of the Rings*, the gaps feel even more absolute.

![Gollum talking to the hobbits in a cave in The Lord of the Rings The Return of the King](https://static0.cbrimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/andy-serkis-as-gollum-in-the-lord-of-the-rings-the-return-of-the-king.jpeg?q=70&fit=crop&w=825&dpr=1)

Image via New Line Cinema

*The Lord of the Rings* earned unprecedented acclaim for its visual standards, a blend of practical effects, miniatures, and pioneering CGI. *The Fellowship of the Ring* took the Spartan approach, using CGI sparingly when necessary. Meanwhile, *The Two Towers* elevated its visuals to a whole new level of impressive with the Battle of Helm's Deep and Gollum's motion-capture, though the latter's brilliance is thanks mostly to Andy Serkis.

*The Return of the King* relied heavily on CGI, from the Battle of the Pelennor Fields and the Army of the Dead to the gorgeous capital city of Gondor. Contemporary viewers were understandably amazed by these effects, which now come across as overblown and dated. The scene with Gollum falling into the pits of Mount Doom while clutching the One Ring looks rather clumsy, effectively taking away from Serkis' Oscar-deserving performance. It just doesn't hold up well in the 2020s.

## 6 Middle-earth's Female Characters Are Severely Underserved

![Éowyn practices her sword fighting in anticipation of a big battle against evil in The Lord of the Rings.](https://static0.cbrimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/eowyn.PNG?q=49&fit=crop&w=500&dpr=2)

Image via New Line Cinema

J.R.R. Tolkien filled his works with both male and female characters, although men were often given the lion's share of the spotlight. This shouldn't have stopped him from granting his female characters more agency, instead of deifying them like Galadriel, Lúthien, and Arwen. And Jackson's adaptations didn't improve upon that problem as much as it should have. [Arwen had a slightly bigger role](https://www.cbr.com/arwen-evenstar-necklace-meaning-explained-lord-of-rings/) as Frodo's savior, but the rest of her arc teemed with romantic undertones.

Galadriel is a symbol of noble femininity, an unnecessary pedestal for women who have shaped the world with their ferocious natures. Éowyn's victory over the Witch-king of Angmar is directly connected to her gender — sneakily implying that *any* woman could have killed the overpowered villain. The women in *The Lord of the Rings* have been relegated to secondary roles: as supporters, wives, and lovers. Even Éowyn falls into this trap with the unrequited love subplot, shipping her with Aragorn.

## 5 Even LOTR Didn't Avoid Cultural Stereotypes

![The Lord of the Rings The Return of the King](https://static0.cbrimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/the-lord-of-the-rings-the-return-of-the-king.jpg?q=49&fit=crop&w=825&dpr=2)

Image via New Line/courtesy Everett Collection

Saying that Tolkien belonged to a different era is a blame-deflecting argument used in defense of the author's borderline-colonial gaze. While all the Elves are compassionate at heart, they clearly represent white characters. *[The Rings of Power](https://www.cbr.com/rings-of-power-season-3-peter-jackson-connection-last-chance/)* [tried to subvert](https://www.cbr.com/rings-of-power-season-3-peter-jackson-connection-last-chance/) this with Arondir, a tactic that failed because it came far too late in the franchise. In fact, even the Dwarves, Hobbits, and Men are entirely white in *The Lord of the Rings*. There is not a single POC actor among the heroes.

For comparison, Tolkien painted the Haradrim and the Easterlings, two races of Men who lent their support to Sauron, as incompetent villains. Both these people were of darker skin and hair, and Tolkien juxtaposed these supposedly tainted Eastern and Southern characters against the purity of the West.

## 4 It's Still Harder Than Ever To Watch the Extended Cuts

![Samwise Gamgee and other hobbits at The Green Dragon from The Lord of the Rings: The Fellowship of the Ring](https://static0.cbrimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/hobbits-at-green-dragon.png?q=70&fit=crop&w=825&dpr=1)

Image via New Line Cinema

As mentioned earlier, Peter Jackson streamlined his trilogy for cinema, removing any scene he believed was inconsequential to the overarching storyline. However, the director included many of these scenes in the extended editions, which run for a combined two hours longer than the theatrical cuts. One would assume that the availability of streaming platforms would bring the extended cuts to the populace, though that isn't always the case.

While there have been platforms highlighting the director's cut, the theatrical versions have always been the most common on streaming. More annoyingly, it's impossible to predict when a service will have the extended edition available. Fans can obtain them in Ultra HD Blu-ray, but that's an extra expense that doesn't seem worth it. Viewers need to engage with the trilogy in its most complete form, and there shouldn't be so many hurdles in a process that's otherwise sleek and efficient.

## 3 Cultural Saturation Has Left Fans Numb

![Gimli trips Legolas' bow then feigns alarm with his hand to his mouth in Return of the King.](https://static0.cbrimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2024/12/screenshot-1397.png?q=70&fit=crop&w=825&dpr=1)

Image via New Line Cinema

*The Lord of the Rings* has faced one of the most common problems known to global franchises: oversaturation. The movie trilogy earned billions from ticket sales and home video purchases, and still continues to be a frequent talking point. The releases of spin-offs, such as *War of the Rohirrim* and [the upcoming *Hunt for Gollum*](https://www.cbr.com/andy-serkis-confirms-a-major-lord-of-the-rings-the-hunt-for-gollum-update/), consistently keep the franchise in the public eye. That said, *The Rings of Power* is probably the biggest contributor to oversaturation.

In addition, Jackson's trilogy has been endlessly satirized, referenced, and dissected by the media and the fandom. People who haven't watched a single second of the films probably know the whole story from the array of memes and references that abound on the Internet. There are no more plot twists capable of genuine surprise — Gandalf's resurrection, Boromir's betrayal, and Frodo's final claim on the One Ring have been an inexorable part of pop culture for decades.

## 2 Modern Fantasy Has Changed The Game

![Robb Stark and Catelyn Stark on Game of Thrones](https://static0.cbrimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/game-of-thrones-season3.jpg?q=49&fit=crop&w=825&dpr=2)

Image via HBO

Although rewatching *The Lord of the Rings* in 2025 might feel as exciting as ever, new viewers are faced with a unique problem. Fantasy has evolved significantly since Peter Jackson, with the best stories often being the most subversive. [*Game of Thrones* stole *LotR* 's crown](https://www.cbr.com/lotr-solved-game-of-thrones-lighting-problem-before-it-started/) almost immediately by depicting a complex world with a diverse range of values and moralities. *House of the Dragon* followed the same trend, effectively reflecting the real world through fantasy narratives.

Meanwhile, there's far too much moral clarity in *The* *Lord of the Rings* for the story to feel authentic and relatable. With very few exceptions, the heroes are always good and the villains are always evil. And yet, the 21st century has no place for black-and-white perspectives. In fact, newer fantasy stories have also been blending genres to rise above the clichéd archetypes — a notable example is *The Green Knight* 's surreal approach. *LotR* simply isn't cool or unique enough anymore.

## 1 Remake Nerves Have Swept the LOTR Fandom

![Frodo The Lord of the Rings](https://static0.cbrimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/frodo-the-lord-of-the-rings.jpg?q=49&fit=crop&w=825&dpr=2)

Arguably one of the most vexing issues in current media, remakes have taken the world by storm. Many studios would rather bank on previously successful projects than fund something completely new, leading to a spate of unwanted reboots. *Harry Potter* is getting a TV show that will follow the exact same story as the movies, though it promises to be a more faithful adaptation. And then there's the endless spate of Disney live-action remakes that few fans even asked for.

Although adapting *The Lord of the Rings* once again is unlikely, given all the side-projects commissioned for the franchise, even the idea of a reboot is off-putting. There's already the fading nostalgia that fans have to contend with, putting even more pressure on how viewers should consume the film trilogy. Some want a reboot to rectify all the problems mentioned in this article, while others want their masterpiece preserved as it is. The tension between nostalgia and interpretation transforms *The Lord of the Rings* into a battlefield for the legacy of fantasy cinema, making rewatches feel less satisfying.
