Democrats’ [internal polls](https://calmatters.org/newsletter/california-candidates-usc-debate/) show the two Republicans running for California governor leading the pack in this very blue state.

To hear it from Democrats and many reporters, that’s because the sprawling Democratic field has failed to consolidate in advance of the June 2 “jungle primary,” in which the top two vote-getters, irrespective of party, proceed to the general election.

In a March 3 open letter to the Democratic gubernatorial hopefuls, state party chairman Rusty Hicks asked those without “ [a viable path](https://cadem.org/open-letter-to-the-democratic-candidates-for-governor/) ” to step aside.

*The New Republic* [has warned](https://newrepublic.com/article/208323/california-governor-race-republicans-ahead-democrats) that, unless “the state’s Democratic honchos” take action, “they’ll hand the state a MAGA governor.”

Ron Filipowski, the editor in chief of the progressive blog *MeidasTouch*, recently hinted that outgoing Democratic governor Gavin Newsom should intervene.

“The CA governor’s race is an absolute shit show on the Democratic side and it requires some degree of sanity to take hold at some point to get it fixed,” [Filipowski posted on X](https://x.com/RonFilipkowski/status/2036756561041707089?s=20). “Leadership at some point from people not in the race wouldn’t be a bad thing. Time is running out fast.”

Democrats I’ve spoken with in the past week all said a variation of the same thing.

It was, a Democratic consultant in Los Angeles said, long past the point of “the great, hoped-for winnowing” of Democratic candidates.

But there’s another, more obvious consideration: California is a mess. The roads and bridges, the homelessness, the public schools (now [ranked No. 38](https://www.usnews.com/news/best-states/rankings/education/prek-12) in the nation), the exorbitant price of gas, the outsourcing of Hollywood, the flight of talent and capital, and of course, the state’s feudal real-estate market. (Last year, nearly one in five property transfers took place via [inheritance](https://www.instagram.com/reel/DWZNE7tidY1/) —a state record that’s double the national average.)

In California, there is no contest between left and right—only between left and left or, really, left and uber-left.

The point is: It’s not that surprising that California voters are less than eager to vote back into power the party that has dominated the state for the past quarter-century. [Since 1999](https://ballotpedia.org/Party_control_of_California_state_government), Democrats have controlled the governor’s mansion constantly except for a seven-year stretch (from 2004 to 2011, when sort-of Republican Arnold Schwarzenegger was governor) as well as both chambers of the state legislature. Since 2018, Democrats have enjoyed a supermajority in the legislature—meaning there is basically nothing they cannot do.

In California, there is no contest between left and right—only between left and left or, really, left and uber-left, which has made both parties dumber, crasser, less responsive to voters.

Recent legislation has phased out [gas-powered leaf blowers](https://legiscan.com/CA/text/AB1346/id/2436582), mandated that prisons house inmates according to their [self-identified gender](https://www.cdcr.ca.gov/prea/wp-content/uploads/sites/186/2023/03/Final-SB132-CDCR-Assessment-Report_ADA.pdf), and banned schools from notifying parents when their children come out as transgender (which the Supreme Court recently [struck down](https://www.nbcnews.com/politics/supreme-court/blocks-california-restrictions-schools-notifying-parents-transgender-rcna255636)). More recently, progressives in Sacramento, always in search of a windmill to slay, have launched a [war on microplastics](https://opc.ca.gov/2022/02/california-takes-decisive-action-to-reduce-microplastics-pollution-state-adopts-a-first-in-nation-approach-to-protecting-ocean-and-human-health/).

Democratic officeholders, including Newsom and Los Angeles mayor Karen Bass, often seem as if they prefer holding press conferences to lash out at President Donald Trump—who lost the state by more than 20 points in 2024. The kerfuffle around the University of Southern California’s planned gubernatorial debate was in keeping with that sort of performative politics. The three leading Democratic contenders—hedge-fund billionaire Tom Steyer, Representative Eric Swalwell, and former representative Katie Porter—all criticized the debate because it did not include any non-white candidates, but they could not identify anything wrong with the methodology used to select debate participants. At a time when the state is in dire need of a serious conversation about how to move forward, the candidates opted for racial pandering.

To be clear: California’s Republican Party has made it easy for Democrats. For decades, it’s been a joke.

Riverside County sheriff Chad Bianco recently cited unfounded accusations that the race had been rigged. (Eric Thayer/ *Los Angeles Times* via Getty Images)

Exhibit A: Riverside County sheriff Chad Bianco, one of the Republican gubernatorial hopefuls, who recently [seized](https://apnews.com/article/sheriff-chad-bianco-seized-ballots-riverside-1c136952f122e323c31d502aab67790c) 650,000 ballots cast in a 2025 special election, citing unfounded accusations that the race had been rigged.

Bianco also has acknowledged he once [belonged to](https://www.npr.org/2021/10/06/1043651361/oath-keepers-california-sheriff-chad-bianco-january-6-us-capitol) the far-right militia group known as the Oath Keepers.

Bianco is not all that different from Brian Dahle, the Republican former state senator who faced off against Newsom in 2022. Not from the coast, hard right, very much in keeping with the state Republican culture of the past three decades. (Dahle lost by nearly 20 points.)

To be fair to Republicans, Bianco’s Republican rival, Fox News personality Steve Hilton, does not conform to type. He’s half-British, half-American, Oxford-educated, and pro-Trump, which is to say, pro-growth. He’s running on an agenda that harks back to a California that many (most?) Californians miss. But he’s the wrong vehicle: a little too colorful, clever, with a long, winding, transcontinental career, and hard for voters to wrap their heads around. It’s easy to imagine any of the Democrats demolishing him.

Bianco’s Republican rival, Fox News personality Steve Hilton, is the wrong vehicle. (Leonard Ortiz/MediaNews Group/ *Orange County Register* via Getty Images)

The bottom line is that it’s exceedingly difficult to imagine California voting Bianco or Hilton into office.

But it’s also exceedingly difficult to understand why anyone would vote for any of the Democrats. It’s not just that the party has overseen California’s self-immolation. It’s that it is mostly owned by the public-sector unions that make it nearly impossible to do much, if anything, about California’s innumerable systemic challenges. The pro-growth abundance agenda imagined by Ezra Klein and Derek Thompson sounds marvelous, but it’s a fantasy in a state in which teachers unions make schools less competitive, and unionized engineers and electrical workers make high-speed rail unaffordable. All of which is to say that the only party that can win statewide in California right now is structurally inhibited (if not prohibited) from delivering California from its woes. (Nor is it enough to elect a billionaire like Steyer. Even if he were to spearhead transformative legislation dismantling California’s sclerotic bureaucracy, he’d have a nearly impossible time ramming that through the state legislature.)

Governor Newsom, who is expected to announce his campaign for president after this year’s midterm elections, no longer seems capable of even leading his own state party. He is, like so many of today’s Democrats, a simulacrum of a politician. Not really a leader as much as someone who pretends to be one. That may explain why Democrats are having trouble imposing much order on the gubernatorial field, to say nothing of running the country’s most populous state.

“There’s only the California Dream, attainable and aspirational,” Newsom [posted to X](https://x.com/gavinnewsom/status/1134928109701160961?s=46&t=pSjtqpXmZQnwmUwIssXLuQ) in June 2019, just a few months into his tenure as governor. Even then—before Covid, before so-called progressives had embraced the new woke orthodoxy—that sounded a tad delusional. “Around the world,” Newsom went on, “it’s a beacon light. Mark my words, California—we are the light at the end of the tunnel.”
