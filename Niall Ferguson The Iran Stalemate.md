Wars always take much less time to start than you think they will, and last much longer than you thought they could.

“In economics, things take longer to happen than you think they will,” the economist Rüdiger Dornbusch famously observed, “and then they happen faster than you thought they could.”

He had financial crises in mind. In history, and especially the history of war, it’s different. Wars take much less time to start than you think they will, and last much longer than you thought they could.

Looking back on the first few months of my writing about the full-scale Russian invasion of Ukraine in February 2022, I consistently underestimated how long the war would last. At first, like most Western intelligence agencies, I assumed Ukraine would be overrun. Then, when I realized the Ukrainians had won the Battle of Kyiv and halted the Russian advance, I expected there would be a ceasefire. It took me until the end of the year to use the word *[stalemate](https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2023-01-01/victory-over-russia-in-ukraine-may-bring-world-war-3-niall-ferguson)*. Four years, two months, and two weeks since it began, the war rages on. I know of no one who at the outset foresaw a war that will soon have lasted as long as World War I.

I begin to wonder if I have made the same mistake with the U.S. war against Iran. When Operation Epic Fury was launched on February 28, I was confident of one thing: that it would be a [short war](https://www.thefp.com/p/niall-ferguson-khamenei-may-be-gone). Well, here we are, nearly 10 weeks later.

Of course, you may be one of those cheerful optimists who thinks the war is already over—indeed, that it ended with the ceasefire announced on April 8. But a ceasefire is not a peace agreement. Consider the events of the past week.

On April 29, leaked American diplomatic cables revealed that the administration was exploring the possibility of a new international coalition to restore freedom of navigation in the Strait of Hormuz. On May 4, U.S. Central Command (CENTCOM) announced Project Freedom, a quasi escort mission designed to open the strait to outbound shipping. Later that day, Iran launched a drone and missile attack on the United Arab Emirates, the first since the ceasefire began. The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) also launched drones and missiles at U.S. ships. The U.S. said it sank six Iranian fast-attack boats as they swarmed commercial vessels.

The ceasefire was literally over. Wasn’t it?

In the early phase of Cold War I, “brinkmanship” was the way diplomacy was conducted by President Dwight Eisenhower. President Donald Trump prefers “blinkmanship.” On May 5, Trump [announced](https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/116524418935002706) that Project Freedom would be “paused for a short period of time,” making it one of the briefest military operations in history. Trump insisted he was acting “on the request of Pakistan and other Countries”—he didn’t mention that it was the Saudis who most vehemently objected—because “Great Progress has been made toward a Complete and Final Agreement with Representatives of Iran.” Less than an hour later, he [added](https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/116527444859592032): “If they don’t agree, the bombing starts, and it will be, sadly, at a much higher level and intensity than it was before.”

The [consensus view is](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Sp4wCkQShu8), as the administration claims, that the U.S. and Iran are indeed close to agreeing on a one-page, 14-point memorandum of understanding to end the war and set a framework for more detailed nuclear negotiations. Credit to Trump’s envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner for having gotten this far. But Woodrow Wilson had 14 points, too. Presented on January 8, 1918, they were supposed to be the basis for ending World War I. It lasted another 10 months.

---

And remember, the memorandum of understanding would simply start a 30-day period of negotiations on a detailed agreement to reopen the Strait of Hormuz, limit Iran’s nuclear program, and lift U.S. sanctions on Iran. Among the 14 points, according to leaks to *[Axios](https://www.axios.com/2026/05/06/iran-us-deal-one-page-memo)*, is an Iranian moratorium on nuclear enrichment. But it remains to be decided how long this would last. Five years? Twelve? Fifteen? Twenty?

That’s not all. The U.S. wants a provision whereby any Iranian violation on nuclear enrichment would prolong the moratorium. Iran would be able to enrich to the low level of 3.67 percent after the moratorium expires, but would commit never to seek a nuclear weapon, nor to operate underground nuclear facilities, and to accept enhanced inspections regime, including snap visits by United Nations inspectors. The U.S. negotiators also want Iran to agree to remove its highly enriched uranium from the country, ideally to the United States.

If the Iranians make these concessions, I shall be flabbergasted. (Jared and Steve, dinner will be on me at the Michelin-starred restaurant of your choice.) To quote *Axios*, citing two unnamed U.S. officials: “Many of the terms laid out in the memo would be contingent on a final agreement being reached, leaving the possibility of renewed war or an extended limbo in which the hot war has stopped but nothing is truly resolved.... If the negotiations collapse, U.S. forces would be able to restore the blockade or resume military action.”

Meanwhile, whatever happened to Operation Economic Fury? Fans of this series will recall that this was the plan by the U.S. Treasury to cause the collapse of the Iranian economy, a key part of which was the blockade imposed by CENTCOM on Iranian shipping through the strait. I have been assured by two experts in the past week that this is working. This is also the view of the financial journalist [Robin Brooks](https://robinjbrooks.substack.com/p/taking-stock-of-the-us-blockade), who has a good track record of smart calls on the economics of the Russia-Ukraine War.

But the problem with economic warfare is that it is even more slow-acting than actual war. Remember the sanctions imposed by the West on Russia in 2022 that were going to bring Vladimir Putin’s war machine to its knees? As the British historian [Nicholas Lambert](https://www.wsj.com/opinion/what-real-economic-warfare-looked-like-russia-ukraine-world-trade-great-britain-11647637729?mod=Searchresults&pos=3&page=1) pointed out at the time, they were “maybe a 3” on the Richter scale of sanctions. The reason is always the same: No state gets to impose pain on its adversary’s economy without some of the pain spilling over to its own economy. That was why the sanctions on Russian oil exports could never really be enforced. The Biden administration was too terrified of making an already bad inflation problem even worse. The Europeans, too, did not dare completely decouple from Russia.

Operation Economic Fury is certainly causing economic pain to Iran. But the closure of the Strait of Hormuz is an American-Iranian joint venture that is causing pain to everyone. As always in such cases, the question is who can last longer, which is another way of asking whose pain threshold is higher.

The most recent International Monetary Fund data from October 2025 projected that Iran would have $26.2 billion of gross official reserves this year. (That excludes around $100 billion of assets that are blocked by sanctions.) Estimates of the decline in net exports from a blockade of the strait vary but tend to center around $120 million per day (assuming the loss of $270 million of exports and $150 million of imports). This puts the timeline for Iran to exhaust its reserves at six to 14 months, depending on its access to external reserve assets held in escrow by partners such as China.

The Iranian currency is certainly in free fall, with the unofficial exchange rate approaching 2 million rial to the dollar. The latest [World Bank](https://thedocs.worldbank.org/en/doc/65cf93926fdb3ea23b72f277fc249a72-0500042021/related/mpo-irn.pdf) data from April 2026 put headline consumer price inflation at 62.2 percent and food inflation at 99 percent. Anecdotal evidence since the war began suggests that food prices in Tehran are now rising by 25 percent a day.

Yet Iran’s economy has at least four pressure valves that could postpone collapse for months.

1. Between a third and a half of Iran’s non-oil imports arrive via land or air and so are immune to the blockade.
2. Iran can sustain and expand these import routes, despite currency depreciation, by bartering oil, petrochemicals, and other manufactured goods for food and other essential imports.
3. Iran has spent the last five years mining Bitcoin and developing crypto-trading capabilities.
4. Approximately 30 percent of Iranian commodities imports are of Chinese origin, and China is almost certainly providing economic as well as military support to the regime.

Operation Economic Fury therefore depends on Iran’s oil storage reaching capacity, forcing it to shut in its oil wells, a procedure fraught with peril when the infrastructure is so antiquated. Iran currently produces roughly 3 million to 3.2 million barrels a day (mmbd) of crude oil, around 1.7 mmbd of which was exported before the war. Iran can refine roughly 2.4 mmbd of crude into diesel or gasoline and will likely maximize this production to offset the stop in seaborne crude exports. Of this refined product, roughly 2 mmbd will be consumed domestically while 150,000 barrels a day are smuggled over Iran’s land borders.

There is a chance of Iranian economic and regime collapse, but it doesn’t feel like the base case.

The remaining crude and refined products will flow to land and seaborne storage. Iran has around 50 million to 55 million barrels of onshore storage, substantially more than half of which is now full. In addition to on-land storage, Iran can load tankers that would sit idle in the Persian Gulf. It’s not easy to quantify Iran’s current seaborne storage capacity. However, during the first eight days of the blockade, 15 empty tankers entered the gulf heading toward Iran. Iran needs roughly one new empty tanker every two days to avoid overflows. In the early days of the U.S. blockade, Iran was also shipping 300,000 to 400,000 barrels a day of crude through Omani waters on small ships that are largely evading interception.

In short, there is a chance of Iranian economic and regime collapse, but it doesn’t feel like the base case.

Meanwhile, a massive supply shock is coming toward the whole world, including the U.S., via multiple supply chains. It’s not just oil and liquefied natural gas (LNG). It’s also fertilizer, sulfur, helium, and much else.

The [World Bank](https://www.worldbank.org/en/news/press-release/2026/04/28/commodity-markets-outlook-april-2026-press-release) projects that average commodity prices will rise by 16 percent this year. Average energy prices are forecast to increase by 24 percent. Average fertilizer prices will rise by more than 31 percent. Increased input costs will also push up prices of food commodities and metals. Average prices for both base metals and precious metals are set to break records. And these estimates assume that the closure of the strait ends this month, with shipping volumes “returning to near pre-war levels by October.”

The worst impacts will be felt in developing economies, as the [Kiel Institute](https://www.kielinstitut.de/fileadmin/Dateiverwaltung/IfW-Publications/fis-import/01b7c020-27e6-4096-8cc5-e037738d2058-KPB_206.pdf) ’s recent paper on the subject shows. But the United States is not immune. U.S. gasoline and distillates inventories are already running low. Consumers are bracing themselves for higher gas prices, as the New York Federal Reserve Bank Survey of Consumer Expectations shows, but they are underestimating the other goods that are about to get more expensive—such as everything Walmart sells.

I know, I know: The market is up, everything is awesome. With the NASDAQ now 14 percent above where it was on the eve of Epic Fury, it is clearly true that—as investor and hedge fund manager [Dan Loeb](https://x.com/DanielSLoeb1/status/2049818072270188896) succinctly put it on X a week ago—“AI > Oil Shock.”

Fine, so I asked Gemini to give me three scenarios for the U.S. economy:

1. Scenario 1: Strait reopened now (May 7).
2. Scenario 2: Strait reopened on July 4 (120 days)
3. Scenario 3: Strait reopened on September 1 (~180 days)

Here are the results:

“In Scenario 1, the primary damage is “baked in” but contained. The 60-day disruption has already caused a surge in fertilizer and energy costs that cannot be fully reversed for the 2026 harvest.... Fertilizer prices—specifically urea, which rose 47 percent in the last two months—will remain elevated through the summer.... U.S. imports of electronics and plastics would see temporary... delays of one to two months. Inflation (CPI): Expected to settle around 3.2 percent to 3.5 percent for 2026 (Allianz, 2026). Growth (GDP): U.S. GDP growth would see a modest revision down to 2.1 percent to 2.3 percent.

“In Scenario 2, a closure through Independence Day pushes the global supply chain past its “breaking point.” Most industrial consumers maintain only four to eight weeks of inventory, which would be entirely exhausted by mid-June. Japan and South Korea, which rely on the Gulf for 42 percent of their naphtha and over 80 percent of their LNG, would face systemic industrial shutdowns. A critical shortage of helium... would halt semiconductor fabrication in both Asia and the U.S., causing lead times for consumer electronics to double. Shortages of aluminum (Gulf is a major exporter) and specialty plastics (via naphtha) would trigger production halts at U.S. car plants. Inflation: Would likely spike to 4 percent-4.5 percent as “grocery supply emergencies” manifest in higher meat and dairy prices due to livestock feed costs. U.S. GDP growth would slow sharply, \[from\] 1 percent to 1.5 percent.

---

“Scenario 3, a six-month closure, represents a “worst-case scenario” resulting in global stagflation and a systemic collapse of the international fertilizer trade. The loss of sulfur (used for sulfuric acid to process phosphate) would cripple the global phosphate chain. Since the U.S. spring planting window is already compromised, a closure through September would destroy the winter wheat and 2027 early-season preparations. U.S. CPI could peak at 4.9 percent to 5.5 percent. U.S. consumers \[would\] face permanent price resets for plastics, medical supplies, and textiles the Fed would likely be forced into aggressive tightening (two to three additional hikes) despite the slowdown. The U.S. would likely enter a technical recession, with GDP growth falling toward 0 percent to 0.5 percent for the year.”

The markets may be up, but that doesn’t sound too great to me, and the polling is already terrible. Disapproval of Trump ([56.4 percent](https://www.realclearpolling.com/polls/approval/donald-trump/approval-rating)) is close to its all-time high. [Net approval](https://www.natesilver.net/p/trump-approval-ratings-nate-silver-bulletin) on inflation—the subject voters care most about—is down to -41.9 percent. On gas prices, approval is nearly off the (bottom of) the charts, as [David Shor](https://x.com/davidshor/status/2051782989894361406) has pointed out. And so the Senate, which Republicans previously assumed they would hold in the midterms, is now surely [in play](https://electionodds.live/). The president is so close to being a lame duck that the 2028 race for the Republican presidential nomination has [begun early](https://www.theatlantic.com/ideas/2026/04/maga-heir-rubio-vance-voters/686904/).

The militant left is on the march, with a shockingly high share ([one in four](https://yougov.com/en-us/articles/52960-charlie-kirk-americans-political-violence-poll)) of young, “very liberal” voters saying political violence can sometimes be justified. Jonathan Rinderknecht, 30, who is accused of intentionally starting the Pacific Palisades fire, killing 12 people and destroying thousands of homes, was inspired by slogans such as “free Luigi Mangione” and “lets take down all the billionaires.” As [he told](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/05/04/us/palisades-fire-suspect-jonathan-rinderknecht.html?smid=nytcore-ios-share) investigators, “We’re basically being enslaved by \[the rich\].”

The [manifesto](https://nypost.com/2026/04/26/us-news/read-whcd-gunman-cole-allens-full-anti-trump-manifesto/) of Cole Allen, the Caltech grad who allegedly tried to kill officials attending the White House Correspondents’ Dinner, revealed that he regards the president as “a pedophile, rapist, and traitor.” Quote:

> “Turning the other cheek is for when you yourself are oppressed. I’m not the person raped in a detention camp. I’m not the fisherman executed without trial. I’m not a schoolkid blown up or a child starved or a teenage girl abused by the many criminals in this administration.”

So the key question that needs to be answered is simple: Why on earth would the Iranians take the 14-point plan outlined by *Axios*? They have politics, too. On one side, according to Iran International, there is President Masoud Pezeshkian, Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi, and parliament speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf, who seem to favor negotiation with the United States. On the other is the IRGC, led by Ahmad Vahidi, who seems to favor more missile and drone attacks.

But why might these two factions not agree that it makes sense for Iran to string these negotiations out—at least until the United States reaches Scenario 2, if not Scenario 3?

Scenario 3 is, after all, just four months away.

It is true that many wars are short. Of all of the interstate conflicts between 1816 and 2003, 23 percent lasted less than two months, and 38 percent between two and six months. But more than a quarter lasted at least a year, and 5 percent more than five years. Once a war has lasted a year, it has a surprisingly high chance of lasting more than two years. As for peace negotiations, by my calculations it took 694 days to end World War I, 749 days to end the Korean War, 1,273 to end the Vietnam War, and 1,978 to secure peace between Israel and Egypt.

I repeat: Wars take much less time to start than you think they will, and last much longer than you thought they could.

I would love to be wrong. But then AI would be wrong, too. And that would be quite a hallucination.
