MIAMI—The Trump administration has secured an indictment against Raúl Castro, Cuba’s aging patriarch and former president, escalating its pressure campaign as it seeks to force economic and political concessions from the country’s Communist regime.

Prosecutors charged Castro with murder, conspiracy to kill U.S. nationals and destruction of aircraft, according to court documents, dealing a blow to a leader of the Cuban revolution who remains the [country’s most powerful figure](https://www.wsj.com/world/americas/trump-cuba-raul-castro-e52f25a4?mod=article_inline) despite his retirement from public life.

The charges relate to the Cuban military’s shootdown of civilian planes flown by the humanitarian group Brothers to the Rescue in 1996, when Castro was serving as defense minister. Three U.S. citizens and one permanent resident—all part of the Cuban exile community—were killed.

“The United States government has not forgotten these innocent men who were shot out of the sky,” said Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche, who received a standing ovation as he announced the indictment at Miami’s Freedom Tower in front of members of the local Cuban-American community, including the mother and father of one of the victims.

Cuban President Miguel Díaz-Canel wrote on social media on Wednesday, “This is a political maneuver, devoid of any legal foundation, aimed solely at padding the fabricated dossier they use to justify the folly of a military aggression againstCuba.”

The indictment, which court records show was returned by a Florida grand jury in April, marks a sharp escalation of the Trump administration’s efforts to [pressure the Cuban government](https://www.wsj.com/world/americas/cubans-are-anxious-desperate-and-hoping-for-change-as-the-u-s-confronts-havana-7b7774c4?mod=article_inline) to make changes as the impoverished island struggles to keep the lights on amid a U.S.-imposed oil blockade. It names a handful of other defendants, who were Cuban air force pilots at the time.

The Trump administration used criminal charges as a pretext to oust Venezuelan strongman Nicolás Maduro, whisking him and his wife out of the country after a January military strike. Some experts see a similar playbook here.

“The U.S. is signaling that it’s preparing to go beyond economic and political pressure,” said Brian Fonseca, a professor at Florida International University. “There’s a lot of similarities in terms of the types and sequencing of approaches that the U.S. is using in both Venezuela and Cuba.”

But unlike the Maduro case, which cast him and his inner circle as the heads of an active narco-trafficking conspiracy, the case against Castro is rooted in a decades-old act of military force against a civilian aircraft. Current and former officials also don’t expect Castro, who is almost 95, to be sent—or taken by force—to the U.S. to stand trial. Blanche on Wednesday still left open that possibility.

The legal escalation could also stiffen the spine of the Cuban government and armed forces to resist giving up concessions in the difficult negotiations taking place with the U.S., some analysts say. “An indictment could give the Cubans more reasons not to give in if giving in means surrendering Raúl, which they won’t do,” said Jorge Castañeda, a former Mexican foreign minister.

Former U.S. officials who have worked on legal cases against Cuban officials say the significance of the indictment is linked to Castro’s recognizable status as the younger of the two brothers who led the Cuban revolution, which still evokes the Cold War hostility between Washington and Havana.

The case is being brought by the U.S. attorney’s office in Miami, led by Jason Reding Quiñones, a Cuban-American prosecutor and son of a Cuban political refugee. Quiñones said Wednesday the indictment is “a step toward accountability.”

For Cuban-Americans, the case of the downed planes has long held emotional significance and rallied exiles who want accountability from the Castro regime. The decision to revive it 30 years later sends a message to Florida hard-liners that the administration remains committed to maximum pressure on the Communist regime.

On Monday, the Trump administration also sanctioned 11 senior Cuban military, political and intelligence figures under a new authority created by a May 1 executive order, freezing any of their U.S. assets and barring Americans from doing business with them.

Asked on Wednesday afternoon what’s next for Cuba, Trump said, “We’re going to see. it’s a failing nation—you see that, it’s falling apart. They have no oil.”

In the early 1990s, U.S. officials considered charging Castro and other high-ranking Cuban officials in a drug trafficking case but it never resulted in an indictment.

For decades, families of the victims and South Florida leaders have lobbied to bring charges against Castro over the plane incident, but his ascension to the presidency—a position he held from 2006 to 2018—complicated those efforts.

“Since 1996, I’ve been on Washington’s case, trying to obtain justice,” said Mario de la Peña, the father of the youngest victim. “They couldn’t bring an indictment because it was considered a national security issue. That was always the excuse. They said no prosecution could be brought against a head of state.”

The renewed federal effort to indict Castro began gaining steam last year, when Cuban Air Force pilot Luis Gonzalez-Pardo Rodriguez was captured on U.S. soil and indicted on a charge of immigration fraud. He was named as one of the defendants in the indictment unveiled Wednesday.

U.S. Rep. Mario Diaz-Balart (R., Fla.), who has spearheaded the effort to break the legal logjam, views the indictment as a harsh blow to the regime.

“Everybody knows that nothing happens in Cuba without Castro being involved,” Diaz-Balart said in an interview. “The entire power structure of Cuba and that regime is Raúl Castro and some of his relatives.”
