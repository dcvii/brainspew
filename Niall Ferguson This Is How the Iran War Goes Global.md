Niall Ferguson: This Is How the Iran War Goes Global

The war with Iran is even more futuristic than the war in Ukraine. Not only are both sides using drones, but many U.S. and Israeli precision strikes are on targets selected by artificial intelligence. This may go down in history as the first AI war.

Yet 17 days into the conflict, the U.S.-Israeli campaign has run into a very old problem: control of a strategic waterway.

Despite being entirely outgunned, despite having lost control of its own airspace, the Islamic Republic retains sufficient firepower to attack shippers in the Strait of Hormuz, deterring them from moving through it. The resulting contraction of the world’s oil and gas supply has caused energy prices to skyrocket and threatens the stability of the global economy, not to mention the political future of Donald Trump’s administration. On Friday, the Pentagon ordered Marines and warships to the Gulf to open the strait.

Nowhere is the law of unintended consequences more binding than when crucial commercial choke points become casualties of war. Although it is fashionable nowadays to focus on [financial choke points](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/726149/chokepoints-by-edward-fishman/) that can be exploited by sanctions, the oldest choke points are natural geographical features such as the Strait of Hormuz, the Black Sea straits, and the Strait of Malacca, as well as man-made trade corridors such as the Suez and Panama Canals. These waterways often become critical in times of war. And their history helps answer the question now facing the Trump administration: Now what?

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!5G6G!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F37ac3a66-2c42-40d0-b98b-4cffd0955527_1320x30.png)

Before oil there was grain. In the preindustrial world, the vital sources of energy were agricultural. As trade barriers fell in the second half of the 19th century, industrialized economies—led by the United Kingdom—became increasingly reliant on imported food. In 1914, on the eve of World War I, Britain imported roughly 80 percent of its wheat, 40 percent of its meat, and 100 percent of its sugar.

Economies with fertile agricultural land scaled production to meet this demand, and the Black Sea Straits (then known as the Dardanelles) became one of the key choke points of the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Grain exported from Black Sea ports accounted for one-third of global supply on the eve of World War I. Up to 15 percent of the UK’s annual wheat imports transited the Dardanelles.

---

Control of the Dardanelles was a key issue in the 19th-century balance of power and played a fatal role in World War I, as Nicholas Lambert showed in his brilliant book, *[The War Lords](https://urldefense.com/v3/__https://academic.oup.com/book/39699__;!!CxwJSw!LzUeGvyW29CMh3Ls4EsuXKhObivA9x9b-jtWbTmAvfIvLmsl3zDigJ0DRlnqWUvNjxZ2l7Nt$)*.

In case of a war with the German Empire, British military planners had long intended to use their superior naval and financial power to blockade Germany, restricting its seaborne imports. Yet Herbert Asquith’s Liberal government was unprepared for the global financial crisis that erupted in the summer of 1914. The crisis led major stock markets to close to avert mass insolvencies. The original strategy of economic warfare was set aside, along with laissez-faire liberal principles.

On August 4, 1914, fearful of food shortages, high prices, and social unrest, the cabinet approved large (about 80 percent) state subsidies for war-risk insurance on British merchant shipping. Four days later, food exports were banned. The government created a state sugar monopoly and subsidized meat importers.

Wheat was more difficult. War disruption compounded droughts in Canada and Australia, a poor Argentine harvest, unusual cold in the United States, and an export ban by the British government of India. Rumors circled of a similar ban in the U.S. In December 1914, the British government covertly bought futures contracts for 0.5 million tons of wheat to influence prices. (Sound familiar? The U.S. Treasury appears to have considered the same thing with oil futures in the past two weeks.) It did not work. By May 1915, the price of an imperial quarter of British wheat was nearly 62 shillings—95 percent above the 1904–1914 average, and perilously close to the 70-shilling level seen as a trigger for strikes and food riots.

Distracted by events in France and Belgium, Asquith’s cabinet underestimated the risks of war with the Ottoman Empire. Winston Churchill, then First Lord of the Admiralty, escalated tensions with the Ottomans by seizing two British-built battleships intended for their navy, and reviving Anglo-Greek plans for an attack on the Gallipoli Peninsula on the western side of the Dardanelles. On September 27, 1914, the Ottomans closed the Dardanelles. Turkey shelled Russian naval installations in the Black Sea one month later. Without consulting the cabinet, Churchill ordered the Royal Navy to “commence hostilities at once against Turkey.”

Policymakers struggle to foresee second- and third-order consequences of their decisions.

Churchill was oblivious to the economic consequences of his order. War with the Ottomans not only further strained the global grain market—it also exposed the financial underbelly of Britain’s wartime ally, Russia. Russia derived 85 percent of its foreign earnings from agricultural and mineral exports, mostly via the Black Sea. These were essential for servicing the czarist regime’s rising external debt. With the gold standard suspended and the ruble falling sharply, Russia scrambled for loans in Paris and London, raising fears of a broader allied fiscal crisis.

In late 1914 and early 1915, Britain’s War Council considered many strategic gambits. Gradually, however, an assault on the Black Sea Straits to force Turkey to reopen the choke point emerged as the solution to all the government’s problems. Militarily, it offered a shortcut around the stalemate in France and Belgium, relief for Russia, and possibly Greece’s entry into the war on the British side. Economically, it promised to ease chronic wheat shortages, lower the price of bread, and reduce the risk of Russia defaulting on its foreign debts or concluding a separate peace with Germany and Austria.

Churchill was always the most enthusiastic proponent of forcing open the Dardanelles. But between January 4 and February 9, 1915, the majority of members of the War Council discarded the counterarguments. As support grew, so did ambition. Asquith and his colleagues began to believe that success could not only reopen the economy but also bring regime change in Constantinople (ah, yes!) and preempt a Russian takeover of the Ottoman capital—something Foreign Secretary Sir Edward Grey had promised Russia in the event of victory.

The events of 1914–1915 illustrate six lessons about such contingencies, all of which apply to the problem of the Strait of Hormuz today:

1. Policymakers struggle to foresee second- and third-order consequences of their decisions. Enemy actions are hard to predict, even with good intelligence, and the complexity of the global economic system means even modest perturbations can produce nonlinear “butterfly” effects.
2. The structure of decision-making at the strategic and operational levels often creates competing arguments about what actions should be taken, reflecting not only the personalities of the principals but also their departmental priorities.
3. In representative governments, military expertise is often overridden by domestic-political calculations.
4. Decision-makers also cannot ignore the different priorities of allied governments or the interests of neutral governments, for fear of turning them into adversaries.
5. In crises that have adverse economic effects, governments are almost always tempted to intervene in markets. These interventions tend to have unintended consequences because even talented politicians and bureaucrats do not fully understand the mechanisms of, say, insurance and futures markets.
6. In a crisis, the tempo of decision-making rises, exacerbating the inherent difficulty of acting under uncertainty.

As those who have seen [the movie](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0082432/?ref_=fn_t_1) *[Gallipoli](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JFH4Cz1RFNQ)* will know, the Dardanelles campaign was one of the greatest military disasters in the history of the British Empire. The timeline from War Council approval (February 9) to execution (February 19) was tight. The initial plan was for a long-range, “low-risk, navy-only” engagement. By early March, however, the operation faced difficulties due to a propellant shortage and worn-out guns. The Turkish forts guarding the Dardanelles proved harder to degrade than expected. Churchill ordered a closer-range assault, even though this brought the British armada within range of Turkish guns, and attempts to sweep mines had failed. On March 18, the attack ended in ignominy as Ottoman guns and mines sank three of the fleet’s 16 capital ships and incapacitated another three.

---

London hastily pivoted to a full-scale amphibious assault on Gallipoli. (The equivalent today is Trump’s [sending for the Marines](https://www.wsj.com/livecoverage/us-israel-iran-war-news-2026/card/pentagon-sends-marine-expeditionary-unit-to-middle-east-WeoODg0XIIe31W3np2aI).) This failed even more disastrously. The attack launched on April 25 by British, French, Australian, and New Zealand troops had to be halted on May 9. Around 30,000 men were killed or wounded, including more than 60 percent of the French contingent. After a second offensive came to grief in August, the government admitted defeat. The cabinet agreed to the evacuation on December 20. It was completed by January 9, 1916.

Meanwhile, ironically, the food crisis had solved itself. By September 1915, the price of wheat had fallen to just 43 shillings a quarter, thanks to a bumper North American harvest. It also became clear that, even if the expedition had succeeded, Russia’s disrupted railway system meant that its wheat would not have poured through the straits. The political consequences were also unintended. In May 1915, Asquith was forced to form a new coalition government with the Conservatives. Churchill, the scapegoat, was replaced at the Admiralty.

By November 1916, wheat prices had risen again, this time above the 70-shilling threshold, where they remained until August 1921. And yet the social unrest the government had feared from 1914 to 1915 did not emerge. Nor did British action keep Russia in the war, as the 1917 Bolshevik Revolution led to both a separate peace with Germany and a default on the Czarist regime’s debts. Ultimately, Britain won the war in 1918, thanks to British advances on the Western Front, the U.S. entry into the war, and the deterioration of German military morale. But victory—achieved despite, rather than because of, British strategic decision-making—was staggeringly costly in blood and treasure.

Contrast that with U.S. operations in the Strait of Hormuz during the 1980s.

Over the 20th century, oil became a more important economic input than wheat. The United States pioneered the exploitation of crude oil as a natural resource. By the 1970s, however, U.S. oil consumption increasingly relied on foreign rather than American wells. This created a new choke point in the Middle East. When the United States backed Israel during the 1973 Yom Kippur War, Saudi Arabia followed through on threats to restrict oil exports to the U.S. and other countries supporting Israel.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!V3iC!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6156b323-3b5d-4856-92b0-521e2bb746f8_1024x683.jpeg)

Secretary of State Henry Kissinger had underestimated these threats: “Had I understood the mechanism of oil pricing better,” he later wrote, “I would have realized that Saudi production cutbacks were more dangerous than the embargo since they critically affected world supply and therefore provided the precondition for the impoverishing price rise.” On an inflation-adjusted basis, crude oil prices rose 52 percent over three months and 381 percent over six. In 1979, the Iranian Revolution caused a comparable reduction in global oil supply (7 percent), though the price increases were less severe (an increase of 13 percent in three months, and 62 percent in six).

Against this backdrop, Ronald Reagan’s administration understandably sought to avoid another major oil disruption during the Iran-Iraq War from 1980 to 1988. As Iran and Iraq began attacking tankers, daily flows through the Strait of Hormuz fell significantly. However, the “oil glut” of the early 1980s and the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries (OPEC) price war of 1985–1986 kept prices low. The U.S. avoided direct intervention, instead helping regional allies defend themselves.

Then, in February 1986, Iran seized the Al-Faw peninsula, and Kuwaiti fears of annexation spiked. In late 1986 and early 1987, Kuwait drew both the U.S. and the Soviet Union into the conflict by requesting protection for its tankers in the Persian Gulf. In March 1987, the U.S. obliged.

Three overlapping domestic and strategic calculations spurred this decision. First, the administration was scrambling to recover its credibility with Gulf and European allies after the [Iran-Contra Affair](https://millercenter.org/issues-policy/foreign-policy/iran-contra-affair), which was exposed in November 1986 and caused Reagan’s domestic approval rating to [fall from](https://news.gallup.com/vault/198164/gallup-vault-reaction-iran-contra-years-ago.aspx) 63 percent to 47 percent in just one month.

---

Second, Iran was testing long-range, Chinese-manufactured Silkworm missiles that significantly threatened shipping and infrastructure. Third, and most importantly, the U.S. sought to counter a growing Soviet presence in the Gulf and the risk of their lasting foothold there eight years after the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan. Just days after learning that Moscow had agreed to reflag five of 11 Kuwaiti tankers, the Reagan administration approved Kuwait’s request and insisted Kuwait reduce the Soviets’ contributions.

Operation Earnest Will escorted its first convoy in July 1987. The maiden journey was overkill—three U.S. warships escorted two tankers, while nearby cruisers and a carrier in the Arabian Sea provided surveillance and additional air support. However, in their haste to marginalize the Soviets, U.S. officials did not go through the full interagency decision-making process. Two key issues were overlooked: the lack of access to local military bases and defense against underwater mine warfare.

The latter proved the worse oversight. The reflagged Kuwaiti supertanker SS *Bridgeton* struck a mine on the first convoy and suffered damage.Iran touted this as a propaganda victory and intensified its mine-laying operations. While highly effective at sinking ships, these mine-laying efforts antagonized previously neutral states, leading U.S., European, and Soviet naval buildup in the Persian Gulf to accelerate and expand minesweeping and escort operations. By April 1988, the U.S. mission widened from escorting reflagged Kuwaiti tankers to covering “friendly, innocent neutral vessels flying a nonbelligerent flag.” By the war’s end in July 1988, the U.S. Navy had escorted over 100 convoys involving over 200 merchant vessels.

The U.S. intervention in the Tanker War was thus as successful as the British intervention at Gallipoli was disastrous.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!5G6G!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F37ac3a66-2c42-40d0-b98b-4cffd0955527_1320x30.png)

The Strait of Hormuz remains the world’s most critical physical choke point for energy markets. Before the current Iran war, energy exports via the Strait of Hormuz accounted for roughly 20 percent of the global oil market and 20 percent of the global liquefied natural gas (LNG) market (or about 3 percent of the global gas market). Gulf exports through the Strait also represent significant shares of the aluminum, urea, and helium markets, among other industrial and agricultural commodities. Therefore, although developed economies are less oil-intensive than they were 50 years ago, the Iranian closure of the Strait still poses a grave threat to global economic stability.

But there are three key differences between the current situation and the 1980s:

1. The United States is a combatant—and is widely perceived to have initiated the war in Iran.
2. Military technology has improved, most notably in the production of cheap but effective drones, increasing Iran’s ability to disrupt shipping traffic. (Iran may now also have the technology to lay mines with drones.)
3. The positions of the world’s two superpowers have changed. In the 1980s, the U.S. was the world’s largest importer of oil, and the USSR was a petrostate hit heavily by the 1985–86 oil price crash. Today, it is China that is the world’s largest oil importer, while the U.S. has become a marginal net exporter of petroleum products.

In other respects, the situation today resembles that of Britain in 1914:

1. The Trump administration began its war confident, seeing Iran as economically and militarily weakened and hoping that decapitation could cause the Islamic Republic to collapse.
2. The administration underestimated the economic costs of its action and the resilience of its adversary.
3. It must now reconcile its desire to avoid a humiliating climbdown, the U.S. electorate’s desire not to find daily life even less affordable, and U.S. allies’ desire for the war to end swiftly and normal service to be resumed in the Strait of Hormuz.

Under these pressures, decision-makers in Washington have been torn between persisting with their aerial strategy in hope of a better outcome, and adopting the seemingly riskier option of deploying “boots on the ground” to accelerate the destruction of Iran’s drone and mine-laying capabilities. This explains the decision to send an amphibious-ready group and an attached expeditionary unit of Marines from Japan to the Persian Gulf.

The danger is that, as in 1915, decision-makers do not consider the downside risks of pursuing military solutions to economic and diplomatic problems.

The biggest risk to the U.S. today is not necessarily the immediately obvious one that the Strait of Hormuz is closed. It is that the country’s most heavily armed adversaries—the People’s Republic of China and the Russian Federation—will see ways to exploit the latest American entanglement in the Middle East.

The danger is that, as in 1915, decision-makers do not consider the downside risks of pursuing military solutions to economic and diplomatic problems.

The Soviet Union, on the brink of imperial overstretch and internal collapse, failed to do that in the 1980s. But Imperial Germany took advantage of Britain’s failure at Gallipoli. The Dardanelles disaster distracted British decision-makers from the crucial Western Front and may have contributed indirectly to the calamitous failure of the 1916 Somme offensive. In the end, German miscalculation, not British strategy, ultimately proved decisive in World War I. Had Berlin handled the U.S. more adroitly, the story might have been different.

As [I wrote](https://www.thefp.com/p/niall-ferguson-could-this-be-the) earlier this month, the scale of the danger now facing the Trump administration is best understood through the framework of the three-body problem. Since World War II, the U.S. has been drawn to three bodies of war: the North Atlantic, the Persian Gulf, and the Indo-Pacific. In practice, because resources are finite, the U.S. military cannot be actively engaged in all three theaters at once. These days, it can handle only one war at a time.

The war against Iran is not, despite the claims of Tucker Carlson and others, a regional undertaking for Israel’s benefit. It does not help other American allies and friends, instead imposing heavy costs on the energy importers of Europe, India, Japan, Korea, and Taiwan. Nor does it bring peace closer in Ukraine. Ukraine will have even more difficulty getting missile interceptors now that the Gulf States crave them. Meanwhile, higher oil prices and the easing of sanctions will benefit Russia’s budget, which had been suffering from the low price of Urals oil.

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth [said earlier this month](https://www.washingtonpost.com/national-security/2026/03/06/russia-iran-intelligence-us-targets/) that China is “not really a factor here.” This is disingenuous. Almost all of Iran’s exported oil went to China last year, about 15 percent of Chinese seaborne crude. Nearly two-fifths of oil passing through the Strait of Hormuz went to China in the first quarter of 2025. Meanwhile, Iran’s aggressive missile-building program was a Chinese-enabled project.

There are some in the administration who see the attack on Iran, coming so soon after the decapitation of the Chavista regime in Venezuela, as a power play ahead of the summit between Presidents Trump and Xi Jinping in Beijing due to happen at the end of this month. That is a delusion. While China does import Iranian oil, it is far less reliant on Gulf oil than America’s Asian allies because it has built up its strategic petroleum reserves to around 1.2 billion barrels.

Which brings us to the next great global choke point: the Taiwan Strait.

More than 90 percent of the world’s most advanced semiconductors, and 99 percent of the chips used for cutting-edge AI training, are manufactured in Taiwan. Taiwan imports 97 percent of its energy supply in the form of oil, LNG, and coal. This makes the Taiwan choke point far more important in relative terms than the Strait of Hormuz has ever been for global energy markets.

Should China revoke Taiwan’s trade autonomy, it would immediately become apparent that the biggest choke point in the world is not the Strait of Hormuz; it is the Taiwan Strait.

The rational strategy for Xi is not an amphibious invasion or blockade of Taiwan. It is to wait for Taiwan’s January 2028 election in the hope of a Chinese Nationalist Party victory and a decisive shift toward Beijing’s goal of “One Country, Two Systems.” However, there is a growing tail risk that he is tempted to act more boldly, taking advantage of yet another American imbroglio in the Middle East.

Rather than risk a kinetic confrontation in the Middle East, Xi’s best option is to deploy Chinese coast guard vessels and assert Beijing’s right to collect customs duties and regulate the flow of goods to Taiwan. He can present this as consistent with international law, satisfying many countries and companies—especially those with significant business interests in the People’s Republic of China.

True, Taiwan is recognized as a “ [separate customs territory](https://www.wto.org/english/thewto_e/countries_e/chinese_taipei_e.htm) ” by the World Trade Organization, defined in the 1994 Marrakesh Agreement as meaning Taiwan has “full autonomy in the conduct of its external commercial relations.” In 2001, China agreed that Taiwan and Hong Kong would enjoy this status. However, as former State Department counselor Philip Zelikow has argued, China could simply revoke it for Taiwan, just as Trump revoked it for Hong Kong in 2020. China could also then apply its export controls to goods leaving Taiwan, again citing American precedents for doing so.

Should China make such a “gray zone” move, it would immediately become apparent that the biggest choke point in the world is not the Strait of Hormuz; it is the Taiwan Strait.

The likely U.S. reaction to such a scenario is beyond the scope of this essay. But as Eyck Freymann argues in his [forthcoming book](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/defending-taiwan-9780197823842), China has accumulated military capabilities that on paper put it close to parity with the U.S. in the Indo-Pacific. The U.S. would likely need to make a large-scale effort to keep goods flowing in and out of Taiwan by a sea and air route not controllable by Beijing.

Unlike Russia’s invasion of Ukraine four years ago, the U.S.-Israeli air war on Iran ought to be short. For domestic-political and economic reasons, the Trump administration needs it to be. But uncertainty lingers over the Iranian regime’s resilience—the depth and breadth of its fanaticism—and its capacity to keep the Strait of Hormuz closed and otherwise to reduce Persian Gulf oil exports.

Under these circumstances, as in 2022—when Russia’s invasion of Ukraine led to [temporary closure](https://ig.ft.com/black-sea/) of the Black Sea Straits—the world’s importers of fossil fuels and fertilizer must scramble for scarcer and dearer resources. The longer the war lasts, the greater the domestic pressure on Trump; the heavier the costs for U.S. allies in Asia and Europe; the more money for Russia; and the greater the temptation for China.

The administration is now trying to salvage a plan that seems to be, like Churchill’s in 1915, disintegrating on contact with the enemy. It may still be salvageable. The Islamic Republic may yet oblige Trump by expiring. I do not rule that out. However, if Trump’s advisers do not keep their wits about them, they may overlook the fact that they have created a huge strategic opportunity for China and Russia—especially if they act in concert. Washington may miss, amid the mood music of a superpower summit, the preparations for a *coup de main* that would upend the international economy and the world’s geopolitical order.

The Strait of Hormuz is blocked. For how long?

The Strait of Taiwan is open. For how long?
