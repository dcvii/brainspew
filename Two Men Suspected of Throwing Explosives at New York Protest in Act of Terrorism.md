“All praise is due to Allah lord of all worlds!” the defendant wrote in a statement after his arrest, according to the complaint. “I pledge my allegiance to the Islamic State. Die in your rage yu kuffar!” Kuffar is an Arabic term referring to an infidel or nonbeliever.

The two men were charged with five criminal counts, including attempted provision of material and resources to a designated foreign terrorist group. Islamic State is a designated terrorist organization.

Mamdani said at a press conference earlier Monday that Balat and Kayumi had traveled from Pennsylvania and were among a group of counterprotesters who showed up at the anti-Islamic demonstration.

“Anyone who comes to New York City to bring violence to our street will be held accountable in accordance with the law,” Mamdani said.

Video showed Balat lobbing an explosive device toward the protesters, according to the complaint. The device emitted smoke but didn’t detonate. Balat was then handed a second device from Kayumi, the complaint said. Video shows Balat lighting the device before dropping it near officers, the complaint said. He and Kayumi were arrested shortly thereafter.

Kayumi also stated after his arrest that he had watched Islamic State propaganda on his phone and that his actions at the protest were inspired by the group.

Investigators asked Balat after the arrest if he’d been trying for an attack similar to the Boston Marathon bombing. “No, even bigger. It was only three deaths,” he responded.

The devices were the size of a Mason jar, according to the complaint. Each had a fuse, and nuts and bolts were attached to the exterior of the device with duct tape. One of the devices contained the substance triacetone triperoxide, a highly volatile homemade explosive.

“They were improvised explosive devices that could have caused serious injury or death,” NYPD Commissioner Jessica Tisch said at the press conference.

Officials said on Sunday that investigators located a vehicle tied to the suspects. They temporarily evacuated the surrounding area and recovered a third suspicious device and a handwritten note listing materials needed to build an explosive. The third device didn’t contain explosive material.

Saturday’s protest was organized by far-right activists who called the event “Stop the Islamic Takeover of New York City.” The event took place near Gracie Mansion, the mayor’s home on Manhattan’s Upper East Side.

Mamdani, who is Muslim, called the event “a vile protest rooted in white supremacy.”

“While I found this protest appalling, I will not waiver in my belief that it should be allowed to happen,” he added. “Ours is a free society where the right to peaceful protest is sacred.”

Two men were charged in federal court Monday with attempting to carry out an Islamic State-inspired terrorist attack at an anti-Islamic protest near New York City Mayor Zohran Mamdani’s residence.

The defendants, Emir Balat and Ibrahim Kayumi, professed their allegiance to the militant group after being arrested at the scene of Saturday’s chaotic protest for throwing two improvised explosive devices, according to a criminal complaint. Balat told investigators that they had hoped to carry out an attack that was bigger than the Boston Marathon bombing in 2013, the complaint said.
