### I don’t understand how we’re having this argument again

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!U2kx!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fde3d8801-f7de-4475-afef-a8c9977807b4_1024x683.jpeg)

If the “controversy” over Joe Rogan endorsing Bernie Sanders hadn’t actually happened, it would sound like something a right-wing internet troll had fabricated. It was that idiotic.

This was back in 2020, when, as you recall, professional progressives were hard at work at their nationwide Make As Many Americans As Possible Hate Us initiative — an undeniable success. On a then-recent episode of his podcast, Rogan had expressed enthusiasm about Sanders. “I think I’ll probably vote for Bernie,” he said, [as quoted by CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2020/01/24/politics/bernie-sanders-joe-rogan-endorsement/index.html). “Him as a human being, when I was hanging out with him, I believe in him, I like him, I like him a lot.”

Rogan, then and now one of the most successful podcasters in the country, not only has a huge audience, but a [huge audience of the very group Democrats have struggled with in recent cycles: young men](https://pro.morningconsult.com/trend-setters/joe-rogan-fans-demographics-political-messaging-persuasion). Naturally, then, the Sanders campaign [tweeted out the clip of Rogan endorsing their man](https://x.com/BernieSanders/status/1220445820505546755).

This, as hard as it might be to believe, sparked a “controversy.” Rogan, you see, had opinions that did not line up 100% with progressive orthodoxy, including the most important, vital-to-defend sort of orthodoxy: orthodoxy that had just been established. CNN dutifully reported, for example, that Rogan had

> questioned — using offensive language — whether a transgender MMA fighter should be able to compete against other women. \[This is a reference to [Fallon Fox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fallon_Fox), a male-to-female MMA fighter.\]
> 
> “If you want to be a woman in the bedroom and, you know, you want to play house and all of that other sh-t and you feel like you have, your body is really a woman’s body trapped inside a man’s frame and so you got a operation, that’s all good in the hood,” Rogan said. “But you can’t fight chicks.”

The Human Rights Campaign put out a [statement denouncing Sanders for accepting the endorsement](https://www.hrc.org/news/hrc-president-on-joe-rogan-endorsement-and-sanders-campaign-response), noting that “In 2019, 25 transgender and gender non-confirming \[*sic*\] people were killed because of the type of transphobia that Rogan stokes.” One relevant fact about HRC here is that the organization vigilantly searches out stories of trans people who have been murdered, [retcons these individual tragedies](https://jessesingal.substack.com/p/its-immoral-to-tell-american-trans) as having been caused by transphobia [even when there’s no evidence that was the case](https://www.city-journal.org/article/human-rights-campaign-transgender-murders-white-supremacy), and then uses these misrepresented killings as a fundraising device. (I wish I were making this up.) “Given Rogan’s comments, it is disappointing that the Sanders campaign has accepted and promoted the endorsement,” tutted moral exemplar Alphonso David, who was president of HRC [until whoops](https://www.npr.org/2021/09/07/1034760697/human-rights-campaign-fired-alphonso-david-cuomo-harassment). “The Sanders campaign must reconsider this endorsement and the decision to publicize the views of someone who has consistently attacked and dehumanized marginalized people.”

If you take what HRC and a bunch of shrieking online weirdos — some of them, then as now, in positions of institutional progressive power — were saying at face value, it would mean that candidates like Sanders are allowed to accept endorsements only from figures whose views are already quite far to the left. This would, of course, make it very difficult to win any sort of national election.

On his podcast, Bill Simmons sometimes dips into his “Conspiracy Bill” persona, which grants him license to float wildly silly conspiracy theories about, like, the NBA draft lottery. Allow me here to briefly 1) don my Conspiracy Jesse hat, which is actually a velvet fedora; 2) note that if you’re living a comfortably upper-middle-class life working at a D.C. organization like HRC, some tiny part of you *must* know that your professional life actually benefits from Democratic losses; 3) ask you not to get offended because this is a simple corollary to the obvious fact that, for example, anti-abortion groups were undeniably hurt by *Dobbs*; and 4) take off the fedora and emphasize that I don’t *really* think this.

Anyway: We’re seeing a different version of this same controversy now, and it concerns a figure who is not seen as too far to the right but as too far to the left: Hasan Piker. The background here is slightly complicated but it comes down to the fact that, [as per](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/03/28/hasan-piker-democrats-midterms-2028-00849453) *[Politico](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/03/28/hasan-piker-democrats-midterms-2028-00849453)*, the centrist group Third Way is putting pressure on Democrats to not associate with Piker after *Politico* revealed that Piker “will stump in Michigan with Senate candidate Abdul El-Sayed next month.” Other big lefty Democrats, like Bernie Sanders and Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez, have previously done events with Piker, but now the pressure appears to be ramping up:

> “Piker is close to — but not over — the Nick Fuentes line, where going on his show itself is indefensible,” Third Way co-founder Matt Bennett, who’s been sounding the alarm about Democrats’ affiliation with Piker, told POLITICO. Bennett added that Democrats “take on all of his baggage if they don’t overtly reject” him, which he said is “dangerous because it empowers the right and gives them an incredibly powerful tool to hit Democrats with that’s very bad.”

This strikes me as extremely naive. The idea that if you appear on a stream with someone, or do an event with them, you “take on all \[their\] baggage” is the sort of thing you could only come to believe if you are an overpaid political analyst. I just don’t think it accurately captures the relationship between influencers and their audiences. A lot of people tune in to folks like Piker and his ilk not because they agree with everything they say, but because of the parasocial feeling of hanging out with someone whose general vibe or worldview they like. This is difficult for those of us who are over a certain age or who don’t consume a lot of streaming (or both) to understand, but I strongly believe it’s true. This is just an extension of the commonly voiced but rarely internalized-among-political-elites finding that the “average American” doesn’t have the same sorts of meticulously labeled ideological commitments as college-educated, politically activated types. Again, it’s more vibes: *We’re* the weird ones for giving ourselves specific political labels and for holding bundles of beliefs that don’t actually “go” together for any coherent reason.

As the Rogan/Sanders incident exemplified, there was a brief, frenzied attempt to gatekeep people away from freewheeling, potentially offensive podcasts and streamers. To say this attempt “failed” would be a profound understatement. It’s positively quaint that in 2020, people thought *Rogan* was a major problem for our political discourse. To even repeat this idea feels like going to [Old Sturbridge Village](https://www.osv.org/) and watching some lady churn butter while dressed in a bonnet. Rogan is a cherubic sweetheart compared to the current media landscape — Candace goddamn *Owens* is [now one of the most successful independent podcasters](https://www.mediamatters.org/candace-owens/candace-owens-crashing-out-online-shes-never-been-more-popular). Nick *Fuentes* has carved out a space in the national conversation.

The question of where to draw the line is a fuzzy one. Going on Fuentes or Owens is, in my mind, disqualifying for a mainstream political candidate. Hasan Piker is not. For better or worse, he represents mainstream media consumption for a certain type of disillusioned young man. The tiger is out of the cage, he is eviscerating the gatekeepers, and there is nothing anyone can do about it. The idea that the Democratic Party and its consultants — honestly some of the most loathed political figures in the country, statistically speaking — can chide people out of listening to Piker or punish political figures for interacting with him is... well, it’s completely delusional. Of course if you do appear with Piker, it’s only fair that people will ask you which of his statements you agree or disagree with, and you should be prepared to answer. That’s fair game, but it’s different from saying that if you appear with him you are automatically saddled with all of his baggage.

And don’t get it twisted: Piker has said some *heinous* stuff. What I’ve seen of him, I intensely dislike. It’s a terrible sign that as longform writing is becoming obsolete, newspapers are shutting down, and journalism is becoming mostly a relic of the past, *this* is what has filled the void. Piker’s particular anti-intellectual perniciousness gets to a broader conversation about streamer culture that’s a whole other story. But in short: He “broadcasts seven days a week, eight to ten hours a day,” [as a](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2025/03/24/the-battle-for-the-bros) *[New Yorker](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2025/03/24/the-battle-for-the-bros)* [profile put it](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2025/03/24/the-battle-for-the-bros). Many other streamers work similarly grueling hours. Remember when everyone was worried about the corrosive impact of 24-hour cable news networks? How having to fill all that airtime would undoubtedly make things dumber and more hysterical and superficial? The Streamer Problem is that times a million: The average 20-year-old is much, much more likely to get their political news and commentary from figures like Piker (or, worse, Fuentes or Owens), whose very job descriptions involve the slow roasting of their own brains. Can you imagine sitting in that chair for that many hours a day, your every observation met with braying approval or deafening anger from your audience, all visible in your chat? I wouldn’t last six hours.

Obviously, figures like Piker have a form of genuine talent and charisma — if they didn’t, they wouldn’t have become as rich and famous as they have. But you can’t survive as a streamer who produces this much content without stoking a lot of drama and outrage. If you are in that seat, you will be pulled toward the dumb and the profane and the hysterical. And generally speaking, the most successful streamers and podcasters tend to land somewhere in the partisan/hyperpartisan/conspiracy theorist part of the spectrum. Piker, unsurprisingly, has a lengthy dossier of offensive remarks; it’s hard to know where to begin, really. He said “America deserved 9/11” ([later apologizing](https://www.thewrap.com/tyt-hasan-piker-deserve-sep-eleventh/)). [He said](https://x.com/TheFP/status/1917304752775254029?s=20) “it doesn’t matter if rapes happened on October 7,” that “any kind of fucking Zionist tendency should be treated in the same way as being a fucking rabid neo-Nazi,” that “You shouldn’t even let someone be the fucking local dog catcher... if they have exhibited any sort of positive feelings about the state of Israel.” [He said, of Dan Crenshaw](https://kotaku.com/twitch-suspends-popular-leftist-streamer-after-controve-1837518859), “Didn’t he go to war and, like, literally lose his eye because some Mujahideen, a brave fucking soldier, fucked his eyehole with their dick?”

For some reason it was his caping for the Chinese Communist Party that *really* got to me. I mean, just [read this](https://www.scmp.com/news/us/diplomacy/article/3343845/hasan-piker-talks-about-his-controversial-china-trip-and-thinking-chinese-thoughts) *[South China Morning Post](https://www.scmp.com/news/us/diplomacy/article/3343845/hasan-piker-talks-about-his-controversial-china-trip-and-thinking-chinese-thoughts)* [article about his visit there last year](https://www.scmp.com/news/us/diplomacy/article/3343845/hasan-piker-talks-about-his-controversial-china-trip-and-thinking-chinese-thoughts) ([archived here](https://archive.is/lsGC0) if you hit the paywall).

Among other highlights:

> That same willingness to defy ideological expectations shapes Piker’s views on China’s political system, where he expressed unabashed admiration for socialism with Chinese characteristics.
> 
> “I don’t measure socialism by ideological purity,” he said. “I measure it by efficacy. Socialism, at its root, is about improving the material conditions of the working class, and China has been able to do that very effectively.”
> 
> While praising outcomes, Piker emphasized that China’s system was neither flawless nor beyond criticism.
> 
> “Are there issues? Absolutely,” he said. “Do I have disagreements with how the state operates? Yes. But I’m not audacious enough to say I know better than the million-plus people inside the Communist Party trying to govern a country of 1.4 billion.”
> 
> That stance places Piker at odds with much of the American left, which remains deeply wary of praising any aspect of China’s governance. But he insists that recognizing reality is not the same as endorsing a system wholesale.

He’s suddenly so circumspect! Who is he to judge, really, versus the million enlightened minds of the CCP? Piker is a guy who had [this to say about a South Vietnamese Donald Trump supporter](https://x.com/Wilson__Valdez/status/2038258564192477186) who said “As Vietnamese refugee\[s\], we endured suffering under the Communist regime”:

> Okay, Fuck you, dude. I mean seriously, fuck you, old lady. Shut the fuck up you stupid fucking idiotic old lady with your stupid fucking gamer headset. Who has fucked you harder, America or fucking Ho Chi Minh? Suck my dick, old lady. Goddamn, dude, yo, fuck this refugee, seriously — fuck this South Vietnamese motherfuckin’ whatever, like, Christian supremacist psychotic fuckin’ refugee living in America now, and able to talk that shit — why don’t you go back and live back in South Vietnam in the same conditions if that’s your perspective?

This is par for the course for Piker. Like many of the most popular lefty internet personalities, his persona is based in large part on swiftly and irrevocably judging individuals for having the incorrect beliefs. Like not only leftists but all hyperpartisans, he grants grace and forgiveness to those on “his” side, but withholds it from his perceived ideological enemies. If you have *any* positive feelings for Israel — maybe because, like, it’s where your family fled after being expelled from Iraq in 1951 — you shouldn’t even be allowed to be a fucking dog catcher.

In this case, it’s “Suck my dick, old lady” to... well, to the old lady. But when it comes to the CCP, which is by any measure one of the most powerful and oppressive institutions on the planet? He’s all polite deference.

Piker is a hack. It’s no wonder he’s wildly successful — he appeals primarily to very young, very disillusioned kids, mostly male, who are new to politics, whose media literacy is nonexistent, and who are looking to rebel. That’s how he can get away with a stunt like the China trip: If you’re very young and very new to media in general, *They want you to hate China, but you shouldn’t!* is a very exciting and transgressive take. (If you are older and familiar with mainstream media, you will know that while Chinese human-rights violations get plenty of coverage, so too does Chinese economic development and the country’s impressive ability to swiftly build infrastructure, albeit “impressive” in a sense that has a lot to do with the country’s totalitarian style of government. You will not find a lot of local environmental groups filing lawsuits to prevent development over there!)

Over at *The Bulwark*, Tim Miller has caught flak for [taking an anti–shunning Piker](https://www.mediaite.com/media/news/shilling-for-the-illiberal-left-tim-miller-gets-eviscerated-after-defending-hasan-piker/) stance. His colleague Mona Charen, on the other hand, is [pro-shunning](https://www.thebulwark.com/p/why-democrats-should-shun-hasan-pikeantisemtisim-israel-gaza-midterms-iran-defund-police). After quoting some of his greatest hits, she writes:

> Frankly, that should be enough right there to exclude that person from polite society. Whatever you think of Crenshaw, that kind of inhuman rhetoric is beyond the pale. *How quaint*, I hear you say. *There is no such thing as polite society anymore. That’s how people talk.* But stay with me for a minute here. Some of us knew in 2015, and frankly long before that if we lived in or near New York, that Trump was a sociopath. We knew because he said things that were cruel, crude, and demeaning to other people. If the Republican party and the country had drawn a line against him then, for mocking a handicapped reporter, making light of rape, disparaging the heroism of John McCain, or vowing to commit war crimes, we would have spared ourselves the current debacle.

What I find confusing about this is that the Republican Party in fact tried *very hard* to keep Trump away from the nomination! It was a whole thing! Trump just trounced all his enemies and took over the party. It wasn’t a matter of not *trying*. But primary voters were drawn to Trump and couldn’t care less about elite chiding. That’s the through line in all of this: Elite chiding doesn’t *work*. If anything, political environments in which voters are made to feel judged for their beliefs or media preferences are more likely to drive people to Trump!

We do need to draw the line somewhere. Again, I’m not suggesting a situation in which politicians adopt *such* an open-minded approach to media that they prattle on about how cool it is that media has become such a big tent that innovative new voices like Nick Fuentes have built audiences. Rather, I’m suggesting that they [go to war with the potential voters they have](https://www.goodreads.com/quotes/1215538-you-go-to-war-with-the-army-you-have-not), not the potential voters they want. It’s unfortunate that so many young people are drawn to Pikeresque figures, but that’s the world we’re living in, and as we’ve seen over and over, any attempt to police millions of young people’s media preferences is likely to cause more harm than good.
