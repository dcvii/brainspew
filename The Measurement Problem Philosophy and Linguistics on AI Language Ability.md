> #### Part 1 The Philosophy of Language: Ellen
> 
> A recent *Quanta* [article](https://www.quantamagazine.org/in-a-first-ai-models-analyze-language-as-well-as-a-human-expert-20251031/) claims that while *we are not there yet*, LLMs may soon surpass the linguistic abilities of humans. It also claims that if this happens, it may be that humans are ‘‘less unique’’ than ‘‘we previously thought’’.
> 
> This kind of framing —that is, of doing ‘comparative work’ on the linguistic capabilities of LLMs and humans— fits well with mainstream framing of AI issues. In much of AI discourse, there is a familiar idea, very resonant in this article, that humans and AI’s are somehow in competition with one another. A kind of ‘survival of the fittest’ mentality that I don’t think we should buy into.
> 
> Consider as an example of the alleged competition we are in with AI, the subtitle of this article. It reads ‘‘if language is what makes us human, what does it mean now that large language models have gained “metalinguistic” abilities?’’
> 
> The claim is not entirely clear. It doesn’t make a declarative point so much as it gestures at something non-specific—perhaps pulling at heart strings or our egos along the way. To the extent that we can glean a declarative statement from the sentence, it seems to be that humans will lose their ‘special status’ (as, what, exactly?) if AI can surpass, or even match, the linguistic capabilities of humans.
> 
> This is a fashionable statement of late, but even taking the claim at face value one wonders what exactly the claim means. Is it a *moral* claim? Are we saying that, supposing LLMs have language, means that they deserve to be treated the way that humans should be treated? Surely not. Otherwise, we are perhaps already too late in attributing retroactive status of subject to these technologies.
> 
> Is it an *epistemic* claim? Are we saying that LLMs, supposing they have language, somehow have the same *capabilities* as humans do? This seems to be getting closer to what people who utter this kind of statement have in mind. But it is surely not capturing the whole spirit of the idea, since, many people who believe LLMs are surpassing human linguistic capabilities would still deny that LLMs have the same cognitive capabilities as human beings across a broad range (e.g., emotional, intellectual, linguistic).
> 
> On reflection, it seems like the subtitle of this article isn’t really sure of the claim it’s trying to make. It is more so representative of an underlying angst —or general concern masked as rigorous intellectual inquiry— that as humans we will somehow become ‘less than’, if technologies begin to produce the same outputs as us. But even thinking of human beings as producing *outputs* is to choose to describe us as we describe LLM’s.
> 
> The irony is that it is *us* who are creating an artificial hierarchy on which humans and LLMs stand, and then, insisting that our once ‘superior’ positioning is being threatened in the age of AI technology.
> 
> When we look more closely at the research being cited to make this angst laden claim, it doesn’t really bear itself out. The article draws on the research of a particular paper, *[Large Linguistic Models: Investigating LLMs’ Metalinguistic Abilities](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/11022724),*
> 
> The term meta-linguistic abilities drew me in, because I wasn’t sure what it meant. Is there such a thing as ‘meta-physical’ or ‘meta-chemical’ abilities, and would an AI that possesses them be more impressive to the researchers?
> 
> Meta-linguistic abilities are defined as ‘‘abilities not only to use language, but also to explicitly reason about it’’. *Metalinguistic* skills, so defined, are then distinguished by the researchers from *behavioral tests,* such as asking a model to tell us whether a certain sentence is ungrammatical.
> 
> The claim in other words, is that there is some important distinction —important enough to study—between linguistic behavior and reasoning about language.
> 
> To test the LLMs linguistics abilities, the researchers had LLMs perform certain linguistic analysis tasks (for example, parsing English language sentences into syntactic trees, or recognizing ambiguous sentence structures). They argue based on the performance of various LLMs at a variety of these tasks, that LLMs are showing an increasing ability to perform not only ‘behaviorally’, as in predicting the next likely token to generate coherent text, but also ‘meta-linguistically’.
> 
> The *Quanta* article takes this one step further, claiming this research suggests humans may be ‘outperformed’ linguistically by LLMs sooner than we think.

#### Language and Metalinguistic Ability

> It’s worth mentioning – as Rebecca will so illustrate – that all of this angst requires us to *de-contextualise* the true complexity of language and its pragmatics. But even supposing we grant this to the research, we can still ask: what would it mean for AI to ‘outperform’ a human being linguistically? Human beings are born with language (i.e., the genetic ability to develop language(s) effortlessly and seamlessly, and without explicit instruction for their environment).
> 
> No research even bothers to test whether this is true of LLMs because we know that it’s not. They require mountains of linguistic data to offer credible linguistic-like outputs. So, putting aside the bias towards *dismissing* the wonders of language acquisition in humans and *emphasizing* the linguistic feats of LLM’s, the alleged ‘advancement’ of LLMs in this domain hinges, according to this research, on the mysterious distinction between metalinguistic abilities and mere behavior. One can’t help but wonder what the distinction really is, or if it exists.
> 
> Moreover, while the article does distinguish between metalinguistic abilities and behavior, it does *not* distinguish between ‘language’, ‘meta-linguistic abilities’ or ‘linguistic abilities’. But if we are to believe the sound scientific research coming out of linguistics for decades, and view language as a computational system of the human mind/brain, this equivocation deserves further scrutiny.
> 
> Of course, viewing language as a biological system of the human mind/brain is not universally accepted. Of significance however, is that the authors draw on this tradition (invoking syntactic structures, hierarchical phrase structure, and its ilk) to test whether LLMs have ‘metalinguistic abilities’.
> 
> In doing so, they indirectly commit to a conception of language that peculiarly undermines their claims on LLM linguistic capacities.

#### Does the Linguist use ‘Meta-Linguistic’ Abilities to Study Language?

> There is a difference between language and what the authors call meta-linguistic abilities. Language is a knowledge-system of the human brain that gets activated at birth and develops, like any other system, over some generally specifiable range of time. But this system is obviously distinct from the ability to ‘reason about language’, which is what these authors consider meta-linguistic abilities.
> 
> This puts the human being who *has language* in a very different epistemological position to the adult who is *researching* language, that is, the adult attempting to discoverthe nature of the latent linguistic system that we intuitively know.
> 
> There is the ability to have and use language, which all humans have, and the ability to systematically study that system, the way we study any other system of the human brain (the visual system, and on).
> 
> An example of a ‘meta-linguistic’ capacity from the paper includes the phrase ‘Eliza wanted her cast out’. This sentence is ambiguous between two interpretations. It could mean either that Eliza wanted to get her cast (that was on her arm) removed, or it could mean that Eliza wanted someone to be cast out of a particular social group.
> 
> As human beings, we can instinctively look for and find these two interpretations of the sentence. A separate question is how we explain the science behind our ability to intuit these alternative interpretations of what the sentence means.
> 
> In the first case —that is, in intuiting the different interpretations — we are using language. In the second case, we are *doing science*, that is, studying language in systematically rigorous ways. This ability is not ‘meta-linguistic’ anymore than the ability to explain motion is ‘meta-physics’ or explaining genomes is ‘meta-biological’. It’s just… plain old science. The study of syntax is science.

#### Here’s why this matters

> The authors analyze the sentence ‘Eliza wanted her cast out’ as follows:
> 
> **Interpretation1:** ‘her cast’ functions as a noun phrase (that is, a medical cast she has on). So, the sentence means Eliza wanted her cast off, as her arm is now better
> 
> **Interpretation2:** ‘her cast’ functions as a verb phrase (where ‘cast out’ means ‘to expel’). So, the sentence means Eliza wanted someone expelled from a group
> 
> If the study of language is a science like any other, then these explanations of how we interpret the sentence ‘Eliza wanted her case out’ serve as *hypotheses* of how human language parses sentences. In turn, this implies a sharp distinction between what these researchers equivocate on, namely, between ‘language’ and ‘metalinguistic abilities’
> 
> When a human being can recognize two separate interpretations of the sentence ‘Eiza wanted her cast out’, this fact is *the data* on which the scientist/linguist is experimenting, not the tool that they are using to study the data.
> 
> (As linguists will tell you, we do not get very far with language if we merely use our intuitions about language to study it, in much the same way physicists don’t get very far if they use intuitions about the motions of objects to study motion)
> 
> Language as a biological system generates the two alternative interpretations. The science of language demands that this requires explaining. But science is not ‘meta-linguistics’. It is ordinary rational inquiry, and that is what is used to actually try to understand and study language as a computational system in serious scientific inquiry.

#### Why This Isn’t Mere Taxonomy

> The paper claiming LLMs have metalinguistic abilities never makes clear if it understands the difference between language and the study of language, and that’s part of the problem. Equivocating on this point means the paper can ambiguously conclude that LLMs have ‘metalinguistic abilities’, without it being clear what that means, and *with* it sounding novel, exciting, and consequential.
> 
> Does the claim mean that LLMs have language in the same way that a human being has language? Or, does it mean that LLMs are simply capable of doing syntactic trees the same way they are capable of coding?
> 
> The first is the stronger claim. It would be claiming that LLMs have the same linguistic system as human beings. If *this* is the claim, there is evidence to suggest it is false and certainly no evidence from this paper to suggest it is true.
> 
> If it’s the second claim, then the claim is a lot less about language, and a lot more about the increasingly sophisticated engineering techniques LLMs have acquired. But this is hardly a controversial or interesting claim. Undoubtedly, LLMs have become quite sophisticated at doing an array of tasks.
> 
> That they can generate very basic syntactic trees is no different than their abilities to code, write an essay, give you advice on a certain decision, or whatever.
> 
> On either reading, there is no reason to think LLMs have ‘surpassed’ the abilities of humans in anything language related: on the first reading, because LLMs being able to produce syntactic structures represents a highly superficial level of similarity to what has been discovered about human languages; on the second reading, because LLMs generating syntactic trees should be understood as a simple task that an LLM can ‘do’, in the same way it can code.
> 
> The real issue, then, is not whether LLMs are surpassing humans linguistically, but whether we are even asking the right questions when we study these systems. When researchers blur the line between having language and studying language, and when journalists transform that blurring into existential provocation, we end up with a discourse that is a lot more about our *anxieties* and a lot less about science. Even if LLMs can parse sentences, generate syntactic trees, and flag ambiguities (the paper suggests they can do so relatively well, but not very well), we should call that what it is: impressive engineering.
> 
> But impressive engineering is not language in any meaningful sense, in much the same way that an AI performing calculus is not a meaningful indication that it understands physics. Viewed this way, the hierarchy that is supposedly being threatened, which we should reject as existing in the first place, is not threatened at all. Perhaps the more honest and productive question is not ‘are humans becoming less unique?’ but rather ‘what exactly are we building, what can it do, and how can these technologies help, rather than hinder, living in a better world’?

> #### Part 2 The Pragmatic Turn: Rebecca’s Response
> 
> Ellen’s critique names something crucial: the research confuses having language with reasoning about language. But the confusion runs deeper than the paper’s framing. It lives in the comparison itself.
> 
> For me, I want to look at what actually gets tested. Syntactic tasks—parsing, recognising ambiguity, drawing trees. These sit at the formal end of linguistics. Rule-governed, pattern-derivable, expressible in notation. They have been written down thousands of times in linguistics textbooks and academic papers. LLMs encounter this knowledge explicitly in training. When they succeed at these tasks, they have learned to replicate what linguists do. This is what the paper measures.
> 
> Here is the problem with the comparison from a linguistics lens. Most native speakers—fluent, competent language users—cannot perform these tasks. They cannot draw syntax trees. They cannot articulate phonological rules. They cannot explain grammatical structure. If you tested native speakers on the metalinguistic tasks this paper uses, most would fail. The research measures explicit reasoning about language structures. Most humans have never needed and do not possess this capacity. The comparison is between LLMs and linguists not between LLMs and humans.
> 
> And the research tests only what can be formalised—syntax, phonology, the level where rules can be stated explicitly. What it cannot test is the rest.
> 
> Pragmatics: what language does in context, how meaning gets negotiated between speakers, how understanding requires knowing the people and the situation.
> 
> Discourse: how extended language operates as social work, how authority gets established and contested across an exchange.
> 
> Sociolinguistics: how language varies with social position and power.
> 
> These aren’t the margins of language, they are the centre of what linguistics is. This is where language stops being a formal system and becomes a social one. The paper’s methodology cannot reach it.
> 
> The gap Ellen identifies philosophically—between having language and studying language—is demonstrable empirically here. What the paper can measure is what linguists do. What it cannot measure is what speakers do. And what speakers do when they use language is linguistic.
> 
> **What the Paper Doesn’t Test**
> 
> Identifying syntactic ambiguity, recognising linguistic recursion, representing syntactic movement, identifying phonological rules from novel datasets all sit at the formal, structural end of the linguistic spectrum—syntax and phonology. Rule-governed, pattern-derivable, expressible in trees and formal notation.
> 
> What is absent from the study is the rest of linguistics.
> 
> No pragmatics—the study of what language actually does in context, how meaning is negotiated between speakers, how implication and inference work. No register analysis—how speakers shift between styles depending on social role, institutional context, and audience, and what those shifts communicate. No discourse analysis—how extended language operates as a social activity, how authority is established, contested, and maintained across an exchange. No sociolinguistics—how language varies according to social position, community membership, and power.
> 
> These are the levels of linguistic analysis where language stops being a formal system and becomes a social one. And they are entirely outside the study’s methodology.
> 
> The consequence is significant for what the paper can claim, or what others will claim it can do. A model that correctly identifies two parse trees for “Eliza wanted her cast out” has demonstrated something about its handling of structural ambiguity. It has demonstrated nothing about its capacity to read the kind of ambiguity that produces real consequences in human interaction—where the same syntactically ordinary sentence means something entirely different depending on who is speaking to whom, in what institutional context, with what history between them. Structural ambiguity sits in the grammar. Pragmatic ambiguity sits in the situation, and the situation is nowhere in the model.
> 
> **What Happens When You Test the Higher Rungs**
> 
> In 2024, a team of researchers analysed 886 mansplaining stories—documented accounts of a specific conversational dynamic in which a speaker assigns themselves the role of epistemic authority regardless of who actually holds the relevant knowledge. They asked large language models, including ChatGPT, to evaluate these accounts.
> 
> ChatGPT failed to identify any discrimination in 63.8% of cases.<sup>1</sup>
> 
> The mechanism was there. The language was there. The model could not see it.
> 
> Mansplaining does not announce itself in the grammar. It operates through epistemic role assignment—through which speaker is positioned as the one who knows, and which is positioned as the one who receives—and that assignment is carried by discourse markers, register shifts, repair refusals, and concessive clause structures. These are linguistic features. They are not formal ones. They require a reader capable of tracking social positioning across an exchange, holding in mind the asymmetry between what the speakers know and what roles the language has assigned them.
> 
> The syntax tree of a mansplaining interaction is identical to the syntax tree of a functional one. The difference is entirely pragmatic, which means it is entirely invisible to the analytic framework the paper uses.
> 
> This is the gap. The tasks in the research paper have correct answers that can be verified against formal linguistic theory. Pragmatic interpretation does not work that way. Reading epistemic role structure requires knowing something about the speakers, the social context, and the expectations that frame the exchange—knowledge that X-bar notation cannot capture and that the study’s methodology cannot access.
> 
> The 63.8% failure rate is what you find when you test language at the level where formal competence runs out.
