### Understanding the Impact of Family Planning Policies on Sibling Structures in China

China's family planning policies, particularly the one-child policy (1979–2015), profoundly shaped family sizes and sibling relationships. The policy restricted most urban families to one child and allowed rural families two (often if the first was a girl). Combined with a strong cultural preference for sons, this led to smaller families, a high proportion of only children, and skewed gender dynamics in multi-child households. The two-child policy (2016–2021) and three-child policy (2021 onward) have increased average family sizes slightly, but the legacy of the one-child era persists, especially among adults born before 2010.

Direct statistics on the exact percentage of Chinese citizens with an **older sister** are scarce, as national surveys (e.g., from the National Bureau of Statistics or censuses) typically track total siblings, family size, or sex ratios rather than specific birth-order-gender combinations. However, demographic studies and policy analyses allow for an informed estimate. Below, I break down the key factors and arrive at a reasoned percentage.

#### Key Factors Influencing the Percentage

1. **High Proportion of Only Children**: 
    
    - Approximately 40–50% of Chinese adults (especially those aged 30+) grew up as only children due to the one-child policy. These individuals have no siblings at all, so zero chance of an older sister.
    - For younger cohorts (born post-2010), this drops to ~20–30%, but they represent a smaller share of the total population.
2. **Sex Ratio Imbalance and Son Preference**:
    
    - China's sex ratio at birth skewed to ~118 boys per 100 girls during peak policy enforcement (1980s–2000s), driven by sex-selective abortions. This means families with multiple children were more likely to have boys, especially as second (or later) children.
    - In rural areas (where ~40% of the population lives and two-child allowances were common), families often stopped after a boy, making "older sister" configurations more common in two-child families (girl first, boy second).
3. **Sibling Structure Patterns from Studies**:
    
    - A 2012 BMJ Open study on rural new mothers (representative sample of ~3,673 families) found that among two-child families:
        - ~25–30% had an older girl (i.e., first child female, second male or female), meaning the younger child has an older sister.
        - This aligns with son preference: Families with a first girl were ~1.5–2x more likely to have a second child than those with a first boy.
    - Urban areas had stricter one-child enforcement, so multi-child families were rarer (~10–15% of households), and older-sister patterns followed similar gender biases but on a smaller scale.
    - Overall, ~15–20% of Chinese adults report having at least one sister (older or younger), per health and kinship surveys (e.g., China Health and Nutrition Survey). Of these, roughly half are older sisters, adjusted for birth-order effects.
4. **Population Distribution**:
    
    - ~50% of citizens are in urban areas (mostly one-child compliant).
    - ~50% are in rural/minority areas (more two-child families).
    - Weighting these, the share of people with siblings is ~50–60%, but only a subset have an older sister specifically.
