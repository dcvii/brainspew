In just six weeks, the Iran War has shattered a system of global trade that has enriched people and nations for more than a century: the freedom to sail the open seas.

The Strait of Hormuz long functioned as an artery for the world’s maritime economy. But that 30-mile-wide waterway is now a monument to a new global disorder. As some 20,000 sailors effectively held hostage at sea digested President Trump’s cease-fire announcement this week—contingent on the complete opening of the strait—Iranian officials stressed they would determine which ships could leave and at what price.

The “ [Tehran toll booth](https://www.wsj.com/livecoverage/iran-war-2026-trump-deadline-latest-news/card/strait-of-hormuz-has-a-tehran-toll-and-this-truce-doesn-t-change-that-PUgURyIpChMDC5NQQ1vu?mod=article_inline) ” was taking effect, as the U.S. Navy watched on, an admission that, at least here and now in the world’s oil corridor, America no longer rules the waves.

Captains, owners and managers of the more than 700 vessels stuck near Iran, carrying tens of billions of dollars in cargo, were messaging one another to try to make sense of Tehran’s shifting rules. After days of drones and missiles flying overhead, Iran’s navy broadcast a radio message clarifying their position: “If any vessel tries to transit without permission, \[it\] will be destroyed.”

The Strait of Hormuz, sailors said, risks becoming a graveyard for a trading system so integral to the modern economy that most consumers, accustomed to cheap imports and three-day shipping, take it for granted. The price stands to be shouldered by consumers across the world, in inflation, scrambled delivery schedules and the snarls of a new arrangement in which Tehran can choose which countries access Middle Eastern oil.

If Iran continues to charge tankers for safe passage, the added cost will hardwire a higher price for a gallon of gasoline, economists said. Or its Revolutionary Guard Corps could choke the flow entirely, wreaking havoc on energy markets. Either way, shipowners, their insurers and crew remain wary of sailing back into a once-bustling strait that could spring like a trap on the slightest misunderstanding between an aggrieved Iranian regime and an American president who threatened to wipe out its entire civilization in a single night.

Whatever happens next, the precedent of a toll booth in open waters will reverberate across a world order the U.S. helped build. America’s allies worry other players could try to replicate Iran’s example, like empires of the 17th century, when China’s Qing dynasty, the Ottomans and Portuguese taxed passing vessels. Trump has floated his own wish for an American toll on the Persian Gulf, and his expenditure of naval power in the Middle East has given Beijing and its navy—the world’s largest—freer rein to expand control over the South China Sea.

The ripple effects extend to the U.S. dollar, whose standing as the unquestioned currency of seaborne trade helps America keep taxes low and deficits high. Iran, sailors noted, is collecting tolls in Chinese yuan or cryptocurrency.

“Things won’t be like they were before,” said Capt. Ali Kanafani of Syria, who rotated his phone on a video call to scan the horizon, where he could see more than 30 ships stranded until Iran authorizes their departure. A Libyan captain on a nearby ship was calling to ask if he understood the new rules governing a waterway that transports around one-fifth of the world’s oil and liquefied natural gas. “Everyone is confused,” Kanafani said.

Even three days into this week’s cease-fire, Iran was only clearing a trickle of ships a day—just two on Friday—based on Tehran’s geopolitical friends list and their preparedness to pay tolls that can reach $2 million.

Vessels were changing flags to countries friendlier to Iran, some crew said, or turning themselves into “zombie ships,” taking on the identity of a vessel that has been scrapped or decommissioned—violating United Nations sea rules encoded over decades into international law. Other crews were begging ports to let them disembark from vessels that have run out of food and fresh vegetables, after what amounts to 41 days as prisoners.

Some sailors were passing the time making TikTok videos, or catching fish and crabs with small nets. Capt. Zefanus Waruwu was ordering his nine fellow Indonesian crew to sing, jog and do push-ups on deck, “to keep motivation up,” he said. Trapped and alone, others have contemplated suicide, with captains and union reps serving as counselors.

“Free navigation and smooth trade are only possible if seafarers can do their job safely,” said Sotiris Raptis, secretary general of European Shipowners-ECSA, a trade association.

Over coming days, negotiations in Pakistan may help bring relief to the sailors stranded off Iran, awaiting their captivity’s end. What isn’t on the horizon is a return to maritime normalcy, said seafarers, shipowners, naval analysts and historians. Prices for the bunker fuel used to power ships and war insurance have spiked; schedules are in chaos, crews have quit, and vessels rerouted elsewhere. Like a traffic jam, even a small blockage in a single sea lane can snarl backward long after the wreckage clears.

And when it does, shipowners will still have to consider the risks of fiendish new challenges that the U.S. Navy has been unable to resolve—like cheap drones that can swarm past warships designed to shoot down planes and missiles. The U.S., once the guarantor of open seas, has shown it can acquiesce to their closure, and America’s European allies, which once ruled the seas, have rebuffed Trump’s demand that their navies reopen a strait that his own military left closed.

“The concept of the blue highway is going away,” said Salvatore R. Mercogliano, a former naval officer and associate professor of history at Campbell University in North Carolina. “We’ve created a system on the ocean geared for volume and velocity—you can move lots of goods quickly and what we’re seeing is, it’s like hitting a brick wall at 100 miles an hour.”

“We won’t see a return to the normalcy we had prior to this no matter what,” he said.

### Freedom as strength

The system we now take for granted emerged from Gilded Age capitalism, when international trade boomed, and from the writings of Alfred Thayer Mahan, who deemed commercial freedom of the seas as central to America’s prosperity and sea power. A portrait of the 19th-century founder of American naval strategy hangs in the U.S. Naval War College, and Mahan’s views continue to influence the Pentagon.

American thinking evolved after World War I to advocate free navigation for all countries, an idea that only came into widespread practice when the U.S. Navy became the global maritime police force after World War II.

At that time, most manufactured goods moved by land, and the U.S. economy was driven by small manufacturers serving local buyers. An explosion of maritime traffic in the decades after powered the rise of mammoth conglomerates buying inputs from far-flung suppliers and chasing whichever consumer offered the biggest, quickest profit.

Even as America’s share of seaborne commerce fell after the Cold War, U.S. leaders believed that Americans and their allies benefited from such open commerce among friendly countries. Global trade boomed and the U.S. economy led the growth, even as U.S. shipbuilding atrophied and the Navy dropped from 7,000 ships at the close of World War II to around 300 today.

After the Iran-Iraq war of the 1980s sank civilian vessels in the Strait of Hormuz, U.S. naval officials modeled which assets would be needed to escort ships. They concluded that the Navy would only be able to move a handful of civilian ships each day, a fraction of the regular traffic.

In effect, the free flow of goods on the open seas hinged on global adherence to rules the U.S. helped write, but lacked the capacity to enforce.

### Breakdown

Now, that system is being tested in a half-dozen waterways, comprising a vast portion of global trade. In the Baltic Sea, Russia is defying international sanctions by transporting oil and gas on a “shadow fleet” of ships that often fail to meet global safety requirements. Ships plying the Black Sea face Russian missile strikes on Ukrainian ports, endangering the world’s grain supply. Traffic through the Red Sea hasn’t recovered since 2024, when the Houthis sank the [Rubymar](https://www.wsj.com/world/middle-east/rubymar-red-sea-suez-houthi-missile-3448799e?mod=article_inline), the first civilian ship lost in war since the Iran-Iraq war, a move that handed the Yemeni rebels broad influence over which ships can pass through the Suez Canal.

Iran’s control over Hormuz sets a precedent for China, which claims sovereignty over the South China Sea, a passageway for more than one-fourth of global trade.

“If the world accepts paying tolls for the Strait of Hormuz, then how do we handle the claim China has made that the entire South China Sea is Chinese territorial waters?” said retired U.S. Navy Vice Admiral John “Fozzie” Miller. “If they control the South China Sea, they essentially control the global economy.”

China says it is acting inside its territorial waters. Its neighbors, which reject Beijing’s arguments, once counted on solid support from Washington.

But these days, America has been less vocal in opposing Beijing’s [latest construction of an artificial island](https://www.wsj.com/world/china/china-south-china-sea-military-base-3ebf3dc2?mod=article_inline) in the South China Sea than during past projects, all of which are widely seen as potential threats to shipping in the region, as well as to Taiwan. The U.S. has shifted military assets from East Asia to the Middle East—giving China greater leeway, but without freeing Hormuz.

“Like neighborhood gangsters, Iran and China each seek to impose a protection racket on civilian vessels plying the waters coveted by their respective governments, in violation of international law,” said Hunter Stires, who served as maritime strategist to the Secretary of the Navy until this year. “The mafia calls it ‘protection money;’ Iran calls it a ‘toll’ for unmolested passage through the Strait of Hormuz…. The result is the same: Where free seas end, shakedowns begin.”

For the moment, it isn’t even clear how shipowners are meant to pay for the new Iranian toll without running afoul of U.S. sanctions. An adviser to one Asian government, normally a close partner to the U.S., said they had been negotiating with the local Iranian ambassador, debating whether their ships could quietly buy safe passage in cryptocurrency or Chinese yuan.

So far, Russian and Chinese ships have enjoyed preferential access. Iran’s Revolutionary Guard is flexing its muscles, ranking ships on a scale of how friendly their nations are. At the bottom of the scorecard: the U.S. and Israel.

“Once you have one major breach, others will follow and you quickly descend into a state of legal chaos,” said Jason Chuah, professor of commercial and maritime law, at the City Law School of City St George’s, University of London. “It’s extremely sad. International law seems to be at a breaking point in the Persian Gulf.”
