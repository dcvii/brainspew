Three-time Moochie Award winner Professor Justin Bronk rejoins Mooch to dig into what actually hit the F-15 Strike Eagle and the F-35 over Iran — two engagements that generated enormous online speculation and, in Bronk's view, a fair amount of nonsense. His answer isn't a dramatic revelation about secret Chinese passive radar systems quietly deployed to theater. It's more operationally interesting than that: Iran has spent years developing and fielding a menagerie of passive, pop-up, short-to-medium-range SAM systems specifically designed to survive sustained air campaigns — first refined in Yemen against Saudi and Emirati aircraft, now showing up in a shooting war against the US Air Force and Navy.

The 358 (SA-67) and 359 loitering SAMs are at the top of Bronk’s candidate list for the F-35 hit. They’re turbojet-powered, subsonic, and originally built to hunt MQ-1s and MQ-9s — which matters because they carry relatively small warheads and generate almost no infrared signature compared to a conventional missile with a full rocket plume. That combination may explain why the F-35’s 360-degree EOIR missile approach warning system didn’t give the pilot enough time to react. The jet’s passive sensor suite is genuinely impressive, but if the incoming threat is small, subsonic, and barely radiating in the infrared band, the detection window closes fast. Another candidate for the Strike Eagle — which notably does not carry a missile approach warning system in its US configuration, unlike the Saudi and Qatari variants — is a repurposed Soviet R-27ET air-to-air missile mounted on a truck, a trick the Iranians already used to down a Saudi F-15 over Yemen. Slaved to an optical camera on the launch vehicle, it produces no radar emissions whatsoever.

Bronk’s broader point is worth sitting with: none of this means US air superiority failed or that the air campaign is in trouble. The loss rate relative to sorties flown is historically low. What it does mean is that without a ground campaign to physically clear and hold terrain, you cannot hunt every passive pop-up system an adversary has pre-positioned and concealed across a large country. They don’t radiate, they don’t move much, and until they fire they’re essentially invisible. A small number of losses from that kind of threat, across a high-tempo air campaign, is the cost of operating in that environment — not a sign that something has gone wrong at the operational level.
