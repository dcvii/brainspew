## Aaron Patzer skipped California and Hawaii, instead buying a home north of Auckland and pouring $500,000 into a renovation

Aaron Patzer steps out of his modern, glassy, five-bedroom home and strolls across the rolling green lawns of his 22-acre oceanfront property. He pauses to taste Manuka honey from one of his beehives, then meanders down a gravel path and dives off his private boat ramp into the clear turquoise water.

“It would cost $50 million or more just for bare land like this on the ocean in Kauai,” he said. “I don’t even know how much it would cost on the coast of California.”

His house is in New Zealand and cost $2 million.

Patzer, 45, moved to the country in 2013, several years after selling Mint.com, the personal financial management website he founded, to Intuit in 2009 for $170 million.

About an hour’s drive north of Auckland, the house has 180-degree views. Patzer got rid of the 100 sheep that were on the property and turned the pens into gardens. He added several greenhouses, including one with lounge chairs to sit on and stare out across the sea at Kawau Island and the beach below. He removed 6 acres of pine trees and replaced them with native trees, including the manuka. “It’s like Napa or Sonoma on the beach,” he said.

Two years ago, Patzer spent about $500,000 on a renovation, adding sliding glass doors to the outdoor deck that wraps around the house, redoing doors and creating a music studio.

He lives there with wife, Michelle Tomlinson, the 28-year-old New Zealand-born musical artist known as Pianika, who has released two EPs, or 30 songs, of remixes with classical and modern electronic music.

The main bathroom has a stand-alone bath that sits outside on a patio with views of the sea.

Nearly every kind of fruit grows on the property, including bananas, grapes, avocados, oranges, persimmons and kumquats, along with a variety of nuts and vegetables. He jars the olives that come off his trees; he makes sauerkraut from his cabbage and milk from his almonds.

They make most meals from what they grow, except for occasionally buying local fish or grass-fed meat. After dinner they take three-mile hikes along the seaside cliffs in a nearby regional park. Their water comes from their own wells. “I’m convinced I’ve gained five to 10 years in my life simply by moving here,” he said.

Patzer’s route here started far from the New Zealand coast. He grew up in Evansville, Indiana, and attended North Carolina’s Duke University on a scholarship. He went on to Princeton for a Ph.D. in engineering but left the program in 2004 after earning his master’s. He got the idea for Mint watching his mother, a home economics teacher, do the household budget by hand.

He spends about eight months a year in the New Zealand house, where he works about 70 hours a week on a startup called Vital—a software program using AI to help patients with wait times, translation of medical jargon, discharge checklists and recovery care. The program is currently used by around seven million patients in around 30 major health systems in the U.S., where he travels about eight times a year.

Holding zoom meetings from New Zealand is easy, he said. New Zealand is one day minus three hours ahead of the West Coast, meaning that when it’s 3 p.m. on a Tuesday in California, it is noon on Wednesday in Auckland. “Everyone says they don’t want to go to New Zealand because it feels so far. But I find it works well,” he said. He ends up working Saturdays (when it’s Friday in the U.S.) but not so much on Mondays (when it’s Sunday in the U.S.)

There are some issues. New Zealanders tell him his emails are too direct. He also gets frustrated with the longer time it often takes to get everything done, he said. As a result, he has spent a lot of time training his local employees on the ethos of American startups, such as putting in what he calls “Silicon Valley hours,” he said, and on skills like how to manage products. To get fast enough internet he invested around $7,000 in a point-to-point high speed network using microwave connections.

When it’s winter in New Zealand, and temperatures get down to the mid 40s—June through September—he and Tomlinson travel to Europe and the U.S. where it’s warm. This way he is always living where it’s summer. “I haven’t had winter in four years,” he said.

He initially [moved to New Zealand](https://www.wsj.com/real-estate/luxury-homes/new-zealand-american-home-buyers-70d344a2?mod=article_inline) with a girlfriend—the niece of one of Mint’s executives. In New Zealand, couples who have lived together for more than three years have legal obligations, which meant that after they split up six years ago, Patzer was required to give her half his New Zealand assets. That included a five-bedroom 19 <sup>th</sup> century farmhouse home he had bought for about $2 million in 2017 in the city of Auckland.

Patzer still owns a share of homes in Florida (where his brother lives) and Indianapolis (where his sister lives). [He left the U.S.](https://www.wsj.com/us-news/americans-leaving-the-us-migration-a5795bfa?mod=article_inline) in part because of gun violence, excess litigation and high healthcare costs. People he knew had become obsessed with their net worth instead of the quality of their lives. “The U.S. isn’t that livable,” he said.

While he has permanent residency in New Zealand, which means he gets government healthcare and can vote, he still has to pay U.S. taxes. The only way to get around that would be to renounce U.S. citizenship and become a New Zealand citizen. That is something he doesn’t want to do right now. “It would mean having to swear allegiance to the King of England. That just feels wrong,” he said.
