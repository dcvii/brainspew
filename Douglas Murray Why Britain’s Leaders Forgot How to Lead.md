Keir Starmer was elected for no reason other than to repudiate the Conservatives. He failed to chart any path forward, like so many of his predecessors.

All countries have periods in which they are said to be ungovernable. The resignation of Keir Starmer as prime minister of the United Kingdom makes it clear that no leader in years has been up to the task. And it captures the comedy of errors that occurs when both the left and right insist on forgoing a clear direction in favor of chasing a muddle of priorities.

Starmer became prime minister in 2024, promising to “stop the endless Conservative chaos.” He was referring to the 14 years of Conservative Party rule that had gifted the country a succession of peculiar crises. Despite their long-running dominance over Labour, the Conservatives managed to fumble the political moment at every turn.

First there was the 2010 election, in which David Cameron’s Conservatives sought to “detoxify” their own ranks and present a more polished and professional face to voters, scrubbed of cultural conservatism. That ended up not being popular enough to win a majority, and they had to form an unusual coalition government with the Liberal Democrats, which tugged the Conservatives even further toward a cosmopolitan view of the country and the world. By 2016, voters shattered this updated version of the Tories by approving the referendum to leave the European Union, which Cameron had opposed with all his might.

The result amazed and enraged the people who thought they were in charge of the country. The British public turned out in unprecedented numbers to vote to leave. Despite having promised to steer the country through whatever verdict the voters issued, Cameron promptly—and unpatriotically—resigned.

---

Fortunately for the Tories, Labour was in an even worse position to seize the Brexit moment. They opposed the referendum with greater fervor, warning that Britain would sink into poverty and bigotry outside the EU. Meanwhile, the shock of Brexit might have led to a new Tory strategy, with a leader ready to carry out the national mandate. Instead, it was followed by years of the Conservative Party doing one of the things it does best—forming a circle and firing at each other. Theresa May presided over a three-year stretch of infighting and indecision, during which the UK remained stuck in the union it had promised to leave.

May resigned by 2019 and handed the reins to Boris Johnson, the chief campaigner of Brexit and seemingly the rightful heir, capable of enacting a Britain First trade policy and tamping down on mass migration. Johnson called an election and won in a landslide, with the promise that his government would finally see through the will of the people.

But Johnson, too, squandered his majority, tacking to the left on issues from taxes to energy regulation in a hapless attempt to retain support from President Joe Biden and the many former Labour voters in Johnson’s expanded Tory base. To the surprise of no one, Johnson’s government also turned out to be chaotic and inept. Covid helped throw it into ill-discipline. A spate of scandals involving Covid lockdown parties at No. 10 Downing Street sealed Johnson’s fate.

Then came the period that sealed the Tories’ fate altogether. The manifestly unfit Liz Truss led the nation for less than two months before the party booted her out, with her principal rival for the top job, Rishi Sunak, installed in her place. Britain was starting to make Italy look stable and well-governed. Sunak sought to stabilize markets and gestured at immigration restrictions, but showed no sign that he grasped the popular will any better than his predecessors.

When Sunak called an early election in 2024, it was in the knowledge that he and his party would lose, but that it was better to get the pain over with fast. The election delivered a landslide victory for Starmer’s Labour Party. But Labour came into office that year without any wave of popularity or promises—Starmer barely told the public what he proposed to do with power. Rather, their only advantage was not being the Conservatives.

The fact that Starmer has now resigned in turn—with a whopping parliamentary majority still in place—speaks to a set of deep problems in Britain.

The first is that the underlying issue of Brexit remains unsolved. Britain is out of the EU but still subject to foreign law, including the immigration laws imposed by continuing a signatory of the European Convention on Human Rights. Instead of leaping away from the continent, Britain tripped and got wedged in a sort of crevice: out of Europe, but not independent from it.

Then there is the underlying issue of the economy. Starmer’s Labour Party said it wanted a “fairer” economy. What this meant in practice was that the party raised taxes and increased the unsustainable welfare budget while cutting popular but minimal welfare bribes, like the winter fuel allowance for pensioners.

Yet the moment Starmer really lost the country came during the summer after he was elected. Axel Rudakubana, a child of Rwandan immigrants, entered a Taylor Swift–themed dance class in the Labour constituency of Southport and started [stabbing young girls](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/04/13/world/europe/southport-attack-killers-parents-report.html), killing three of them and maiming eight others, along with two adults. Conscious of how febrile the atmosphere in the country would be after such an attack, the Starmer government tried to cover up the identity of the attacker along with his potential motives. At the same time, the administration came down as hard as possible not just on rioters but also on social-media users who made incendiary statements.

Starmer badly misjudged the mood of the country. Out of touch with public anger, he seemed, in fact, to have a sort of suspicion of the British public. For the rest of his time in office, the term “two-tier Kier” stuck. People claimed that there was a two-tier justice system: On the one hand, illegal immigrants were landing on the beaches of the south of England most days, and the public was told that it couldn’t object to these illegals being housed in hotels at taxpayers’ expense. On the other hand, anyone who objected too forcefully to this was seen as the actual problem, and one that needed squashing.

The government’s response to the [killing of Henry Nowak](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cpvpv2xylgeo) in December compounded the damage. An 18-year-old student, Nowak was stabbed by a Sikh man of immigrant background only to be handcuffed by police in his dying moments. The officers instantly believed the killer’s lie that Nowak had targeted him with a racist attack.

---

A failure to grow the economy is one thing. An uninspiring and bland political public-relations problem is another. But the growing sense that Starmer actually disliked ordinary Britons became hard to shake off. Despite its huge Parliamentary majority, the Labour Party started behaving as the Conservatives had before them. They panicked. When Defense Secretary John Healey [resigned this month](https://apnews.com/article/britain-defense-secretary-john-healey-quits-533cb2637192f045ca6247ab5a402bac), he warned that Starmer would not commit to the spending necessary to keep Britain and her allies safe.

All the time, the so-called “King of the North” was waiting: Andy Burnham, the mayor of Manchester and a former minister in the Tony Blair government. Burnham won his way back into Parliament last week and announced his run for Labour Party leader, a race he will almost certainly win.

Labour backbenchers will rejoice. But there is little in Burnham’s record or plans to suggest he’ll beat a different path from Starmer, or make voters feel that Labour is for the British interest. The country will get yet another unelected prime minister, who may soon recall May and Sunak by calling an election his party is destined to lose.

Britain isn’t ungovernable, but it is hard to see how it can be governed well given the number of promises that the public now expect to be fulfilled simultaneously. There is the promise of keeping out of Europe yet hewing close to it. There is the promise to cut the national debt while not cutting government spending or borrowing. There is the promise to reach net zero while energy prices soar. And there is the promise to be a world power without the means to fund the military that could keep Britain as any such thing.

None of this can be done at the same time. Burnham will be elected as a fresh old face, both shiny and new but supposedly experienced and mature. The reality is that he will almost certainly come to regret his coronation.

Shakespeare’s King Henry IV famously says, “Uneasy lies the head that wears a crown.” For modern British prime ministers, the phrase can be slightly adapted. Whoever wears the crown of prime minister wears it unenviably. It is a job nobody would want, because until Britain addresses its tectonic problems, running the country will truly be a job no one can do.
