The Justice Department will have a hard time proving that the firm’s use of informants amounts to fraud.

The Department of Justice on Tuesday issued a stunning indictment of the Southern Poverty Law Center (SPLC), a public interest law firm representing purported victims of discrimination.

The indictment charges the SPLC with secretly funding some of the very “hate groups,” including Ku Klux Klan affiliates, that the SPLC tells its donors it is fighting against. According to the government, [between 2014 and 2023](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/21/doj-southern-poverty-law-center-indictment-extremist.html), the SPLC paid over $3,000,000—laundered through bank accounts registered to fictitious entities—to insiders at those groups, often people in leadership positions.

Supporters of the SPLC will see the indictment as yet another vindictive Trump prosecution of a left-wing adversary. Haters will see it as overdue justice finally coming to a radical left nonprofit that worked with the Biden administration to target conservative parents and to call right-leaning Catholic organizations domestic terrorists.

---

But the question many will have is why—why on earth would the Southern Poverty Law Center, which all agree is sharply left-leaning, fund right-wing entities the SPLC itself calls hate groups?

The indictment offers no answer at all.

The SPLC has said it paid informants in these entities to obtain clandestine information about their activities—information it would share with law enforcement. [The indictment](https://www.justice.gov/opa/media/1437146/dl) does nothing to refute this claim. Instead, it simply recounts in detail numerous payments that were made and the fact that these payments were kept hidden.

That’s not going to be sufficient—either as a PR matter, or with a jury. Criminal fraud cases require a theory: Why was the fraudulent scheme undertaken, and who did it benefit? The government needs to explain that theory, yet the indictment doesn’t. It does offer troubling claims about the SPLC, but ones that may belong more in the court of public opinion than in the court of law.

In its youthful heyday, the SPLC, founded in 1971, did things like overturning the convictions of black men falsely accused of rape. Today, it is better known for its “ [hate map](https://www.splcenter.org/hate-map/),” a map of the U.S. that flags hundreds of “hate groups.” The map has been extremely influential and skews wildly left wing. For example, the SPLC’s hate groups do not include antifa, but they do include Charlie Kirk’s Turning Point USA, Moms for Liberty—a parents’ organization opposing trans ideology and critical race theory being taught in schools—and Alliance Defending Freedom, a serious right-wing legal organization that played an important role in overturning *Roe v. Wade*.

Why on earth would the Southern Poverty Law Center, which all agree is sharply left-leaning, fund right-wing entities the SPLC itself calls hate groups?

At a press conference yesterday, acting attorney general Todd Blanche suggested what the government’s theory of the case will be, and it’s a hair-raiser. [According to Blanche](https://reason.com/2026/04/22/doj-indicts-southern-poverty-law-center-for-secretly-funding-extremists/), “the SPLC is manufacturing racism to justify its existence.” On this view, the SPLC has been “stoking racial hatred” to jet-propel fundraising—collecting hundreds of millions of dollars in donations to dismantle the very hate groups it was actually secretly funding.

Is there any evidence that the SPLC collected substantial donations by “stoking” the “racial hatred” it told donors it was fighting?

That’s a shocking, vicious accusation, and the story recounted in the indictment contains nearly nothing specifically supporting it. But there is one allegation that, if true, might bear it out.

In 2017, a year after Trump was first elected, the famous “Unite the Right” march took place in Charlottesville, Virginia, ostensibly protesting the removal of a Robert E. Lee statue and the renaming of Lee Park. Some participants carried Nazi and Confederate flags. When counterprotesters showed up, violence broke out. A self-identified white supremacist rammed his car into a group of counterprotesters, killing one and injuring 35. He fled but was apprehended and later convicted of first-degree murder. Afterward, President Trump condemned the “hatred” and “bigotry” at the march, but [made other remarks](https://www.politico.com/story/2017/08/15/full-text-trump-comments-white-supremacists-alt-left-transcript-241662) (“fine people on both sides”) seized upon by Trump critics as though he had defended racism and white supremacy.

It’s been known for some time that the Unite to Right event seems to have been a fundraising bonanza for the Southern Poverty Law Center. Overall in 2017, the SPLC collected [more than $132 million](https://urldefense.com/v3/__https://www.newyorker.com/news/news-desk/the-reckoning-of-morris-dees-and-the-southern-poverty-law-center__;!!CxwJSw!NisMeCSC1wSjexSH9kNgadDP0voPctsPufd7ZqlGngZSWTRFNBjBdf5apjOu4syS2lBbu4160FlTPbyDS7T-wwRHlj0$) in donations, “much of it coming after” the rally. Not known, however, until yesterday’s indictment was the SPLC’s secret connections to a leader of that rally. According to the indictment, the SPLC paid over $270,000 to a “member of the online leadership chat group that planned” the Unite the Right rally. This unnamed individual allegedly “attended the event at the direction of the SPLC,” “made racist postings under the supervision of the SPLC,” and “helped coordinate transportation to the event for several attendees.” If true, this course of conduct might show the SPLC raking in major funds off racial hatred it helped foment.

But legally speaking, the indictment has glaring weaknesses.

The primary count is that the SPLC defrauded its donors by telling them it was dedicated to “dismantling white supremacy” and “fighting” hate. To make this count really stick, the government would have to show that these broad mission statements were false, but that’s going to be very hard to do. The fact that the SPLC paid informants inside organizations that the SPLC saw as hate groups does not come anywhere close to proving that the SPLC was not, in fact, trying to dismantle white supremacy or fight hate.

Is there any evidence that the SPLC collected substantial donations by “stoking” the “racial hatred” it told donors it was fighting?

The government could argue that even if these mission statements were not false, donors were still misled by the SPLC’s concealment of its field-agent payments. But fraud by omission is a tough sell under the federal wire fraud statute. First, the government will need to argue that donors would not have donated had they known of the paid-informant scheme, but donors to the SPLC might well have been perfectly content to learn that the organization was infiltrating hate groups and cooperating with law enforcement as it did so. In addition, because the statute doesn’t explicitly criminalize fraud by omission, this theory might require a finding that the SPLC was under a legal duty to disclose its paid-informant activities, which would be problematic given that the SPLC will surely say that confidentiality was necessary to keep these programs going and to protect its sources.

On the other hand, the government may find SPLC insiders to support its claims. In 2019, Bob Moser, a former SPLC employee, published a remarkable [whistleblowing piece](https://www.newyorker.com/news/news-desk/the-reckoning-of-morris-dees-and-the-southern-poverty-law-center) in the *New Yorker*, right after the SPLC had fired Morris Dees, its famous co-founder, amid allegations of rampant sexual harassment and racial discrimination. Moser described the shock he and many co-workers felt when they started at the SPLC and discovered how little actual legal work the organization was doing. He also described the “guilt” they felt “about the legions of donors who believed that their money was being used, faithfully and well, to do the Lord’s work in the heart of Dixie.” Moser suggested that SPLC workers knew they were participating in a “highly profitable scam,” with “hate group” designations artificially inflated to propel donations. He concluded: “We were part of the con, and we knew it.”

---

At the end of the day, the nonlegal case against the Southern Poverty Law Center may be stronger than the legal case. The SPLC is fantastically rich, with assets of more than $820 million in 2024. According to Moser’s description, it spends surprisingly little of its astronomical funding on lawyers and legal work. Instead, it has built a spectacular office building in Montgomery—“a massive modernist glass-and-steel structure” dubbed the “Darth Vader building”—“while paying lavish salaries to its highest-ranking staffers and spending far less than most nonprofit groups on the work that it claimed to do.”

At the same time, its hate group designations have been slanted and may have led to violence. The SPLC added Turning Point USA to its hate map only a few months before Kirk was murdered. In 2012, according to Tyler O’Neil, author of the book *Making Hate Pay: The Corruption of the Southern Poverty Law Center*, a man used the SPLC “hate map” to target the Family Research Council—a pro-life and anti–same sex marriage organization— [planning an attack](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/floyd-lee-corkins-pleads-guilty-in-family-research-council-shooting/) that would have killed everyone inside its Washington, D.C. building.

O’Neil also reports that, in order to justify pinning a “hate group” label on a Catholic organization, the SPLC “repeatedly cited as evidence of ‘hate’ a quote directly from the Catechism of the Catholic Church.” What to call an entity that demeans and calumniates the entire Catholic Church? Well, I suppose you might call it a hate group.

But spreading hate, as the SPLC has done, is not a criminal offense. If the SPLC wants to vilify Catholicism, it has a First Amendment right to do so. Acting attorney general Blanche says the investigation of the Southern Poverty Law Center is ongoing. He’d better hope it proves successful, because he may need to come up with a lot more to make this indictment hold up.
