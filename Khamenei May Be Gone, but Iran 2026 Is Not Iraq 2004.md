***Early Saturday morning, air strikes rained down on Iran, as the United States and Israel launched a joint operation against military installations across the country. The attack follows weeks of indirect negotiations that ended in stalemate. “They’ve rejected every opportunity to renounce their nuclear ambitions,” President Donald Trump proclaimed this morning. “We can’t take it anymore.” On Saturday afternoon, Trump announced that Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei had been killed.***

***Earlier today, The Free Press’s*** ***Rafaela Siewert spoke live with Douglas Murray, Michael Oren, and others to unpack the latest developments and what they mean for an already fragile global order. Watch the [full livestream here](https://www.thefp.com/p/watch-the-us-and-israel-strike-iran). And don’t miss the rest of our coverage of this extraordinary moment, including Haviv Rettig Gur on [how the Islamic Republic will fall](https://www.thefp.com/p/this-is-how-the-islamic-republic), and Eli Lake on why this is [the biggest gamble](https://www.thefp.com/p/trump-keeps-his-promise-to-iranand) of Trump’s presidency. But first, Niall Ferguson explains why this is not a replay of the 2003 invasion of Iraq—and why America is more interested in “regime alteration” than regime change. —The Editors***

Since the news of the joint U.S.-Israeli strikes on Iran this morning, I have been thinking a lot about a song in the 2004 movie *Team America:World Police*. The movie was co-written by the creators of *South Park* and follows a group of heroic American puppets waging kinetic war on Islamic terrorists, the North Korean dictator Kim Jong Il, and liberal Hollywood, leaving cataclysmic collateral damage (the Eiffel Tower, Cairo, the Sphinx) in their wake. But the real highlight is a song called “America, Fuck Yeah.” Here’s how it goes:

> America, fuck yeah  
> Comin’ again to save the motherfuckin’ day, yeah  
> America, fuck yeah  
> Freedom is the only way, yeah  
> Terrorists, your game is through  
> ’Cause now you have to answer to …  
> America, fuck yeah  
> So lick my butt and suck on my balls  
> America, fuck yeah  
> What you gonna do when we come for you now?  
> It’s the dream that we all share  
> It’s the hope for tomorrow  
> Fuck yeah

*Team America* was an ambivalent movie at the time. That was what made it funny. It simultaneously mocked the liberal opponents of an aggressive foreign policy and the neoconservatives who advocated policies such as regime change in Iraq. The *South Park* team understood before many commentators that the United States has a track record of coming to save the day and leaving a trail of devastation.

For the habitual critics of U.S. foreign policy in general and Donald Trump’s in particular, the analogy between today’s air raids against Iran and the invasion of Iraq nearly 23 years ago is too obvious to be resisted.

“Once again,” [Simon Tisdall](https://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2026/feb/28/donald-trump-bombs-iran-war-middle-east) ranted in *The* *Guardian*, “a bellicose U.S. president has unleashed overwhelming military firepower to force a sovereign nation to its knees. Once again, blatant lies and exaggerated claims are being propagated to justify the attack. Duplicitous American diplomacy became a fig leaf for premeditated aggression. The cautionary advice of allies was spurned. The UN, international law, and public opinion were ignored. Democratic consent is lacking. And once again, there are few defined goals by which to gauge success, and no long-term plan.... Like Bush, Trump manufactured a crisis, founded on falsehood, and effectively cornered himself.”

And so on. If we were [still allowed to use](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/02/27/us/politics/anthropic-military-ai.html) Anthropic’s Claude, I could have generated a hundred different versions of this op-ed. *The* *New Republic* can have one of them; *Mother Jones* another.

However, Iran 2026 is not Iraq 2003. Back in those days, I shed no tears for Saddam Hussein and had considerable sympathy with the project of covert empire-building, but [I was a critic](https://bookshop.org/a/93116/9780143034797) of the Bush administration’s post-invasion nation-building strategy because I believed the U.S. lacked the key structural attributes to make it work. By comparison with the British Empire, American power in the 2000s had three fateful deficits: a manpower deficit, a fiscal deficit, and an attention deficit. I argued that the occupying force was too small relative to Iraq’s population. The complete destruction of the Ba’athist regime laid the foundations for anarchy and civil war, which swiftly mutated into an insurgency against the U.S.-led coalition. And I came to see that the principal beneficiary of Hussein’s downfall was none other than Iran.

And yet, contrary to the criticism already being aired on both the left and the right, Trump is not reverting to George W. Bush and Dick Cheney’s “regime change” playbook.

After the U.S. captured Venezuelan dictator Nicolás Maduro last month, I suggested to a senior administration official that what we had just seen was not regime change but regime *alteration* —in the sense that, while Maduro had been replaced by Delcy Rodríguez, the structure of the Chavista regime remained in place. The alteration was that Delcy would now report to Washington, not to Havana or Beijing.

“Regime alteration,” he said, writing it down. “I might use that.”

Indeed, regime alteration is the practical consequence of the approach laid out in Trump’s [National Security Strategy](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/2025-National-Security-Strategy.pdf) published late last year. The strategy rules out the deployment of American ground forces other than special forces. It requires a short time frame for military operations. It will disappoint those who want to fast-track Venezuela and Iran to democracy. But the lesson of Iraq has not been lost on Trump.

That is why it misses the point to say, “Trump claimed to be an isolationist and he’s just started another forever war.” One thing I can confidently promise about the U.S.-Israeli war against the Islamic Republic: It will not last long.

Here’s what we know so far: The joint U.S.-Israeli operation (it is Roaring Lion as well as Epic Fury) began early this morning with a series of strikes in Tehran and other Iranian cities against targets including missile storage and launchers, military and security bases, and the residences of political and military leaders. Israeli sources are already [reporting success](https://www.timesofisrael.com/liveblog_entry/initial-israeli-strikes-targeted-some-30-key-iranian-leaders-30-bombs-said-dropped-on-khameneis-compound/) —prominent regime leaders they say they have killed. On Saturday afternoon, Trump announced that Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei had been killed.

“To the great proud people of Iran, I say tonight that the hour of your freedom is at hand,” Trump [proclaimed this morning](https://apnews.com/article/iran-us-trump-address-f662a4f3378535d81197be699fb35a3e). “When we are finished, take over your government. It will be yours to take. This will be probably your only chance for generations.”

It is a foolish commentator who attempts to pass definitive judgment on a major military operation while it is still underway. And of course, there are risks. Yes, Iran can lash out and is lashing out, firing missiles—of which it still has many—at Israel and at U.S. bases in the Gulf region. Yes, the Iranians may try to replay 1973 and 1979 by causing an oil price shock, which there will certainly be if they are able to block the crucial Strait of Hormuz. And yes, it is a lot to expect the Iranian people to rise up against a regime that just weeks ago had the ruthlessness to [slaughter protesters](https://www.thefp.com/p/a-survivor-of-irans-protests-speaks) in their tens of thousands.

But this intervention still seems unlikely to degenerate into Operation Epic Fail. Part of the reason is that the operation was preceded by a massive concentration of force. By this past Friday, the U.S. Navy had at least 13 ships in the region, including the aircraft carrier USS *Abraham Lincoln,* and at least nine destroyers. It had deployed F-22s and F-35s, the stealth fighters that escorted the B-2s on their way to and from Iran’s nuclear sites in June last year. Also in theater were EA-18G jamming aircraft to disrupt Iran’s missile launchers, as well as F-15E and F-16 fighters to intercept Iranian drones. Finally, there were THAAD and Patriot interceptors to protect American assets and allies in the region.

Contrary to the criticism already being aired on both the left and the right, Trump is not reverting to George W. Bush and Dick Cheney’s “regime change” playbook.

The chairman of the Joint Chiefs, General Dan “Raisin” Caine, might not seem to have much in common with the late Colin Powell. But the preparations for this operation recall the massive accumulation of force that preceded the expulsion of Iraqi forces from Kuwait in 1991, when Powell was in the chairman’s seat. Trump likes to think of Caine as a fire-breathing *Top Gun* action hero. However, [according to CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2026/02/25/politics/caine-iran-hegseth-trump), Caine made it clear in recent weeks that he was “unable to predict what the result of a regime change operation would be” and that military action in the Middle East may have unintended consequences elsewhere. He deserves full credit for his prudent preparation.

As last year, the diplomacy of the last few weeks was merely *maskirovka* —a disguise—to persuade the Iranians that they might talk their way out of the destruction Trump threatened in early January, when Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei was slaughtering protesters in the streets. Three weeks ago, on February 7, we were told by the foreign minister of Iran, Abbas Araghchi, that talks in Oman were proceeding on “a framework” for “future talks,” even as he insisted that Iran refused to end its uranium enrichment and its ballistic missiles program.

The U.S. demands, *[The](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/02/07/world/europe/iran-us-trump-diplomacy.html)* *[New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/02/07/world/europe/iran-us-trump-diplomacy.html)* reported, were that Iran hand over all of its enriched uranium, especially the roughly 440 kilograms that was near to bomb-grade; limit the range of its ballistic missiles so they could not reach Israel; and end its support for its regional proxy militias, like Hamas, Hezbollah, and the Houthis.

Never in a hundred years was Khamenei going to agree to those terms. Far from being strung along by wily Persian negotiators, the Trump administration was surprisingly explicit in its warnings—even more so than last June. On February 19, [Trump told Iran](https://www.reuters.com/world/middle-east/russia-warns-escalating-iran-tensions-amid-us-military-build-up-2026-02-19/) that it had just 10 to 15 days to avert potential military action. A week ago, his special envoy [Steve Witkoff declared](https://www.foxnews.com/media/witkoff-warns-iran-a-week-away-from-bomb-material-trump-weighs-action) that Iran was now “probably a week away from having industrial-grade bomb-making material” and insisted that “zero enrichment” was one of Trump’s “red lines.” Secretary of State Marco Rubio [said on](https://www.ft.com/content/fb27e869-54de-4dea-8d4a-da6d73a5421b) Wednesday that Tehran’s refusal “to talk about \[its\] ballistic missiles to us or to anyone” was “a big problem.”

Omani-mediated talks continued in Geneva until Thursday, but anyone who thought that Witkoff and Jared Kushner were planning to return from the U.S. after “consultations” wasn’t reading the Situation Room. Trump [told reporters](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-20/trump-s-iran-ultimatum-sets-stage-for-strike-during-iaea-meeting) last Thursday that “we’re either going to get a deal, or it’s going to be unfortunate for them.” By refusing to yield on enrichment and missiles, the Iranians chose the unfortunate option.

Operation Epic Fury differs from Operation Iraqi Freedom—the 2003 invasion of Iraq—in two key respects. Yes, the justification is preemption against a regime intent on acquiring weapons of mass destruction and implicated in international terrorism. But the goal is not to march into Iran and confer, much less impose, freedom on the Iranians. It is to decapitate the Islamic Republic’s political structure and leave the Iranians to take their freedom from the mullahs and their murderous henchmen. As Trump said in his speech this morning, “members of the Islamic Revolutionary Guard, the armed forces and all of the police” can “have complete immunity” if they lay down their weapons.

Will this succeed? We don’t yet know, but it’s important to note that the Iranian regime has few effective friends. Unlike in 1973, when the Soviet Union could credibly threaten to deploy forces to tilt the balance of the war between Israel and the Arabs, Russia has seen its influence in the Middle East significantly degraded. President Vladimir Putin has his bloody hands full in Ukraine, where the sudden (and very significant) [denial of Starlink’s services](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c0q3ndj7052o) to his frontline troops is causing havoc. China has its attention focused on the Indo-Pacific. Aside from some hasty weapons deliveries, the other members of the Axis of Authoritarians can do little to assist the regime in Tehran.

Moreover, Iran’s barrage of missile attacks on its neighbors’ territory has achieved little other than to convert fence-sitters into supporters of the U.S.-Israeli effort. In what will surely prove to be a spasm of self-immolation, the Iranians have hit Bahrain (which hosts the U.S. Navy’s Fifth Fleet headquarters) and other targets in the United Arab Emirates, Qatar, Kuwait, Jordan, and Saudi Arabia. This is how to lose friends and alienate people.

The real question is: Who rules in Tehran after Khamenei? Or, to put it bluntly, who’s the Iranian Delcy in this regime alteration? The 86-year-old head of state is now either dead or so deep underground that he can at least be considered buried. (His nickname these days is “Moushe-Ali,” or “Ali the Mouse,” because he’s mostly in a deep hole.)

Will he be succeeded by Ali Larijani, secretary of the Supreme National Security Council? It seems unlikely, as he was responsible for so brutally crushing the recent protests. What about the former commander in chief of the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC), Major General Yahya Rahim Safavi? Or maybe Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf, the former Guards commander who is currently speaker of the Iranian parliament? (The Mossad thinks Ghalibaf [now effectively runs](https://understandingwar.org/research/middle-east/iran-update-february-23-2026/#:~:text=Parliament%20Speaker%20Mohammad%20Bagher%20Ghalibaf,%2Dday%20war.%5B44%5D) the country in partnership with Major General Mohammad Pakpour, though the Israelis say they killed Pakpour today.) How about former president Hassan Rouhani?

I asked one Trump adviser if one of these would be Delcy. This was his answer:

Some say that real power in Tehran lies with the Bayt-e Rahbar, or the Office of the Supreme Leader. According to Iran scholars [Saeid Golkar and Kasra Aarabi](https://www.unitedagainstnucleariran.com/press-releases/exposed-khameneis-secretive-power-structure-built-to-ensure-regimes-survival#:~:text=The%20Bayt%20is%20a%20political,remains%20with%20the%20Supreme%20Leader.), the Bayt is led by Ayatollah Mohammadi Golpayegani, whose son is married to one of Khamenei’s daughters. Also said to be influential are Ali Asghar Hejazi, Khamenei’s deputy chief of staff, and Hossein Fadaei, a former member of the IRGC. But “the true power brokers are Khamenei’s four sons: Mostafa, Mojtaba, Masoud, and Meysam.” I doubt there is a Delcy here.

Is this the cast of characters for a full-blown Iranian civil war, as opposed to regime alteration? No one knows. Or is the real risk that Iran implodes and creates a vacuum of power that gets filled—as former Israel defense minister [Yoav Gallant warns](https://generalyoavgallant.substack.com/p/the-next-strategic-shift-in-the-middle?r=5lpmfi) —by an ascendant Turkey? Again, no one knows.

The bigger downside risk, in my view, lies beyond the Middle East. Remember, Team America has to deal with the whole world—not just the Western Hemisphere and not just MENA (the Middle East and North Africa). The United States is still in Cold War II, regardless of who is president. It has been since 2018. And China is the most formidable economic rival the U.S. has faced.

Cold wars are a geopolitical version of the [Three-Body Problem](https://www.npr.org/2014/11/13/363123510/three-body-problem-asks-a-classic-sci-fi-question-in-chinese). In the world according to the Pentagon, beyond the Americas there are three regions, each exerting a powerful gravitational attraction on Washington and its finite military resources: Europe, the Middle East, and East Asia.

The legacy of Joe Biden’s administration was that we ended up in Zbigniew Brzezinski’s worst-case post-Cold-War scenario, as described in *[The Grand Chessboard](https://bookshop.org/a/93116/9780465094356)* (1997): “a grand coalition of China, Russia, and perhaps Iran, an ‘antihegemonic’ coalition united not by ideology but by complementary grievances.”

The good news is that regime alternation is easier than regime change, as we have already seen in Venezuela. In the Middle East, the U.S. has unrivaled dominance. As we have seen, neither Russia nor China can do much to help Iran.

The real question is: Who rules in Tehran after Khamenei?

The bad news is that “triangular diplomacy” now works against the United States. In late February 2025, [Xi Jinping said](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/02/24/world/asia/xi-putin-call-russia-china-trump.html) after a video call with Putin that Russia and China are “true friends who have been through thick and thin together.” Their Axis meets regularly and appears to have a game plan. Europe is in danger of becoming the battleground of Cold War II, as Asia was the battleground of Cold War I. A Ukrainian defeat by Russia would be disastrous for European security, which is why Trump wants to end the war there. But the biggest risk the world faces is still a Taiwan Crisis, the economic consequences of which would be greater than the [1973-74 Oil Shock](https://www.federalreservehistory.org/essays/oil-shock-of-1973-74) because of the centrality of Taiwan’s [semiconductor fabs](https://newsroom.intel.com/tech101/how-a-semiconductor-factory-works) to the world economy. It’s hard to see how Trump and Xi will find common ground on this issue during their summit in April.

To avoid a Taiwan Semiconductor Crisis, analogous to the Cuban Missile Crisis in 1962, Trump needs to rekindle the spirit of 1972, when Richard Nixon met Mao Tse-tung in Beijing. He needs, in short, to dust down the playbook of détente. And I am sure that is what he intends—why else be open to [delaying arms sales](https://www.taipeitimes.com/News/front/archives/2026/03/01/2003853056) to Taiwan? But détente with China will not be easy if Venezuela—not to mention Cuba and parts of Mexico—has descended into chaos, peace is nowhere in sight in Ukraine, and a new war is still raging in the Middle East.

We have come a long way since Operation Iraqi Freedom. Operation Epic Fury is a very different kind of war, because regime alteration is very different from regime change. But Team America still has its century-old three-body problem. There are three regions of the world that require America to save the motherfuckin’ day: Europe, the Middle East, and East Asia. It can’t do so simultaneously in all three.

Reminder: [According to William Burns](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/cia-director-william-burns-i-wouldnt-underestimate-xis-ambitions-for-taiwan/), when he was running the CIA during the Biden administration, Xi told the Chinese military to be ready for war in 2027. Fuck yeah: That’s next year.
