**The most likely sources:**

**1. Senator Russ Feingold (D-WI)** — April 30, 2008, chairing the Senate Judiciary Subcommittee on the Constitution hearing titled _"Secret Law and the Threat to Democratic and Accountable Government."_ Feingold's opening statement is the single most concentrated articulation of the argument you're describing. He said the Yoo torture memorandum was "classified and withheld from Congress and the public for years on the claim that it contained information that could not be disclosed without harming national security," then pointed out it contained no operational information at all — only legal reasoning. His core argument was that the classification was being used to hide _the law itself_, and since the classification decision couldn't be challenged without seeing the document, the public was trapped in a recursive loop. He concluded that an executive branch operating under secret law "makes a mockery of the democratic principles and freedoms on which this country was based."

**2. Senator Ron Wyden (D-OR)** — Multiple statements between 2011 and 2013, most comprehensively in his July 23, 2013 speech at the Center for American Progress. Wyden described how Senate classification rules barred him from giving any specifics of what he'd seen except to describe it as "secret law — a secret interpretation of the Patriot Act, issued by a secret court, that authorizes secret surveillance programs." He described the recursive problem directly: he and Senator Udall "tried again and again to get the executive branch to be straight with the public, but under the classification rules observed by the Senate we are not even allowed to tap the truth out in Morse code." Wyden also made the meta-classification argument: he noted that the Obama administration had committed in 2009 to create a process for declassifying FISA Court opinions, and "in the last four years exactly zero opinions have been released." The process to review whether something should remain classified was itself opaque and produced nothing.

**3. J. William Leonard** — Former Director of the Information Security Oversight Office, testifying at that same April 30, 2008 Feingold hearing. Leonard said that learning the Yoo torture memo had been classified "had the same effect on me as waking up one morning and learning that after all these years, there is a 'secret' Article to the Constitution that the American people do not even know about." He testified that the classification markings on the memo were improperly applied — no declassification instructions, no identified classifying authority — and yet no one had challenged it, precisely because the classified status made the document invisible.

**4. The "Catch-22" framing** — At the July 17, 2013 House Judiciary Committee hearing on FISA, a member described being "in a Catch-22 situation, in a classified briefing, in a secure setting, and we cannot discuss it publicly, certainly not even with our constituents.
