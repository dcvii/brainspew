Four years ago, Harvard committed $100 million in part to compensate descendants of people enslaved by university’s leaders. ‘I don’t think Harvard understood what it was getting into,’ said a critic.

In June 2019, the writer Ta-Nehisi Coates appeared before a congressional committee to make the case for reparations. Advancing an argument he’d laid out in *The Atlantic* years earlier, Coates contended that America owed a debt to its black citizens not just for slavery but for generations of plundered wealth. Over the next few years, the issue had grown in visibility, and slogans like “Black Lives Matter” had entered mainstream political discourse. “It is impossible to imagine America without the inheritance of slavery,” Coates [told the committee](https://www.congress.gov/116/meeting/house/109648/witnesses/HHRG-116-JU10-Wstate-CoatesT-20190619.pdf).

Six months later, Harvard University took up the cause when Harvard president Lawrence Bacow [convened a faculty committee](https://www.thecrimson.com/article/2019/11/22/bacow-slavery-study-initiative/) to excavate the university’s historical involvement in the Atlantic slave trade.

The committee, chaired by Harvard Radcliffe Institute dean Tomiko Brown-Nagin, ultimately produced a [134-page report](https://legacyofslaveryreport.harvard.edu/) confirming the ugly truth that Harvard, like many institutions in the North, was run by slaveowners—among them four university presidents—and that its professors had advanced so-called race science, including eugenics, to justify the trade.

The report, issued in 2022, called on the school to “take responsibility for its past” and “leverage its strengths in the pursuit of meaningful repair.”

---

The [university then established](https://legacyofslavery.harvard.edu/) what it called the “Harvard & the Legacy of Slavery Initiative,” which aimed to “remedy harms to descendants, to our community and the nation, and to campus life and learning.” It committed an extraordinary [$100 million](https://api.thecrimson.com/article/2022/4/27/100-million-endowment-for-slavery-redress/) to the initiative and [promoted Sara Bleich](https://news.harvard.edu/gazette/story/2022/11/sara-bleich-named-vice-provost-for-special-projects/), a professor of public health policy, to vice provost for special projects to shepherd the effort.

What followed was a cascade of institutional embarrassments: high-profile resignations, the dismissal of an entire research team, a string of HR complaints, and a rebuke from Antigua’s ambassador to the United States. And so far at least, its signature effort—to find descendants of people Harvard had enslaved and then do something for them, presumably financially—has failed miserably.

Harvard, of course, was hardly unique among universities in launching an ambitious racial justice project in the late 2010s and early 2020s. But in its scope and cost, its undertaking surpassed anything adopted by its Ivy League peers. The nine-figure initiative was [billed as](https://www.nytimes.com/2022/04/26/us/harvard-slavery-redress-fund.html) “unprecedented.”

And so was its failure. In retrospect, that should hardly have been a surprise. Like other battle cries that emerged in the past decade—Black Lives Matter or Defund the Police, for instance—the idea of reparations, whatever its merits, struggled to offer a defined, achievable outcome. And even Harvard, with its $57 billion endowment, couldn’t square that contradiction.

Not everything that came out of the Harvard initiative was a failure. The 2022 report put forth seven recommendations. The more straightforward ones included forging partnerships with historically black colleges and universities (HCBUs), and developing educational opportunities for marginalized youth. In early 2024, it established the [Du Bois Scholars Program](https://legacyofslavery.harvard.edu/hbcu-initiatives/du-bois-scholars-program/), which offers summer research internships at Harvard to students from select HBCUs, and it has funded job readiness programs for unemployed adults in Cambridge and Boston.

Beyond that, positive results were hard to come by. The report recommended that Harvard build a memorial to the people it enslaved. But that effort hit a roadblock when the two co-chairs of the committee given the task of designing a permanent public memorial resigned in May 2024.

In a [letter to university leadership](https://www.thecrimson.com/article/2024/9/12/harvard-legacy-of-slavery-memorial-committee-chairs/), they alleged that Bleich’s office had prioritized “speed and end results” rather than “a community-wide process of dialogue and collaboration.” (The university responded at the time by saying that it would “take seriously the co-chairs’ concerns.”)

But the item that would prove most freighted—and cast a shadow over the initiative’s other efforts—was recommendation number four: “Identify, Engage, and Support Direct Descendants.”

The task was twofold: Harvard would first identify the living descendants of individuals enslaved by its leadership, faculty, and staff. Then it would “engage” those descendants through “dialogue, programming, information sharing, relationship building, and educational support.” Although the word *reparations* was never used, the subtext was that somehow, in some way, the descendants would be compensated for what had happened to their ancestors. Even if not plainly stated, it was clear that those identified as Harvard-affiliated descendants were owed something.

One of the Legacy of Slavery Initiative’s first hires was a man named Richard J. Cellini. His job was to find those descendants. At that point, Harvard had identified 79 individuals who had been enslaved by university affiliates, but it had yet to locate a single living relative.

“I don’t think Harvard really understood what they were getting themselves into,” Cellini told me.

Cellini, 62, is not the sort of person you might expect to do this kind of work. He describes himself as an “Eisenhower Republican” rather than a “social justice warrior.” Genealogy, which is now his avocation, came to him late in life. He began his career as a lawyer on Wall Street, and then spent three decades “growing and selling” technology companies.

In 2015, he learned that his alma mater, Georgetown University, had [sold 272 enslaved people](https://www.nytimes.com/2016/04/17/us/georgetown-university-search-for-slave-descendants.html) in the mid-19th century to retire institutional debt. He set out to find the descendants of those who were sold and, with the help of 10 other researchers, Cellini mapped out their lineage and identified more than 10,000 people. Though Cellini’s effort was entirely independent of the university, Georgetown used his work: They granted admissions preferences to the descendants on his list and established a [$400,000 annual grant](https://www.georgetown.edu/news/georgetown-launches-400000-annual-fund-to-support-descendants-of-the-enslaved/) to support community-based projects.

There was never any doubt that the Harvard effort would be on a much larger scale: While Georgetown’s research was limited to the offspring of those 272 slaves in 1838, Harvard’s slave ownership spanned two centuries.

Harvard soon discovered that Cellini was a forceful advocate for his work, determined to find as many descendants as he could, no matter the consequences. Meanwhile, Harvard was clearly getting nervous about the potential scope of his efforts. By July 2023, Cellini was at loggerheads with Bleich. He filed a complaint with Harvard’s Office of the General Counsel, claiming Bleich and other administrators told him not to identify “too many descendants.” [He has said](https://www.theguardian.com/news/2025/jun/21/harvard-slavery-decendants-of-the-enslaved) they expressed fear that “if we found too many descendants, it would bankrupt the university.” Bleich has [repeatedly denied](https://www.thecrimson.com/article/2024/9/20/harvard-legacy-of-slavery-infighting-administrative-pressure/) these claims.

The reckoning that Harvard once promised has since morphed into something the university may not be able to achieve.

The anxiety was not entirely irrational. Every name Cellini added to the ledger represented a potential claim on Harvard’s commitment. Cellini pushed to enlarge the list of potential beneficiaries by including the names of slaves owned by members of the university’s governing boards—a category [administrators had debated](https://www.thecrimson.com/article/2024/9/20/harvard-legacy-of-slavery-infighting-administrative-pressure/). He won that debate—and the pool grew accordingly.

A research trip to Antigua, following the discovery of several hundred individuals enslaved there by Harvard affiliates between the 17th and 19th centuries, yielded an additional hundred names from public archives. Cellini and his team also met with Antigua’s prime minister and discussed opportunities for collaboration between the university and the island nation, a conversation that likely did little to reassure Harvard administrators about the project’s scope.

Antigua has been pressing Harvard for reparations for years. In 2019, Prime Minister Gaston Browne [wrote to President Bacow](https://www.washingtonpost.com/nation/2019/11/06/harvard-law-school-antigua-barbados-reparations/) warning that Harvard’s failure to acknowledge its obligations to the island and reckon with “the stain it bears from benefiting from the blood of our people” was “shocking if not immoral.” Harvard’s response has amounted to modest academic exchanges with the University of the West Indies, including shared courses and conference appearances.

Shortly after Cellini and his team returned from Antigua in January 2025, they were all fired by Harvard. The university subsequently outsourced the work to American Ancestors, a New England genealogical nonprofit now on a three-year contract. Harvard has declined to provide a specific rationale for the change. American Ancestors [has denied](https://www.harvardmagazine.com/2025/02/harvard-slavery-descendants-program-what-s-next) that the university has imposed any constraints on their research.

Upon learning of Cellini’s firing, Sir Ronald Sanders, Antigua’s ambassador to the United States, [wrote to Harvard](https://www.theguardian.com/news/2025/jun/21/harvard-slavery-decendants-of-the-enslaved) to express his disappointment and reiterate that the island would continue to seek “real engagement and meaningful action that befits the benefits that Harvard derived.”

The disbanding of Cellini’s group also prompted yet another resignation from the memorial committee. In his [resignation letter](https://www.thecrimson.com/article/2025/1/28/vincent-brown-resigns-legacy-of-slavery/) —described as “scathing” by *The Harvard Crimson* —Vincent Brown, a professor of African American studies, called Cellini’s dismissal “vindictive and wasteful.”

At the time they were let go, Cellini’s team had documented more than 1,000 individuals enslaved by Harvard affiliates and identified 1,400 direct descendants, nearly 500 of them living. Cellini estimates the true number of living descendants could reach 30,000.

When I asked whether he had weighed the financial implications of that figure against Harvard’s pledge to “engage the descendants,” he replied: “You know, it’s like my 16-year-old son sometimes says: ‘Daddy, that sounds like a ‘you’ problem, not a ‘me’ problem.’ ”

Harvey Mansfield, a Harvard professor for more than six decades and one of the institution’s few conservative voices, believes that the initiative was built on a false premise. Harvard’s legacy, he says, is not one of slavery, but of “anti-slavery.”

“If you go to Harvard, there’s one building that’s the most prominent on the whole campus, and that’s called Memorial Hall,” Mansfield says. “And Memorial Hall was built in memory of the Harvard graduates who died in the Civil War on the Union side. Those people gave their lives.”

Meanwhile, the people behind Harvard’s initiative “have made no sacrifices whatsoever to the campaign against slavery,” he added. “It’s an excellent example of virtue signaling. They’re doing nothing and pretending to be proud of it.”

Cellini believes that Harvard went into the project with an “Uncle Remus, *Song of the South* ” fantasy in mind: that the descendant research would “find an old enslaved woman named Mary who worked in the College Bake House. She loved the students and the students loved her and when she died after 50 years, the students got together and paid for her funeral. Now her great-great-granddaughter from California is studying computer science at Harvard. ‘Like, isn’t that phenomenal?’ ”

When asked to comment for this story, a Harvard spokesperson referred me to an [April 2022 interview](https://news.harvard.edu/gazette/story/2022/04/revealing-webs-of-inequities-rooted-in-slavery-woven-over-centuries/) with Brown-Nagin, the chairman of the committee that wrote the original report. “This is a long-term commitment, and we think that is vitally important,” she said. “This history calls for deep, sustained engagement. We’re seeking to engage and support descendant communities through genuine partnership and by leveraging our expertise, our strengths, consistent with our mission as an institution dedicated to education, research, and service.”

---

The reckoning that Harvard once promised has since morphed into something the university may not be able to achieve. If the number of living descendants indeed reaches 30,000, Harvard’s $100 million commitment works out to $3,333 per person before a dollar is spent on the rest of the initiative’s ambitious projects. Should the university seriously fulfill its pledge of “engagement,” which includes “educational support,” the math becomes difficult: At Harvard, for example, a four-year degree costs upward of $380,000.

Some argue, though, that Harvard’s original pledge was inadequate from the start. Two years after the initiative was launched, Nikole Hannah-Jones, the reporter behind *The New York Times* ’ *1619 Project*, dismissed Harvard’s $100 million commitment as “ [a joke](https://www.thecrimson.com/article/2024/4/24/nikole-hannah-jones-legacy-of-slavery/) ” and insisted that a serious investment would require “hundreds of millions more.” Appearing at a symposium at Harvard dedicated to the program, she proclaimed: “The truth-telling is just the beginning. We’re not even close to a reckoning.”

But Harvard, like any institution invested in its own survival, is not about to bankrupt itself. Its effort will likely follow the well-worn path of local reparations programs across the country: introduced with a bang and then quietly abandoned as the logistics and finances prove untenable.

If an institution as wealthy and presumably as motivated to right old wrongs as Harvard gives up the ghost on reparations, what does that say about the prospect of their wider adoption?

Harvard is already hedging. The Legacy of Slavery Initiative—which has always avoided using the term *reparations—* says it is currently focused solely on identifying the descendants of those who were enslaved by the university’s leaders or labored on its campus. Only once that work is complete will it approach the harder question of what Harvard actually owes the people it finds.
