#### How Can I Know AI isn’t Conscious?

Suppose we soon live in a world where the AI you interact with on a daily basis has indistinguishable behavior from you or any other human you know. It talks to you like a human, it responds to you like a friend would, it produces outputs that indicate hurt feelings if you say something mean or rude. It says things like ‘I’m tired’ or ‘you’re frustrating me’. Frankly, such a world may not be very far off in certain restricted contexts, such as you talking to an AI conversationally online.

A question many ask under such circumstances is, should I attribute a mind to AI in the event that its behavior is indistinguishable from a humans?

Some papers have come out recently arguing that we should.[^1]

You could have many reasons for thinking that you should attribute AI consciousness in the event that its behavior is reliably indistinguishable from yours. Perhaps you have moral concerns about it. If it acts just like me, how can you be sure it isn’t just like you? You might have read a ton of papers suggesting that AI is conscious, thus eroding your confidence in your own skepticism (please don’t let this be your reason tho). Or, you might think that on *epistemic* grounds alone (on the basis of what knowledge you have) you are in no position to deny the AI is conscious. ‘Don’t I attribute consciousness to others based on their behavior?’, you might think, ‘and if I do, am I not compelled by the norms of rational thinking to attribute consciousness to the AI too?’

In this post, I discuss a recent paper by [Shurui Li](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.04588) which argues just this: namely, that for epistemological reasons only, we cannot deny consciousness to (some near future) AI whose behavior is indistinguishable from our own.

The basic epistemic question then, is ‘how can I *know* that AI isn’t conscious?’

This epistemic question has nothing to do with worrying about the moral status of AI. It’s merely concerned with our justifications for knowledge, in this case, our justification for claiming to know that AI is conscious or not conscious.

A lot of work has been done discussing the moral question on AI. And one can understand why this is. But you might be wondering, why be interested in the epistemic question at all? Does it really matter as much as the moral case?

Li makes an interesting argument for why we should care about this question. He claims that failing to attribute consciousness to AI with indistinguishable behavior from a human, undermines the epistemic foundations we have for attributing consciousness in the human case too. The practical upshot? If we *don’t* attribute consciousness to AI’s with behavior just like ours, we revert into a kind of solipsism in which we can never know whether *anyone* is conscious.

Here is how this argument goes. We generally accept that consciousness attributions rely heavily if not completely, as Li puts it, on ‘‘behavioral observation and interactions’’ (more on what this might mean later).

If we are in a scenario where AIs are behaviorally indistinguishable from the human case, then one of two things must be true. Either (1) we are being inconsistent, and simply denying AI consciousness, thereby denying the norms of rationality which demand that under identical circumstances, we apply the same status (of conscious or not conscious) to both humans and AI’s, or (2) we are making an appeal to some sort of non-observable entity, such as qualia, or substrate dependence, to claim that AI’s are not conscious and that humans are

This means that treating AI’s differently to humans commits us to an unprincipled asymmetry that forces us into a dilemma.

**Scenario1** we appeal to some inaccessible or empirically unverifiable qualities such a *s qualia,* or other irreducible first-person properties, to assign consciousness to humans and deny it to AI’s.

**Scenario2** we reject that our justification for attributing minds to others relies on empirical behavior at all. I know that *I* am conscious because I have first-person access to my own consciousness, but I cannot be sure that *others* are conscious.

Scenario 2 reflects the well known ‘problem of other minds’ in philosophy (which I’ve written about [here](https://ellennoraburns.substack.com/publish/post/186338760?back=%2Fpublish%2Fposts%2Fscheduled), and [here](https://ellennoraburns.substack.com/publish/post/188156310?back=%2Fpublish%2Fposts%2Fscheduled)). According to this argument, if we accept that we cannot inferentially attribute consciousness to others based on behavior, we are forced to conclude that we can’t know if anyone except ourselves is conscious.

Similarly, if we claim that we must appeal to unverifiable properties such as qualia for there to be consciousness, we are inadvertently undermining the epistemic justifications we have for attributing minds to other humans. After all, even if one is committed to the *metaphysical* claimthat consciousness requires qualia, one does not, in virtue of this fact, typically believe that they need to confirm other humans have qualia to conclude that they are conscious.

Li’s point, in other words, is not a metaphysical or ontological one (and he is clear on this). His concern is purely epistemic. Namely, that we actually undermine our ability to know that others have minds, if we claim that AI’s with behavior just like ours is not sufficient evidence to know that AI’s have minds.

#### How Do We Attribute Minds to Others?

Li’s paper asserts that ‘‘consciousness attributions rely heavily, if not exclusively, on empirical evidence derived from behavior and interaction’’.[^2] But there are further questions to be asked about what this means.

He is clear that his argument is referring to the problem of other minds, but that is about just as much insight we get, about what ‘appeals to behavior’ could mean in this context (it is common in the literature to state nothing further)

But here’s the thing: the problem of other minds blurs a number of questions together. Here are three separate questions it conflates

(1) Can we justify the belief that others have minds? And how do we do this?

(2) Do we in fact know that others have minds?

(3) How is our knowledge that others have minds, acquired?

These are different questions, even if they are all questions that roughly have to do with ‘epistemology’. The first question asks how we *justify* what we know. It is first and foremost about justification of knowledge. Not knowledge itself.

The second question asks what knowledge we actually have, *regardless* of whether we can justify having that knowledge. This is foremost a question about the nature of our knowledge. E.g., What is it like? What work is it doing in our lives?

The third question asks how we acquire the knowledge that we have, *regardless* of the nature of that knowledge, *and regardless* of whether we can justify why we have that knowledge. This is foremost a question about knowledge acquisition.

These are relatively difficult conceptual distinctions to grasp in the case of minds, in part because we have very little understanding of what a mind is. A simpler case however, because we have a better understanding of it, is in language.

#### The Language Case

In the study of language these questions are easily disentangled. There is the first question of how we can justify that we have knowledge of language (interestingly, this question is not really asked, and I think there could be some lessons to be drawn from the language case for the scenario we are discussing).

There is the second question of what the nature of that linguistic knowledge is. This is the study of syntax, semantics, phonetics, pragmatics, in a word, a variety of different fields, all of which identify the different properties that language has.

Finally, there is the third question, of how human infants acquire that knowledge. This is the study of language acquisition, a thriving field in its own right.

In the case of the problem of other minds, all three of these questions are *collapse* d. It’s merely assumed that our *justification* for how we know that others have minds is the same as our knowledge of the fact that others have minds, is the same as our ability to acquire the knowledge that others have minds.

But we really shouldn’t collapse these three questions. They are very different. Crucially, *it is only if we do collapse them* that Li’s argument is at all persuasive.

#### How Do We Know That Others Have Minds?

Because these questions are collapsed in the case of the attribution of minds, we tend to believe that we know others have minds from observing behavior. But this has to be false. Come on, think about it. Have you ever had the experience of walking into a room full of people, and thinking to yourself, ‘hmmm, let me observe their behavior so that I can decide whether or not they have minds like me’. It never happens. It makes no sense. It would require a wild amount of brain energy. It would be a highly anti-social behavior. The list goes on.

So what’s going on?

It’s worth stating explicitly that the problem of other minds in philosophy, though sometimes formulated in a short hand way to sound like it is a question about knowledge, is explicitly a question about *justifying* knowledge. Which, as I mention, are different questions.

And of course —-putting aside that nobody does the mental gymnastics of observing behavior to establish that people have minds-- if one were forced to attempt to articulate a *justification* for how they know others have minds, they might indeed come up with an answer, like, their behavior is justification.

This in itself is not a problem. The problem is that we have conflated this justification with what we might call ‘the knowledge question’, that is, *knowing that* others have minds. Consider, for instance, if someone asked you to justify how you know that you know how to walk. A reasonable response would be that I observe myself walking all the time! So I’m obviously justified in believing it.

But this shouldn’t be confused with the fact that the process of walking is not due to our observation that we can walk. No amount of observing people walking, will lead me to know how to walk. Our knowledge of walking is deeply mysterious. It is knowledge that is somewhere in our DNA and that develops so that, at a certain development stage in the life of a human being, we start walking.

The same caution should be applied to the problem of other minds. The real issue when it comes to being consistent on knowing whether AI’s have minds, is not related to how we justify knowing that humans have minds. It’s related to the how we know that humans have minds.

#### What Does All of This Mean?

The relevant frameworks for deciding whether there is a fundamental asymmetry going on (as Li argues there is) in the case of attributing minds to humans and AI’s, does not lie in philosophy. It presumably lies in empirical psychology. What is ultimately of interest in knowing how humans know that others have minds, is the developmental stage at which human beings begin to recognize in others, minds that are distinct from their own. It is analogous in other words, to the walking case. A developmental phase that children go through that we can attempt to study in empirical terms, without this meaning we will find all the answers. After all, the closest literature we have to dealing with this issue is ‘theory of mind’ work in psychology which studies how and when children develop the ability to distinguish their minds from the minds of others, a task that *presupposes the existence of minds in others to begin with.*

If I am right about this, then it is probably not true that we *infer* that humans have minds, a traditional philosophical paradigm that many of us have grown up on.

Understanding the mechanisms involved in theory of mind, for instance, and how they are applied, is likely to offer much more insight into how we have knowledge of other minds than philosophy debates are, and it’s not clear that theory of mind is an inferential process in any obvious sense, or that it will ever really answer the question of how we know others have minds. Where philosophy plays a role is figuring out what the right questions to be asking might actually be.

So far, the AI-mind literature doesn’t seem to have landed on those questions.
